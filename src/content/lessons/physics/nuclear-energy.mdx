<Lesson>

<Step title="Where the energy hides" kind="explain">

The energy locked inside a nucleus dwarfs anything chemical. Burning, exploding, digesting — all of these just rearrange the *electrons* on the outside of atoms. Nuclear energy reaches into the **nucleus itself**, where the strong force binds with millions of times more energy per particle.

The key to unlocking it is the most famous equation in physics:

<Formula tex="E = mc^2" block />

It says mass and energy are the same thing in different clothes, exchangeable at a rate set by **c²** — the speed of light, squared. Because c² is an enormous number, even a whisker of vanished mass releases a colossal amount of energy.

</Step>

<Step title="The binding-energy valley" kind="explain">

Here's the surprising key fact: a bound nucleus weighs *less* than its loose protons and neutrons added up separately. That missing mass — the **mass defect** — was converted into the **binding energy** that holds the nucleus together.

How tightly each nucleus is bound depends on its size, and the pattern has a clear bottom.

<Callout type="info" title="Everything rolls toward iron">
Plot binding-energy-per-nucleon against size and you get a valley with **iron** at its lowest point — the most tightly bound, most stable nucleus there is. **Heavy** nuclei release energy by **splitting** toward iron. **Light** nuclei release energy by **joining** toward iron. Either move toward iron sheds mass as energy. Iron itself is the dead end where no more can be extracted.
</Callout>

So there are two roads to nuclear energy, starting from opposite ends.

</Step>

<Step title="Fission: splitting the giants" kind="interactive">

A **heavy** nucleus like uranium-235 is barely held together. Nudge it with a stray neutron and it splits into two lighter fragments — releasing energy *and* a few more neutrons.

Toggle to **fission** and watch.

<FissionFusion />

Those freed neutrons can strike *other* uranium nuclei, splitting them too, freeing yet more neutrons: a self-amplifying **chain reaction**. Let it run wild and you have a bomb. Control it — soaking up just enough neutrons to keep the rate steady — and you have a **nuclear reactor**, boiling water to spin a turbine. This is the nuclear power running on the grid today.

</Step>

<Step title="Fusion: joining the lightweights" kind="interactive">

Now run it the other way. Take the lightest nuclei — isotopes of **hydrogen** — and force them together hard enough to fuse into helium. The product again weighs slightly less than the ingredients, and that lost mass blazes out as energy.

Toggle the visual above to **fusion**. Two hydrogen nuclei merge, spit out a neutron, and release a burst of energy — even more per kilogram than fission.

This is the power source of **every star**, including our Sun: in its crushing core, hydrogen fuses to helium, and the energy released streams out as the sunlight that warms the Earth.

</Step>

<Step title="The promise and the problem" kind="explain">

The two processes pull in opposite directions, not just physically but practically.

- **Fission** works today, but it leaves behind long-lived **radioactive waste** and carries the risk of runaway accidents.
- **Fusion** would be cleaner — its main product is harmless helium, and there's no chain reaction to run away — but it demands temperatures of *millions* of degrees to force the nuclei together. Stars manage it with sheer gravity; on Earth we're still learning to bottle a piece of the Sun.

Decades of research are aimed at making fusion produce more energy than it consumes — a goal that, if reached, would change the world's energy future.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- Nuclear energy comes from converting tiny amounts of **mass into energy**, governed by **E = mc²**.
- A nucleus is bound by its **mass defect**; binding-energy-per-nucleon peaks at **iron**, the most stable nucleus.
- **Fission** splits heavy nuclei (uranium) and can sustain a **chain reaction** — the basis of reactors and bombs.
- **Fusion** joins light nuclei (hydrogen → helium), powers the **stars**, and releases even more energy — but is extraordinarily hard to achieve on Earth.

</Step>
