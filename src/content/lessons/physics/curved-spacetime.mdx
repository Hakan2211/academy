<Step title="Matter tells spacetime how to bend" kind="explain">

The equivalence principle pointed to a breathtaking idea, and in 1915 Einstein turned it into the full theory of **general relativity**. Its core is captured in one famous sentence by the physicist John Wheeler:

> *Spacetime tells matter how to move; matter tells spacetime how to curve.*

Gravity is not a force pulling across empty space. It is the **curvature of spacetime itself**, produced by mass and energy. The Sun doesn't reach out and grab the Earth — it warps the spacetime around it, and the Earth simply coasts along the resulting valley.

</Step>

<Step title="The warped grid" kind="interactive">

The classic picture is a heavy ball resting on a stretched rubber sheet. Place a mass down and the grid of spacetime sags around it.

<SpacetimeWell />

Add mass and the well deepens. The orbiting marble isn't held by a string or pulled by a force — it's just **rolling along the curved surface**, taking the straightest path the warped geometry allows. That's gravity in Einstein's universe: not a pull, but the shape of the space you're moving through.

(Keep this visual in mind — in the next lesson we'll crank the mass to its extreme and watch the well become a black hole.)

</Step>

<Step title="Orbits are “straight lines”" kind="explain">

This reframes motion completely. In curved spacetime, a freely falling object follows a **geodesic** — the straightest possible path through the warp. The Earth orbiting the Sun is, in this view, going as "straight" as it can; the path only *looks* curved because the spacetime it's threading is curved.

It also explains why **free fall feels like weightlessness**. An astronaut in orbit, or you in a falling lift, feels no gravity at all — because you're not being pushed or pulled, just following your natural geodesic through spacetime. You only feel "weight" when the ground shoves you *off* that path.

<Callout type="info" title="The clue Newton couldn't explain">
The planet Mercury's orbit slowly rotates over time — and Newton's gravity got the rate slightly wrong, a discrepancy that puzzled astronomers for decades. General relativity's curved spacetime predicted Mercury's motion *exactly*. It was the theory's first great triumph.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Bending light, lensing galaxies" kind="explain">

If mass curves spacetime, then **everything** travelling through it follows the curve — including light, which has no mass at all. We already saw starlight bend around the Sun in the 1919 eclipse. On grander scales, the effect becomes a cosmic tool.

<Callout type="info" title="Gravitational lensing">
A massive galaxy cluster bends the light from objects far behind it, acting like a giant warped lens. Astronomers see distant galaxies **smeared into arcs**, multiplied into several images, or magnified into view when they'd otherwise be too faint. By measuring how much the light bends, we can even "weigh" the invisible matter doing the bending — one of our main pieces of evidence for **dark matter**.
</Callout>

Light takes the straightest path available; if space itself is curved, that path curves with it. The universe is full of these natural lenses, and we now use them to probe the most distant and most hidden things in the cosmos.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **General relativity**: mass and energy **curve spacetime**, and objects follow the straightest path (a **geodesic**) through it — gravity is geometry, not a force.
- The **warped-grid** picture shows orbits as rolling along the curvature; **free fall feels weightless** because you're following your natural path.
- It fixed the long-standing puzzle of **Mercury's orbit**.
- Curved spacetime bends light, producing **gravitational lensing** — now a tool for mapping distant galaxies and **dark matter**.

</Step>
