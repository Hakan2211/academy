<Step title="Turning motion into electricity" kind="explain">

Induction needs a *changing* field through a coil. Moving a magnet by hand works, but it's clumsy. There's a far better way to keep the flux changing continuously: **spin a coil inside a magnetic field** (or spin a magnet inside a coil).

As the coil rotates, the amount of field passing through it rises and falls over and over — so a voltage is induced continuously. That spinning machine is a **generator**, and it's where nearly all the world's electricity comes from. A motor run backwards, in effect: motion in, electricity out.

</Step>

<Step title="Why the output alternates" kind="interactive">

Watch the coil turn and the output voltage trace itself out.

<Generator />

As the coil sweeps through the field, the induced voltage rises to a peak, falls back to zero, then reverses as the coil's sides swap places — producing a smooth back-and-forth wave. This is **alternating current (AC)**: the voltage cycles positive and negative, once per rotation. Spin faster and the cycles come quicker *and* climb higher.

</Step>

<Step title="AC versus DC" kind="explain">

This naturally-produced alternating current is different from a battery's steady **direct current (DC)**, which always flows one way.

- A plain generator produces **AC** — and as you'll see next lesson, AC turns out to be a huge advantage for delivering power.
- Add a **commutator** (the same half-turn switch from the motor) and you can flip every other half-cycle to get bumpy **DC** instead — a **dynamo**, like an old bicycle light.

So the same spinning machine can give you AC or DC depending on how you tap it.

</Step>

<Step title="Where your power really comes from" kind="explain">

Here's the secret behind the entire power grid: almost every power station, whatever its fuel, does the **same final thing** — spin a generator.

<Callout type="info" title="It's all spinning magnets">
- **Coal, gas, nuclear**: heat water into steam, and the steam spins a turbine.
- **Hydro and wind**: falling water or moving air spins the turbine directly.
- The turbine turns the generator, and induction does the rest.
</Callout>

The fuel just provides the *rotation*. Whether it's a hydroelectric dam or a wind farm, the electricity itself is made by Faraday's induction — a coil turning in a magnetic field.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- A **generator** spins a coil in a magnetic field, continuously changing the flux to induce a voltage — motion in, electricity out.
- The output naturally **alternates (AC)**, cycling once per rotation; faster spin gives higher frequency and voltage.
- A **commutator** can convert it to **DC** (a dynamo).
- Nearly all power stations work by **spinning a generator** — only the source of rotation differs.

</Step>
