<Step title="What is everything made of?" kind="explain">

You've met the atom: a nucleus of protons and neutrons, wrapped in electrons. But it goes deeper. The electron seems truly **fundamental** — no smaller parts — while protons and neutrons are *not*: each is built from smaller particles called **quarks**.

Through the twentieth century, accelerators smashing particles together revealed a bewildering "**zoo**" of new ones. The triumph of modern physics was to tame that zoo into a single, elegant scheme — the **Standard Model** — listing every known fundamental building block of reality.

</Step>

Two families of **matter particles** — six **quarks** and six **leptons** (the electron and its cousins, plus the ghostly neutrinos) — then the **force carriers** that bind them, and finally the **Higgs**. That's the whole cast. Stars, planets, and you are assembled entirely from it.

</Step>

<Step title="Matter: quarks and leptons" kind="explain">

The twelve matter particles come in three **generations**, each a heavier copy of the last.

- **Quarks** feel the strong force and never appear alone — they clump together. Two *up* quarks and one *down* make a **proton**; two *down* and one *up* make a **neutron**.
- **Leptons** include the familiar **electron** and the nearly-massless **neutrinos** that stream through you by the trillion every second, barely interacting at all.

Remarkably, everything in ordinary matter is built from just the **first generation**: up quarks, down quarks, and electrons. The heavier generations exist but decay away in fractions of a second.

</Step>

<Step title="Forces as exchanged particles" kind="explain">

In the Standard Model, a force is what happens when matter particles **exchange** a force-carrier particle (a *boson*) — like two skaters tossing a ball and recoiling apart.

- The **photon** carries **electromagnetism** (it's the particle of light itself).
- **Gluons** carry the **strong force**, gluing quarks into protons and neutrons.
- The **W and Z bosons** carry the **weak force**, responsible for radioactive beta decay — turning one quark flavour into another.

Three of nature's four forces fit this picture beautifully. The fourth — **gravity** — stubbornly does *not*, and that gap is one of physics' deepest open problems.

</Step>

<Step title="The Higgs: where mass comes from" kind="explain">

One question haunted the model: why do particles have **mass** at all? The answer is the **Higgs field** — an invisible field filling all of space. Particles gain mass by "dragging" through it; those that interact strongly feel heavy, those that don't (like the photon) stay massless.

Every field has a particle, and in 2012 the **Large Hadron Collider** finally detected the Higgs field's particle — the **Higgs boson** — confirming a prediction made nearly fifty years earlier.

<Callout type="info" title="A theory tested to absurd precision">
The Standard Model is the most rigorously tested theory in all of science. Some of its predictions match experiment to better than one part in a billion. It is, by that measure, the most successful description of nature ever written down.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="What it leaves out" kind="explain">

For all its success, the Standard Model is known to be **incomplete**. It says nothing about **gravity**. It offers no explanation for **dark matter** or **dark energy**, which together outweigh everything it describes. It doesn't say why there are exactly three generations, or why the masses take the values they do.

So the most successful theory in physics is also a signpost pointing beyond itself — toward a deeper theory we haven't yet found. That frontier is where the final lesson of this unit goes.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- The **Standard Model** catalogues every known fundamental particle.
- **Matter** = six **quarks** + six **leptons**, in three generations (ordinary matter uses just the first).
- **Quarks** build protons and neutrons; **neutrinos** barely interact at all.
- **Forces** act by exchanging **bosons**: photon (EM), gluons (strong), W & Z (weak).
- The **Higgs boson** (found 2012) confirms the field that gives particles **mass**.
- It's the best-tested theory ever — yet omits **gravity, dark matter, and dark energy**, so it can't be the final word.

</Step>
