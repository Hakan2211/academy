<Lesson>

<Step title="Three faces of stuff" kind="explain">

Ice, water, steam — three completely different things, yet all of them are just **H₂O**. Nothing was added or removed between them. So what actually changed?

Most everyday matter shows up in one of three **states**, and we tell them apart by how they handle *shape* and *volume*:

- **Solid** — keeps its own shape and its own volume. A brick stays a brick.
- **Liquid** — keeps its volume but takes the shape of its container. Water becomes glass-shaped in a glass.
- **Gas** — keeps neither. It expands to fill whatever space it can reach.

</Step>

<Step title="It's all about the particles" kind="interactive">

Zoom in far enough and every material is a swarm of particles. The *state* is simply how tightly those particles are bound and how much they move.

<StatesOfMatter />

- In a **solid**, particles are locked into a fixed pattern and only vibrate on the spot — so the shape holds.
- In a **liquid**, they still touch but can slide past one another — so it flows.
- In a **gas**, they've broken free and fly around independently — so it fills everything.

Same particles every time. The only difference is how much energy they carry.

</Step>

<Step title="Energy changes the state" kind="explain">

Add energy — usually as **heat** — and particles move faster until they break their bonds and jump to a looser state. Take energy away and they settle back down. Each crossing has a name:

- Solid → liquid: **melting**.  Liquid → solid: **freezing**.
- Liquid → gas: **boiling / evaporating**.  Gas → liquid: **condensing**.
- Solid → gas directly: **sublimation** (think dry ice, or frost vanishing on a cold morning).

Heating and cooling don't create or destroy the substance — they just move it up and down this ladder.

</Step>

<Step title="The fourth state" kind="explain">

There's one more state most people never meet by name: **plasma**. Heat a gas hot enough and the atoms start tearing apart into charged pieces. It sounds exotic, but plasma is actually the *most common* state of matter in the universe — every star, including the Sun, is a ball of it. Lightning and neon signs are plasma too.

<Callout type="info" title="Beyond the big four">
Push matter to extremes and even stranger states appear — like the **Bose–Einstein condensate**, formed a hair above absolute zero, where atoms blur into a single quantum blob. States of matter go far past the three you can find in your kitchen.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- Matter usually appears as **solid**, **liquid**, or **gas** — distinguished by whether it holds its shape and volume.
- The difference is **particle arrangement and energy**: locked & vibrating → sliding & touching → free & flying.
- Adding or removing **heat** moves matter between states (melting, freezing, boiling, condensing, subliming).
- A hot fourth state, **plasma**, is actually the most abundant in the universe.

</Step>
