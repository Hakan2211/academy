<Step title="The Sun's family" kind="explain">

Our solar system is the Sun and everything gravity binds to it: eight **planets** and their moons, dwarf planets, asteroids, comets, and dust. One fact dwarfs all the rest — the **Sun contains about 99.8% of all the mass**. Everything else, Jupiter included, is a rounding error orbiting a giant ball of glowing plasma.

That overwhelming mass is exactly why the planets orbit the Sun and not the other way around: the Sun's gravity rules the entire system.

</Step>

<Step title="Orbits, and Kepler's rule" kind="interactive">

The planets travel around the Sun on paths that are nearly circular **ellipses**. Watch them move:

<SolarSystem />

Notice the pattern: **inner planets orbit faster** than outer ones. Mercury laps the Sun in just 88 days; Neptune takes 165 *years*. This is **Kepler's third law** — the farther out a planet is, the slower it travels and the longer its year — and it falls straight out of the gravity you met in *Forces & Motion*: gravity weakens with distance, so distant planets are held more gently and need less speed to stay in orbit.

</Step>

<Step title="Two families of planets" kind="explain">

The eight planets split cleanly into two groups, divided by the asteroid belt:

- **Terrestrial (rocky) planets** — Mercury, Venus, Earth, Mars. Small, dense, solid surfaces, close to the Sun.
- **Giant planets** — Jupiter and Saturn (gas giants) and Uranus and Neptune (ice giants). Huge, mostly hydrogen, helium and ices, with rings and many moons.

Why the split? Close to the young Sun it was too hot for ices to survive, so only rock and metal could clump together. Farther out, beyond the "frost line," gas and ice were abundant — so those planets grew enormous.

<Callout type="info" title="What happened to Pluto?">
Pluto was a planet until 2006, when astronomers found it's just one of many icy bodies out past Neptune. It was reclassified a **dwarf planet** — round like a planet, but not massive enough to have cleared its orbital neighbourhood of other objects. It didn't change; our definition did.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="The small stuff and the far edge" kind="explain">

Between and beyond the planets drifts a great deal of leftover debris from the solar system's birth:

- The **asteroid belt** between Mars and Jupiter holds millions of rocky chunks that never formed a planet — kept stirred up by Jupiter's gravity.
- **Comets** are icy "dirty snowballs" from the cold outer system. When one swings near the Sun, its ice vaporises into the glowing tail we see from Earth.
- Far past Neptune lie the **Kuiper Belt** (home to Pluto) and, vastly farther out, the **Oort Cloud** — a shell of icy bodies marking the true edge of the Sun's domain, perhaps a thousand times farther than Neptune.

All of it formed together, 4.6 billion years ago, from one collapsing cloud of gas and dust.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- The **Sun holds ~99.8% of the system's mass**, so its gravity governs everything.
- Planets follow elliptical orbits; **inner planets orbit faster** (**Kepler's third law**), an echo of gravity weakening with distance.
- Two families: small **rocky** inner planets and giant **gas/ice** outer planets, split by the **asteroid belt**.
- Leftover **asteroids, comets**, the **Kuiper Belt** and **Oort Cloud** fill out and bound the system — all born together 4.6 billion years ago.

</Step>
