<Step title="The accident that linked two worlds" kind="explain">

For centuries, electricity and magnetism were thought to be entirely separate mysteries. Then, in 1820, the Danish physicist **Hans Christian Ørsted** was giving a lecture when he noticed something nobody expected: a compass needle twitched whenever he switched on a nearby electric current.

That tiny twitch was a revolution. It meant **an electric current creates a magnetic field**. Electricity and magnetism weren't separate after all — they were two faces of one thing. Almost every technology in this unit flows from that single observation.

</Step>

<Step title="The field around a wire" kind="interactive">

The magnetic field around a straight current-carrying wire doesn't point along the wire or radiate outward — it wraps around it in **circles**.

<WireField />

Which way do the circles point? The **right-hand rule**: point your right thumb along the (conventional) current, and your curled fingers show the field's direction. Reverse the current and the field circles reverse with it. Turn the current up and the field grows stronger.

</Step>

<Step title="What sets the field's strength" kind="explain">

The magnetic field around a wire depends on two things:

- **The current** — more amps make a stronger field. Double the current, double the field.
- **The distance** — the field weakens the farther you get from the wire (it falls off with distance).

So the field is strongest hugging the wire and fades into the space around it. A single straight wire makes only a weak, spread-out field — which is why, in the next lesson, we'll coil it up to concentrate it.

</Step>

<Step title="Why this changes everything" kind="explain">

Ørsted's link is the hinge the rest of this unit turns on. Once you know current makes a magnetic field, a cascade of inventions follows:

- Coil the wire and you get a switchable **electromagnet**.
- Put that field near a magnet and you get a force — an **electric motor**.
- And, as Faraday would later ask, if current makes magnetism, can magnetism make current? (It can — that's **induction**, the basis of generators.)

Every one of these is just Ørsted's discovery, engineered.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Ørsted (1820)**: an electric current produces a magnetic field — linking electricity and magnetism.
- Around a straight wire, the field forms **concentric circles**; the **right-hand rule** gives its direction.
- Field strength grows with **current** and weakens with **distance**.
- This single fact underlies **electromagnets, motors, and generators** — the whole rest of the unit.

</Step>
