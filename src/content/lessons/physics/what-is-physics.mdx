<Lesson>

<Step title="The search for rules" kind="explain">

Drop a stone and it falls. Pluck a string and it sings. Heat a pot and the water boils. The world is full of happenings — and **physics** is the bet that underneath them all lie a small number of simple, dependable *rules*.

Physics is the science of matter, energy, motion, and the forces between them. Its quiet ambition is enormous: to describe the entire physical universe, from a falling apple to a colliding galaxy, with the **same** handful of laws. Astonishingly, it largely works.

Before any of those laws, though, comes a *method* — a disciplined way of asking nature questions and trusting only the answers it gives back.

</Step>

<Step title="How science actually works" kind="interactive">

Science isn't a pile of facts to memorise. It's a **process** — a loop you ride again and again, each turn sharpening your understanding.

<ScientificMethod />

You observe something, propose an explanation, work out what that explanation predicts, then test it against reality. The result sends you round again. There's no final step where you "finish" — every good answer breeds a better question.

</Step>

<Step title="A guess that can be wrong" kind="explain">

The heart of the loop is the **hypothesis** — a proposed explanation. But not just any guess will do. A scientific hypothesis must be **testable** and, crucially, **falsifiable**: there has to be some possible result that would prove it *wrong*.

"Heavier objects fall faster" is a fine scientific claim — you can test it and, as it happens, show it's false. "Invisible undetectable fairies push things down" is not, because no experiment could ever contradict it. If nothing could prove a claim wrong, science has nothing to say about it.

<Callout type="tip" title="Why falsifiability matters">
A theory that forbids nothing explains nothing. The power of a law like F = ma is precisely that it sticks its neck out — it makes sharp predictions that an experiment could shatter. That it survives test after test is what earns our trust.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="From laws to models" kind="explain">

When a tested pattern holds up everywhere we look, we call it a **law** — like the law of gravity. To *use* a law on a messy real situation, we build a **model**: a deliberately simplified picture that keeps what matters and ignores the rest.

Treating a planet as a single point, or ignoring air resistance on a falling ball, isn't cheating — it's the whole art of physics. A good model is "wrong" in its details but **right** about the essentials, and simple enough to actually calculate. The skill is knowing what you can safely leave out.

</Step>

<Step title="Galileo drops the ball" kind="explain">

For 2,000 years everyone "knew" heavy things fall faster — Aristotle had said so. Around 1600, **Galileo** did something radical: he *tested* it. Rolling balls down ramps and timing them, he found that, ignoring air, all objects fall with the **same** acceleration, whatever their weight.

That's the method in one story: an old authority, a testable claim, a real experiment, and a result that overturns centuries of belief. Nature, not reputation, gets the last word.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Physics** seeks the small set of rules behind all physical phenomena.
- It advances by a **method**, not a list of facts: observe → hypothesise → predict → experiment → refine, on a loop.
- A scientific hypothesis must be **testable and falsifiable** — some result could prove it wrong.
- We capture patterns as **laws** and apply them through simplified **models**.
- As Galileo showed, **evidence outranks authority** — nature has the final say.

</Step>
