<Lesson>

<Step title="A question Maxwell left hanging" kind="explain">

At the end of the magnetism unit, Maxwell's equations delivered a bombshell: light is an electromagnetic wave travelling at a fixed speed, **c ≈ 300,000 km/s**. But the equations were strangely silent on an obvious question — *fixed relative to what?*

Every other speed you know is relative. A ball thrown at 20 km/h on a train doing 100 km/h moves at 120 km/h past the platform. So light from a fast-moving source should surely come at you faster than light from a slow one. Maxwell's theory flatly refused to include any such adjustment. For decades that looked like a flaw. It turned out to be the doorway into a new physics.

</Step>

<Step title="Galileo's common sense" kind="explain">

First, the intuition we're about to lose. **Galilean relativity** says velocities simply add. Walk forward at 5 km/h inside a train moving at 100 km/h, and the ground sees you doing 105 km/h. Throw a ball forward and its speeds add the same way.

This feels so obvious it's hard to imagine it failing. It's also genuinely useful — it's how motion works at every speed you'll ever personally experience. The trouble only appears when something travels close to the speed of light. And one thing always does: light itself.

</Step>

<Step title="The light that refuses to add up" kind="interactive">

Experiments at the end of the 1800s tried to detect Earth's motion by measuring how it changed the speed of light. The most famous, by **Michelson and Morley**, was sensitive enough to catch the effect easily. It found *nothing*. The speed of light was the same in every direction, whatever the Earth was doing.

Compare a thrown ball with a light pulse from a moving source.

<LightPostulate />

The ball obeys Galileo — speed up the source and the ball's measured speed climbs. But the light's speed sits stubbornly at **c**, no matter how fast the source moves or how fast you chase it. Nature was sending an unmistakable message, and it made no classical sense.

</Step>

<Step title="Einstein's two postulates" kind="explain">

In 1905, a 26-year-old patent clerk named **Albert Einstein** took the strange result at face value and built a whole new theory — **special relativity** — on just two assumptions:

1. **The laws of physics are the same** for every observer moving at constant velocity. (No experiment can tell you your "absolute" speed.)
2. **The speed of light in a vacuum is the same — c — for every such observer**, regardless of the motion of the source or the observer.

The second postulate is the radical one, and it has a staggering price. If everyone always measures light at the same speed — even observers moving relative to each other — then the things we *use* to measure speed, namely **distance and time**, cannot be absolute. Space and time themselves must bend to keep c constant. The next lessons follow exactly what bends, and by how much.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- Maxwell's fixed light speed **c** raised the question "relative to what?" — and experiment (**Michelson–Morley**) found c is the same in every direction.
- **Galilean relativity** (velocities add) works for everyday objects but fails for light.
- Einstein's **two postulates**: the laws of physics are the same for all constant-velocity observers, and **light always travels at c** for all of them.
- Holding c constant forces **space and time themselves to be relative** — the theme of this whole unit.

</Step>
