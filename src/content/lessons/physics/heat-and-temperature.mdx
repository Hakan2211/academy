<Lesson>

<Step title="Not the same thing" kind="explain">

We use *hot* and *heat* loosely, but physics keeps two ideas apart:

- **Temperature** — how vigorously the particles in something are jiggling, on average.
- **Heat** — energy that *flows* from a hotter thing to a colder one.

Temperature is a measurement; heat is a transfer. Mixing them up is the root of most confusion about thermal energy.

</Step>

<Step title="Temperature is average motion" kind="interactive">

Zoom into any material and you'll find its particles in ceaseless motion. **Temperature is just the average kinetic energy** of that jiggling. Heat the box and they fly faster; cool it and they nearly freeze in place.

<ThermalJiggle />

There's a floor to this: at **absolute zero** (−273 °C) the jiggling would stop entirely. You can't get colder than *no motion at all*.

</Step>

<Step title="Heat flows downhill" kind="explain">

Put a warm spoon in cold water and energy always flows the **same way**: from hot to cold, never the reverse, until both reach the same temperature.

The fast-jiggling particles bump the slow ones, sharing their motion. This is **thermal equilibrium** — not because the cold "pulls" energy, but because random collisions even things out.

</Step>

<Step title="Big and cool can beat small and hot" kind="explain">

A single spark from a sparkler is over 1000 °C, yet it barely stings — because it's tiny. A bathtub of warm water is far cooler but holds vastly **more total thermal energy**, because it has so many more particles.

<Callout type="info" title="The distinction in one line">
**Temperature** = how fast each particle moves (average). **Thermal energy** = that, times *how many* particles there are.
</Callout>

So a small, blistering-hot object can carry less energy than a big, lukewarm one.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Temperature** = the *average* kinetic energy of jiggling particles.
- **Heat** = thermal energy *flowing* from hot to cold until equilibrium.
- At **absolute zero** the motion stops — you can't go lower.
- **Total thermal energy** depends on temperature *and* the number of particles, so big-and-cool can beat small-and-hot.

</Step>
