<Lesson>

<Step title="A force on a wire" kind="explain">

You've seen that a current creates its own magnetic field. So what happens if you put a current-carrying wire **inside another magnetic field** — say, between the poles of a magnet?

The wire's field and the magnet's field interact, pushing on each other. The result is a **force on the wire** that shoves it sideways. This is the **motor effect** — the phenomenon that turns electrical energy into motion, and the beating heart of every electric motor.

</Step>

<Step title="Fleming's left-hand rule" kind="interactive">

The force isn't along the current or along the field — it's at **right angles to both**. To find its direction, use **Fleming's left-hand rule**: thumb, first finger, and second finger held mutually perpendicular stand for **F**orce (thumb), **F**ield (first finger), and **C**urrent (second finger).

<MotorEffect />

Reverse the current **or** the field and the force flips. Reverse **both** and it stays the same. Try the toggles and watch the wire's push change direction.

</Step>

<Step title="How big is the force?" kind="explain">

The size of the force depends on three things, captured in a tidy equation:

<Formula tex="F = B \, I \, L" block />

- **B** — the strength of the magnetic field,
- **I** — the current in the wire,
- **L** — the length of wire sitting in the field.

Stronger field, more current, or more wire in the field — each one increases the push. (The force is greatest when the wire is at right angles to the field, and drops to zero if the wire runs *along* the field.)

</Step>

<Step title="Making it spin: the electric motor" kind="explain">

Now the payoff. Bend the wire into a **loop** in the field. The current runs *one way* down one side of the loop and the *other way* back up the opposite side — so the motor effect pushes one side **up** and the other side **down**. That pair of opposite forces makes the loop **rotate**.

<Callout type="info" title="The commutator trick">
There's a catch: after half a turn, each side would be pushed the wrong way and the loop would stall. A clever switch called a **commutator** reverses the current every half-turn, so the push always keeps it spinning the same way. That's a working **electric motor** — in your fan, drill, washing machine, and electric car.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- The **motor effect**: a current-carrying wire in a magnetic field feels a **force**, at right angles to both current and field.
- **Fleming's left-hand rule** gives the direction; reversing current *or* field flips it.
- Force size: **F = B I L** — bigger field, current, or wire length means more push.
- A current **loop** in a field rotates, and a **commutator** keeps it spinning — that's an **electric motor**.

</Step>
