<Lesson>

<Step title="Turning is accelerating" kind="explain">

Swing a ball on a string in a steady circle. Its **speed** is constant — but its **velocity** isn't, because velocity includes direction, and the direction is changing every instant.

Changing velocity means **acceleration**, and acceleration needs a **force**. So even at constant speed, going in a circle requires a continuous net force.

</Step>

<Step title="Which way does it point?" kind="interactive">

Watch the two arrows. The **velocity** always points *along* the circle (tangent). The **force** always points *inward*, toward the center.

<CircularMotion />

<Callout type="tip" title="Notice">
The inward force never speeds the ball up or slows it down — it only bends its path, constantly redirecting the velocity.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Centripetal force" kind="explain">

That center-pointing force is the **centripetal force** ("center-seeking"). Its size grows with speed and shrinks with the radius:

<Formula tex="a = \dfrac{v^2}{r}" block />

Something real always provides it: the **tension** in a string, **gravity** for an orbiting moon, **friction** for a car rounding a bend, the track pushing on a looping roller-coaster.

</Step>

<Step title="There is no outward force" kind="explain">

When a car turns and you're "thrown against the door," it feels like a force flings you outward. There isn't one. Your body's **inertia** wants to keep going straight; it's the **car that turns into you**, and the door then pushes you inward along the curve.

That phantom outward "centrifugal force" is just inertia seen from inside the spinning frame.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- Moving in a circle is **accelerating**, because direction (and so velocity) constantly changes.
- The required force is **centripetal** — always toward the center: **a = v²/r**.
- The "outward" feeling is **inertia**, not a real outward force.

<Formula tex="a = \dfrac{v^2}{r}" block />

</Step>
