<Lesson>

<Step title="Changing the voltage" kind="explain">

A generator gives you AC at some voltage — but the grid needs to *change* that voltage many times between the power station and your phone charger. The device that does it is the **transformer**, and it runs entirely on induction.

It's beautifully simple: two coils wound on a shared iron core. AC in the **primary** coil makes the core's magnetic field rise and fall, and that changing field induces a voltage in the **secondary** coil. No moving parts, no electrical connection between the two sides — just a shared, changing magnetic field.

</Step>

<Step title="The turns ratio" kind="interactive">

The trick is in the number of turns on each coil. The output voltage scales with the **ratio of turns**.

<Transformer />

<Formula tex="\dfrac{V_{\text{out}}}{V_{\text{in}}} = \dfrac{N_{\text{out}}}{N_{\text{in}}}" block />

- **More** turns on the secondary than the primary → voltage goes **up** (a *step-up* transformer).
- **Fewer** turns → voltage goes **down** (*step-down*).

Drag the secondary turns and watch the output voltage track the ratio exactly.

</Step>

<Step title="Why AC won the day" kind="explain">

Here's the crucial limitation: a transformer **only works on AC**. It needs a *changing* magnetic field to induce anything — and steady DC produces no change once it's flowing, so a DC transformer does nothing.

This was the heart of the 19th-century **"war of the currents."** DC couldn't easily change voltage, but AC could — cheaply and efficiently — using transformers. That single advantage is why the world's grid runs on **alternating current** to this day.

</Step>

<Step title="Sending power across a country" kind="explain">

Why bother changing the voltage at all? Because it's the key to moving power long distances without wasting it.

<Callout type="info" title="High voltage = low loss">
Power lost heating the cables is <Formula tex="P_{\text{loss}} = I^2 R" />. Since power delivered is `P = V × I`, sending it at very **high voltage** means very **low current** — and because the loss depends on current *squared*, cutting the current slashes the waste dramatically.
</Callout>

So the grid **steps up** to hundreds of thousands of volts for transmission across pylons, then a chain of substation transformers **steps it back down** to the safe ~230 V at your wall socket. Generators, transformers, and the grid are induction, working at national scale.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- A **transformer** uses two coils on one core: changing flux from the primary induces a voltage in the secondary.
- Output follows the **turns ratio**: `V_out/V_in = N_out/N_in` — step **up** (more turns) or **down** (fewer).
- Transformers work **only on AC** (they need a changing field) — the reason the grid is AC.
- The grid steps voltage **up for transmission** (low current → low `I²R` loss) and **down for homes**.
