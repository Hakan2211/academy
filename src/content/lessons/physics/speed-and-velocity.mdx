<Lesson>

<Step title="How fast? — speed" kind="explain">

**Speed** answers a simple question: how much ground do you cover in how much time?

<Formula tex="\text{speed} = \dfrac{\text{distance}}{\text{time}}" block />

Cover 100 m in 10 s and your speed is 10 m/s. Speed is a **scalar** — just a number, no direction.

</Step>

<Step title="Speed with a direction — velocity" kind="explain">

**Velocity** is speed *plus the direction you're heading*. Watch these two objects: they move at the **same speed**, but one goes right and one goes left — so their **velocities are opposite**.

<SpeedCompare />

We write velocity as displacement over time, and its sign tells you the direction:

<Formula tex="v = \dfrac{\Delta x}{\Delta t}" block />

So two cars both doing 30 m/s — one north, one south — have the **same speed** but **different velocities**.

</Step>

<Step title="Average vs in-the-moment" kind="explain">

A 2-hour road trip might average 60 km/h — even though you sped up, slowed down, and stopped for coffee.

- **Average velocity** uses the *total* displacement over the *total* time.
- **Instantaneous velocity** is what the **speedometer** shows: your velocity *right now*.

Both are useful; just be clear about which one you mean.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Speed** = distance ÷ time — a scalar (size only).
- **Velocity** = speed **with direction** — a vector. Opposite directions ⇒ opposite-sign velocities, even at the same speed.
- **Average** velocity is over a whole trip; **instantaneous** is the speedometer reading now.

<Formula tex="v = \dfrac{\Delta x}{\Delta t}" block />

</Step>
