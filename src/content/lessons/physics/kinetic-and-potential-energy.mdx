<Lesson>

<Step title="The two energies of motion" kind="explain">

Most everyday energy lives in two complementary forms:

- **Kinetic energy** — the energy a thing has *because it's moving*.
- **Potential energy** — the energy a thing has *because of where it is*, ready to be released.

A boulder at a cliff edge has lots of potential energy and zero kinetic. Push it off, and that potential turns into kinetic as it falls.

</Step>

<Step title="Kinetic energy: speed matters most" kind="explain">

The faster something moves — and the heavier it is — the more kinetic energy it carries:

<Formula tex="E_k = \tfrac{1}{2} m v^2" block />

Notice the **v²**. Double the speed and kinetic energy *quadruples*. That's why a car at 60 km/h is far more than twice as dangerous as one at 30 — it's carrying four times the energy.

</Step>

<Step title="Potential energy: height matters" kind="explain">

Lift something up and you store **gravitational potential energy** in it. The higher and heavier it is, the more it stores:

<Formula tex="E_p = m g h" block />

Here **g ≈ 9.8 m/s²** and **h** is the height. Let go, and gravity converts every bit of that stored energy back into motion.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Kinetic energy** = energy of motion: **Eₖ = ½ m v²** (grows with the *square* of speed).
- **Potential energy** = stored energy of position: **Eₚ = m g h**.
- As things rise and fall, energy flows back and forth between the two — one peaks exactly where the other vanishes.

<Formula tex="E_k = \tfrac{1}{2} m v^2 \qquad E_p = m g h" block />

</Step>
