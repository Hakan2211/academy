<Step title="How far is that star?" kind="explain">

Look up at night and every star sits on the same flat-looking dome. Nothing tells you which are near and which are unimaginably far. Yet astronomers can quote distances to stars and galaxies with confidence. How?

You can't stretch a tape measure to a star. Instead, astronomers built a clever **chain of methods**, each measuring a little farther and calibrating the next. It starts with a trick your own eyes use every day.

</Step>

<Step title="Parallax: the cosmic tape measure" kind="interactive">

Hold a finger up and blink one eye, then the other — your finger jumps against the background. That's **parallax**, and the closer the finger, the bigger the jump. Astronomers do the same with the widest baseline they have: the **Earth's orbit**, viewing a star from opposite sides six months apart.

<Parallax />

A nearby star shifts against the distant background; a far one barely moves. Measure the shift and simple geometry gives the distance. This defines the **parsec** — the distance at which a star shows a parallax of one arcsecond (about 3.26 light-years). Parallax is the bedrock of all cosmic distances — but it only works for relatively nearby stars before the shift becomes too tiny to detect.

</Step>

<Step title="Climbing the distance ladder" kind="explain">

To reach farther, astronomers use **standard candles** — objects whose true brightness they know. Compare true brightness to how bright it *appears*, and the dimming reveals the distance, exactly as a known lightbulb looks fainter the farther away it is.

<Callout type="info" title="The cosmic distance ladder">
Each rung calibrates the next. **Parallax** measures nearby stars directly. Among those are **Cepheid variable** stars, which pulse at a rate tied to their true brightness — so we can use them as standard candles out to nearby galaxies. Farther still, exploding **Type Ia supernovae** all peak at nearly the same brightness, blazing bright enough to be seen billions of light-years away. Rung by rung, the ladder reaches across the observable universe.
</Callout>

No single method spans everything — but chained together, they let us map the cosmos.

</Step>

<Step title="A telescope is a time machine" kind="explain">

Distance in astronomy comes with a profound twist. Light is fast but not infinitely so, and space is vast — so light takes **time** to arrive. We never see distant objects as they are *now*, only as they *were* when their light left.

- The Sun you see is **8 minutes** old.
- The nearest star is seen as it was **4 years** ago.
- A galaxy a billion light-years away appears as it was a **billion years** ago.

So looking deep into space is literally looking **back in time**. The farther we peer, the younger the universe we observe — which, in the coming lessons, lets us actually watch cosmic history unfold and see the universe in its infancy.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- We can't measure cosmic distances directly, so astronomers use a calibrated **chain of methods**.
- **Parallax** — a star's shift against the background as Earth orbits — measures nearby stars and defines the **parsec**.
- **Standard candles** (Cepheids, Type Ia supernovae) extend the **distance ladder** across the universe.
- Because light takes time to travel, **looking far is looking back in time** — distant objects are seen as they were long ago.

</Step>
