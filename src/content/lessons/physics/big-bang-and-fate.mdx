<Step title="The boldest idea in science" kind="explain">

The expanding universe carries a breathtaking implication: the cosmos had a **beginning**. Around 13.8 billion years ago, everything we can see — all the matter, energy, space, and time itself — emerged from a state of unimaginable heat and density and has been expanding and cooling ever since. This is the **Big Bang** theory.

It's worth being precise about one thing: the Big Bang was not an explosion *into* empty space, like a bomb going off somewhere. It was the rapid expansion *of space itself*, happening everywhere at once. There was no center, because there was no "outside." Let's follow the story forward.

</Step>

Each era we can probe with real evidence. Three pieces of that evidence are worth dwelling on.

<Step title="The first three minutes" kind="explain">

When the universe was just seconds to minutes old, it was a furnace at billions of degrees — hot enough for the **nucleosynthesis** you met in the atoms unit. Protons and neutrons fused into the very first nuclei: mostly hydrogen, about a quarter helium, and a trace of lithium.

This is a sharp, testable prediction. The theory says the universe should be about **75% hydrogen and 25% helium** by mass — and when we measure the most ancient, unprocessed gas in the cosmos, that's *exactly* what we find. The Big Bang correctly predicts the recipe of the early universe.

</Step>

<Step title="The afterglow of creation" kind="explain">

For its first ~380,000 years the universe was an opaque, glowing plasma — light couldn't travel far without scattering off free electrons. Then it cooled enough for electrons to join nuclei into neutral atoms, the fog cleared, and light streamed free for the first time.

<Callout type="info" title="The cosmic microwave background">
That ancient light is still arriving today, stretched by 13.8 billion years of cosmic expansion from a fierce glow into faint microwaves: the **cosmic microwave background (CMB)**. Discovered by accident in 1965, it fills the entire sky, matches the predicted temperature of about **2.7 K** with astonishing precision, and is the single strongest piece of evidence for the Big Bang. Its tiny temperature ripples — just thousandths of a degree — are the seeds from which all galaxies grew.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="From ripples to galaxies" kind="explain">

Those faint ripples in the CMB mark places where matter was very slightly denser. Over hundreds of millions of years, **gravity** amplified them: denser regions pulled in more matter, collapsed into the first stars, gathered into galaxies, and wove the **cosmic web** you saw earlier.

But the visible matter couldn't have done it alone — there wasn't enough gravity, acting quickly enough, to grow structure from such tiny seeds. Something invisible provided the extra scaffolding. That something is one of the deepest mysteries in physics.

</Step>

<Step title="The dark universe" kind="explain">

Add up everything we can see — stars, gas, galaxies — and it accounts for only a small slice of what must be there. The modern inventory of the universe is humbling:

- **~5% ordinary matter** — everything made of atoms: stars, planets, gas, you.
- **~27% dark matter** — invisible mass that neither emits nor absorbs light, betrayed only by its gravity (it bends light via lensing, holds galaxies together, and built the cosmic web). We don't know what it is.
- **~68% dark energy** — a mysterious energy filling space, driving its expansion.

In other words, the matter you've spent this whole physics course studying is barely a twentieth of the universe. **95% of the cosmos is stuff we cannot yet identify.**

</Step>

<Step title="The expansion is speeding up" kind="interactive">

You might expect cosmic expansion to be *slowing* as gravity pulls everything back together. In 1998, two teams measuring distant supernovae found the opposite, to everyone's shock: the expansion is **accelerating**.

<HubbleExpansion />

Some repulsive influence is pushing the universe apart ever faster — the **dark energy** above. As space expands, more of it appears, more dark energy comes with it, and the acceleration builds. What dark energy actually *is* remains completely unknown; it may be an intrinsic energy of empty space itself. Its discovery won the 2011 Nobel Prize and rewrote our picture of the cosmic future.

</Step>

<Step title="How does it all end?" kind="explain">

If expansion keeps accelerating, the long-term future is bleak but calm. Galaxies drift apart faster and faster until they vanish over each other's horizons. Stars exhaust their fuel and die; no new ones form. The universe slides toward a cold, dark, dilute state of maximum **entropy** — the "arrow of time" you met in the energy unit, played out to its ultimate conclusion.

<Callout type="info" title="Three possible fates">
- **The Big Freeze (heat death)** — expansion continues forever; everything cools toward absolute zero. Current evidence favours this.
- **The Big Rip** — if dark energy strengthens, expansion eventually tears apart galaxies, stars, and even atoms.
- **The Big Crunch** — if gravity somehow won, expansion would reverse and everything would collapse back together. This now looks unlikely.
</Callout>

Which path the universe takes hinges on the nature of dark energy — which we don't yet understand.

</Step>

<Step title="The unanswered questions" kind="explain">

For all its triumphs, cosmology leaves the deepest questions wide open — and they're where this entire physics journey converges.

- **What are dark matter and dark energy?** Together they're 95% of the universe, and we don't know what either is.
- **What happened at the very first instant?** To describe the Big Bang's first moment — and the singularity inside a black hole — we'd need to unite **general relativity** (the cosmos and gravity) with **quantum mechanics** (the very small). The two theories you met in the last two units don't yet fit together. A theory of **quantum gravity** is the great unfinished business of physics.
- **Was there anything "before"?** Or other universes? These edge from physics toward the unknowable — for now.

The expanding cosmos, the dark sector, and the clash of relativity with the quantum are the live frontier. The story isn't finished — and that's exactly what makes it science.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- The **Big Bang**: the universe began ~13.8 billion years ago in a hot, dense state and has expanded and cooled since — an expansion *of space*, with no center.
- Key evidence: **nucleosynthesis** (the predicted 75% H / 25% He) and the **cosmic microwave background** (the 2.7 K afterglow, whose ripples seeded galaxies).
- Gravity grew those ripples into stars, galaxies and the cosmic web — but only with **dark matter** (~27%); the universe is ~**5% ordinary matter, 27% dark matter, 68% dark energy**.
- **Dark energy** drives an **accelerating expansion**, pointing toward a likely **Big Freeze** — and the natures of dark matter, dark energy, and **quantum gravity** remain the great open questions.

</Step>
