<Lesson>

<Step title="What is a force?" kind="explain">

A **force** is simply a **push or a pull**. Forces can start motion, stop it, change its direction, or squash and stretch things.

We measure force in **newtons (N)**, and like velocity, a force is a **vector** — it has a size *and* a direction. An arrow is the perfect way to draw one: longer arrow = bigger force, and it points the way the force pushes.

</Step>

<Step title="Adding forces up" kind="interactive">

Objects usually feel more than one force at once. When forces act along the same line, you just **add them up** (counting direction) to get the single **net force** — also called the **resultant**.

<ForceDiagram leftLabel="Push left" rightLabel="Push right" leftInit={6} rightInit={10} />

<Callout type="tip" title="Try this">
Make both pushes equal. The net force drops to zero. Now make one bigger — a net force appears, pointing the way of the stronger push.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Balanced or unbalanced?" kind="explain">

That gives us two cases that decide everything about motion:

- **Balanced forces** — they cancel, the net force is **zero**, and the motion **doesn't change** (it stays still, or keeps gliding steadily).
- **Unbalanced forces** — there's a leftover net force, so the object **accelerates** in that direction.

Those two ideas are the seeds of Newton's first two laws, coming up next.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- A **force** is a push or pull, measured in **newtons** — a vector (size + direction).
- Forces along a line combine into a single **net (resultant) force**.
- **Balanced** (net 0) → motion unchanged. **Unbalanced** → acceleration.

</Step>
