<Lesson>

<Step title="The quantity of motion" kind="explain">

**Momentum** measures how much motion something carries. It's mass times velocity:

<Formula tex="p = m v" block />

A truck and a bicycle at the same speed are worlds apart — the truck has far more momentum, so it's far harder to stop. Like velocity, momentum is a **vector**: direction matters.

</Step>

<Step title="Watch a collision" kind="interactive">

Send ball **A** into a stationary ball **B** and see how the masses decide the outcome.

<Collision />

<Callout type="tip" title="Try all three">
Equal masses **swap** velocities. A **heavy** ball barely slows. A **light** ball **bounces back** off a heavy one.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Momentum is conserved" kind="explain">

The thread tying every case together: with no outside forces, the **total momentum before a collision equals the total after**. None is lost — it's only **transferred** between the objects.

This conservation law is one of the most powerful tools in physics: even when forces during the crash are complicated, the before-and-after bookkeeping always balances.

</Step>

<Step title="Recoil and rockets" kind="explain">

Conservation cuts both ways. Fire a bullet forward and the gun kicks **backward** — equal and opposite momentum. A rocket hurls exhaust one way and gains momentum the other. It's Newton's third law, viewed through the lens of momentum.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Momentum** = mass × velocity (**p = mv**), a vector.
- In a collision with no outside forces, **total momentum is conserved** — only transferred.
- Recoil and rockets are momentum conservation in action.
