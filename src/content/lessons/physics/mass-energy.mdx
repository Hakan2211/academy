<Lesson>

<Step title="Why you can't just keep pushing" kind="explain">

Here's a natural question: if I keep applying a force to a spaceship, why can't I push it past the speed of light? Newton would say its speed just keeps climbing. Relativity says something has to give — and what gives is the ship's **inertia**.

As an object moves faster, it becomes harder and harder to accelerate, as though its mass were growing. Its **momentum** climbs faster than its speed, and the closer it gets to c, the more energy each extra sliver of speed demands. Approaching the speed of light isn't like approaching the edge of a cliff — it's like climbing a hill that gets infinitely steep.

</Step>

<Step title="The speed limit" kind="interactive">

Watch how an object's energy grows with its speed.

<SpeedLimit />

The curve is governed by the same Lorentz factor γ from time dilation. Near everyday speeds, energy rises gently. But as v approaches c, the curve rockets toward **infinity**. To actually *reach* c, a massive object would need an **infinite** amount of energy — which is impossible.

So the speed of light isn't merely how fast light happens to go. It's a fundamental **cosmic speed limit**: nothing carrying mass can ever reach it, let alone exceed it.

</Step>

<Step title="Mass is frozen energy" kind="explain">

Tracking that growing energy carefully, Einstein found that an object's total energy is:

<Formula tex="E = \gamma m c^2" block />

Now look at an object sitting completely still, where v = 0 and γ = 1. Its energy doesn't drop to zero — it settles at the most famous equation in physics:

<Formula tex="E = mc^2" block />

This is **rest energy**. Mass itself is a form of energy — an incredibly concentrated one, because c² is such a colossal number. A single gram of *anything*, fully converted, would release the energy of a large bomb. Mass and energy are not two things but one, freely convertible.

</Step>

<Step title="Where we already saw it" kind="explain">

You've met E = mc² before — in the **atoms** unit. It's the source of all nuclear energy:

- In **fission and fusion**, the products weigh slightly less than the ingredients, and that missing mass emerges as energy. The Sun converts about four million tonnes of mass into sunlight every second.
- In **particle accelerators**, the reverse happens: pump in enough energy and brand-new particles, with real mass, pop into existence out of pure energy.
- **Light itself** has no mass — which is precisely why it *must* travel at exactly c, no more and no less. Massless things don't just *can* move at light speed; they *must*.

The cosmic speed limit and E = mc² are two faces of the same insight: mass, energy, space, and time are all bound together.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- As speed rises, an object's **inertia and energy** grow by the factor γ — reaching c would need **infinite energy**, making c a **cosmic speed limit**.
- Total energy is **E = γmc²**; at rest this becomes **E = mc²**, the **rest energy** locked in mass.
- **Mass is concentrated energy**, convertible both ways — seen in **nuclear reactions**, the **Sun**, and **particle accelerators**.
- **Massless light must travel at exactly c** — nothing with mass can.

</Step>
