<Lesson>

<Step title="The thing that lets us see" kind="explain">

Almost everything you know about the world arrives as **light**. It pours out of the Sun, bounces off objects, and lands in your eyes. Without it, there's nothing to see.

The first thing to know about light is beautifully simple: in everyday situations it travels in **perfectly straight lines**, called **rays**. That single fact explains shadows, pinhole cameras, and why you can't see around corners.

</Step>

<Step title="Straight lines make shadows" kind="interactive">

Because rays go straight, an object blocks the light directly behind it — carving out a **shadow**. Move the lamp and watch the shadow swing and resize.

<ShadowCast />

If light could curve around the object, there'd be no sharp shadow at all. The crisp edge is your proof that light runs straight.

</Step>

<Step title="Faster than anything" kind="explain">

Light is the **fastest thing in the universe**, tearing along at almost

<Formula tex="c \approx 300{,}000{,}000\ \text{m/s}" block />

That's 300,000 km every second. Sunlight takes about **8 minutes** to reach us; light from the next star over takes **4 years**. A "light-year" — the distance light covers in a year — is how we measure the cosmos.

</Step>

<Step title="A wave that needs no medium" kind="explain">

Light is an **electromagnetic wave** — a self-sustaining ripple of electric and magnetic fields. Unlike sound, it needs **no material to travel through**.

That's why sunlight crosses the empty vacuum of space to reach us, while the Sun's roar never does. Light goes everywhere; sound is stuck needing air.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Light** lets us see; in everyday life it travels in straight **rays**.
- Straight-line travel is why objects cast sharp **shadows**.
- It moves at **c ≈ 3 × 10⁸ m/s** — the cosmic speed limit.
- Light is an **electromagnetic wave** and needs **no medium**, so it crosses empty space.

</Step>
