<Lesson>

<Step title="Ray, wave, or something stranger?" kind="explain">

So far we've treated light as straight **rays**, and that view explains shadows, mirrors, and lenses beautifully. But rays are only half the story. Two phenomena refuse to fit the ray picture and reveal light's true **wave** nature: **diffraction** and **interference**.

Pin these down and you understand why a soap bubble shimmers, how we measured the wavelength of light, and why a CD throws rainbows.

</Step>

<Step title="Waves bend around edges" kind="explain">

When a wave passes through a gap or past an edge, it doesn't just cast a sharp shadow — it **spreads out** into the region behind. This spreading is **diffraction**.

You hear it with sound (voices carry around a doorway) and see it with water (ripples fan out through a harbour mouth). Light diffracts too — but because its wavelength is so tiny, the effect is subtle, showing up only at very small slits and edges.

</Step>

<Step title="Two waves, added" kind="interactive">

The key to everything that follows is **superposition**: where two waves overlap, they add. In step, they reinforce; out of step, they cancel.

<Superposition />

Hold that idea — now imagine it happening across a whole screen, from two sources at once.

</Step>

<Step title="Young's double slit" kind="explain">

In 1801, Thomas Young sent light through **two narrow slits** and let it fall on a screen. If light were simple particles, he'd have seen two bright lines. Instead he saw a row of **alternating bright and dark bands**.

The only explanation: light from the two slits **interferes** — reinforcing at some points, cancelling at others. That experiment settled a century-long debate: light is a wave.

</Step>

<Step title="See the fringes form" kind="interactive">

Each slit becomes a source of spreading waves. Where crests meet crests you get a bright band; where crests meet troughs, darkness. Adjust the slits and wavelength and watch the pattern breathe.

<DoubleSlit />

Closer slits — or longer wavelengths — spread the fringes wider apart.

</Step>

<Step title="Reading the pattern" kind="explain">

Whether a point is bright or dark depends on the **path difference** — how much farther one wave travelled than the other to reach it.

- A path difference of a **whole** number of wavelengths → crests align → **bright**.
- A path difference of a **half**-odd number → crest meets trough → **dark**.

Because the fringe spacing depends on the wavelength, Young could run the experiment backwards and **measure the wavelength of light** — a few hundred nanometres — just from the spacing of stripes on a wall.

</Step>

<Step title="Colours from thin films" kind="explain">

Interference doesn't need slits. In a **soap bubble** or a film of **oil on water**, light reflects off both the top and bottom surfaces. Those two reflections interfere.

For each colour, the thin film is either the right thickness to reinforce it or to cancel it — and since the film varies in thickness, different colours win in different spots. The result is those swirling, shifting rainbows. The same trick, run deliberately, makes the **anti-reflective coatings** on glasses and camera lenses.

</Step>

<Step title="The wave model triumphant — with a twist" kind="explain">

Diffraction gratings split light more sharply than any prism; the microscopic grooves of a **CD** act as one, scattering a rainbow. All of it is wave interference, and the wave model of light reigned supreme through the 1800s.

<Callout type="info" title="But not the final word">
A century later, the **photoelectric effect** showed light *also* arrives in discrete packets — **photons**. Light is somehow both wave and particle at once. That paradox launched quantum physics (and waits for you in **Atoms, Radioactivity & Quantum**).
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- Rays explain shadows and lenses, but **diffraction** and **interference** prove light is a **wave**.
- **Diffraction**: waves spread around edges and through gaps.
- **Young's double slit**: two coherent sources interfere into **bright/dark fringes**; spacing reveals the **wavelength**.
- **Path difference** of whole λ → bright; half λ → dark.
- Thin films, gratings, and CDs are everyday interference — though light later turns out to be **both wave and particle**.

</Step>
