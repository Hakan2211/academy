<Lesson>

<Step title="The rhythm of repetition" kind="explain">

A pendulum swings, a heart beats, a guitar string buzzes. Each one repeats the **same motion over and over** — and physics describes that rhythm with two numbers.

- **Period (T)** — the time for *one* complete cycle.
- **Frequency (f)** — how *many* cycles happen each second.

A slow, lazy swing has a long period and a low frequency. A fast buzz is the opposite.

</Step>

<Step title="Two sides of one coin" kind="explain">

Period and frequency are simply reciprocals of each other:

<Formula tex="f = \dfrac{1}{T} \qquad T = \dfrac{1}{f}" block />

Frequency is measured in **hertz (Hz)** — cycles per second. If a wave repeats every 0.5 s (T = 0.5 s), then f = 1 / 0.5 = **2 Hz**: two full cycles every second.

</Step>

<Step title="Speed up the cycles" kind="interactive">

Drag the **frequency** slider. As you raise it, cycles pack in faster and the period shrinks — the readout shows them tracking each other.

<WaveLab />

Notice the wave doesn't get taller or longer when you change frequency alone — it just oscillates more often.

</Step>

<Step title="Frequencies everywhere" kind="explain">

The same idea spans the whole universe of vibration:

- A resting **heartbeat**: about 1 Hz.
- **Mains electricity**: 50 or 60 Hz.
- Musical note **A**: 440 Hz.
- **Radio** waves: millions of Hz (MHz).
- Visible **light**: hundreds of trillions of Hz.

Different names, different scales — but every one is just *cycles per second*.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Period (T)** = time for one cycle; **frequency (f)** = cycles per second, in **hertz**.
- They're reciprocals: **f = 1/T**.
- Changing frequency changes how *often* something oscillates — not how big.

<Formula tex="f = \dfrac{1}{T}" block />

</Step>
