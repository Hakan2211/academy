<Lesson>

<Step title="Energy on the move" kind="explain">

Every machine is really an **energy converter**. A car turns chemical energy into motion; a lamp turns electrical energy into light; your body turns food into movement and warmth.

But no converter is perfect. Some of the input energy always slips away into a form you didn't want — almost always **heat**. That stray energy is still conserved; it's just no longer **useful**.

</Step>

<Step title="Watch the leak" kind="interactive">

Turn the **friction** up. Now the swap isn't fair anymore — a little energy escapes into the **heat** bar on every pass, and the dashed total sinks. The motion slowly dies.

<EnergySkater friction={4} />

That orange bar is energy that's been *transferred*, not destroyed — but it's spread into warmth you can't get back as motion.

</Step>

<Step title="Useful vs. wasted" kind="explain">

We split a machine's output into two piles:

- **Useful** energy — the form you actually wanted (light, motion, lift).
- **Wasted** energy — everything that leaked out, usually as heat.

**Efficiency** is the fraction that ends up useful:

<Formula tex="\text{efficiency} = \dfrac{\text{useful energy out}}{\text{total energy in}}" block />

It's always between 0 and 1 (or 0–100%), because you can never get more useful energy out than you put in.

</Step>

<Step title="How good is good?" kind="explain">

Some real numbers:

- An old **incandescent bulb**: only ~5% of the electricity becomes light — the rest is heat.
- A modern **LED**: around 40–50% — far less waste.
- A **car engine**: roughly 25–30% becomes motion; most is lost as exhaust and engine heat.
- A large **electric motor**: can top 90%.

<Callout type="tip" title="Why we care">
Higher efficiency means the same job for less fuel — cheaper to run and easier on the planet.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- Machines **convert** energy from one form to another, but never perfectly.
- Wasted energy is usually **heat** — still conserved, just no longer useful.
- **Efficiency = useful out ÷ total in**, always ≤ 100%.
- Better efficiency means less fuel wasted for the same result.
