<Lesson>

<Step title="Shorthand for big and small" kind="explain">

Nobody says a journey is 5,000 metres — they say **5 kilometres**. Nobody measures a virus in metres — they use **nanometres**. A *prefix* is a one-word shortcut for a power of ten, bolted onto any unit so the numbers stay friendly.

"Kilo" means a thousand, "milli" a thousandth, "nano" a billionth. Learn the ladder once and it works for every unit: kilometres, kilograms, kilowatts; milliseconds, millilitres, milligrams.

</Step>

<Step title="The prefix ladder" kind="interactive">

Each common prefix is a jump of a thousand — three powers of ten — up or down from the base unit. Slide along and see what each one means.

<PrefixLadder />

Going up: kilo (10³), mega (10⁶), giga (10⁹), tera (10¹²). Going down: milli (10⁻³), micro (10⁻⁶), nano (10⁻⁹), pico (10⁻¹²). The base unit sits in the middle at 10⁰ = 1.

</Step>

<Step title="Converting is just multiplying by one" kind="explain">

To switch units, multiply by a clever form of the number **1**. Since 1 km = 1000 m, the fraction <Formula tex="\tfrac{1000\ \text{m}}{1\ \text{km}}" /> equals 1 — and multiplying by 1 never changes the real quantity, only how it's written.

<Formula tex="2.5\ \text{km} \times \dfrac{1000\ \text{m}}{1\ \text{km}} = 2500\ \text{m}" block />

The "km" cancels top-and-bottom, leaving metres. Arrange the conversion factor so the unit you *don't* want cancels, and you can never go wrong.

</Step>

<Step title="Let the units check your work" kind="explain">

Units aren't just labels — they obey algebra, and that's a free error-detector. If you compute a speed and the units come out as kilograms, you know you've made a mistake *before* you ever check the number.

<Callout type="info" title="Dimensional checking">
Whatever you do to the numbers, do to the units. If a formula claims that distance = speed × time, check it: (m/s) × s = m. The seconds cancel and you're left with metres — a distance. The units balance, so the formula is at least plausible.
</Callout>

This habit — watching that both sides of an equation carry the **same** units — catches more mistakes than almost anything else in physics.

</Step>

<Step title="A worked conversion" kind="explain">

How fast is 90 km/h in metres per second? Convert each part:

<Formula tex="90\ \tfrac{\text{km}}{\text{h}} \times \dfrac{1000\ \text{m}}{1\ \text{km}} \times \dfrac{1\ \text{h}}{3600\ \text{s}} = 25\ \tfrac{\text{m}}{\text{s}}" block />

The kilometres and hours cancel, leaving metres per second. A handy rule falls out: **divide km/h by 3.6 to get m/s**. So a 90 km/h motorway speed is 25 m/s.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- A **prefix** is shorthand for a power of ten — kilo (×1000), milli (÷1000), and so on.
- The engineering ladder steps by **factors of a thousand**: …nano, micro, milli, base, kilo, mega, giga…
- **Convert** by multiplying by a fraction equal to 1, arranged so the unwanted unit cancels.
- Units follow algebra, so **checking the units** is a quick, powerful way to catch errors.

</Step>
