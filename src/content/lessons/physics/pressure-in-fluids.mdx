<Step title="Pressure has depth" kind="explain">

Dive to the bottom of a swimming pool and your ears ache. Stay at the surface and they don't. Liquids and gases — **fluids** — push on everything inside them, and that push gets stronger the deeper you go.

The reason is simple weight. At any depth you're holding up *all the fluid stacked above you*. Go deeper and there's more fluid overhead, so it presses down harder. Pressure in a fluid grows steadily with depth.

</Step>

The deepest hole feels the most pressure, so its water shoots out fastest and hardest. This is also why dam walls are built **thick at the bottom and thin at the top** — the engineers are bracing against pressure that's far greater down low.

</Step>

<Step title="The ocean of air" kind="explain">

It isn't just water. You're sitting at the bottom of an **ocean of air** kilometres deep, and all that air has weight pressing down on you — about **101 kPa** at sea level, the equivalent of a 1 kg mass on every square centimetre of your skin.

You don't feel crushed because the fluids *inside* you push back out with equal pressure. But climb a mountain and there's less air above you, so the pressure drops — which is why your ears pop and why high peaks need oxygen tanks.

</Step>

<Step title="Pascal and hydraulics" kind="explain">

Blaise Pascal spotted something powerful: squeeze a confined, enclosed fluid and the pressure increase is felt **equally everywhere** in it. That gives us a force multiplier.

<Callout type="info" title="The hydraulic trick">
Push a small piston with a small force, and an equal *pressure* appears at a large piston — which turns it into a large *force* (`F = P × A`). A gentle press on a narrow cylinder can lift a car. That's how hydraulic brakes, jacks, and diggers work.
</Callout>

You don't get something for nothing: the big piston moves only a tiny distance for the small piston's big stroke. Energy still balances — it's force you're multiplying, not energy.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Pressure in a fluid increases with depth** — you're supporting the weight of all the fluid above you.
- The **atmosphere** presses at about 101 kPa at sea level and drops as you climb.
- **Pascal's principle**: pressure applied to an enclosed fluid is transmitted equally throughout — the basis of **hydraulics**, which multiply force.

</Step>
