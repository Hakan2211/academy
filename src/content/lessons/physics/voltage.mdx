<Step title="The push behind the flow" kind="explain">

Current is charge flowing — but what *makes* it flow? Charges don't move on their own; something has to push them. That push is **voltage**.

A handy picture: think of a water circuit. Current is like the flow rate of water; voltage is like the **pressure** from a pump driving it round. No pressure, no flow. The bigger the voltage, the harder the charges are pushed through the circuit.

Voltage is also called **potential difference**, because it measures a *difference* between two points — the drop in energy a charge experiences as it moves between them.

</Step>

<Step title="More push, more current" kind="interactive">

Let's see the push in action. Raise the voltage and watch the charges speed up and the bulb glow brighter.

<CircuitLab />

For a fixed resistance, **more voltage means more current**. The battery's voltage is the energy it gives each unit of charge to drive it around the loop — and with more energy behind them, the charges flow faster.

</Step>

<Step title="Where voltage comes from" kind="explain">

Anything that maintains a voltage is a kind of charge "pump":

- A **battery** uses chemical reactions to push electrons to one terminal, building up a potential difference between + and −.
- A **generator** uses motion and magnetism to do the same (you'll meet this in the magnetism unit).

The voltage a source can provide on its own is its **electromotive force (EMF)** — a slightly misleading name, since it's an energy-per-charge, not a force. A typical AA battery offers 1.5 V; a car battery 12 V; a wall socket around 230 V.

</Step>

<Step title="Energy per charge" kind="explain">

Precisely defined, voltage is the **energy given to each unit of charge**:

<Callout type="info" title="What one volt means">
<Formula tex="V = \dfrac{E}{Q}" /> — one **volt** is one **joule of energy per coulomb of charge**. A 12 V battery hands 12 joules to every coulomb that passes through it.
</Callout>

This is the key to understanding why circuits *do* anything: the source loads each charge up with energy, and the charge carries that energy off to be spent in the bulbs, motors, and heaters around the loop.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Voltage** (potential difference) is the **push** that drives charge around a circuit.
- For a fixed resistance, **more voltage → more current**.
- Sources like **batteries** and **generators** maintain voltage; their on-their-own value is the **EMF**.
- Precisely, voltage is **energy per unit charge**: `V = E / Q`, measured in volts (joules per coulomb).

<Formula tex="V = \dfrac{E}{Q}" block />

</Step>
