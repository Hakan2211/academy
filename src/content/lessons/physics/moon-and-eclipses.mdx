<Step title="Our nearest neighbour" kind="explain">

The Moon is the one other world we can study with the naked eye, and it has shaped life on Earth for billions of years. It's a ball of rock about a quarter of Earth's width, orbiting us roughly once a month, and it makes **no light of its own** — everything you see is reflected sunlight.

One curious fact: the Moon is **tidally locked**, so it always shows us the same face. The "dark side of the Moon" isn't permanently dark; it just permanently faces *away*.

</Step>

<Step title="The phases" kind="interactive">

Since the Moon only reflects sunlight, exactly **half of it is always lit** — the half facing the Sun. As the Moon orbits, we see that lit half from a changing angle, and *that* is the cycle of phases.

<MoonPhases />

- **New Moon**: the lit side faces away from us — the Moon is nearly invisible.
- **Full Moon**: the lit side faces us completely.
- In between we see crescents and gibbous shapes, **waxing** (growing) toward full and **waning** (shrinking) back to new.

Nothing about the Moon itself changes — only our viewing angle on its sunlit half.

</Step>

<Step title="Eclipses: when three line up" kind="explain">

Occasionally the Sun, Earth, and Moon fall into a near-perfect line, and one casts a shadow on another. There are two kinds:

- **Solar eclipse** — at **New Moon**, the Moon passes directly between Earth and Sun, briefly blocking the Sun and dropping a shadow on the Earth.
- **Lunar eclipse** — at **Full Moon**, the Earth passes between Sun and Moon, and the Moon slides through Earth's shadow, often glowing deep red.

<Callout type="info" title="Why not every month?">
If eclipses needed only a New or Full Moon, we'd get one of each every month. We don't — because the Moon's orbit is tilted about 5° from Earth's orbit around the Sun. Usually the Moon passes a little above or below the line, and the shadow misses. Only when the alignment is near-exact do we get an eclipse.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="The Moon makes the tides" kind="explain">

The Moon's gravity tugs hardest on the side of Earth nearest it, and least on the far side. That difference stretches the oceans into two bulges — one facing the Moon, one opposite — and as the Earth rotates beneath them, each coast sweeps through roughly **two high tides and two low tides a day**.

The Sun adds a smaller pull of its own. When Sun and Moon line up (at new and full Moon), their effects combine into especially large **spring tides**; when they pull at right angles, the gentler **neap tides** result. The rhythm of the sea is, quite literally, written by the sky.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- The Moon shines only by **reflected sunlight**, and is **tidally locked** to show one face.
- **Phases** are our changing view of the Moon's permanently half-lit surface — new, waxing, full, waning.
- **Eclipses** happen when Sun, Earth and Moon align: **solar** at New Moon, **lunar** at Full Moon — rare because the Moon's orbit is **tilted ~5°**.
- The Moon's gravity raises the ocean **tides**, with the Sun adding spring and neap variations.

</Step>
