<Step title="Why time has a direction" kind="explain">

Play a film of a swinging pendulum backwards and it looks perfectly normal. Play a film of a shattering glass backwards and it's absurd — shards leaping off the floor and reassembling. Yet **energy conservation allows both**. Nothing in "energy is conserved" says which way time should run.

So something *else* gives time its arrow. That something is **entropy** — and the law that governs it is the most quietly profound rule in physics.

</Step>

<Step title="Order falls apart" kind="interactive">

Start with the gas packed into the left half of the box — neat, ordered, low entropy. Release it.

<EntropyBox />

It spreads to fill the space and the disorder meter climbs. Now wait as long as you like: you will **never** see the particles spontaneously crowd back into the corner. The process runs one way only.

</Step>

<Step title="What entropy actually measures" kind="explain">

**Entropy** is a measure of how many microscopic arrangements look the same from the outside — roughly, how *disordered* a state is.

A tidy state (all gas in one corner, a whole egg, a sorted deck) can be built in very **few** ways. A messy state (gas everywhere, a scrambled egg, a shuffled deck) can be built in astronomically **more** ways. More ways to arrange it → higher entropy.

<Callout type="info" title="Boltzmann's insight">
Entropy counts possibilities: <Formula tex="S = k \ln W" /> where **W** is the number of microscopic arrangements consistent with what you see.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="The Second Law of Thermodynamics" kind="explain">

Stated simply:

> **The total entropy of an isolated system never decreases.**

Left alone, things drift from order toward disorder. You can create local order — tidy a room, freeze ice, grow a crystal — but only by dumping *more* disorder (as heat) into the surroundings. The books always balance in disorder's favour.

</Step>

<Step title="It's really just counting" kind="explain">

Why is the Second Law so unbreakable? Because it's **statistics on a colossal scale**.

For the gas to gather back into one corner, every one of its trillions of particles would have to happen to move there at once. That arrangement isn't *forbidden* — it's just one outcome among a number so vast it dwarfs the count of atoms in the universe. Disorder doesn't win by force. It wins by overwhelming odds.

</Step>

<Step title="Orderly motion decays into heat" kind="interactive">

Entropy is why friction is a one-way street. Here the ball's neat, organised motion is ground down into the **random jiggling of heat** (orange) — and it never reassembles back into a clean swing.

Organised kinetic energy → disorganised thermal energy. That's an entropy increase you can watch.

</Step>

<Step title="Why heat won't flow uphill" kind="explain">

Recall that heat always moves hot → cold. The Second Law is *why*. Spreading energy from a hot object to a cold one raises total entropy; the reverse would lower it, so it simply doesn't happen on its own.

This is also why **no engine can be 100% efficient**. To turn heat into work, an engine must dump some energy into a cooler reservoir — that waste heat is the entropy tax the Second Law demands. There's no way around it.

</Step>

<Step title="Quality, not quantity" kind="explain">

The First Law says energy is never lost. The Second Law adds a twist: energy steadily becomes **less useful**.

Concentrated energy (a tank of fuel, a charged battery) is low-entropy and can do work. Once spent, the *same* joules end up smeared out as low-grade warmth — still there, but too disordered to harness. Every time energy is used, a little of its **quality** is gone for good.

</Step>

<Step title="The fate of the universe" kind="explain">

Scale this up to everything. Stars burn out, gradients level off, hot and cold blur together. Physicists call the endpoint the **heat death**: a cosmos at uniform temperature where no useful energy gradients remain and nothing more can happen.

It's billions upon billions of years away — but the direction is set. The arrow of time you feel every day is entropy, quietly counting upward.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Entropy** measures disorder — the number of arrangements a state can take.
- **Second Law**: the total entropy of an isolated system never decreases.
- It holds because disordered states vastly outnumber ordered ones — pure statistics.
- It sets the **arrow of time**, forces heat to flow hot → cold, caps engine efficiency, and degrades energy's *quality* even as the First Law keeps its *quantity*.
