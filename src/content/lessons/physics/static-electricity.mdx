<Lesson>

<Step title="Two kinds of charge" kind="explain">

Rub a balloon on your hair and it sticks to the wall. Pull off a wool jumper in the dark and it crackles with sparks. Behind all of it is one of nature's most fundamental properties: **electric charge**.

Charge comes in two flavours, **positive** and **negative**. Every atom contains both: positive **protons** locked in its core and negative **electrons** buzzing around the outside. Normally their numbers match exactly, so the atom — and most objects — are **neutral**, with no overall charge.

Electrons are light and loosely held, so they're the ones that move. *Static* electricity is simply what happens when some electrons get transferred and an object ends up with too many (negative) or too few (positive).

</Step>

<Step title="Like repels, opposite attracts" kind="interactive">

The rule governing charges is one of the simplest in physics — and you can feel its strength.

<ChargeLab />

**Like charges repel; opposite charges attract.** And the force isn't fixed: bring the charges closer and it grows steeply, following an inverse-square law (force ∝ 1 / distance²) — **Coulomb's law**. Halve the gap and the force quadruples.

</Step>

<Step title="Charging by rubbing" kind="explain">

When you rub two different materials together, friction drags electrons from one onto the other. One object steals electrons and becomes **negative**; the other loses them and becomes **positive**.

That's why a balloon rubbed on hair clings to a wall: the charged balloon pushes electrons in the wall's surface away, leaving the near side oppositely charged, so the two attract. The same effect lets a charged comb pick up tiny scraps of paper — even though the paper itself is neutral.

</Step>

<Step title="Discharge and sparks" kind="explain">

Charge wants to even out. When enough builds up, electrons jump across a gap all at once — a **discharge** — releasing their energy as a spark, a snap, or a shock.

<Callout type="info" title="Lightning is static electricity, scaled up">
Inside a storm cloud, colliding ice and water build up an enormous charge separation. When it finally bridges to the ground, billions of electrons leap across in a fraction of a second: **lightning**. The doorknob zap you get on a dry day is the very same physics, just smaller.
</Callout>

This is also why we **ground** sensitive electronics and fuel trucks — giving charge a safe path to drain away before it can build up and jump.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- Electric charge comes in **two kinds**: positive (protons) and negative (electrons); most matter is **neutral**.
- **Like charges repel, opposite charges attract**, with a force that grows as **1 / distance²** (Coulomb's law).
- **Friction** transfers loosely held electrons, leaving objects charged.
- Built-up charge evens out through a **discharge** — from a doorknob spark to lightning.

</Step>
