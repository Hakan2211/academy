<Lesson>

<Step title="Crossing the line" kind="explain">

Heat ice and it melts; keep heating and it boils. Each crossing between solid, liquid, and gas is a **change of state**, and every one is driven by adding or removing energy:

- Add energy to climb up: **melting** (solid → liquid), then **boiling** (liquid → gas).
- Remove energy to come back down: **condensing** (gas → liquid), then **freezing** (liquid → solid).

These changes happen at fixed temperatures for a given substance — water melts at 0 °C and boils at 100 °C — which is what makes them so reliable.

</Step>

<Step title="The flat steps" kind="interactive">

Here's something surprising. Heat a block of ice at a steady rate and plot its temperature. You'd expect a straight climb — but watch what happens at the state changes.

<PhaseChange />

The temperature **pauses** during melting and again during boiling. For those stretches you're pouring in heat, yet the thermometer doesn't budge. So where is all that energy going?

</Step>

<Step title="Latent heat: the hidden energy" kind="explain">

During a change of state, the incoming energy isn't speeding the particles up (which is what temperature measures) — it's being spent **breaking the bonds** that held the state together. This "hidden" energy is called **latent heat**.

<Formula tex="Q = m \, L" block />

It takes a lot of it. Turning 100 °C water into 100 °C steam needs over *five times* more energy than heating that water all the way from freezing to boiling. That stored energy is exactly why **steam burns far worse than boiling water**: when steam hits your skin and condenses, it dumps all that latent heat back out.

</Step>

<Step title="Evaporation cools" kind="explain">

A liquid doesn't have to boil to lose particles — the fastest ones can escape from the surface at any temperature. That's **evaporation**, and it has a neat consequence.

<Callout type="tip" title="Why sweating works">
When the most energetic particles leave, the ones left behind are slower on average — so the liquid **cools down**. Sweat evaporating from your skin carries away heat and keeps you from overheating. The same effect makes you shiver when you step out of a pool.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- Changes of state — **melting, freezing, boiling, condensing** — are driven by adding or removing energy at fixed temperatures.
- Temperature **pauses** during a change: the energy goes into **latent heat**, breaking or forming bonds rather than changing speed.
- That's why **steam scalds** worse than boiling water and why **evaporation cools** (sweating).
