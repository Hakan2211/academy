<Lesson>

<Step title="Weight is a force" kind="explain">

Earth pulls every object toward its center. That pull is the object's **weight** — a force, measured in newtons. It depends on mass and the local gravity **g**:

<Formula tex="W = m g" block />

Near Earth's surface **g ≈ 9.8 m/s²**. Weight isn't the same as mass: take your mass to the Moon and it's unchanged, but your weight drops to about a sixth.

</Step>

<Step title="Heavy and light fall together" kind="interactive">

Here's the surprise that fooled people for 2,000 years: with no air, **all objects fall at the same rate**, whatever their mass.

<FreeFall />

Galileo argued it; Apollo 15 proved it on the Moon by dropping a hammer and a feather — they hit the dust together.

</Step>

<Step title="Why does mass cancel?" kind="explain">

A heavier object *is* pulled harder — but it also has *more inertia* to move. The two effects cancel exactly. Watch it fall out of **F = ma**:

<Formula tex="a = \dfrac{F}{m} = \dfrac{m g}{m} = g" block />

The mass divides away, leaving the same acceleration **g** for everything. That's **free fall**: motion under gravity alone.

</Step>

<Step title="The catch: air" kind="explain">

So why does a real feather flutter down slowly? **Air resistance** — not gravity. Air pushes up on the broad, light feather far more than on a dense hammer.

"Free fall" means *gravity only*. Remove the air (or drop dense objects where air barely matters) and the textbook result snaps back into view.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Weight** is the force of gravity: **W = mg** (g ≈ 9.8 m/s² near Earth).
- In **free fall**, every object accelerates at the same **g** — mass cancels out in F = ma.
- **Air resistance**, not gravity, is what slows light, fluffy objects.

<Formula tex="a = \dfrac{m g}{m} = g" block />

</Step>
