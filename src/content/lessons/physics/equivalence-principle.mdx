<Lesson>

<Step title="The piece special relativity left out" kind="explain">

Special relativity was a triumph — but it had a blind spot. It only handled observers moving at **constant velocity**, in straight lines, never speeding up. And it said nothing at all about **gravity**, the most familiar force of all.

Einstein spent the next decade fixing this, and he later called the key insight his "happiest thought." It begins with a simple, almost childlike question about what you'd feel inside a sealed box.

</Step>

<Step title="The elevator you can't see out of" kind="interactive">

Imagine waking in a windowless box. You feel your normal weight pressing you to the floor. Are you (a) sitting in that box on Earth's surface, or (b) being towed through deep space by a rocket accelerating upward at just the right rate?

Try to find an experiment that tells the two apart.

<Equivalence />

Drop a ball: in both cases it falls to the floor in exactly the same way. Shine a light across the box: in both cases it bends by the same tiny amount. Whatever you try, the results are **identical**. This is the **equivalence principle**: locally, the pull of **gravity** and the push of **acceleration** are completely indistinguishable.

</Step>

<Step title="A startling prediction: light falls" kind="explain">

Look again at that light beam. Inside the accelerating rocket, it *obviously* bends: while the light crosses the box, the box speeds up beneath it, so the beam lands lower than it started. There's nothing mysterious about it.

But if acceleration and gravity are truly equivalent, then **gravity must bend light too** — even though light has no mass for gravity to "pull" on in the Newtonian sense.

<Callout type="info" title="The 1919 eclipse that made Einstein famous">
Einstein predicted that starlight grazing the Sun would be deflected by its gravity. In 1919, **Arthur Eddington** photographed stars near the Sun during a total eclipse and found their apparent positions shifted — by exactly the amount Einstein predicted. Overnight, Einstein became the most famous scientist on Earth. Gravity really does bend light.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="So gravity isn't a force?" kind="explain">

This is the conceptual earthquake. If gravity bends the path of light — which travels in *straight lines* — then maybe gravity isn't a force reaching out to tug on things at all. Maybe **gravity is what happens when the space the light travels through is itself bent**.

Think about why everything falls at the same rate (the lesson you met back in *Gravity & Free Fall*). For Newton it was a curious coincidence that an object's "gravitational mass" exactly matched its "inertial mass." For Einstein it's no coincidence: things fall the same way because they're all just following the **same curved geometry**, regardless of what they're made of. A dropped feather and a dropped hammer trace the same path because the path belongs to *space*, not to them.

That radical idea — gravity as curved geometry — is **general relativity**, and it's the subject of the next lesson.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- Special relativity ignored **acceleration** and **gravity**; fixing that began with the **equivalence principle**.
- Inside a sealed box, **gravity and acceleration are indistinguishable** — a dropped ball and a light beam behave identically.
- This predicts that **gravity bends light**, confirmed by the **1919 eclipse**.
- It reframes gravity not as a force but as the effect of **curved spacetime** — explaining why everything falls at the same rate.

</Step>
