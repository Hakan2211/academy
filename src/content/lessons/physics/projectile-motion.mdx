<Step title="Two motions in one" kind="explain">

Throw a ball and it traces a graceful arc. That curve looks complicated — but it hides a beautiful secret: it's really **two simple motions happening at once**.

<Video
  src="/lessons/physics/projectile-motion/throw.mp4"
  poster="/lessons/physics/projectile-motion/throw-poster.svg"
  caption="A thrown ball: steady sideways drift, gravity-driven rise and fall."
/>

Sideways, the ball just drifts at a steady pace. Up and down, gravity is busy slowing it, stopping it, and pulling it back. Treat those two directions **separately** and the whole arc falls into place.

</Step>

<Step title="Split the velocity" kind="explain">

The trick is to break the launch velocity into a **horizontal** part and a **vertical** part:

<Formula tex="v_x = v\cos\theta \qquad v_y = v\sin\theta" block />

<Figure
  src="/lessons/physics/projectile-motion/decomposition.svg"
  alt="The launch velocity decomposed into a constant horizontal component and a vertical component"
  caption="One arrow, two jobs: vₓ carries it forward, v_y fights gravity."
/>

From here on, we can solve each direction on its own and recombine at the end.

</Step>

<Step title="Launch it" kind="interactive">

Play with the **angle** and the **speed**. Watch the teal arrow (horizontal velocity) stay the same length while the blue arrow (vertical velocity) shrinks, flips, and grows back.

<Scene3D component="projectile" />

<Callout type="tip" title="Notice">
The teal arrow never changes — nothing pushes the ball sideways. Only the blue arrow does, because only gravity acts.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Horizontal: nobody's pushing" kind="explain">

Once the ball leaves your hand, **no force acts sideways** (ignoring air). By Newton's first law, the horizontal velocity is simply constant:

<Formula tex="x = v_x\, t" block />

So in the horizontal direction the ball is boringly steady — it covers equal distances in equal times.

</Step>

<Step title="Vertical: gravity takes over" kind="explain">

Up and down is a different story. Gravity pulls with a steady acceleration **g ≈ 9.8 m/s²**, so the vertical velocity changes every instant:

<Formula tex="v_y(t) = v\sin\theta - g\,t" block />

The height follows the familiar falling-body equation:

<Formula tex="y(t) = v\sin\theta\, t - \tfrac{1}{2} g t^2" block />

At the top of the arc, **v_y = 0** for a single instant — the ball is momentarily neither rising nor falling.

</Step>

<Step title="Hit the target" kind="interactive">

Here's the payoff: with the two motions combined you can *aim*. Try to land the ball as far away as possible.

</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Range and the magic 45°" kind="explain">

Combine both directions and the distance the ball travels — its **range** — works out to:

<Formula tex="R = \dfrac{v^2 \sin(2\theta)}{g}" block />

Because **sin(2θ)** is biggest when **2θ = 90°**, the maximum range (on flat ground, no air) comes from a launch angle of exactly **45°**. Steeper sends it high but not far; shallower keeps it low and short.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

Projectile motion is two independent stories told at once:

- **Horizontal** — constant velocity, because nothing pushes sideways.
- **Vertical** — constant acceleration **g**, exactly like a dropped object.
- They share only one thing: **time**.
- On flat ground, **45°** gives the greatest range.

<Formula tex="R = \dfrac{v^2 \sin(2\theta)}{g}" block />

</Step>
