<Lesson>

<Step title="One law for apples and moons" kind="explain">

Newton's great leap was to realize that the force pulling an **apple** to the ground is the *same* force holding the **Moon** in its orbit. Not two phenomena — one **universal** law of gravitation governing everything with mass, from dust grains to galaxies.

</Step>

<Step title="The law of gravitation" kind="explain">

Every mass attracts every other mass. The pull grows with the masses and weakens with the **square** of the distance between them:

<Formula tex="F = G\,\dfrac{m_1 m_2}{r^2}" block />

Here **G** is the gravitational constant — a tiny number, which is why gravity feels weak until one of the masses is planet-sized. Double the distance and the force drops to a **quarter**.

</Step>

<Step title="Launch an orbit" kind="interactive">

Give a planet a sideways shove past a star and let gravity do the rest. Try different launch speeds:

<OrbitLab />

<Callout type="tip" title="Explore">
Start slow — the planet falls inward. Speed up to find a near-perfect **circle**. A bit more gives a stretched **ellipse**.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="An orbit is endless falling" kind="explain">

So what *is* an orbit? Newton imagined firing a cannon horizontally from a mountain. Fire gently and the ball lands nearby. Fire faster and it lands farther — because the **ground curves away** beneath it.

Fire fast enough and the surface curves away exactly as fast as the ball falls. Now it **never lands** — it perpetually falls *around* the Earth. That's an orbit: free fall that keeps missing the ground.

</Step>

<Step title="Circular speed" kind="explain">

For a circular orbit, gravity must supply *exactly* the centripetal force from the last lesson. Setting them equal gives the speed needed at radius **r** around a mass **M**:

<Formula tex="v = \sqrt{\dfrac{GM}{r}}" block />

A neat consequence: **higher orbits are slower**. The Moon, far out, ambles along; a low satellite has to race around in about 90 minutes.

</Step>

<Step title="Ellipses and escape" kind="interactive">

Now push the launch speed higher and higher. The ellipse stretches… until the planet never returns.

<OrbitLab />

<Callout type="tip" title="Challenge">
Find the speed where the orbit stops closing back on itself. It happens around **1.41× the circular speed** — that's the edge of escape.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Escape velocity" kind="explain">

Reach that threshold and gravity can never reel you back. The **escape velocity** is exactly **√2 ≈ 1.41** times the circular speed:

<Formula tex="v_{\text{esc}} = \sqrt{\dfrac{2GM}{r}}" block />

It's why launching a probe to deep space takes so much more fuel than reaching low orbit — you must pay the full escape toll.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Universal gravitation:** every mass attracts every other, **F = G m₁m₂ / r²** — an inverse-square law.
- An **orbit** is continual free fall that keeps missing the ground; gravity provides the centripetal force.
- **Circular speed** v = √(GM/r); **escape velocity** is √2 times larger.

<Formula tex="v_{\text{esc}} = \sqrt{2}\,\cdot v_{\text{circular}}" block />

</Step>
