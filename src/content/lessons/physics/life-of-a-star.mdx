<Step title="A star is a balancing act" kind="explain">

A star is born when a vast cloud of gas and dust — a **nebula** — collapses under its own gravity. As the core is squeezed, it grows hotter and denser, until something switches on: **nuclear fusion**, the very process you met in the atoms unit. Hydrogen nuclei begin fusing into helium, releasing enormous energy.

From then on, a star spends its life in a standoff. Gravity relentlessly tries to crush it inward; the outward push of energy from fusion holds it up. As long as the fuel lasts, the two stay balanced and the star shines steadily. This long, stable phase is called the **main sequence** — and our Sun is halfway through its own.

</Step>

<Step title="The furnace in the core" kind="interactive">

What's actually happening in that core is the fusion you studied in *Nuclear Energy*. Set the visual to **fusion**:

<FissionFusion />

Hydrogen nuclei merge into helium, and the product weighs slightly *less* than the ingredients — the missing mass becomes energy via **E = mc²**. The Sun fuses about 600 million tonnes of hydrogen every second, and has done so for 4.6 billion years. That steady outpouring of energy is sunlight: the power source for nearly all life on Earth.

</Step>

<Step title="Live fast or live long" kind="interactive">

How a star lives and dies is set almost entirely by **one number at birth: its mass**.

<StarLifeCycle />

- **Low-mass stars** (like the Sun) burn slowly for billions of years, then swell into a **red giant**, gently puff off their outer layers, and leave behind a slowly cooling **white dwarf**.
- **High-mass stars** burn furiously and briefly, balloon into **red supergiants**, then detonate as a **supernova** — one of the most violent events in the universe — leaving a **neutron star** or, for the heaviest, a **black hole** (the objects from the relativity unit).

The more massive the star, the shorter and more spectacular its life.

</Step>

<Step title="We are made of stardust" kind="explain">

Stars are the universe's foundries. The Big Bang made almost only hydrogen and helium; **every heavier element was forged inside stars** or in their explosive deaths.

<Callout type="info" title="Where your atoms came from">
Fusion in stellar cores builds elements up to iron. The heavier ones — the gold in jewellery, the iodine in your body, the uranium in the atoms unit — are blasted out in **supernovae** and neutron-star collisions, then swept into new clouds that form new stars and planets. The carbon in your cells and the oxygen you breathe were literally cooked inside long-dead stars. As Carl Sagan put it, *"we are made of star-stuff."*
</Callout>

So the periodic table you learned in chemistry is, in a real sense, a catalogue of stellar history — and you are one of its products.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- Stars ignite when a collapsing **nebula** gets hot enough for **fusion**, then balance gravity against fusion energy on the **main sequence**.
- The core fuses **hydrogen into helium**, converting lost mass to energy via **E = mc²** — the source of sunlight.
- A star's **mass** decides its fate: low-mass → red giant → **white dwarf**; high-mass → supergiant → **supernova** → **neutron star** or **black hole**.
- Stars **forged the elements**: everything heavier than helium, including the atoms in you, was made inside stars and their explosions.

</Step>
