<Lesson>

<Step title="Charge on the move" kind="explain">

Static electricity is charge that's *sitting still*. Make that charge **flow** and you get something far more useful: an **electric current** — the thing that lights your room and runs your phone.

In a metal wire, the movers are **free electrons**. Metals are special: their outer electrons aren't tied to any one atom but drift through the whole material like a kind of electron "sea." Give them a push and they all start flowing in step — that organised flow is current.

</Step>

<Step title="Measuring the flow" kind="interactive">

Current measures **how much charge flows past a point each second**. Its unit is the **ampere** (amp, A).

<CurrentFlow />

Turn up the current and the charges sweep past faster — because more charge per second *is* more current. One amp is a coulomb of charge every second, which is about **6 × 10¹⁸ electrons** streaming past each second. We measure it with an **ammeter**, connected so the current flows straight through it.

<Formula tex="I = \dfrac{Q}{t}" block />

</Step>

<Step title="Two directions, one history" kind="explain">

Here's a quirk worth knowing. Long before electrons were discovered, scientists guessed current flowed from **positive to negative** and built all their conventions around it. We now know the electrons in a wire actually drift the *other* way, from negative to positive.

Rather than rewrite everything, we kept the original convention. So **conventional current** points from + to −, while the **electrons** physically move from − to +. Both describe the same flow — just don't be surprised when they point opposite ways.

</Step>

<Step title="You need a complete loop" kind="explain">

Charge can only flow if it has an unbroken path to flow *around*. A current needs a **complete circuit** — a continuous loop from one terminal of the source, through the wires and components, and back to the other terminal.

<Callout type="tip" title="Why a switch works">
A switch is just a deliberate **break** in the loop. Open it and the path is cut, so no charge can flow and the current stops instantly. Close it and the loop is whole again. Every on/off switch you've ever flicked is opening and closing a circuit.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Current** is the flow of electric charge — in metals, a flow of free **electrons**.
- It's measured in **amperes**: `I = Q / t`, the charge passing each second.
- **Conventional current** runs + to −; electrons actually drift − to + (a historical convention).
- Current needs a **complete circuit**; a **switch** stops it by breaking the loop.

<Formula tex="I = \dfrac{Q}{t}" block />

</Step>
