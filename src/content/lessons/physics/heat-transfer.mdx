<Step title="Three ways heat moves" kind="explain">

Heat always flows from hot to cold — but *how* does it actually get there? There are exactly three routes, and almost every warm or cold thing in your life uses some mix of them:

- **Conduction** — through direct contact, particle to particle.
- **Convection** — carried along by a moving fluid.
- **Radiation** — beamed across space as invisible waves.

Tell them apart and you can explain everything from why a metal spoon burns your hand to how the Sun warms a planet across empty space.

</Step>

<Step title="Pick a mechanism" kind="interactive">

Switch between the three and watch what's actually moving in each.

<HeatTransfer />

In **conduction**, energy is handed from one jiggling particle to the next without the particles themselves travelling. In **convection**, the heated fluid itself rises and circulates, *carrying* its energy with it. In **radiation**, nothing material moves at all — the heat travels as electromagnetic waves.

</Step>

<Step title="Conductors and insulators" kind="explain">

Some materials pass heat along easily (**conductors** — especially metals, packed with free electrons that shuttle energy fast). Others barely pass it at all (**insulators** — air, wool, foam, wood).

This is why a metal railing feels colder than a wooden one at the same temperature: the metal conducts heat out of your hand quickly, while the wood doesn't. Insulators trap heat by trapping *air* — a duvet, a woolly jumper, and double-glazed windows all work by holding still air in place, since air is a poor conductor (as long as it can't circulate and convect).

</Step>

<Step title="All three at once" kind="explain">

Real objects usually combine the trio. A vacuum flask is the perfect case study — it's built specifically to block all three:

<Callout type="info" title="How a vacuum flask keeps coffee hot">
- A **vacuum** gap between the walls stops **conduction and convection** (no particles to carry heat).
- **Silvered, shiny** surfaces reflect **radiation** back in.
Block every route and the heat has nowhere to go.
</Callout>

Convection also drives the world's weather: warm air and water rise, cool ones sink, and the resulting currents give us sea breezes, ocean circulation, and the rising heat above a radiator.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- Heat moves three ways: **conduction** (contact), **convection** (moving fluid), **radiation** (waves, no medium needed).
- **Conductors** (metals) pass heat fast; **insulators** (air, wool, foam) resist it.
- Radiation is the only one that crosses a **vacuum** — how the Sun reaches us.
- Real devices like a **vacuum flask** are designed to block all three at once.

</Step>
