<Lesson>

<Step title="A galaxy-sized surprise" kind="explain">

In the 1920s, **Edwin Hubble** measured the distances to dozens of galaxies and, crucially, how fast each was moving. He expected a random mix — some approaching, some receding. Instead he found something staggering: **almost every galaxy is rushing away from us**, and the farther away a galaxy is, the faster it flees.

At first glance this seems to put us at the centre of a cosmic explosion. The real explanation is stranger and more elegant — and it forms the bedrock of modern cosmology, building directly on the curved, dynamic spacetime of the relativity unit.

</Step>

<Step title="Reading speed in colour: redshift" kind="interactive">

How do we know a galaxy is receding? From its **light**. When a source moves away, its light waves are stretched to longer wavelengths — shifted toward the **red** end of the spectrum. This is **redshift**, the light-wave cousin of the falling pitch of a passing siren.

<SpectrumBar />

Slide toward longer wavelengths (the red and beyond): that's the direction a receding galaxy's light is shifted. The faster it recedes, the larger the redshift. Measuring this shift in a galaxy's spectrum tells us its speed precisely — and it was these redshifts that revealed almost everything is moving away.

</Step>

<Step title="It's space itself that's stretching" kind="interactive">

Here's the resolution to "why is everything fleeing us?" The galaxies aren't flying *through* space away from a special center. **Space itself is expanding**, and carrying the galaxies along with it.

<HubbleExpansion />

Pick **any** galaxy as home and the view is identical: everything recedes, and farther means faster. There's no center and no edge — just as every spot on an inflating balloon sees all the other spots moving away from it. This neat proportionality between distance and speed is **Hubble's law**:

<Formula tex="v = H_0 \, d" block />

where H₀ (the Hubble constant) sets the expansion rate. The redshift isn't really galaxies barrelling through space — it's the light itself being **stretched by the expansion of space** during its long journey to us.

</Step>

<Step title="Rewind the cosmos" kind="explain">

Now play the most important tape in science **backwards**. If space is expanding and everything is flying apart today, then in the past everything was **closer together**. Run it back far enough and all the matter and energy in the universe converges into an unimaginably hot, dense point.

That beginning is the **Big Bang**. And because Hubble's law links expansion rate to time, we can estimate *when* it happened: about **13.8 billion years ago**. The expanding universe isn't just a present-day curiosity — it's a clock and a history book, pointing to a definite origin. Following that history forward, instant by instant, is the journey of the final lesson.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Hubble** found nearly all galaxies are **receding**, faster the farther they are.
- We measure their speed by **redshift** — light stretched to longer wavelengths as the source recedes.
- The cause is **expanding space** itself (not galaxies flying through space): **v = H₀d**, with no center or edge — the balloon picture.
- Rewinding the expansion points to a hot, dense origin **~13.8 billion years ago**: the **Big Bang**.

</Step>
