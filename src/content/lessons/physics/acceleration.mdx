<Lesson>

<Step title="Changing your velocity" kind="explain">

**Acceleration** is how quickly your **velocity** changes. Press the gas and you accelerate; hit the brakes and you accelerate too — just in the negative direction.

<Formula tex="a = \dfrac{\Delta v}{\Delta t}" block />

Crucially, *any* change in velocity counts: speeding up, slowing down, **or turning** — because velocity includes direction.

</Step>

<Step title="See it on the graphs" kind="interactive">

Compare a steady cruise, speeding up, and slowing down. Keep an eye on the **velocity–time** graph on the right.

<KinematicsLab scenarios={["uniform", "accelerate", "decelerate"]} />

<Callout type="tip" title="Look for this">
When the **velocity–time** line *slopes up*, the object is accelerating. A *flat* line means zero acceleration — even if it's moving fast.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Acceleration is a slope too" kind="explain">

Just as velocity is the slope of a position–time graph, **acceleration is the slope of a velocity–time graph**:

- **flat** velocity line → no acceleration (steady speed),
- **upward** slope → positive acceleration (speeding up),
- **downward** slope → negative acceleration (slowing down).

</Step>

<Step title="The units: m/s per second" kind="explain">

Acceleration is "velocity change per second," so its units are metres-per-second… **per second**:

<Formula tex="1\ \text{m/s}^2 = 1\ \tfrac{\text{m/s}}{\text{s}}" block />

A famous example: near Earth's surface, gravity accelerates falling objects at about **g ≈ 9.8 m/s²** — every second of free fall adds 9.8 m/s of downward speed. You'll meet g again soon.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Acceleration** = rate of change of velocity: speeding up, slowing down, or turning.
- It's the **slope of a velocity–time graph**: up = speeding up, flat = none, down = slowing.
- Units are **m/s²**; gravity gives about **9.8 m/s²**.

<Formula tex="a = \dfrac{\Delta v}{\Delta t}" block />

</Step>
