<Lesson>

<Step title="Two ways to connect" kind="explain">

Once you have more than one component, you have a choice about how to wire them up — and the choice changes everything about how the circuit behaves.

- **Series** — components sit one after another on a *single* loop. There's only one path, so the same current flows through every component in turn.
- **Parallel** — components sit on *separate* branches across the same two points. The current splits between the branches, and each branch gets the full voltage.

</Step>

<Step title="Compare them side by side" kind="interactive">

Switch between the two layouts, and try pulling a bulb out of each.

<SeriesParallel />

Two lessons jump out. In **series**, the bulbs share the voltage so each glows dimmer — and removing one **breaks the only loop, killing both**. In **parallel**, each bulb has its own full-voltage loop, so both shine brightly — and removing one **leaves the other completely unaffected**.

</Step>

<Step title="Adding it up" kind="explain">

The two arrangements combine resistance in opposite ways:

- **Series resistances add**: `R = R₁ + R₂ + …`. More components in a row means more total resistance, so less current overall.
- **Parallel resistance drops** below even the smallest branch — because you've opened up extra paths for current to flow. More routes means *less* total opposition.

For current and voltage: in series the **current is the same everywhere** but the **voltage splits** across the components. In parallel the **voltage is the same** across each branch but the **current splits** between them.

</Step>

<Step title="Why your home is wired in parallel" kind="explain">

Look around and almost everything important is parallel — for very practical reasons.

<Callout type="info" title="The case for parallel">
- Every device gets the **full mains voltage** it's designed for.
- Each can be **switched on and off independently** — your kettle doesn't dim when you turn on a lamp.
- If one device **fails**, the rest keep working (unlike old series Christmas lights, where one dead bulb killed the whole string).
</Callout>

Series still has its uses — for switches and **fuses**, which must sit in series so they can cut the current to everything downstream when needed.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Series**: one loop, **same current** through all, **voltage splits**; resistances **add**; one break stops everything.
- **Parallel**: separate branches, **same voltage** across each, **current splits**; total resistance **drops**; branches are independent.
- **Homes are wired in parallel** so devices get full voltage, switch independently, and survive each other's failures.

</Step>
