<Step title="The smallest piece of you" kind="explain">

Take anything — this screen, a glass of water, your own hand — and imagine cutting it in half, then in half again, and again. The ancient Greeks wondered whether you could do this forever. One of them, Democritus, guessed not: eventually you'd reach a piece so small it couldn't be cut. He called it *átomos* — "uncuttable."

He was right that matter comes in tiny building blocks, and wrong that they can't be split. The **atom** turned out to have a rich inner structure of its own — and learning to read it unlocked chemistry, electricity, the stars, and the strange rules of the quantum world you'll meet later in this unit.

</Step>

<Step title="Three particles, two regions" kind="explain">

Every atom is built from just **three** kinds of particle:

- **Protons** — positive charge (+), found in the centre.
- **Neutrons** — no charge, also in the centre. Protons and neutrons together are the **nucleons**.
- **Electrons** — negative charge (−), whizzing around the outside.

These are arranged in two regions. A dense **nucleus** of protons and neutrons sits at the very centre, holding almost all the mass. Around it, light electrons occupy a much larger space in layers called **shells**.

A neutral atom has **equal numbers of protons and electrons**, so the + and − charges cancel out exactly. Tip that balance and you get a charged atom — an **ion**.

</Step>

<Step title="Build an atom" kind="interactive">

Here's the rule that organises all of chemistry: **the number of protons decides which element you have.** One proton is always hydrogen; six is always carbon; nothing else can change that identity.

Add and remove particles and watch what each one does.

<AtomBuilder />

- Change the **protons** and the element itself changes.
- Change the **neutrons** and you get a heavier or lighter version of the *same* element — an **isotope** (the next lesson's whole story).
- Change the **electrons** and the atom picks up a charge, becoming a positive or negative **ion**.

The **mass number** A is just protons + neutrons — the count of heavy particles in the nucleus.

</Step>

<Step title="Mostly empty space" kind="explain">

Here's the part that should feel strange. The nucleus holds over **99.9%** of the atom's mass, yet it's almost unimaginably tiny compared to the atom as a whole.

<Callout type="info" title="A cathedral with a fly in it">
If an atom were blown up to the size of a sports stadium, the nucleus would be a single grain of rice on the centre spot. Everything else — the entire volume you think of as "solid matter" — is the near-empty space where the electrons roam. You, and everything you touch, are mostly nothing at all.
</Callout>

We know this thanks to **Rutherford's** famous 1909 experiment: he fired positive particles at a wafer of gold foil. Almost all sailed straight through the "empty" atoms — but a rare few bounced sharply back, as if they'd hit something small, hard, and positively charged. That something was the nucleus, and it overturned the idea that an atom was a uniform blob.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- Atoms are built from **protons (+)**, **neutrons (0)**, and **electrons (−)**.
- Protons and neutrons cluster in a tiny, dense **nucleus**; electrons occupy **shells** around it.
- The **proton count names the element**; neutrons set the mass (isotopes); electron imbalance makes **ions**.
- The atom is **mostly empty space** — the nucleus is minuscule but carries virtually all the mass, as Rutherford's gold-foil experiment revealed.

</Step>
