<Step title="Power: energy every second" kind="explain">

Every electrical device is rated in **watts** — a 60 W bulb, a 2000 W kettle, a 5 W phone charger. That number is its **power**: how much electrical energy it converts each second.

Power ties voltage and current together. Voltage is energy *per charge*, current is charge *per second* — multiply them and the charges cancel, leaving energy *per second*, which is power:

<Formula tex="P = V \times I" block />

Watts are just joules per second, the same unit of power you met back in the energy unit.

</Step>

<Step title="Watch the power" kind="interactive">

Here's the circuit again, now reporting power as well as current. Push the voltage or ease the resistance and watch the wattage — and the bulb's brightness — respond.

<CircuitLab showPower={true} />

A brighter bulb is simply one converting more **power**. Notice how raising the voltage drives power up sharply: more voltage gives both more energy per charge *and* more current, so the effect compounds.

</Step>

<Step title="Three faces of the same formula" kind="explain">

Combine `P = VI` with Ohm's law (`V = IR`) and power can be written three equivalent ways:

- **P = V × I** — when you know voltage and current.
- **P = I² × R** — when you know current and resistance.
- **P = V² / R** — when you know voltage and resistance.

The middle form, `P = I²R`, explains why wires get warm: any current through any resistance dissipates power as heat. It's why kettles and heaters use high current through a resistive element on purpose — and why a **fuse** (a deliberately thin wire) melts and breaks the circuit if the current climbs dangerously high.

</Step>

<Step title="Paying for energy" kind="explain">

Power is the *rate* of using energy; the total **energy** used is just power multiplied by time:

<Formula tex="E = P \times t" block />

<Callout type="info" title="What's on your electricity bill">
Energy companies don't charge for watts — they charge for energy, in **kilowatt-hours (kWh)**: one kilowatt running for one hour. A 2 kW heater left on for 3 hours uses 6 kWh. That's why leaving a high-power device on for a long time is what really costs money: it's power *times time* that you pay for.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Power** is energy converted per second, in **watts**: `P = V × I`.
- Equivalent forms via Ohm's law: **P = I²R** and **P = V²/R**; `I²R` is why resistance produces heat (and why fuses work).
- **Energy = power × time** (`E = P × t`); bills are charged in **kilowatt-hours**.

</Step>
