<Step title="A planet we can't dig into" kind="explain">

The deepest hole humanity has ever drilled reaches about 12 km down — barely a pinprick into a planet 6,370 km to its centre. We will never dig to the core. And yet we know, with real confidence, that the Earth has a thin rocky crust, a vast hot mantle, a **liquid** outer core, and a **solid** iron inner core.

How can we possibly know? The answer is a beautiful piece of physics: we listen to **earthquakes**, and let waves do the digging for us.

</Step>

<Step title="Listening with earthquakes" kind="interactive">

A large earthquake sends **seismic waves** racing through the entire planet, picked up by stations all over the world. Two kinds matter here. **P-waves** are pressure waves (like sound) that travel through solids *and* liquids. **S-waves** are shear waves that wobble side-to-side — and, crucially, **cannot travel through liquid at all.** Flip between them.

<EarthInterior />

The giveaway: S-waves vanish across a huge swath of the far side — the **shadow zone**. Something down there is stopping them. The only explanation is a region of **liquid**: the molten outer core. We deduced a liquid layer 2,900 km down without ever seeing it, purely from missing waves.

</Step>

<Step title="The layers within" kind="explain">

Piecing together how waves speed up, slow, and bend at each boundary reveals the structure (tap the layers above):

- **Crust** — a thin, cool, brittle rock skin, 5–70 km thick.
- **Mantle** — hot solid rock that creeps like putty over geological time; it makes up most of the Earth's volume.
- **Outer core** — molten iron and nickel, ~2,900 km down.
- **Inner core** — iron squeezed **solid** by immense pressure, despite a temperature of ~5,400 °C, comparable to the Sun's surface.

Wave speeds jump at each boundary because the material's density and stiffness change — and tracking those jumps maps the interior like an ultrasound of the planet.

</Step>

<Step title="The engine that drives the surface" kind="explain">

That interior is not inert. Heat from the Earth's formation and from radioactive decay keeps the mantle slowly churning in giant **convection** currents (the same heat-transfer process you met with fluids). This churn drags the brittle crust along, broken into a dozen great **tectonic plates**.

Where plates grind, dive, and pull apart, you get **earthquakes, volcanoes, and mountain ranges** — and, over hundreds of millions of years, the slow rearrangement of the continents themselves. The restless surface is the visible exhaust of the planet's hidden inner heat.

</Step>

<Step title="A magnetic shield from a molten core" kind="explain">

The liquid outer core does more than block S-waves. As that electrically-conducting molten iron churns and the planet spins, it acts as a colossal natural **dynamo** — moving conductor plus motion generating electric currents and, with them, the Earth's **magnetic field** (exactly the induction principle from the magnetism unit, on a planetary scale).

<Callout type="info" title="Why the field matters">
That magnetic field isn't just for compasses. It deflects the Sun's stream of charged particles — the solar wind — that would otherwise strip away our atmosphere over time. A planet's molten, convecting core helps keep its air, and so its prospects for life. Mars, whose core cooled, largely lost both.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- We map the Earth's interior with **seismic waves** from earthquakes, not by digging.
- **P-waves** pass through solid and liquid; **S-waves** can't cross liquid — their **shadow zone** reveals the molten **outer core**.
- The layers: thin **crust**, vast creeping **mantle**, liquid iron **outer core**, solid iron **inner core**.
- Mantle **convection** drives **plate tectonics** — earthquakes, volcanoes, mountains, drifting continents.
- The churning molten core is a **dynamo** generating Earth's protective **magnetic field**.

</Step>
