<Lesson>

<Step title="The big idea" kind="explain">

Push a shopping cart gently and it creeps forward. Shove it hard and it leaps. Load it with bricks and the same shove barely moves it. **Newton's second law** captures all of that in one line:

<Formula tex="F = ma" block />

Force equals mass times acceleration. The harder you push (**F**), the more something accelerates (**a**) — but the heavier it is (**m**), the less it responds to the same push.

</Step>

<Step title="It's the *net* force that counts" kind="explain">

Real objects feel several forces at once. Gravity pulls a block down; the floor pushes it up. Those cancel. What's left over — the **net force** — is what actually accelerates it.

<Figure
  src="/lessons/physics/newtons-second-law/free-body.svg"
  alt="A block with weight and normal force cancelling vertically, leaving the applied force to drive acceleration"
  caption="The up/down forces balance, so the leftover horizontal force sets the acceleration."
/>

So a better way to read the law is: **net force = mass × acceleration**.

</Step>

Time to feel it. Change the **force** and the **mass** and watch the acceleration — and the block's pickup — respond instantly.

<Callout type="tip" title="Try this">

</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="What exactly is a Newton?" kind="explain">

A **newton (N)** is just the force needed to accelerate **1 kilogram** at **1 metre per second squared**:

<Formula tex="1\ \text{N} = 1\ \text{kg} \cdot \text{m/s}^2" block />

That's why the law is so useful: plug in kilograms and metres-per-second-squared, and the force comes out in newtons — no fudge factors.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

Newton's second law ties force, mass, and acceleration together:

- More **net force** → more acceleration.
- More **mass** → less acceleration for the same force.
- It's always the **net** (leftover) force that matters.

<Formula tex="a = \dfrac{F}{m}" block />

</Step>
