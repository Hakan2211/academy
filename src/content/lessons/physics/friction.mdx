<Lesson>

<Step title="The force that resists sliding" kind="explain">

Slide a book across a table and it slows to a stop. Something pushed back against its motion: **friction**.

Friction acts **along surfaces in contact** and always **opposes** the relative sliding. It comes from the microscopic roughness — and even tiny bonding — between two surfaces pressed together.

</Step>

<Step title="Applied force vs friction" kind="interactive">

Push a resting block and friction pushes back. While your push is small, friction **matches it** and the net force stays zero — the block doesn't budge. Push hard enough and you win: there's a net force, and it slides.

<Callout type="tip" title="In real life">
Real friction isn't a free dial — it automatically grows to match your push, but only **up to a maximum**. Beyond that limit, the block breaks free and slides.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Static vs kinetic" kind="explain">

There are two flavors:

- **Static friction** — grips a stationary object, adjusting to cancel your push *up to a maximum*. This is what you must overcome to get something moving.
- **Kinetic friction** — acts once the object is sliding. It's usually a bit **weaker** than the static maximum, which is why a heavy box suddenly "gives" and then slides more easily.

</Step>

<Step title="Friend and foe" kind="explain">

Friction wastes energy as **heat** and wears parts down — but we'd be lost without it. It's what lets you **walk**, lets **tyres grip**, and lets **brakes** stop a car. Engineers add it where they need grip and remove it (with oil, wheels, air cushions) where they don't.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Friction** opposes sliding between surfaces in contact.
- **Static** friction holds things put (up to a maximum); **kinetic** friction acts while sliding and is usually a little weaker.
- It costs energy as heat, but gives us walking, grip, and braking.

</Step>
