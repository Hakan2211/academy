<Lesson>

<Step title="Forces come in pairs" kind="explain">

**Newton's third law:** for every force, there's an **equal and opposite** force. Push on something, and it pushes back on you *just as hard*.

The famous phrasing is "every action has an equal and opposite reaction." The crucial fine print: the two forces act on **different objects**.

</Step>

<Step title="Equal and opposite" kind="interactive">

Watch two objects shove each other apart. The force on A and the force on B are the **same size** (equal arrows) but point in **opposite directions** — and each acts on a *different* block.

<ActionReaction />

<Callout type="tip" title="Why don't they cancel?">
Because they're on **different objects**. Forces only cancel when they act on the **same** object. Here, each block feels just one of the pair, so each one moves.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="How you walk (and rockets fly)" kind="explain">

You walk by pushing the ground **backward**; by the third law the ground pushes **you forward**. A swimmer pushes water back and gets shoved ahead. A rocket throws gas **downward** and the gas throws the rocket **up** — no air required.

Every one of these is one action–reaction pair doing useful work because the two halves land on different things.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Third law:** forces always come in **equal, opposite pairs**.
- The two forces act on **different objects**, which is why they don't cancel.
- Walking, swimming, and rockets all work by pushing one way to be pushed the other.

</Step>
