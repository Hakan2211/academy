<Step title="The restoring force" kind="explain">

A **pendulum** is just a mass on a string. Pull it aside and gravity tugs it back toward the middle — but it overshoots, swings past, and comes back. That endless back-and-forth is **simple harmonic motion**.

The time for one complete swing is the **period**, T:

<Formula tex="T = 2\pi\sqrt{\tfrac{L}{g}}" block />

Look closely at what's *inside* that formula — and, just as importantly, what isn't.

</Step>

<Step title="Watch the rhythm" kind="explain">

If you plot the bob's position over time, you get a smooth wave. One full cycle of that wave is exactly one period.

<SineWave />

The motion is perfectly regular — like a metronome. That steadiness is why pendulums kept time in clocks for centuries.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

The period of a pendulum depends on just two things:

- the **length** L — longer string, slower swing
- the local **gravity** g — stronger gravity, faster swing

It does **not** depend on the mass of the bob, nor (for small swings) on how far you pull it aside.

<Formula tex="T = 2\pi\sqrt{\tfrac{L}{g}}" block />

</Step>
