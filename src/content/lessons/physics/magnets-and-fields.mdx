<Step title="Poles always come in pairs" kind="explain">

Every magnet has two ends — a **north pole** and a **south pole** — and they behave by one simple rule, the magnetic echo of the charge rule you met in electricity:

**Like poles repel, opposite poles attract.** Two norths push apart; a north and a south pull together.

Here's the strange part. With electric charge you can have a lone positive or a lone negative. With magnets you *can't* isolate a single pole. Snap a bar magnet in half and you don't get a separate N and S — you get **two smaller magnets**, each with its own N and S. Cut those again and the same thing happens. A lone magnetic pole has never been found.

</Step>

<Step title="The field around a magnet" kind="interactive">

A magnet reaches out into the space around it with a **magnetic field** — the region where its force can be felt. We map that invisible field with **field lines**, and a small compass needle will always swing to line up with them.

<MagnetField />

The lines run from **north to south** outside the magnet, and they bunch together where the field is strongest — right at the poles. Switch the second magnet between attract and repel and watch the whole field reshape: opposite poles let their lines join up and pull together; like poles crowd their lines into a barrier and shove apart.

</Step>

<Step title="What makes something magnetic" kind="explain">

Only a few materials — **iron, nickel, cobalt**, and their alloys (like steel) — are strongly magnetic. Inside them, groups of atoms act as tiny magnets called **domains**. Normally these point every which way and cancel out. Bring a magnet near and the domains swing into alignment, and the whole object becomes magnetic.

- In a **permanent magnet**, the domains stay locked in alignment.
- In a **temporary magnet** (a paperclip near a magnet), they spring back to random once the magnet leaves.

Heating a magnet red-hot or hammering it jostles the domains out of line — which is why you can *demagnetise* a magnet by abusing it.

</Step>

<Step title="Earth is a giant magnet" kind="explain">

Why does a compass work at all? Because the entire planet behaves like one enormous bar magnet, its field generated by churning molten iron in the core.

<Callout type="info" title="A useful confusion of names">
A compass needle's north end points roughly toward Earth's **geographic** north — which means there must be a magnetic **south** pole up there (opposite poles attract). The labels are a historical quirk, but the navigation works.
</Callout>

Earth's field does more than guide hikers: it deflects the Sun's charged particles, shielding life from radiation. Where those particles leak in near the poles, they light up the sky as the **auroras**.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- Every magnet has a **north and south pole**; **like repel, opposite attract**, and you can't isolate a single pole.
- A **magnetic field** surrounds a magnet; field lines run **N → S** outside and bunch where it's strongest. Compasses align to it.
- Magnetism comes from aligned **domains** in iron, nickel, and cobalt; heat or shock can demagnetise.
- **Earth itself is a magnet** — guiding compasses and shielding us from solar particles.

</Step>
