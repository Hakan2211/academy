<Lesson>

<Step title="Same element, different weight" kind="explain">

You just saw that the **proton** count fixes which element an atom is. But the **neutron** count is free to vary — and atoms of the same element with different neutron counts are called **isotopes**.

Because they have the same number of protons (and therefore electrons), isotopes are chemically *identical*: they bond the same way, burn the same way, taste the same way. The only real difference is their **mass**, and — as you'll see — whether the nucleus is stable.

</Step>

<Step title="How we name them" kind="explain">

Every isotope is pinned down by two numbers:

- the **atomic number** Z = number of protons (fixes the element),
- the **mass number** A = protons + neutrons (the count of nucleons).

We write an isotope as the element symbol with the mass number attached, like **carbon-12** or **carbon-14**. Since the element already tells you the protons, the neutrons are just the leftover:

<Formula tex="N = A - Z" block />

So carbon (Z = 6) with mass number 14 has 14 − 6 = **8 neutrons**.

</Step>

<Step title="Meet the carbon family" kind="interactive">

Carbon always has 6 protons. Slide the neutron count and watch the same element take on different masses — and different fates.

<IsotopeLab />

- **Carbon-12** (6 neutrons) and **carbon-13** (7) are perfectly **stable** — they make up essentially all the carbon on Earth.
- **Carbon-14** (8) is **radioactive**: its nucleus slowly falls apart, which turns out to be enormously useful for dating ancient things.
- Push the neutrons further from that sweet spot and the nucleus becomes so lopsided it can't survive at all.

</Step>

<Step title="Why atomic masses aren't whole numbers" kind="explain">

Glance at a periodic table and you'll see carbon listed as **12.011**, not a tidy 12. Why the messy decimal?

Because the value is a **weighted average** over all the isotopes as they actually occur in nature. Carbon is 98.9% carbon-12 and 1.1% carbon-13, so the average sits just a hair above 12:

<Formula tex="(0.989 \times 12) + (0.011 \times 13) \approx 12.011" block />

<Callout type="info" title="Relative atomic mass">
The figure on the periodic table — the **relative atomic mass** — is this natural average. An element with two common isotopes of very different abundance, like chlorine (mostly Cl-35 with some Cl-37), lands at about 35.5: a value no single atom ever actually has.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Isotopes** are atoms of the same element (same protons) with **different neutron counts** — and so different masses.
- An isotope is labelled by its **mass number** A = Z + N; neutrons are N = A − Z.
- Isotopes are **chemically identical**, but some are stable and others **radioactive**.
- The periodic table's decimal masses are **weighted averages** over an element's natural isotope mix.

</Step>
