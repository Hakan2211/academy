<Step title="Objects are lazy" kind="explain">

**Newton's first law:** an object keeps doing whatever it's already doing — staying still, or moving in a straight line at constant speed — **unless a net force acts on it**.

This stubbornness has a name: **inertia**. A resting book won't slide on its own; a puck gliding in space would coast forever. Change requires a *net* force.

</Step>

<Step title="When forces balance" kind="interactive">

Here's the key link: when the forces on something are **balanced** (net force zero), its motion **doesn't change**. Balance the pushes below and notice the verdict.

<Callout type="tip" title="Notice">
Balanced doesn't mean "not moving" — it means "not *changing* motion." A balanced object could be parked **or** cruising at steady speed.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Why you lurch in a bus" kind="explain">

When a bus brakes hard, you pitch forward. The bus stopped — but **your inertia** kept you moving until your seatbelt supplied the force to stop you. Same reason a coffee cup slides when you accelerate away from a light.

The more **mass** something has, the more inertia it has — so mass is really a *measure of how hard it is to change an object's motion.*

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **First law / inertia:** motion stays the same unless a **net force** acts.
- **Balanced forces** (net 0) → no change in motion (rest *or* steady cruise).
- **Mass** measures inertia — how strongly an object resists changing its motion.

</Step>
