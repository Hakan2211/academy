<Lesson>

<Step title="Resistance vanishes" kind="explain">

In an ordinary wire, electrons bump and scatter as they move, losing energy as heat — that's electrical **resistance**, the reason cables warm up and power is lost across the grid. For most materials it shrinks as you cool them, but never to zero.

In 1911, cooling mercury close to absolute zero, Heike Kamerlingh Onnes saw its resistance suddenly drop not just low but to **exactly zero**. A current started in a superconducting loop will circle for years without fading. This is **superconductivity** — and it's one of the strangest, most useful states of matter we know.

</Step>

<Step title="A critical temperature" kind="interactive">

Superconductivity switches on abruptly below a material's **critical temperature**, Tc. Above it: an ordinary resistor. A hair below it: zero resistance, and a second trick — the material **expels magnetic fields from its interior**, the **Meissner effect**. Drag the temperature across Tc and watch.

<Meissner />

Because the field is pushed out, a magnet hovering above a superconductor is held aloft on a cushion of excluded field — stable, frictionless levitation. It's not just a very good conductor doing this; expelling the field is a distinct quantum behaviour all its own.

</Step>

<Step title="Why it happens: Cooper pairs" kind="explain">

How can electrons stop scattering entirely? Below Tc, electrons quietly **pair up** into *Cooper pairs*, bound together through subtle interactions with the vibrating crystal lattice.

These pairs behave utterly unlike lone electrons. They all drop into a single, shared quantum state and move in perfect lockstep — a coordinated flow that the lattice's imperfections simply cannot disturb one pair at a time. With nothing to scatter off, the current glides on forever. Superconductivity is **quantum mechanics made macroscopic**: a single quantum state spanning a whole wire.

</Step>

<Step title="What it's good for" kind="explain">

Zero resistance and strong, stable magnetic fields make superconductors quietly indispensable:

- **MRI scanners** use superconducting magnets to image inside your body.
- **Particle accelerators** like the LHC steer beams with thousands of superconducting magnets.
- **Maglev trains** float and glide on superconducting magnetic suspension.
- Future **lossless power lines** could carry electricity with no waste at all.

The catch is the cold. Early superconductors needed liquid helium near −269 °C — expensive and awkward.

</Step>

<Step title="The race for warmth" kind="explain">

In 1986 came a shock: certain ceramic compounds superconduct at far higher temperatures — above the boiling point of cheap liquid **nitrogen** (−196 °C). These **high-temperature superconductors** made the technology far more practical, and nobody fully understands *why* they work; Cooper-pair theory doesn't cleanly explain them.

<Callout type="tip" title="The room-temperature dream">
A material that superconducts at everyday temperatures and ordinary pressure would be revolutionary — lossless grids, effortless maglev, cheap powerful magnets. It remains one of the great unsolved goals of physics, and a frontier of active, sometimes controversial, research.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- A **superconductor** has **exactly zero** electrical resistance below its **critical temperature** Tc.
- It also expels magnetic fields (the **Meissner effect**), enabling stable magnetic **levitation**.
- The cause is **Cooper pairs** of electrons sharing one quantum state and flowing without scattering — quantum mechanics on a macroscopic scale.
- Uses include **MRI, accelerators, maglev, and lossless power**.
- **High-temperature superconductors** work above liquid-nitrogen temperatures but aren't fully understood; **room-temperature** superconductivity is a major open frontier.

</Step>
