<Lesson>

<Step title="Graphs tell the story" kind="explain">

Words like "fast" and "speeding up" are vague. A **graph** pins them down. Two graphs do most of the work in motion:

- a **position–time** graph — *where* the object is at each moment, and
- a **velocity–time** graph — *how fast* it's going at each moment.

Learn to read these and you can describe almost any motion at a glance.

</Step>

<Step title="Play with the graphs" kind="interactive">

Switch between the scenarios. Watch the object move while the dots trace both graphs **at the same time**.

<KinematicsLab scenarios={["rest", "uniform", "accelerate"]} />

<Callout type="tip" title="Look for this">
On the **position–time** graph, a *steeper* line means the object is moving *faster*. A *flat* line means it isn't moving at all.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Slope is the secret" kind="explain">

The single most useful idea here: **the slope (steepness) of a position–time graph is the velocity.**

- **Flat** line → position isn't changing → **at rest**.
- **Straight, sloped** line → position changes steadily → **constant velocity**.
- **Curved** line → the slope itself is changing → the velocity is changing.

Steeper up = faster forward; sloping down would mean moving backward.

</Step>

<Step title="What the velocity graph adds" kind="explain">

The **velocity–time** graph shows speed directly, so you don't have to judge slopes:

- a **flat** line sitting above zero → steady speed,
- a line **climbing** upward → speeding up,
- a line at **zero** → parked.

The two graphs are just two views of the same motion.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Position–time** graph: shows *where*; its **slope = velocity**.
- Flat → at rest · straight slope → constant velocity · curve → changing velocity.
- **Velocity–time** graph: shows speed directly; flat = steady, climbing = speeding up.

Next we'll name that "climbing line" — it's **acceleration**.

</Step>
