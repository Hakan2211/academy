<Lesson>

<Step title="Visible light is just a sliver" kind="explain">

The colours your eyes can see are a *tiny* fraction of a vast family of waves — all of them **electromagnetic waves**, all travelling at the same speed **c**. They differ in just one thing: **wavelength**.

Stretch the wavelength out and you get radio waves; squeeze it down and you get X-rays and gamma rays. Visible light sits in a narrow band in the middle.

</Step>

<Step title="Tour the spectrum" kind="interactive">

Slide the marker from one end to the other. Notice how small the visible strip is compared to the whole range — and how wavelength and frequency trade off.

<SpectrumBar />

From kilometres-long radio waves to wavelengths smaller than an atom, it's all the same kind of wave.

</Step>

<Step title="What each band does" kind="explain">

Each region has a personality set by its wavelength:

- **Radio** — broadcasting, Wi-Fi, phones.
- **Microwave** — ovens, radar, satellite links.
- **Infrared** — heat, TV remotes, night vision.
- **Visible** — the narrow band we see.
- **Ultraviolet** — sunburn, sterilising lamps.
- **X-rays** — seeing through flesh to bone.
- **Gamma rays** — from nuclear reactions and deep space.

</Step>

<Step title="One family, ranked by energy" kind="explain">

Because they're all light, they all obey **c = f λ**. Shorter wavelength means higher frequency — and **higher energy**:

<Formula tex="c = f\lambda" block />

That energy ladder explains the danger gradient: harmless radio waves at one end, cell-damaging gamma rays at the other. UV already carries enough punch to burn skin; X-rays and gamma rays can break molecules apart.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Visible light** is a thin slice of the **electromagnetic spectrum**.
- All EM waves travel at **c** and differ only in **wavelength/frequency**.
- The bands — radio, microwave, infrared, visible, UV, X-ray, gamma — are one family with different uses.
- **c = f λ**: shorter wavelength → higher frequency → **more energy**.

</Step>
