<Lesson>

<Step title="Does “now” mean the same thing everywhere?" kind="explain">

We carry a deep, unspoken assumption: that there is a single universal "now" ticking away everywhere at once. Two events either happen at the same time or they don't — surely that's just a fact, the same for everyone.

Special relativity says no. Once you accept that light travels at c for every observer, you're forced to give up absolute simultaneity. Whether two separated events happen "at the same time" **depends on how you're moving**. This is the strangest consequence of relativity, and in a sense the one underneath all the others.

</Step>

<Step title="The train and the lightning" kind="interactive">

Einstein's own thought experiment makes it vivid. Lightning strikes both ends of a moving train. An observer on the embankment stands exactly halfway between the two strike points; an observer on the train sits at its midpoint, rushing toward one end.

<Simultaneity />

Switch between the two observers. The **embankment** observer is midway between the strikes, so the two light flashes — each travelling at c — reach her together: for her, the strikes are **simultaneous**. The **train** observer is speeding toward the front strike and away from the rear one, so the front flash reaches her *first*: for her, the front strike happened **before** the rear.

Both used light at speed c. Both reasoned correctly. They simply disagree about what happened "at the same time."

</Step>

<Step title="Why this is the deep one" kind="explain">

Notice that this isn't about delays in *seeing* things — both observers correctly account for travel time. It's that the events are genuinely **unordered in time** in a way that depends on the observer's motion. Time dilation and length contraction both flow from this: once observers can't agree on simultaneity, they can't agree on durations or lengths either.

<Callout type="warn" title="But cause and effect are safe">
If the order of events can flip, can an effect come before its cause? No. The reordering only ever applies to events far enough apart that **not even light** could travel between them — so neither could have caused the other. Any two events that *can* influence each other keep their order for everyone. Relativity bends time, but it never lets you break causality.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Spacetime: one fabric, sliced differently" kind="explain">

If different observers split events into "space" and "time" differently, maybe space and time aren't truly separate things. That was the insight of **Hermann Minkowski**, Einstein's former teacher: the universe is a single four-dimensional **spacetime**, and each observer merely slices it into "now" at their own angle.

<Callout type="info" title="A loaf of bread">
Picture spacetime as a loaf of bread. "Now" is a single slice through it. Observers moving differently cut the loaf at slightly different angles — so they include different events in their "present moment." The loaf is the same for everyone; only the slicing differs.
</Callout>

This unified spacetime is the stage on which the rest of relativity plays out — and, in the general theory, the very thing that gravity will bend.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- There is **no universal “now.”** Whether two separated events are simultaneous depends on the observer's motion.
- Einstein's **train**: the midpoint embankment observer sees the strikes as simultaneous; the moving train observer sees one first.
- This underlies time dilation and length contraction — but **causality is preserved**: events that can influence each other never reorder.
- Space and time merge into a single **spacetime** (Minkowski) that each observer simply slices differently.

</Step>
