<Lesson>

<Step title="Seeing is bouncing" kind="explain">

You can read this because light from your screen or lamp **bounces off** every surface in the room and into your eyes. That bouncing is **reflection**, and it follows a strict rule.

To describe it we draw the **normal** — an imaginary line perpendicular to the surface at the point the ray hits. Every angle is measured from this normal, not from the surface.

</Step>

<Step title="The law of reflection" kind="interactive">

Here's the rule: the **angle of incidence equals the angle of reflection**. Drag the incoming ray and watch the reflected ray mirror it exactly.

<MirrorReflect />

Whatever angle light arrives at, it leaves at the same angle on the other side of the normal. Always.

</Step>

<Step title="Smooth vs. rough" kind="explain">

If every surface reflects light, why can you see yourself in a mirror but not in a wall?

- A **smooth** surface (mirror, still water) reflects parallel rays all the same way — **specular** reflection — preserving the image.
- A **rough** surface scatters rays in every direction — **diffuse** reflection — so you see the object but no clear image.

Zoom in far enough and a wall is a jumble of tiny angled facets, each obeying the law of reflection but pointing every which way.

</Step>

<Step title="Your reflection in a flat mirror" kind="explain">

A plane mirror gives a very particular image. It is:

- **Upright** and the **same size** as you,
- **Laterally inverted** — left and right appear swapped,
- **Virtual** — it seems to sit *behind* the glass, where no light actually goes.

Your brain assumes light travelled straight, so it places the image behind the mirror, as far back as you stand in front.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- We see objects by light **reflecting** off them into our eyes.
- **Angle of incidence = angle of reflection**, measured from the **normal**.
- **Smooth** surfaces give clear (specular) images; **rough** ones scatter (diffuse).
- A plane mirror's image is upright, same-size, laterally inverted, and **virtual** (behind the glass).

</Step>
