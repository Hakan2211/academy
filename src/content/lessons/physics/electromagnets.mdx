<Lesson>

<Step title="From a wire to a coil" kind="explain">

A single straight wire makes only a weak, circular field. But bend that wire into a loop and the field inside the loop all points the same way, reinforcing itself. Stack many loops into a **coil** — a **solenoid** — and every turn adds its field to the next.

The result is striking: a solenoid's field looks exactly like a **bar magnet's**, with a clear north pole at one end and a south pole at the other. You've built a magnet out of nothing but a coil of wire and a current.

</Step>

<Step title="Build a stronger magnet" kind="interactive">

Three controls make an electromagnet stronger — and one makes it *flip*.

<Solenoid />

- **More current** → stronger field.
- **More turns** in the coil → stronger field.
- **An iron core** inside the coil → far stronger field, because the iron's own domains line up and add to it.
- **Reverse the current** → the north and south poles swap ends.

</Step>

<Step title="The killer feature: control" kind="explain">

A permanent magnet is always on. An electromagnet is something far more useful: a magnet you can **command**.

- Switch it **on and off** with the current.
- Make it **stronger or weaker** by changing the current.
- **Reverse its poles** by reversing the current.

That controllability is why electromagnets, not permanent magnets, sit at the heart of almost every electrical machine. They turn an electrical signal into a mechanical magnetic action on demand.

</Step>

<Step title="Electromagnets are everywhere" kind="explain">

Once you can switch a magnet, you can build astonishing things:

<Callout type="info" title="Hidden electromagnets around you">
- **Scrapyard cranes** lift tonnes of steel, then drop it by cutting the current.
- **Relays** let a small current switch a big one — the basis of early computing.
- **Doorbells, loudspeakers, and headphones** turn electrical signals into movement and sound.
- **Maglev trains** float and glide on switchable fields, and **MRI scanners** use enormous electromagnets.
</Callout>

Every one of these is just Ørsted's coil, scaled and switched to do a job.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- Coiling a wire into a **solenoid** concentrates the field into a **bar-magnet shape** with N and S poles.
- Strength rises with **current**, **number of turns**, and adding an **iron core**; reversing the current **swaps the poles**.
- The decisive advantage is **control** — switchable, variable, reversible magnetism.
- Electromagnets power **cranes, relays, speakers, maglev, and MRI**.

</Step>
