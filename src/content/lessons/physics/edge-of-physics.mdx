<Step title="Standing at the edge of the map" kind="explain">

You've now travelled the whole sweep of physics — from a swinging pendulum to the Big Bang, from electric charge to curved spacetime, from the atom to the quark. It's a staggering map of reality, tested to absurd precision and woven into every piece of technology you own.

But every map has edges, and beyond them: *here be dragons*. This final lesson is about what physics **doesn't** know — the open questions at the frontier, where today's students might draw tomorrow's discoveries. Not knowing isn't an embarrassment. It's the most exciting place to stand.

</Step>

<Step title="The universe is mostly missing" kind="interactive">

Recall the Standard Model — the complete inventory of known particles, the stuff of stars, planets, and you.

<StandardModel />

Here's the humbling part: **everything** on this chart — every atom in every galaxy — makes up only about **5%** of the universe. Galaxies spin so fast they should fly apart, held together by the gravity of something we cannot see. We call it **dark matter**: roughly 27% of the cosmos, made of *none* of the particles above. We know it's there only by its pull. What is it? Nobody knows.

</Step>

<Step title="And it's flying apart faster" kind="interactive">

The other ~68% is stranger still. In the cosmology unit you saw the universe expanding. In 1998, astronomers measured that expansion expecting it to be *slowing* under gravity — and found it **accelerating** instead.

<HubbleExpansion />

Something is pushing space apart, growing stronger as space grows. We call it **dark energy**, and it dominates the universe — yet we have almost no idea what it is. Together, dark matter and dark energy mean that **95% of reality is a mystery** we've only just learned to notice.

</Step>

<Step title="Two theories that won't talk" kind="interactive">

Modern physics rests on two magnificent pillars. **Quantum mechanics** rules the tiny — particles, atoms, the Standard Model. **General relativity** rules the large — gravity as the curvature of spacetime.

<SpacetimeWell />

Each is fantastically accurate in its own domain. The trouble: they are mathematically **incompatible**. Try to combine them and the equations spit out nonsense — infinities that refuse to cancel. There is no agreed theory of **quantum gravity**. Two of our deepest truths about nature contradict each other, and reconciling them is perhaps the single greatest unsolved problem in physics.

</Step>

<Step title="Where the theories break" kind="explain">

The clash isn't just abstract. There are real places in the universe where the tiny and the enormous collide and *both* theories fail at once:

- At the **centre of a black hole**, matter is crushed into a point of infinite density — a *singularity* where relativity's equations break down.
- At the **first instant of the Big Bang**, the entire universe was smaller than an atom — quantum *and* gravitational at the same time.

To understand these, we'd need the quantum-gravity theory we don't have. Candidates exist — **string theory**, **loop quantum gravity** — but none has yet made a testable prediction we could check. They remain beautiful mathematics in search of an experiment.

</Step>

<Step title="What is the quantum world, really?" kind="explain">

Even where quantum mechanics *works* flawlessly, nobody fully agrees on what it **means**. The maths says a particle exists in a haze of probabilities until measured — but what counts as a "measurement"? Why does observing collapse the haze into one outcome?

This is the **measurement problem**, and serious physicists answer it in wildly different ways: a genuine random collapse, countless parallel universes branching at every event, or that the wavefunction was never "real" to begin with. The equations make perfect predictions; their *interpretation* is still genuinely unsettled a century on.

<Callout type="info" title="A working tool we don't understand">
It's a peculiar situation: we use quantum mechanics to build lasers, chips, and MRI machines with total confidence — while still arguing about what it's actually telling us about reality. Useful and mysterious at once.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="The dream of unification" kind="explain">

Physics has a long, glorious habit of discovering that two things are secretly one. Electricity and magnetism became **electromagnetism**. That, in turn, was unified with the weak force into the **electroweak** force. The strong force is partly folded in too.

The dream is a single **"theory of everything"** — one set of equations from which all four forces, all particles, and spacetime itself emerge. Einstein chased it for the last thirty years of his life and failed. Gravity remains the holdout, refusing to join the others. Whether a final unification exists, or whether nature is simply messier than our craving for elegance, is unknown.

</Step>

<Step title="Why these numbers?" kind="explain">

The Standard Model contains around twenty numbers — particle masses, force strengths — that we can only **measure**, never derive. And they appear strangely **fine-tuned**: nudge several of them even slightly and stars couldn't form, atoms couldn't bind, and life would be impossible.

Why these values? Three camps argue: it's a coincidence; there's a deeper theory that *fixes* them (we just haven't found it); or there are countless universes with different constants — a **multiverse** — and we inevitably find ourselves in one of the rare ones that permits observers. Each answer is profound, and none is yet testable. The question sits right at the boundary between physics and philosophy.

</Step>

<Step title="How we'll find out" kind="explain">

These aren't questions we just shrug at — they're being **attacked** right now, with the same method you met at the very start of this course: observe, predict, test.

- Ever-larger **particle colliders** hunt for particles beyond the Standard Model.
- **Gravitational-wave detectors** listen to colliding black holes, testing relativity in its most extreme regime.
- Deep-underground experiments wait patiently for a **dark-matter** particle to reveal itself.
- Giant **telescopes** map dark energy by watching the expansion across cosmic time.

The history of physics is a string of "unanswerable" questions that fell to a clever experiment. There's no reason to think these are different.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- The Standard Model + relativity form a superb map of reality — but it has **edges**.
- **Dark matter (~27%)** and **dark energy (~68%)** mean ~**95% of the universe** is unidentified.
- **Quantum mechanics** and **general relativity** are both right yet mathematically **incompatible** — there's no accepted theory of **quantum gravity**.
- Both break down inside **black holes** and at the **Big Bang**; candidates (string theory, loop quantum gravity) aren't yet testable.
- The **measurement problem** means we still debate what quantum mechanics *means*, even as we use it daily.
- Open too: a full **unification** of the forces, and *why* the constants of nature are so finely tuned.
- These frontiers are being tested **now** — by colliders, gravitational waves, dark-matter detectors, and telescopes — with the very same scientific method this course began with.

</Step>
