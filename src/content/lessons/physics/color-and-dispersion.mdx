<Lesson>

<Step title="White light is a disguise" kind="explain">

Sunlight looks colourless, but it's a **mixture of every colour at once**. The colours are hiding in plain sight — you just need a way to spread them apart.

Each colour is light of a different **wavelength**: red is the longest, violet the shortest, with orange, yellow, green, and blue in between.

</Step>

<Step title="Split it with a prism" kind="interactive">

Send white light through a glass prism and the colours fan out into a **spectrum**.

<PrismDispersion />

This works because refraction bends each wavelength by a *slightly different amount* — so the single white beam separates into a rainbow on the way out.

</Step>

<Step title="Dispersion, and rainbows" kind="explain">

The spreading of colours by wavelength is called **dispersion**. Violet light slows down most in glass, so it bends the most; red bends the least.

A **rainbow** is the same trick on a giant scale: millions of raindrops each act as a tiny prism, refracting and reflecting sunlight back to your eyes — red on the outer edge, violet on the inner.

</Step>

<Step title="Why things have colour" kind="explain">

If white light contains every colour, why is an apple red? Because its skin **reflects** red wavelengths and **absorbs** the rest. The colour you see is the light an object *fails* to soak up.

<Callout type="tip" title="A quick consequence">
A "white" shirt reflects all colours; a "black" one absorbs nearly all of them (which is why black clothes get hot in the Sun). Under a pure red lamp, a blue object looks black — there's no blue light for it to reflect.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **White light** is a blend of all colours, each a different **wavelength**.
- A prism **disperses** it because each wavelength refracts by a different amount (violet most, red least).
- **Rainbows** are raindrops doing the same thing at scale.
- Objects look coloured because they **reflect some wavelengths and absorb others**.

</Step>
