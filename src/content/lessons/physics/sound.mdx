<Lesson>

<Step title="Sound is squeezed air" kind="explain">

A loudspeaker cone pushes forward and pulls back, over and over. Each push **squeezes** the air in front of it (a compression); each pull lets it **spread out** (a rarefaction). Those pressure ripples race outward as a **longitudinal wave** — and when they reach your eardrum, you hear.

Sound isn't a substance flying through the air. It's a *pattern of pressure* moving through air that mostly stays put.

</Step>

<Step title="Watch the compressions travel" kind="interactive">

Switch the toggle to **longitudinal** and watch the particles shuffle back and forth, bunching into moving compressions — exactly how sound propagates.

<WaveType />

The particles barely go anywhere; the *compression pattern* is what travels across the room.

</Step>

<Step title="No air, no sound" kind="explain">

Because sound needs particles to pass the squeeze along, it **cannot travel through a vacuum**. In space, no one can hear anything — there's nothing to compress.

This is the big difference from light, which is an electromagnetic wave and sails happily through empty space. Sound is stuck needing a medium: air, water, or solid.

</Step>

<Step title="Pitch and loudness" kind="explain">

Two wave properties map straight onto what you hear:

- **Frequency → pitch.** Fast vibrations sound high; slow ones sound low. Humans hear roughly **20 Hz to 20,000 Hz**.
- **Amplitude → loudness.** Bigger pressure swings sound louder.

A dog whistle is simply a frequency above your ceiling — real sound your ears can't reach, but a dog's can.

</Step>

<Step title="The speed of sound" kind="explain">

Sound travels through air at about **343 m/s** — fast, but a *million* times slower than light. That gap is why you **see lightning before you hear thunder**.

<Callout type="tip" title="Count the gap">
Every 3 seconds between flash and rumble means the storm is roughly 1 km away — the light arrives instantly, the sound lags behind.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Sound** is a **longitudinal** pressure wave — compressions and rarefactions.
- It needs a medium, so there's **no sound in a vacuum**.
- **Frequency = pitch**, **amplitude = loudness**; we hear ~20 Hz–20 kHz.
- It travels ~**343 m/s** in air — far slower than light (thunder lags lightning).

</Step>
