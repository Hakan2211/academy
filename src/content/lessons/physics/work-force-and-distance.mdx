<Lesson>

<Step title="Work has a precise meaning" kind="explain">

In physics, **work** isn't effort or sweat — it's something you can measure exactly. You do work whenever a **force moves something through a distance**:

<Formula tex="W = F \times d" block />

Push a box 3 m with a steady 10 N and you've done 30 joules of work — handing 30 J of energy to that box. Work is simply **energy transferred by a force**.

</Step>

<Step title="Dial it in" kind="interactive">

Change the **force** and the **distance** and watch the work change. The shaded rectangle is the key idea: work is the **area** of force × distance.

<WorkLab />

Make the force bigger *or* push it further and the area — the work — grows. Both levers matter.

</Step>

<Step title="No distance, no work" kind="explain">

Here's the surprise: hold a heavy suitcase perfectly still and, in physics terms, you do **zero work** on it. Your muscles strain, but the case doesn't move, so **d = 0** and **W = 0**.

<Callout type="info" title="Effort ≠ work">
Work cares only about motion in the direction of the force. A force that pushes but moves nothing transfers no energy to the object.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Work is energy" kind="explain">

Do work on something and you change its energy. Push a cart and the work becomes **kinetic energy** — it speeds up. Lift a crate and the work becomes **potential energy** — it's stored at height.

<Formula tex="W = \Delta E" block />

That's why work and energy share the same unit, the **joule**. Work is energy on the move.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Work = Force × Distance** (W = F·d), measured in **joules**.
- It's the **area** under a force acting over a distance — both factors count.
- **No movement → no work**, however hard you push.
- Work is **energy transferred**: it shows up as kinetic or potential energy.

<Formula tex="W = F \times d = \Delta E" block />

</Step>
