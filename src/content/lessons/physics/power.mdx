<Lesson>

<Step title="How fast you spend energy" kind="explain">

Two cranes lift the same load to the same shelf. One takes a leisurely ten seconds, the other snaps it up in two. They do **identical work** — but the fast one is far more **powerful**.

**Power** is the *rate* of doing work — how much energy you move per second:

<Formula tex="P = \dfrac{W}{t}" block />

</Step>

<Step title="Same work, different power" kind="interactive">

Both hoists raise the same load the same height, so each does the same 600 J of work. Slide the fast hoist's time up and down and watch its power respond — quicker means more watts.

<PowerRace />

The slow hoist isn't doing *less* work. It's just spreading the same energy over more time.

</Step>

<Step title="Measured in watts" kind="explain">

Power's unit is the **watt (W)** — one joule per second:

<Formula tex="1\ \text{W} = 1\ \text{J/s}" block />

A glowing LED sips a few watts; a kettle gulps about 2,000; a small car engine pumps out tens of thousands. The old unit **horsepower** is just another name for a chunk of watts (1 hp ≈ 746 W).

</Step>

<Step title="The energy bill" kind="explain">

Turn the formula around and power tells you the energy used:

<Formula tex="W = P \times t" block />

Your electricity bill is measured in **kilowatt-hours** — a 1000 W appliance running for 1 hour. It's literally power × time, which is energy. Power says how *fast*; multiply by time to get the *total*.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Power = Work ÷ Time** — how fast energy is transferred.
- Measured in **watts (W)**, where 1 W = 1 J/s.
- Same work in less time means **more power**.
- Rearranged, **energy = power × time** — the basis of your electricity bill.

<Formula tex="P = \dfrac{W}{t}" block />

</Step>
