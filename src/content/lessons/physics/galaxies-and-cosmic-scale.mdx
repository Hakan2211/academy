<Step title="Our home galaxy" kind="explain">

Every star you can see with the naked eye belongs to one enormous structure: the **Milky Way**, our galaxy. It's a spinning disk of a few hundred billion stars, about **100,000 light-years** across, with graceful spiral arms and a supermassive black hole at its centre. Our Sun is an unremarkable star roughly halfway out, in one of the spiral arms.

That faint band of light arcing across a dark night sky — the "milky way" the galaxy is named for — is what our own galaxy's disk looks like when we gaze along it from the inside.

</Step>

<Step title="A universe full of galaxies" kind="explain">

For a long time, people thought the Milky Way *was* the universe. Then in the 1920s **Edwin Hubble** showed that faint smudges in the sky were entire other galaxies, far beyond our own. The universe suddenly grew unimaginably larger.

We now know there are **hundreds of billions of galaxies**, and they come in distinct shapes:

- **Spirals** like the Milky Way — rotating disks with arms, rich in gas and new stars.
- **Ellipticals** — rounded, featureless balls of mostly old stars.
- **Irregulars** — chaotic shapes, often warped by collisions.

Galaxies are not eternal islands; they drift, collide, and merge over billions of years. The Milky Way is on a collision course with the Andromeda galaxy — though not for another four billion years.

</Step>

From a person, it's only about 27 factors of ten out to the edge of the observable universe. Notice how the "important" object keeps changing — first planets, then stars, then whole galaxies become mere dots in something larger. There is no single scale at which the universe "really" lives; structure exists at every level.

<Step title="The cosmic web" kind="explain">

Galaxies aren't scattered at random. Gravity gathers them into ever-larger groupings:

- Our Milky Way belongs to the **Local Group**, a few dozen galaxies bound together.
- The Local Group sits within a vast **supercluster** of thousands of galaxies.
- On the largest scales, galaxies trace out the **cosmic web** — immense filaments and sheets of galaxies surrounding enormous, near-empty **voids**, like a three-dimensional cosmic foam.

<Callout type="info" title="The largest structures there are">
These filaments stretch hundreds of millions of light-years and are the biggest structures in existence. Remarkably, they grew from almost nothing: tiny density ripples in the infant universe, amplified over billions of years by gravity (helped enormously by dark matter). The shape of the cosmos today is a fossil of its earliest moments — the subject of the final lessons.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- The **Milky Way** is our galaxy — a ~100,000-light-year spiral of hundreds of billions of stars; its night-sky band is our inside view of the disk.
- There are **hundreds of billions of galaxies**, in **spiral, elliptical, and irregular** forms, that drift, collide and merge.
- The **cosmic scale** spans ~27 powers of ten from a person to the observable universe.
- Gravity organises galaxies into the **Local Group**, **superclusters**, and the vast **cosmic web** of filaments and voids — grown from tiny early ripples.

</Step>
