<Lesson>

<Step title="A nucleus under tension" kind="explain">

Inside the nucleus, protons are crammed shoulder to shoulder — and like charges *repel*. So what holds them together? A separate, incredibly powerful force called the **strong nuclear force**, which glues nucleons together but only across the tiniest distances.

In a stable nucleus, the strong force wins and everything holds. But if a nucleus has too many protons, or a badly mismatched number of neutrons, that balance breaks. The nucleus is **unstable** — and sooner or later it will throw out particles or energy to reach a more stable arrangement. That spontaneous breakdown is **radioactivity**.

</Step>

<Step title="Three kinds of radiation" kind="interactive">

Unstable nuclei shed their excess in three distinct ways, discovered and named after the first three Greek letters. They differ dramatically in what they're made of — and in how far they can punch through matter.

<RadiationTypes />

- **Alpha (α)** — a chunk of two protons and two neutrons (a helium nucleus). Heavy, slow, strongly charged (+2). It's stopped by a sheet of paper or your skin.
- **Beta (β)** — a fast electron flung from the nucleus. Lighter, quicker, charge −1. It slips through paper but is stopped by a few millimetres of aluminium.
- **Gamma (γ)** — not a particle at all, but a burst of high-energy electromagnetic radiation. No charge, no mass, fantastically penetrating. Even thick lead only weakens it.

</Step>

<Step title="What each decay does to the atom" kind="explain">

Because alpha and beta decay change the count of protons, they literally turn one element into another — the alchemists' dream of **transmutation**, happening for real.

- **Alpha decay** removes 2 protons and 2 neutrons, so the element moves *down* the periodic table. Uranium, for example, edges toward lead.
- **Beta decay** is sneakier: a neutron turns into a proton (firing out the electron), so the proton count goes *up* by one — a new element, one step higher.
- **Gamma** carries away surplus *energy* only. The nucleus loses no protons or neutrons, so the element stays the same; it just settles into a calmer state.

</Step>

<Step title="Useful, and dangerous" kind="explain">

Radiation is **ionising**: it carries enough energy to knock electrons off atoms it passes through. That's exactly why it can damage living cells — and exactly why it's so useful when aimed with care.

<Callout type="warn" title="The same property, two faces">
Ionising radiation can break the molecules in living tissue, which is why exposure is dangerous. But focus it precisely and it can destroy tumours, sterilise medical equipment, trace blood flow through the body, or check a weld for hidden cracks. The danger and the usefulness are the same property pointed in different directions.
</Callout>

We detect it with instruments like the **Geiger counter**, whose familiar click sounds once for each particle that flies through. Radioactivity is also completely natural — it streams from rocks, soil, the air, and even bananas — which is why we measure *doses* rather than treating any exposure as alike.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Radioactivity** is the spontaneous breakdown of an **unstable nucleus** seeking a more stable arrangement.
- It comes in three forms: **alpha** (helium nucleus, stopped by paper), **beta** (electron, stopped by aluminium), and **gamma** (energy, needs lead).
- **Alpha and beta decay change the element** (transmutation); **gamma** just sheds energy.
- Radiation is **ionising** — both hazardous to cells and invaluable in medicine, industry, and research.

</Step>
