<Lesson>

<Step title="How fast does a wave move?" kind="explain">

In one **period**, a wave advances by exactly one **wavelength**. Speed is distance over time, so:

<Formula tex="v = \dfrac{\lambda}{T} = f \lambda" block />

That tidy little equation — **v = f λ** — ties together everything from the last few lessons. Frequency and wavelength always trade off to give the wave's speed.

</Step>

<Step title="See the trade-off" kind="interactive">

Watch the speed readout as you drag the sliders. Raise the **frequency** *or* stretch the **wavelength** and the wave moves faster; the product f × λ is the speed.

<WaveLab />

Try to find two different slider settings that give the *same* speed — a fast, short wave can match a slow, long one.

</Step>

<Step title="The medium sets the speed" kind="explain">

Here's the twist: for a given material, the wave speed is essentially **fixed by the medium**, not by the source.

- **Sound** in air: about **343 m/s**.
- **Sound** in water: ~1,500 m/s; in steel, ~5,000 m/s.
- **Light** in a vacuum: **300,000,000 m/s** — the cosmic speed limit.

Shout louder or softer and your voice still travels at 343 m/s. The medium calls the shots.

</Step>

<Step title="So what changes when v is fixed?" kind="explain">

If the speed is locked by the medium and you change the frequency, then **wavelength must adjust** to keep v = f λ true:

<Formula tex="\lambda = \dfrac{v}{f}" block />

A high-pitched (high-f) sound has a short wavelength; a deep bass note has a long one — both travelling at the same 343 m/s. This is also why a wave's wavelength shrinks when it enters a slower medium (coming up in refraction).

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- A wave moves one wavelength per period, so **v = f λ**.
- For a given medium the **speed is roughly constant**, set by the material.
- With v fixed, raising **frequency shortens the wavelength** (and vice versa).

<Formula tex="v = f \lambda" block />

</Step>
