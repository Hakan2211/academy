<Step title="Following the theory to its extremes" kind="explain">

General relativity began as a way to understand falling apples and orbiting planets. But a theory is most revealing when you push it to its limits — and Einstein's equations, followed without flinching, predict some of the most extraordinary objects and events in the universe.

What happens if you keep concentrating mass into a smaller and smaller space, curving spacetime ever more steeply? What does the theory say about time near such a place, about ripples in spacetime itself, and about the universe as a whole? Einstein himself doubted some of the answers. The universe has since confirmed nearly all of them.

</Step>

<Step title="When the well has no bottom" kind="interactive">

Take the curved-spacetime grid from last lesson and keep piling on mass. The well deepens, the curvature steepens — and at a critical point something irreversible happens.

<SpacetimeWell />

Crank the mass to its maximum. The well becomes effectively bottomless: a **black hole**. Around it lies the **event horizon** (the gold ring) — a boundary where the curvature is so extreme that the escape velocity exceeds the speed of light. Since nothing outruns light, **nothing that crosses the horizon can ever come back** — not matter, not light, not information. At the very centre, our equations predict a **singularity**, a point of infinite density where general relativity itself breaks down.

</Step>

<Step title="Time at the edge" kind="interactive">

Black holes warp not just space but time, and dramatically. Recall from the equivalence principle that the deeper you sit in a gravity well, the slower your clock runs. Near a black hole's horizon, that **gravitational time dilation** becomes overwhelming.

<GravityTime />

To a distant observer watching someone fall toward the horizon, the falling clock appears to run slower and slower, the image freezing and reddening at the brink — they seem to take *forever* to cross. To the person falling, their own clock ticks normally and they cross the horizon in moments. Both descriptions are true; they're just told from different places in the warped spacetime. (This is the real physics behind the planet-side time loss in the film *Interstellar*.)

</Step>

<Step title="And it runs your phone" kind="explain">

Gravitational time dilation isn't reserved for black holes — it's measurable on Earth, and you rely on a correction for it every day.

<Callout type="info" title="Why GPS needs Einstein">
GPS satellites orbit high above Earth, where gravity is weaker, so their clocks tick **faster** than clocks on the ground — by about 38 microseconds per day once both special- and general-relativistic effects are combined. That sounds trivial, but light travels 11 km in 38 microseconds. Without continuously correcting for relativity, GPS positions would drift by **kilometres within a day**. Every satnav is a working relativity experiment.
</Callout>

The effect was also measured directly in the lab in the **Pound–Rebka experiment** (1959), which detected light shifting in frequency as it climbed just 22 metres up a tower — exactly as general relativity predicts.

</Step>

<Step title="Ripples in spacetime" kind="explain">

If mass curves spacetime, then *violently moving* mass should send **ripples** through it — waves of curvature spreading outward at the speed of light. Einstein predicted these **gravitational waves** in 1916, then doubted they'd ever be detectable. They're fantastically faint: a passing wave stretches and squeezes space by less than the width of a proton.

<Callout type="info" title="LIGO hears the universe">
In 2015 — a century after the prediction — the **LIGO** detectors caught a gravitational wave from two black holes, each tens of times the Sun's mass, spiralling together and merging 1.3 billion light-years away. The collision converted about three Suns' worth of mass into pure spacetime ripples (E = mc² again). It opened an entirely new way to observe the cosmos: not with light, but by **listening to spacetime itself**.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="The universe is expanding spacetime" kind="explain">

General relativity doesn't just describe stars and black holes — it describes **the entire universe** at once. When Einstein's equations are applied to all of space, they refuse to sit still: the universe must be either expanding or contracting.

In 1929 **Edwin Hubble** found that distant galaxies are rushing away from us, and the farther they are, the faster they recede. The universe is **expanding** — but not into anything. It's **space itself stretching**, carrying the galaxies apart like dots on an inflating balloon. Run that expansion backward and everything converges to a hot, dense beginning roughly 13.8 billion years ago: the **Big Bang**. Relativity gave us, for the first time, a scientific theory of the cosmos as a whole.

</Step>

<Step title="The unfinished business" kind="explain">

For all its successes, relativity has led us straight to the edge of what we understand — and the open questions are enormous.

- **Dark matter**: galaxies rotate as though far more mass is present than we can see. Gravitational lensing confirms the extra mass is there — but we don't know what it is.
- **Dark energy**: the cosmic expansion is *accelerating*, driven by an unknown energy that fills empty space and makes up most of the universe.
- **Quantum gravity**: at a black hole's singularity, and at the Big Bang itself, general relativity collides with the **quantum mechanics** you met in the atoms unit — and the two theories are incompatible. Reconciling them is the greatest unsolved problem in physics.

The smooth, curved spacetime of relativity and the probabilistic, granular world of the quantum have never been made to agree. Somewhere beyond both lies a deeper theory we haven't found.

</Step>

<Step title="Tested to the hilt" kind="explain">

It would be easy to mistake all this strangeness for speculation. It isn't. General relativity is one of the most rigorously tested theories ever devised, and it has passed **every** experimental check:

- **Mercury's orbit** — explained exactly.
- **Starlight bending** at the 1919 eclipse — confirmed.
- **GPS** time corrections — relied on every second.
- **Gravitational waves** — detected by LIGO, repeatedly, since 2015.
- The **first image of a black hole** — released by the Event Horizon Telescope in 2019, showing the glowing ring of matter around the dark shadow exactly as predicted.

A theory built from a thought about a falling man in an elevator now guides spacecraft, weighs invisible matter, and lets us photograph the edge of a black hole. That is the reach of relativity.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- Pushed to extremes, general relativity predicts **black holes**: an **event horizon** from which not even light escapes, hiding a **singularity** where the theory breaks down.
- **Gravitational time dilation** is real — extreme near black holes, and large enough on Earth that **GPS** must correct for it.
- Violent masses radiate **gravitational waves**, detected by **LIGO** in 2015 — a new way to observe the universe.
- Applied to everything, relativity predicts an **expanding universe** (the **Big Bang**), and leads to the open frontiers of **dark matter, dark energy, and quantum gravity** — where relativity meets the quantum world.
- It has passed **every test**, from Mercury's orbit to the first **image of a black hole** (2019).
