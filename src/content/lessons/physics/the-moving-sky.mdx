<Lesson>

<Step title="The oldest science" kind="explain">

Long before telescopes, people watched the sky with care — and noticed it *moves*. The Sun rises and sets. The stars wheel overhead through the night. The Sun's noon height drifts across the year, and the patterns of stars change with the seasons. Whole calendars, religions, and farming cycles were built on these rhythms.

For most of history it looked as though everything circled a stationary Earth. The truth is the reverse: almost all of that motion is **us** — the Earth spinning and orbiting. Untangling which motions are real and which are apparent is where astronomy begins.

</Step>

<Step title="A day and a year" kind="explain">

Two motions of the Earth explain most of what you see:

- **Rotation** — the Earth spins on its axis once every 24 hours. That's why the Sun appears to cross the sky and the stars wheel overhead: *we* are turning. This gives us **day and night**.
- **Revolution** — the Earth orbits the Sun once every 365¼ days. Over the year this changes which direction the night side faces, so different **constellations** are visible in different seasons. This gives us the **year**.

The stars aren't really moving across the sky each night, and they aren't really different in winter and summer. The Earth's spin and orbit just keep changing where we're pointed.

</Step>

<Step title="Why we have seasons" kind="interactive">

Here's a question almost everyone gets wrong: are summers hot because the Earth is *closer* to the Sun? No. The Earth's distance barely changes — and when the Northern Hemisphere has summer, the Southern has winter, which distance could never explain.

The real cause is the Earth's **23.5° axial tilt**.

<Seasons />

The axis stays pointed the same way in space all year. When your hemisphere leans **toward** the Sun, sunlight strikes more directly and the days are longer — summer. Six months later, on the far side of the orbit, that same hemisphere leans **away** — winter. Tilt, not distance, makes the seasons.

</Step>

<Step title="Finding your way by the sky" kind="explain">

Because the Earth spins on a fixed axis, one point in the sky barely moves: the spot the axis points at. In the north that's marked almost exactly by **Polaris**, the North Star. Everything else appears to circle around it through the night.

<Callout type="info" title="A clock and a compass overhead">
For thousands of years the sky was humanity's clock, calendar, and GPS. Sailors found their latitude from the height of Polaris; farmers timed planting by which constellations rose at dawn; the regular turning of the heavens defined the hour and the season. The "fixed" stars made a dependable backdrop precisely because their motion is just the Earth's spin reflected back at us.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- The sky's daily and yearly motion is mostly the **Earth's own movement**, not the heavens turning.
- **Rotation** (24 h) gives day and night; **revolution** (1 year) changes which constellations we see.
- **Seasons** come from the **23.5° axial tilt** — hemispheres leaning toward or away from the Sun — not from distance.
- The spin axis points near **Polaris**, which is why the sky long served as clock, calendar, and compass.

</Step>
