<Lesson>

<Step title="Numbers need units" kind="explain">

"The bridge is 200 long." Two hundred *what* — metres, feet, paces? A number in physics is meaningless on its own. It needs a **unit**: an agreed-upon amount to count against.

Every physical measurement is really a comparison. Saying a table is 1.5 metres long means "1.5 times the standard length we all call a metre." Pick the standard, and measuring becomes counting how many of it fit.

</Step>

<Step title="The seven yardsticks" kind="interactive">

Physics measures a dizzying variety of things — yet almost everything is built from just **seven base quantities**, each with one agreed unit. Together they form the **SI system** (from the French *Système International*), the language science speaks worldwide.

<UnitExplorer />

Length, mass, time, electric current, temperature, amount of substance, and luminous intensity. Master these seven and you can express nearly any measurement in physics.

</Step>

<Step title="Building everything else" kind="explain">

Most quantities you'll meet aren't on that list — they're **derived** by combining the base units.

- **Speed** is length per time: metres per second, written <Formula tex="\text{m/s}" />.
- **Area** is length × length: square metres, <Formula tex="\text{m}^2" />.
- **Force** is mass × acceleration, which works out to <Formula tex="\text{kg·m/s}^2" /> — a combination so common we give it its own name, the **newton (N)**.

So the seven base units are like an alphabet, and derived units are the words you spell with them.

</Step>

<Step title="Why a metre is a metre" kind="explain">

A standard is only useful if everyone agrees on it *exactly* and forever. For a long time the kilogram was a literal lump of platinum in a vault near Paris — but a lump can be scratched, can gather dust, can drift.

So modern SI units are pinned to **unchanging constants of nature** instead. The metre is defined by the speed of light; the second by the vibrations of a caesium atom; the kilogram by a fixed value of the Planck constant. A lab on Mars could rebuild every unit from scratch and get exactly the same metre.

<Callout type="info" title="A standard you can't lose">
In 2019 the last physical artifact — that Paris kilogram — was retired. Now nature itself holds the standards. No object, anywhere, can be damaged or stolen to corrupt them.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Choosing the right unit" kind="explain">

The SI base units aren't always *convenient*. You wouldn't measure the distance to the Sun in metres, or an atom's width in metres, without drowning in zeros.

That's why physics leans on **prefixes** (kilo-, milli-, nano-…) and **scientific notation** to keep numbers manageable — the subjects of the next two lessons. The unit stays the same; only the shorthand changes.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- A measurement is a number **and** a unit — neither means much without the other.
- The **SI system** rests on **seven base units**: metre, kilogram, second, ampere, kelvin, mole, candela.
- Every other unit is **derived** by combining these (speed = m/s, force = kg·m/s² = the newton).
- Modern units are defined by **constants of nature**, so they never drift and can be rebuilt anywhere.

</Step>
