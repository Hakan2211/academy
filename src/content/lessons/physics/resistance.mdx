<Lesson>

<Step title="Opposing the flow" kind="explain">

Voltage pushes charge through a circuit — but the circuit pushes back. Every material puts up some opposition to current, and we call it **resistance**.

Where does it come from? As electrons drift through a wire, they keep bumping into the vibrating atoms of the material. Each collision slows them and transfers a little energy, which shows up as **heat**. Resistance is essentially this microscopic friction. It's measured in **ohms (Ω)**.

</Step>

<Step title="Choke the current" kind="interactive">

Hold the voltage steady and increase the resistance instead. Watch the current drop and the bulb fade.

<CircuitLab />

**More resistance means less current** for the same push. It's the opposite lever to voltage: voltage drives the flow, resistance restrains it. Together they decide exactly how much current you get.

</Step>

<Step title="What sets a material's resistance" kind="explain">

Four things determine how much a wire resists:

- **Material** — copper and silver conduct beautifully (low resistance); nichrome and carbon resist far more.
- **Length** — a longer wire means more collisions, so more resistance.
- **Thickness** — a thicker wire gives electrons more room, so *less* resistance.
- **Temperature** — for most metals, hotter atoms vibrate more and get in the way more, raising resistance.

Think of water again: a long, narrow pipe resists flow far more than a short, wide one.

</Step>

<Step title="Resistance as a tool" kind="explain">

Resistance isn't just a nuisance — it's deliberately useful, precisely *because* it turns electrical energy into heat and light.

<Callout type="info" title="Resistance put to work">
- A **kettle** or **heater** uses a high-resistance coil to get hot on purpose.
- An old-style **light bulb** runs current through a thin filament until it glows white-hot.
- A **thermistor** changes resistance with temperature, making a simple electronic thermometer.
</Callout>

So engineers add resistance where they want heat or light, and minimise it (thick copper cables) where they want to deliver energy efficiently without wasting it as warmth.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Resistance** is opposition to current, caused by electrons colliding with atoms and losing energy as **heat**; measured in **ohms (Ω)**.
- For a fixed voltage, **more resistance → less current**.
- It depends on **material, length, thickness, and temperature**.
- Resistance is used on purpose in **heaters, filaments, and thermistors**, and minimised in power cables.

</Step>
