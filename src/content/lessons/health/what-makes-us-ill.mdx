<Step title="Two big families of disease" kind="explain">

Walk into any hospital and you will find two very different kinds of patients. Some are fighting off something that invaded them — a virus, a bacterium, a parasite. Others are managing a condition that built up quietly over years: a blocked artery, rising blood sugar, a tumour. Medicine calls these the two great families of disease.

**Infectious (communicable) diseases** are caused by pathogens — living agents that can spread from person to person, or from animal to person, or from the environment into the body:

- **Bacteria** (tuberculosis, strep throat, cholera)
- **Viruses** (influenza, COVID-19, HIV, measles)
- **Fungi** (athlete's foot, some lung infections)
- **Parasites** (malaria, intestinal worms, toxoplasmosis)

**Non-communicable diseases (NCDs)** cannot be "caught." They develop over time from a mix of lifestyle, environment, genes, and ageing. The four largest killers in this family are:

- Cardiovascular disease (heart attack, stroke)
- Type 2 diabetes
- Cancer
- Chronic respiratory disease (e.g. COPD)

</Step>

<Step title="Other causes of illness" kind="explain">

Infectious and non-communicable disease account for most of the global burden, but two other categories round out the picture.

**Deficiency diseases** arise from a shortage of an essential nutrient. Scurvy (vitamin C deficit), rickets (vitamin D), iron-deficiency anaemia — these were catastrophic in history and still matter in low-income settings. The body simply cannot work without its raw materials.

**Genetic conditions** are present from birth because of mutations inherited from parents or that arise during development. Some are serious single-gene disorders (cystic fibrosis, sickle-cell disease); others are polygenic tendencies that raise the risk of conditions like breast cancer or type 2 diabetes. Genes load the gun — environment and lifestyle often decide whether it fires.

A useful way to think about it: infectious diseases are driven by **what gets into the body**, NCDs by **what happens inside it over time**, deficiency diseases by **what is missing**, and genetic conditions by the **blueprint the body was given at the start**.

</Step>

<Step title="The body's first response" kind="interactive">

When a pathogen does get in, the immune system mobilises. Antibodies mark invaders, and white blood cells (phagocytes) hunt them down.

<ImmuneDefenders />

This is why infectious disease often resolves on its own — a healthy immune system has powerful weapons. NCDs, by contrast, usually involve damage that accumulates over years and does not trigger the same rapid response.

</Step>

<Step title="Why it matters which family" kind="explain">

The category of a disease determines how we respond to it.

For **infectious diseases**: the priority is breaking transmission — vaccines, hygiene, clean water, isolation. Speed matters. One infectious person can seed dozens of cases in days.

For **NCDs**: the priority is reducing the modifiable risk factors that build up over years — smoking, poor diet, physical inactivity, high blood pressure, excess alcohol. Prevention is long-term and cumulative.

Conflating the two leads to policy mistakes. Treating type 2 diabetes as if it were a pathogen (just find a "cure") misses the long chain of lifestyle and environmental factors behind it. Treating an infectious outbreak as if it were a personal lifestyle failure ignores how disease spreads through networks.

<Callout type="info">
Globally, NCDs now cause **~74% of all deaths** per year (WHO, 2023), overtaking infectious disease as the leading cause of mortality — yet many of those deaths are substantially preventable.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Infectious (communicable) diseases** are caused by pathogens — bacteria, viruses, fungi, or parasites — and can spread between people or from the environment.
- **Non-communicable diseases (NCDs)** cannot be caught; they develop over time from lifestyle, environment, genes, and ageing. Heart disease, type 2 diabetes, cancer, and COPD are the biggest four.
- **Deficiency diseases** arise from nutrient shortages; **genetic conditions** arise from inherited or developmental mutations.
- The category shapes the response: break transmission for infectious disease; reduce modifiable risk factors for NCDs.
- NCDs now cause ~74% of global deaths — mostly preventable.

</Step>
