<Step title="A note before we begin" kind="explain">

<Callout type="info">
Disordered eating — including restrictive eating, binge eating, and conditions like anorexia, bulimia, and orthorexia — is more common than most people realise. These are serious, treatable conditions, not personal failings. If you recognise patterns of distress, guilt, or preoccupation around food in yourself or someone you know, reaching out to a GP, dietitian, or a service like the Butterfly Foundation (1800 33 4673 in Australia) or NEDA (1-800-931-2237 in the US) is a genuine act of self-care.

This lesson is about building a healthier everyday relationship with food — not a prescription for perfection.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="The perfectionism trap" kind="explain">

One of the most counterproductive patterns in nutrition is **perfectionist eating** — the belief that you must eat "perfectly" at every meal, that any deviation is a failure, and that failure justifies abandoning all effort ("I already had a biscuit, so the day is ruined — I may as well eat everything").

This is sometimes called the "what-the-hell effect" in behaviour science, and it drives a great deal of binge-restrict cycling. The target is not perfection; it is a healthy **average** across weeks and months.

A more useful frame is **flexible restraint**: general awareness of food quality and quantity, combined with the understanding that individual meals — including indulgent ones — do not derail a good overall pattern. Research consistently shows that people with flexible attitudes to eating have better diet quality and healthier body weights than those with rigid, all-or-nothing rules.

The skill is recovering quickly when you eat something outside your usual pattern, not preventing it from ever happening. Resilience, not rigidity.

</Step>

<Step title="No 'good' or 'bad' foods" kind="explain">

Labelling foods as morally "good" or "bad" is one of the most pervasive and least helpful concepts in popular nutrition. It borrows the language of ethics to describe a purely practical matter of quantities and patterns.

The reality:
- **No food is toxic in normal amounts.** The dose makes the poison — as Paracelsus observed in the 16th century, and as toxicology confirms every day.
- **No food is obligatory.** If you don't eat broccoli, you can get the same nutrients from other vegetables. Nutritional adequacy is achievable in many ways.
- **Labelling foods as "bad" increases their appeal.** Restraint theory research shows that designating a food as forbidden reliably increases craving for it.

A more accurate language: **everyday foods** (nutrient-dense, filling, minimally processed — eat these most of the time) vs. **sometimes foods** (energy-dense, less nutrient-rich — enjoy in smaller amounts without guilt). This framing is frequency and quantity, not morality.

<Callout type="tip">
The "80/20 principle" applied to eating: if around 80% of your eating over a week is whole, varied, and reasonably balanced, the other 20% — birthday cake, chips with friends, a glass of wine — has minimal impact on long-term health outcomes. This is not a licence to ignore 20%; it is a permission structure to stop obsessing over it.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Food as culture, connection, and joy" kind="explain">

Every human culture that has ever existed has organised significant social life around food. Meals mark transitions — celebrations, funerals, courtship, hospitality, religious observance. Food is memory: specific dishes are often the most vivid triggers of childhood and family identity.

Reducing eating purely to nutrient delivery ignores this reality. A diet that achieves perfect macronutrient ratios but destroys your enjoyment of meals, isolates you from shared eating, or fills every meal with anxiety is not healthy in any meaningful sense of the word.

**Enjoyment of food is a legitimate health goal**, not a concession to weakness. Sustainable eating patterns are pleasant ones. The Mediterranean diet — consistently among the best-evidenced dietary patterns — is built around shared meals, varied whole foods, olive oil, and the enjoyment of food as a social act.

A healthy relationship with food includes:
- Eating food you genuinely enjoy most of the time.
- Participating fully in social and cultural food occasions without guilt.
- Thinking about food in proportion — a pleasant, functional part of life, not a source of anxiety or constant preoccupation.

</Step>

<Step title="Intuitive eating" kind="explain">

**Intuitive eating** is a framework developed by dietitians Evelyn Tribole and Elyse Resch that integrates the physiological and psychological sides of eating. Its ten principles include:

- Reject the diet mentality — give up on external, arbitrary eating rules.
- Honour your hunger — recognise physical hunger signals and respond to them.
- Make peace with food — remove the forbidden/allowed framework entirely.
- Respect your fullness — learn to stop at satisfaction.
- Discover the satisfaction factor — find genuine pleasure in eating.
- Cope with emotions without using food as the primary tool.
- Respect your body — regardless of shape or size.
- Move and feel the difference — exercise for how it feels, not purely for calorie burn.
- Honour your health — make food choices that feel good *and* are nourishing.

Research on intuitive eating shows associations with improved psychological wellbeing, reduced disordered eating patterns, better diet quality, and greater life satisfaction — even in the absence of dramatic weight change. It is not about ignoring nutrition; it is about integrating nutritional knowledge with body awareness rather than replacing one with the other.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Flexible restraint** — not perfectionism — produces better long-term eating outcomes; the skill is recovering quickly, not preventing every slip.
- Drop the **"good/bad food" moral language**: use "everyday foods" vs. "sometimes foods" (frequency and quantity, not ethics).
- The **80/20 principle**: a broadly healthy pattern most of the time means occasional indulgences have minimal long-term impact.
- **Food is culture, connection, and joy** — a diet that works nutritionally but destroys your enjoyment is not truly healthy.
- **Intuitive eating** integrates physical cues (hunger/fullness) with enjoyment and nutritional awareness — research links it to better wellbeing and reduced disordered eating.
- Disordered eating is common and treatable — support is available and seeking it is a strength.

</Step>
