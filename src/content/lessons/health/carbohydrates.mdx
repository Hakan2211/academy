<Step title="The body's preferred fuel" kind="explain">

Carbohydrates are the body's **primary energy source**. The brain alone burns roughly 120 g of glucose per day — almost exclusively carbs. When you eat carbohydrates, digestion breaks them down into **glucose**, which enters the bloodstream and powers cells directly, or is stored as **glycogen** in the liver and muscles for later use.

Every carbohydrate is built from sugar units (monosaccharides). The difference between a teaspoon of table sugar and a bowl of oatmeal is simply how many of those units are chained together and how tightly.

- **Simple carbohydrates** (monosaccharides and disaccharides) — glucose, fructose, sucrose, lactose. Short chains; digested quickly.
- **Complex carbohydrates** (polysaccharides) — starch and glycogen. Long chains; digested more slowly.
- **Dietary fibre** — also a polysaccharide, but with bonds humans can't break. Passes largely intact to the colon.

</Step>

<Step title="Fibre: the carb that doesn't fuel you" kind="explain">

Fibre is technically a carbohydrate, but since we can't digest it, it contributes almost no direct calories. Yet it's one of the most important dietary components:

- **Slows digestion** — traps glucose in a viscous gel (soluble fibre), blunting blood sugar spikes.
- **Feeds gut bacteria** — fermented by microbes in the colon, producing beneficial short-chain fatty acids.
- **Adds bulk** — keeps stool moving through the bowel (insoluble fibre), preventing constipation.
- **Promotes satiety** — high-fibre meals are more filling and help prevent overeating.

Most people in high-income countries eat around 15 g of fibre per day. Health guidelines suggest **25–35 g**. Good sources: vegetables, legumes, wholegrains, fruit with skin, nuts and seeds.

<Callout type="tip">
Switching from white rice to brown rice, or white bread to wholegrain bread, adds fibre and micronutrients with very little other change. Small swaps, genuine gains.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Blood sugar: the glycaemic idea" kind="interactive">

Different carbs cause very different blood-glucose responses. Toggle between a sugary drink/sweets and a whole-grain meal to see why the *type* of carb matters, not just the total.

<BloodSugarCurve />

The sharp spike from simple carbs triggers a large insulin release. When insulin over-corrects, blood sugar drops below baseline — the "crash" that leaves you tired and reaching for another snack.

</Step>

<Step title="Blood sugar regulation — a brief look" kind="explain">

The body works hard to keep blood glucose in a narrow range (roughly 70–100 mg/dL fasting). Two pancreatic hormones balance each other:

- **Insulin** (released when glucose rises) — tells cells to absorb glucose; tells the liver to store it as glycogen.
- **Glucagon** (released when glucose falls) — tells the liver to break down glycogen and release glucose into the blood.

This is a textbook negative-feedback loop. When carb quality is poor — lots of simple sugars, little fibre — the swings are larger and the system has to work harder. Over many years, chronic high-glucose exposure can impair insulin sensitivity, a key feature of type 2 diabetes.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- Carbohydrates are the **body's primary fuel**; glucose powers the brain and muscles.
- **Simple carbs** digest quickly → rapid glucose spike; **complex carbs** digest more slowly → gentler rise.
- **Dietary fibre** slows digestion, feeds gut bacteria, promotes fullness, and improves blood sugar control.
- Insulin and glucagon maintain blood glucose in a narrow range via negative feedback.
- Prefer wholegrains, vegetables, and legumes over refined and sugary foods for steadier energy.

</Step>
