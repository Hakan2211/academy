<Lesson>

<Step title="Why are some people healthier than others?" kind="explain">

It's a fair question with a surprising answer. We tend to credit (or blame) two things: good genes and good doctors. Both matter — but neither is the biggest factor.

Public-health researchers group the forces that shape our health into a handful of **determinants of health**:

- **Daily behaviour** — what you eat, how you move and sleep, smoking, alcohol.
- **Social & economic factors** — income, education, work, community, relationships.
- **Physical environment** — air, water, housing, safety, access to green space.
- **Medical care** — access to and quality of healthcare.
- **Genes & biology** — the body you inherited.

When you measure how much each contributes to health *outcomes*, the ranking shocks most people.

</Step>

<Step title="The healthcare paradox" kind="explain">

Here's the counter-intuitive part: **medical care explains only a small slice** of how healthy a population is — often estimated at only around 10–20%.

Far more comes from **behaviour** and **social and economic conditions** — the stuff happening outside the clinic, in ordinary life. A doctor can patch you up after a heart attack, but whether you have one in the first place is shaped much more by decades of food, movement, stress, smoking, and circumstance.

This isn't an argument against medicine — good care saves lives every day. It's a recalibration of where health actually *comes from*. If you want to move the needle, the clinic is not the main lever.

</Step>

<Step title="Fixed odds, not fixed fate" kind="explain">

What about genes? They're real and they matter — some people are dealt a harder hand for heart disease, certain cancers, or addiction. But genes usually set the **odds**, not the outcome. For most common conditions, behaviour and environment decide whether those odds ever play out.

And "changeable" is a spectrum. Your own habits are the most directly in your control. Social and economic conditions are changeable too — but often by *society* (policy, fair wages, safe neighbourhoods) more than by one person alone. That's worth naming: health isn't purely a matter of willpower, and judging people for being unhealthy ignores the hand they were dealt and the conditions they live in.

The empowering takeaway stands: a lot is in reach — for individuals *and* for the societies they live in.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- Health is shaped by **determinants**: behaviour, social/economic factors, environment, medical care, and genes.
- **Medical care explains only ~10–20%** of health outcomes — most health comes from outside the clinic.
- **Daily behaviour** is the single biggest changeable slice you personally control.
- **Genes set odds, not fate** for most common conditions.
- Health isn't pure willpower — **social conditions** matter too, and deserve change at the society level.

</Step>
