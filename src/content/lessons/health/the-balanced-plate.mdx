<Step title="From nutrients to foods" kind="explain">

The last three lessons zoomed in to the molecular level — macronutrients, vitamins, minerals, water. That knowledge matters, but most people do not eat nutrients: they eat **foods**. The challenge of healthy eating is translating nutrient science into everyday meal choices without a chemistry degree.

The "balanced plate" model is that translation. Rather than counting milligrams of vitamin C, you think in food groups and proportions. Get those proportions roughly right, choose whole foods within each group, and the micronutrients largely take care of themselves.

The model used by most national health authorities today is the **healthy eating plate** concept:

- **½ plate: vegetables and fruit** — the foundation, not the side dish.
- **¼ plate: whole grains** — the energy base (brown rice, wholemeal bread, oats, wholegrain pasta).
- **¼ plate: quality protein** — meat, fish, eggs, legumes, tofu.
- **Dairy/alternatives on the side** — milk, yoghurt, or fortified plant equivalents for calcium and B12.
- **Water as the default drink** — not juice, not soft drinks, not sports drinks for everyday life.

Note what is *not* given a plate segment: refined grains, processed foods, sweets, and ultra-processed snacks. They can exist in a healthy diet occasionally but do not anchor it.

</Step>

<Step title="Why vegetables and fruit take half the plate" kind="explain">

The case for making vegetables and fruit the largest portion is unusually robust in nutrition science (a field full of contested claims):

- They provide **fibre** — the indigestible carbohydrate that feeds the gut microbiome, slows digestion, and dramatically lowers risk of bowel cancer and cardiovascular disease.
- They deliver the widest variety of **micronutrients** — vitamins, minerals, and phytonutrients — per calorie of any food group.
- They have low energy density — you can eat a large volume without excess calories, which supports healthy weight management.
- Large population studies consistently link high vegetable and fruit intake to lower risk of heart disease, stroke, type 2 diabetes, and several cancers.

The key is **variety** — a plate of only potatoes every day does not give you what a mixture of colours does. (We cover the "eat the rainbow" idea in the next lesson.)

<Callout type="info">
Potatoes are starchy vegetables — nutritionally they sit closer to the "whole grains" quarter than the vegetable half. Fill the vegetable half with non-starchy choices: leafy greens, broccoli, capsicum, carrots, tomatoes, cucumber, legumes.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Portion intuition — no scales needed" kind="explain">

Calorie counting and weighing portions is accurate but tedious. A set of hand-based rules is practical:

- **Fist** ≈ one serving of vegetables or fruit — aim for 5–9 fists a day.
- **Cupped hand** ≈ one serving of grains or starchy vegetables.
- **Palm** ≈ one serving of protein (meat, fish, tofu).
- **Thumb** ≈ one serving of fat or cheese.

Portion intuition also means reading context. Restaurant meals are routinely 2–3× the portions needed for one meal. Snack bags often contain 2 "servings." The plate model re-orients the default: start with the vegetables, fill the grains, add the protein — not the reverse.

**Variety over the day matters as much as any single meal.** A lunch heavy in grains is fine if dinner is vegetable-rich. The plate model is a useful average, not a rigid rule for every plate.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- The **balanced plate** translates nutrient science into simple food-group proportions.
- **½ plate: vegetables and fruit** — the most evidence-backed healthy eating behaviour.
- **¼ plate: whole grains** — for sustained energy and fibre.
- **¼ plate: protein** — meat, fish, eggs, legumes, tofu.
- **Dairy/alternatives on the side** for calcium; **water as the default drink**.
- Portion intuition (fist, palm, cupped hand) makes healthy eating practical without counting.
- **Variety across the day** matters — the model is an average target, not a per-meal rule.

</Step>
