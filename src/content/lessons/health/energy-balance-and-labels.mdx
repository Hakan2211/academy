<Step title="Energy balance is cumulative, not daily" kind="explain">

Body weight change is not a daily event — it's the **accumulated result** of energy balance over time. The body doesn't "reset" at midnight. Every day of surplus or deficit adds to (or subtracts from) a running total.

The key number: roughly **7,700 kcal of surplus ≈ 1 kg of body-fat gained** (and vice versa for deficit). This is because:
- 1 kg of human adipose tissue stores about 7,000–8,000 kcal of chemical energy.
- The "7,700" figure accounts for the mix of fat plus small amounts of protein and water in real tissue.

What this means in practice:
- A daily surplus of **100 kcal** (roughly half a banana) → ~500 kcal per week → ~1 kg gained over 15 weeks, without you noticing.
- A daily deficit of **500 kcal** → ~3,500 kcal per week → ~0.5 kg lost per week (a common clinical target).
- A single "bad day" of +1,500 kcal is not 0.2 kg of fat — it takes 7,700 kcal to accumulate that. One day barely registers.

<Callout type="info">
The 7,700 kcal/kg figure is a useful approximation. In reality, as body composition changes, so does metabolic rate — the body adapts, which is why weight loss often slows after the first few weeks.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Macros and their calorie yields — a quick recap" kind="explain">

Before reading labels, it helps to have the calorie-per-gram values memorised:

- **Carbohydrate: 4 kcal/g** — sugars and starches.
- **Protein: 4 kcal/g** — amino acids.
- **Fat: 9 kcal/g** — the densest macro.
- **Alcohol: 7 kcal/g** — not a nutrient, but calorie-bearing; often invisible on food labels.

So a food with 10 g fat, 20 g carbs, and 5 g protein contains:
<Formula tex="(10 \times 9) + (20 \times 4) + (5 \times 4) = 90 + 80 + 20 = 190 \text{ kcal}" block />

You can usually verify a label's calorie count this way — small discrepancies are normal because fibre (a carb) yields less than 4 kcal/g in practice.

</Step>

<Step title="The serving size trap" kind="explain">

The most important line on a nutrition label is not the calorie number — it's the **serving size**. Everything else on the label is per serving, not per package.

Common traps:
- A small bag of chips states 150 kcal per serving, but **the bag contains 4 servings**. Eating the bag = 600 kcal.
- A bottle of juice may list "1 serving = 250 mL," but the bottle holds 500 mL. Read twice.
- A granola bar that looks like one serving can be listed as **2 servings**.

The fix is simple: find "servings per container" at the top of the label, decide how many you're actually eating, and multiply.

**Added sugar** is a separate line below "Total Sugars." It distinguishes sugars added during processing (problematic in excess) from naturally occurring sugars in fruit or dairy (less of a concern). Guidelines suggest limiting added sugar to under 10% of daily calories — about **50 g** on a 2,000 kcal diet.

**%DV (Percent Daily Value)** is based on a 2,000 kcal reference diet. It's a rough guide:
- ≤ 5% DV = low in that nutrient.
- ≥ 20% DV = high in that nutrient.

Use it to flag nutrients you want to limit (sodium, saturated fat, added sugar) and nutrients you want to get enough of (fibre, vitamins, minerals).

</Step>

<Step title="Putting it together: what a good daily pattern looks like" kind="explain">

No single food is "healthy" or "unhealthy" in isolation — dietary patterns over time are what matter. A few evidence-based principles that hold across many dietary traditions:

- **Eat mostly whole, minimally processed foods** — they tend to be higher in fibre, micronutrients, and protein relative to their calorie content.
- **Don't fear any one macro** — the healthiest diets in the world vary enormously in their fat and carb ratios. Ultra-processed foods are the consistent concern.
- **Portion awareness without obsession** — you don't need to count every calorie, but knowing rough densities (fat is dense; vegetables are not) helps.
- **Protein at each meal** helps with satiety and muscle maintenance.
- **Fibre is almost universally under-consumed** — adding vegetables, legumes, and wholegrains addresses this.
- **Liquids can be invisible calories** — juice, smoothies, alcohol, and flavoured drinks add up quickly without triggering satiety.

Balance is cumulative, just like energy balance itself — a week of bad eating doesn't permanently derail health, and one salad doesn't guarantee it. The pattern over months and years is what leaves a mark.

</Step>
