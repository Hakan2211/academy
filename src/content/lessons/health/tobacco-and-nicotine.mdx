<Step title="The most preventable cause of death" kind="explain">

Tobacco smoking is the single largest **preventable** cause of death globally, responsible for around 8 million deaths per year. It causes roughly one in five deaths in high-income countries. Yet it remains common — almost entirely because nicotine is one of the most powerfully addictive substances known.

Tobacco smoke is a complex mixture of over 7,000 chemicals. Three are particularly relevant to health:

**Tar** — the brown, sticky residue left when tobacco burns. Tar coats the airways and lungs, paralyses the cilia (tiny hairs that clean the airways), and contains dozens of known carcinogens including benzene, formaldehyde, and polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons.

**Carbon monoxide (CO)** — a colourless, odourless gas produced by incomplete combustion. CO binds to haemoglobin 200–300× more tightly than oxygen, displacing it and reducing the blood's ability to carry oxygen to tissues. The heart compensates by working harder.

**Nicotine** — the addictive stimulant. Nicotine itself causes relatively little of the cancer and organ damage — but it is why people keep smoking, exposing themselves to tar and CO day after day.

</Step>

<Step title="How nicotine hijacks the brain" kind="explain">

Nicotine reaches the brain within **10–20 seconds** of inhaling — faster than intravenous drugs. It binds to nicotinic acetylcholine receptors and triggers a dopamine release in the reward pathway, producing a brief sense of alertness and mild pleasure.

With regular use, the brain adapts by growing more nicotinic receptors (upregulation). When nicotine is absent, those receptors sit empty, producing the characteristic withdrawal: irritability, poor concentration, anxiety, and craving. The fastest and most reliable way to relieve withdrawal is to smoke again — which is exactly why the addiction perpetuates itself.

**Dependence develops remarkably quickly** — sometimes within days to weeks of regular use, and even before daily smoking. Young people's brains are particularly vulnerable.

</Step>

<Step title="The full toll on the body" kind="explain">

Smoking harms virtually every organ system:

**Lungs:** Chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD) — chronic bronchitis and emphysema — affects millions of smokers and causes progressive, irreversible breathlessness. Lung cancer is the world's leading cause of cancer death; smoking causes ~85% of cases.

**Heart and circulation:** Carbon monoxide and nicotine both raise heart rate and blood pressure. Smoking accelerates atherosclerosis (plaque build-up in arteries), dramatically raising risk of heart attack and stroke.

**Cancer:** Smoking causes cancers not only of the lung but also the mouth, throat, oesophagus, stomach, pancreas, kidney, bladder, and cervix — at least 15 cancer types in total.

**Reproduction and pregnancy:** Smoking reduces fertility, raises miscarriage risk, and causes low birth weight and premature birth if it continues during pregnancy.

**Immune system and healing:** Smokers have impaired immune function and slower wound healing.

</Step>

<Step title="Vaping and e-cigarettes" kind="explain">

**E-cigarettes and vaping** deliver nicotine in an aerosol rather than smoke, avoiding tar and combustion products — making them substantially less harmful than cigarettes by most current evidence. They are used as cessation aids in some countries.

However, "less harmful than cigarettes" is not the same as "safe":
- Long-term effects are not yet fully known (vaping has only been widespread since ~2007).
- Youth vaping rates are high in many countries, raising concern about a new generation developing nicotine dependence.
- Some vaping-related lung injuries (EVALI) have been linked to vitamin E acetate in some products.

For someone who smokes and cannot quit otherwise, switching to vaping is likely to reduce harm. For someone who does not smoke, starting vaping carries real risk with no benefit.

</Step>

<Step title="The good news: quitting works" kind="interactive">

No matter how long someone has smoked, quitting at any age provides measurable health benefits. A 60-year-old who quits gains, on average, three to four years of life expectancy — and years of better quality life before that.

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- Cigarette smoke contains three key harmful agents: **tar** (carcinogens), **carbon monoxide** (displaces oxygen), and **nicotine** (the addictive stimulant).
- Nicotine reaches the brain in seconds, triggers dopamine release, and causes rapid dependence.
- Smoking causes cancers of 15+ types, COPD, heart disease, and strokes — it harms almost every organ.
- Vaping is likely less harmful than smoking but carries its own risks and is not safe for non-smokers.
- **Benefits of quitting start within 20 minutes** and continue for 15+ years — it is never too late.

</Step>
