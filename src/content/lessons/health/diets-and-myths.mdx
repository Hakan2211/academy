<Step title="Why fad diets fail" kind="explain">

A new diet trend emerges roughly every 18 months. Keto, paleo, juice cleanse, raw food, intermittent fasting (as sold), blood-type diet, alkaline diet — the list is long. Some are harmless; a few are genuinely dangerous; almost all fail to produce lasting change for most people.

The common thread is not the macronutrient ratio or the eating window. It is **why they fail long-term**:

- **They require radical behaviour change**, which is hard to sustain. A diet you follow for 8 weeks and then abandon has not changed your health.
- **They are built on restriction and rules**, which tend to create a "good food / bad food" mindset. This often triggers rebound over-eating when the rules are eventually broken.
- **They ignore individual biology and preferences.** The best diet for you is the one you can sustain, not the one with the best short-term clinical trial results.
- **They exploit the placebo and novelty effects.** Any significant dietary change tends to produce initial improvement, which is then attributed to the specific mechanism of that diet.

The most important finding in weight-management research: **long-term adherence trumps short-term effectiveness every time.** A modest, sustainable change you keep for years outperforms a dramatic one you abandon after months.

</Step>

<Step title="Evaluating diet claims" kind="explain">

Before accepting a nutritional claim, ask these questions:

**1. What is the evidence?**
A randomised controlled trial (RCT) with adequate sample size, conducted in humans, for a sufficient duration, by independent researchers — that is strong evidence. A single case study, an anecdote, a rat-model experiment, or a testimonial is not.

**2. Is there a plausible mechanism?**
A claim should not only show an association ("people who eat X live longer") but explain a biological mechanism for *why*. Correlations in nutrition research are riddled with confounders.

**3. Who funded it?**
Industry-funded nutrition research is significantly more likely to show favourable results for the funder's products. This does not make it wrong, but it warrants extra scrutiny.

**4. Is it falsifiable?**
Legitimate science makes specific, testable predictions and acknowledges when evidence contradicts the theory. Pseudoscientific nutrition claims shift their goalposts when challenged.

<Callout type="warn">
"Detox," "cleanse," "alkaline," and "superfood" are marketing terms with no agreed scientific definition. Their use in a health claim should prompt immediate scepticism.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="No magic foods, no forbidden foods" kind="explain">

Two of the most persistent myths in popular nutrition:

**The magic food myth:** that some specific food — acai, goji berries, green tea extract, bone broth, celery juice — will produce dramatic health or weight results. No single food has this power. Health outcomes are determined by overall dietary patterns over months and years, not by any one ingredient.

**The forbidden food myth:** that certain foods (carbs, fat, red meat, dairy, gluten for non-coeliacs) are toxic or must be entirely eliminated for good health. The research does not support eliminating whole food groups for most people. Context, quantity, and overall diet quality matter far more than whether any single food is present.

A useful mental model: imagine your diet as a mosaic, not a single tile. Individual tiles — one chocolate bar, one burger, one bag of crisps — matter very little. The overall pattern of the mosaic determines the picture.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- Most fad diets fail because they require **unsustainable radical restriction** — not because the mechanism is wrong, but because adherence collapses.
- **Long-term adherence beats short-term effectiveness.** A modest change you maintain for years is more valuable than an extreme one you abandon.
- Evaluate diet claims by asking: what is the evidence, is there a mechanism, who funded it, is it falsifiable?
- **No magic foods** produce dramatic health outcomes; **no single food** needs to be eliminated for most people.
- Think in patterns (the mosaic), not individual foods (single tiles).

</Step>
