<Step title="From theory to tray" kind="explain">

Knowing that vegetables should fill half your plate is one thing. Doing it at Tuesday's lunch is another. Nutrition knowledge only pays off when it becomes habit — and habits form through practical, repeatable systems, not willpower.

This lesson is about the practical side: how to assemble a genuinely balanced meal from real foods, how to estimate portions without a food scale, and how to make the healthy choice the easy choice in your own kitchen.

The three questions to ask when assembling any main meal:

- **Where are my vegetables?** They should be the largest component, not a garnish or afterthought.
- **What is my grain or starchy base?** Whole grains — brown rice, wholemeal pasta, oats, wholegrain bread, sweet potato — give sustained energy and fibre.
- **What is my protein source?** Lean meats, fish, eggs, legumes, or tofu anchor the meal and keep you full.

Notice what is not on that checklist: a sweet drink, a side of chips, a dessert as a default. These can appear occasionally in a healthy diet, but they should be conscious choices, not structural parts of every meal.

</Step>

<Step title="Portion sizes — the hand method" kind="explain">

Food scales are accurate, but no one weighs lunch every day. A much more sustainable approach is the **hand-based portion method**. It works because your hands are roughly proportional to your body — bigger people have larger frames and larger hands, so the amounts naturally scale.

The four rules:

- **Palm** ≈ one serving of protein (meat, fish, tofu, eggs × 2). Aim for one to two palms per meal.
- **Fist** ≈ one cup of vegetables or one piece of fruit. Aim for two or more fists of vegetables per meal.
- **Cupped hand** ≈ ½ cup of cooked grains or starchy vegetables. One to two cupped hands per meal is typical.
- **Thumb** ≈ one tablespoon of added fat (olive oil, butter, nut butter). One to two thumbs is enough for cooking and dressing.

The most common finding when people apply these rules: they dramatically under-estimate their grain servings and over-estimate their protein servings. Restaurant portions of pasta or rice are often three to four cupped hands; a single restaurant steak might be three palms.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- Ask three questions at every meal: where are my vegetables, what is my grain base, and what is my protein?
- **Palm** ≈ protein, **fist** ≈ vegetables, **cupped hand** ≈ grains, **thumb** ≈ fats.
- The hand method works because your hands are proportional to your body — no scales needed.
- A *serving* is a standardised label measure; a *portion* is what you actually eat — these often differ dramatically.
- Healthy eating is about **averages across the day**, not perfection at every meal.
