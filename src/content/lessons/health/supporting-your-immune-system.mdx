<Step title="Can you boost your immune system?" kind="explain">

Walk into any pharmacy and you will find shelves of products promising to "boost," "supercharge," or "strengthen" your immune system. It is one of the most successful marketing phrases in health retail. It is also, largely, meaningless.

Here is the key insight from immunology: **you can support and protect your immune system, but you cannot boost it beyond its set point**. An immune system running above normal is not "stronger" — it is associated with inflammation, autoimmune disease, and allergies. The goal is a well-functioning immune system, not an overactive one.

What actually matters is not taking it above normal — it is making sure it is not *below* normal. Deficiencies (too little sleep, chronic stress, malnutrition) impair immunity. Fixing deficiencies restores it. That is what lifestyle interventions actually do.

</Step>

<Step title="What genuinely helps: sleep" kind="explain">

Sleep is probably the most important immune support there is. During sleep:

- The immune system performs maintenance and ramps up cytokine production.
- Memory T cells proliferate and consolidate immune memory.
- The body produces more infection-fighting immune cells.

The evidence is striking: people who sleep fewer than 6 hours per night are **more than four times as likely** to catch a cold after exposure to a rhinovirus than those sleeping 7 or more hours. A single night of poor sleep reduces natural killer cell activity by up to 70%.

The recommendation: **7–9 hours for adults**, consistently.

</Step>

<Step title="What genuinely helps: nutrition" kind="explain">

Immune cells are made of protein. Antibodies are proteins. White blood cell production requires adequate zinc, vitamins A, C, D, and B6, iron, and selenium.

- **Vitamin C** — essential for neutrophil function and skin barrier integrity. Getting *enough* matters; megadoses above adequate intake do little extra.
- **Vitamin D** — receptors on virtually every immune cell; deficiency is common (especially at northern latitudes in winter) and impairs innate and adaptive responses.
- **Zinc** — required for development of immune cells; even mild deficiency reduces immune function.
- **Protein** — the raw material for antibodies and immune cell membranes.

The goal is a **balanced diet** that covers all these bases — not a single "super supplement." No supplement compensates for a consistently poor diet.

</Step>

<Step title="What genuinely helps: exercise, stress, hygiene, and vaccines" kind="explain">

**Regular moderate exercise** — 150 minutes per week of moderate activity — improves immune cell circulation and reduces chronic low-grade inflammation. Importantly, *extreme* over-training can temporarily suppress immunity (the "open window" hypothesis), but this applies to elite athletes training at very high volumes, not to ordinary exercise.

**Managing chronic stress** — cortisol (the main stress hormone) directly suppresses immune function. Chronically elevated cortisol reduces lymphocyte counts, impairs antibody production, and makes you more vulnerable to infections. Effective stress management — sleep, social connection, exercise, relaxation practices — reduces cortisol.

**Handwashing and hygiene** — not a "booster," but reducing your exposure to pathogens means your immune system is not constantly overwhelmed. 20-second handwashing with soap is still one of the most evidence-backed interventions in public health.

**Vaccines** — the single most direct, evidence-based way to expand your immune memory. They are not a lifestyle hack; they are medicine.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- You cannot "supercharge" immunity — the goal is keeping it **at its optimal set point**, not above it.
- **Sleep** (7–9 h) is one of the most powerful immune supports; even one short night measurably impairs function.
- **Balanced nutrition** — covering protein, zinc, vitamins C and D, and other micronutrients — provides the raw materials for immune cells.
- **Regular moderate exercise** improves immune surveillance; chronic stress suppresses it.
- **Hygiene** and **up-to-date vaccines** are among the most effective evidence-based interventions.
- "Immune-boosting" supplements and detoxes are marketing, not medicine.

</Step>
