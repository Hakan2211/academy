<Step title="Cuts and bleeding" kind="explain">

Most cuts can be managed at home with calm, methodical care.

**Steps:**

1. **Apply firm, direct pressure** with a clean cloth, pad, or bandage. Hold it firmly — do not keep lifting it to check, as this disturbs the clot forming underneath.
2. Keep the pressure on for **at least 10 minutes**.
3. **Elevate** the injured area above heart level if possible — this reduces blood flow to the wound.
4. Once bleeding stops, clean gently with clean running water, pat dry, and cover with a sterile dressing.

**Seek medical help if:**
- Bleeding does not stop after 10–15 minutes of pressure
- The wound is deep, gaping, or has jagged edges that may need closure
- An object is embedded in the wound — do not remove it; stabilise it and go to A&E
- The injury is to the face, hands, or over a joint
- There are signs of infection later: increasing redness, warmth, swelling, pus, or fever

</Step>

<Step title="Burns and scalds" kind="explain">

Burns are graded by depth: superficial (red, painful), partial-thickness (blistered), and full-thickness (white/charred, may be painless due to nerve damage). Most household scalds are superficial or partial-thickness.

**The golden rule for any burn:**

> **Cool it. Cover it. Call for help if needed.**

- **Cool under cool (not ice-cold) running water for at least 20 minutes.** This is the single most effective first-aid action — it limits burn depth and eases pain. Start cooling as quickly as possible.
- **Do not use ice, butter, toothpaste, or any cream** — they trap heat or cause infection.
- **Do not pop blisters** — they are a natural protective barrier against infection.
- Loosely cover with a sterile dressing, clean cling film, or a clean plastic bag over a limb.
- Remove jewellery near the burn before swelling begins — but do not pull off stuck clothing.

**Seek urgent medical help if:**
- The burn is larger than the person's palm
- It is on the face, hands, feet, genitals, or a joint
- It is deep (pale, waxy, or painless) or caused by chemicals or electricity
- The person is a child, elderly, or pregnant

</Step>

<Step title="Sprains, nosebleeds, and when to get help" kind="explain">

**Sprains — RICE**

A sprain is a stretched or torn ligament, usually in the ankle or wrist. Apply the **RICE** protocol in the first 24–48 hours:

- **Rest** — avoid putting weight on it
- **Ice** — wrapped in a cloth, 15–20 minutes every 2–3 hours (never ice directly on skin)
- **Compression** — a firm bandage to reduce swelling
- **Elevation** — keep the limb raised above heart level

Seek medical assessment if the person cannot bear weight at all — this may indicate a fracture.

**Nosebleeds**

Lean slightly **forward** (not back — that allows blood into the throat), pinch the **soft, fleshy** part of the nose (not the bony bridge), and breathe through the mouth for **10–15 minutes** without releasing. Seek help if it does not stop within 30 minutes or if the person is on anticoagulants.

**When to get professional help**

Good first aid also means knowing your limits:
- **Call emergency services (999/911/112):** loss of consciousness, suspected heart attack or stroke, difficulty breathing, severe allergic reaction, major bleeding or burns.
- **Go to A&E:** suspected fracture, wound needing closure, chemical exposure, any burn on the list above.
- **See a GP:** infection signs, injury not improving after 48 hours, persistent pain.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Cuts:** firm direct pressure for 10+ minutes, elevate, seek help if bleeding does not stop or wound is deep.
- **Burns:** cool under running water for 20 minutes — not ice, not butter, do not pop blisters. Cover loosely.
- **Sprains:** RICE — Rest, Ice (wrapped), Compression, Elevation for 24–48 hours.
- **Nosebleeds:** lean forward, pinch the soft part, hold for 10–15 minutes.
- Know when to escalate: emergency services, A&E, or a GP — each has its threshold.

</Step>
