<Lesson>

<Step title="More than just 'not being sick'" kind="explain">

Ask most people if they're healthy and they'll answer, "I guess — I'm not ill." But that sets the bar on the floor. Being free of disease is the *start* of health, not the whole of it. You can have no diagnosis and still feel exhausted, anxious, lonely, and weak. Is that really "healthy"?

In 1948 the World Health Organization wrote a definition that still anchors the field:

> **Health is a state of complete physical, mental, and social well-being — not merely the absence of disease or infirmity.**

The radical part is the second half. Health isn't the *absence* of something bad; it's the *presence* of something good — energy, resilience, connection, the capacity to live the life you want.

</Step>

<Step title="A working, everyday definition" kind="explain">

The WHO line is inspiring but a little idealistic — "complete" well-being is rare. A more practical modern view treats health as **the capacity to cope and adapt** — to handle life's physical, mental, and social demands, recover from setbacks, and function well day to day.

That shift matters. It means:

- Health is **dynamic**, not a fixed trophy you win once.
- Someone with a chronic condition can still be **healthy** in the sense that matters: adapting, coping, living fully.
- Health is something you **build and maintain**, like fitness — not something you either have or don't.

This whole island is about that building work: what the body needs, how the mind stays well, and the everyday choices that move you toward thriving.

</Step>

Most of us live in the broad middle and drift up or down with sleep, stress, food, and circumstance. The goal isn't permanent perfection — it's tending in the right direction.

</Step>

<Step title="Why the definition matters" kind="explain">

Definitions aren't just word games — they decide what we *do*.

If health = "no disease," then healthcare = hospitals and pills, and you only act once something breaks. If health = "well-being and the capacity to thrive," then health includes your sleep, your friendships, your stress, your neighbourhood, and the small daily habits that keep you off the operating table in the first place.

That second view is why a walk, a good night's sleep, and a phone call to a friend are genuinely *health* behaviours — not luxuries you get to after the "real" health stuff. They **are** the real health stuff.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Health is more than the absence of disease** — it's positive well-being across body, mind, and social life (the WHO definition).
- A practical modern view: health = the **capacity to cope, adapt, and function well**.
- Health is **dynamic** — a continuum you move along, not a fixed state.
- People with chronic conditions can still be healthy in the way that counts.
- This broad definition makes sleep, food, movement, and connection genuine **health behaviours**.

</Step>
