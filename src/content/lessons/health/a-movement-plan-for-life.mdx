<Step title="What the evidence says: the guidelines" kind="explain">

Decades of population research have been distilled into practical public health guidelines. The numbers that appear again and again across WHO, US, UK, and Australian recommendations are:

- **~150 minutes per week of moderate-intensity aerobic activity**, *or*
- **~75 minutes per week of vigorous-intensity activity**, *or*
- An **equivalent combination** of both (vigorous counts double — 1 minute vigorous ≈ 2 minutes moderate)
- **Plus: strength training for all major muscle groups at least 2 days per week**
- **Plus: reduce prolonged sitting** — break up sedentary time throughout the day

These numbers are not arbitrary. They represent the threshold above which large-scale studies consistently show significant reductions in cardiovascular disease, type 2 diabetes, cancer risk, all-cause mortality, and mental health conditions. Below this threshold, risk rises; above it, additional (though diminishing) benefits continue.

<Callout type="info">
150 minutes sounds like a lot. Spread across 7 days it's just over 21 minutes a day — easily achievable in bouts as short as 10 minutes.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="NEAT: the movement you're already (not) doing" kind="explain">

**NEAT** stands for *Non-Exercise Activity Thermogenesis* — the energy you expend in all physical activity that isn't deliberate exercise. It includes:

- Walking to the bus stop or car
- Taking stairs instead of the lift
- Doing household chores — vacuuming, gardening, carrying shopping
- Fidgeting, standing, moving around at a desk
- Playing with children or pets

NEAT turns out to be surprisingly important. Research comparing sedentary people with highly active ones of similar body size found that active people weren't necessarily doing more formal exercise — they were simply moving more throughout their entire day.

For people with desk jobs, NEAT can represent the **difference between burning 1,400 or 2,400 calories per day** — a gap of 1,000 kcal that no reasonable gym routine can fully bridge.

<Callout type="tip">
Simple NEAT boosters: standing desk periods (even 20 minutes per hour), walking calls, parking farther away, taking stairs, walking to a colleague rather than emailing. None requires changing into gym clothes.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Making movement enjoyable" kind="explain">

The most evidence-based exercise routine is one you actually do. Adherence is everything — and adherence is primarily an enjoyment problem, not a willpower problem.

**What predicts long-term exercise adherence:**

- **Enjoyment of the activity itself** — people who enjoy running run more than people who run because they feel they should. Finding movement you genuinely like, or even mildly prefer, is the primary goal.
- **Social connection** — team sports, group fitness classes, a regular walking partner, or even a digital training community all dramatically improve consistency. Accountability and belonging are powerful.
- **Convenience and low friction** — the activities you actually do are those with the shortest path from intention to action. A gym kit bag ready the night before, a route from home that requires no detour, a habit stacked onto something you already do.
- **Intrinsic motivation over appearance goals** — people who exercise to "look better" tend to have lower long-term adherence than those motivated by feeling stronger, having energy, or stress relief. The intrinsic rewards are more reliable.
- **Variety** — doing the same workout indefinitely leads to boredom and plateaus. Rotating activities keeps both the body and the mind engaged.

</Step>

<Step title="Fitting movement into real life" kind="explain">

"I don't have time" is the most common barrier to exercise. It's worth examining honestly.

**Time reality check:** 150 minutes a week is 2.5 hours — about 1.5% of a waking week. Most of us spend more than that passively scrolling or watching TV. The time is usually there; the question is priority and habit architecture.

**Practical strategies:**

- **Habit stacking** — attach movement to existing habits. Walk after breakfast. Do 10 minutes of stretching after brushing your teeth at night.
- **Micro-sessions count** — three 10-minute walks produce similar health benefits to one 30-minute walk. Remove the "I need a full session or it doesn't count" mental barrier.
- **Protect the schedule** — treat exercise appointments like meetings. Block them in a calendar. Missing a meeting requires rescheduling, not cancellation.
- **Plan for imperfect weeks** — travel, illness, stress, and season changes will disrupt routine. Having a minimum viable version (a 15-minute home workout, a lunchtime walk) keeps the habit alive through disruption.
- **Start stupidly small** — beginners consistently underestimate how much progress they make from what feels like "too little." A 15-minute walk three times a week, maintained for months, builds the habit and the fitness base for everything else.

The goal isn't peak athletic performance — it's a sustainable relationship with movement that continues at 30, 50, 70, and beyond.

</Step>

<Step title="A complete picture" kind="explain">

Pulling everything from this world together: a movement plan for life looks like this.

**Each week, aim for:**
- **150 min moderate aerobic** (or 75 min vigorous, or a mix) — this is the cardiovascular baseline
- **2+ sessions of strength work** — for muscle, bone, and metabolic health
- **Regular flexibility and balance work** — to stay injury-free and functional long-term
- **Reduced and broken-up sedentary time** — standing up, taking short walks, NEAT throughout the day

**How you do it matters less than that you do it.** Brisk walking, swimming, dancing, cycling, football, yoga, lifting, gardening — all count. The best routine is the one you're doing next year.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **~150 min/week moderate** (or 75 min vigorous, or equivalent mix) is the aerobic guideline; **2+ strength sessions/week** are additional.
- Vigorous activity **counts double** — 1 min vigorous ≈ 2 min moderate.
- **NEAT** (non-exercise movement: steps, chores, stairs) can account for a 1,000 kcal/day gap between sedentary and active people — as important as planned exercise.
- **Adherence is everything** — enjoyment, social connection, convenience, and habit stacking predict long-term success more than any programme design.
- Bouts as short as 10 minutes count; start small, stay consistent, and build across months and years.

</Step>
