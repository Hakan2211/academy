<Step title="Emotions are information, not the enemy" kind="explain">

We often speak of emotions as things that *happen to us* — "I was hit by sadness" or "anxiety overwhelmed me." But a more useful frame is this: **emotions are signals**. They carry information about our situation and our needs.

- **Fear** signals: *Something here might be dangerous — pay attention.*
- **Anger** signals: *A boundary has been crossed or a goal is blocked.*
- **Sadness** signals: *Something that mattered to me has been lost.*
- **Joy** signals: *This is good — do more of this.*

None of these are "bad" emotions. They all serve a purpose. The goal is not to eliminate difficult emotions — it is to listen to what they are telling you, respond thoughtfully, and let them pass. Emotions that are suppressed or ignored tend to grow louder, not quieter.

</Step>

<Step title="Name it to tame it" kind="interactive">

Research by psychologist Lisa Feldman Barrett and others shows that people who can name their feelings with precision — a quality called **emotional granularity** — experience those feelings less intensely and recover from them more quickly.

The difference between saying "I feel bad" and "I feel ashamed about a specific thing I did" is enormous. The precise name points to what actually needs addressing.

<Step title="The thought–feeling–behaviour loop" kind="interactive">

Emotions do not arrive from nowhere. Cognitive Behavioural Therapy (CBT) — one of the most evidence-based approaches to mental wellbeing — shows that feelings are shaped by the **thoughts** we have about a situation, and our feelings then drive our **behaviour**. That behaviour often feeds back into the original thought.

The good news: because the loop connects at every node, we can intervene at any point. Changing the thought changes the feeling. Changing the behaviour changes what we think about ourselves. This is one of the core mechanisms behind effective therapy — and it is something we can practise ourselves.

<Step title="Mood versus emotion" kind="explain">

It is worth knowing the difference between an **emotion** and a **mood**:

- An **emotion** is brief and tied to a specific trigger — it rises, peaks, and fades, usually within minutes.
- A **mood** is a background state that colours everything for hours or days, often without a clear cause.

A single bad emotion does not define your mood. But a pattern of unhelpful thought loops, poor sleep, or sustained stress can tip your background mood lower. Conversely, noticing your emotions, naming them, and responding to them thoughtfully helps keep your mood more stable over time.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- Emotions are signals carrying information about our situation and needs — not enemies to eliminate.
- Naming feelings precisely (emotional granularity) reduces their intensity and points to what needs addressing.
- The CBT loop — Situation → Thought → Feeling → Behaviour — shows how thoughts shape feelings and how we can intervene at any point.
- Emotions are brief and specific; moods are background states that can be shaped by thought patterns, sleep, and self-care.

</Step>
