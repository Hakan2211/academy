<Step title="Why recognition is half the battle" kind="explain">

In a serious medical emergency, the gap between recognition and action is where lives are won or lost. People die or suffer permanent harm not because help was unavailable, but because no one recognised what was happening quickly enough to call for it.

This deep-dive lesson covers four emergencies that are common enough to encounter, serious enough to be life-threatening, and recognisable enough for a first-aider to act on:

1. **Heart attack** — sudden blockage of a coronary artery
2. **Stroke** — interrupted blood supply to the brain
3. **Anaphylaxis** — a severe, life-threatening allergic reaction
4. **Seizures** — uncontrolled electrical activity in the brain

For each one: know the signs, know the single most important action.

</Step>

<Step title="Heart attack: recognise and act" kind="explain">

A heart attack occurs when a coronary artery is blocked — usually by a blood clot — starving part of the heart muscle of oxygen. Every minute without treatment, more heart muscle dies.

**Classic signs:**
- **Central chest pain or pressure** — often described as crushing, tight, squeezing, or like a heavy weight
- Pain spreading to the **left arm, jaw, neck, back, or stomach**
- **Sweating**, nausea, or vomiting
- **Breathlessness** — even at rest or with minimal exertion
- Feeling of **dread or doom** (people sometimes describe "knowing something is very wrong")

**Important:** heart attack presentations vary. Women, older adults, and people with diabetes are more likely to have atypical presentations — jaw pain, back pain, fatigue, or nausea without prominent chest pain.

**What to do:**
1. Call emergency services **immediately** — do not drive yourself or wait to see if it improves.
2. Ask them to sit or lie in the position that is most comfortable (often seated, leaning slightly forward — the "W position").
3. If they are conscious and not allergic to aspirin, offer a single adult aspirin (300 mg) to chew slowly — this has evidence for reducing clot size.
4. Monitor and reassure them until help arrives. If they lose consciousness and stop breathing, begin CPR.

</Step>

A stroke occurs when blood supply to part of the brain is cut off — either by a clot (ischaemic stroke, ~85% of cases) or a burst vessel (haemorrhagic stroke). Brain tissue is extraordinarily sensitive: **every minute counts**.

<Step title="Anaphylaxis: severe allergic reaction" kind="explain">

Anaphylaxis is a rapid, severe, whole-body allergic reaction that can be fatal within minutes if untreated. Common triggers include peanuts, tree nuts, shellfish, milk, eggs, insect stings, latex, and certain medications (especially penicillin).

**Signs — onset is fast (seconds to minutes after exposure):**
- **Skin:** hives (raised, itchy red welts), flushing, swelling — especially of the face, lips, or tongue
- **Airways:** throat tightening, hoarse voice, stridor (high-pitched breathing), wheeze
- **Circulation:** rapid weak pulse, drop in blood pressure, dizziness, collapse
- **Gut:** nausea, vomiting, abdominal cramps

The hallmark of anaphylaxis — distinguishing it from a milder allergic reaction — is the involvement of **airways or circulation** (or both).

**What to do:**
1. **Adrenaline (epinephrine) auto-injector** — if the person has one (EpiPen, Jext, Emerade), administer it to the outer thigh immediately. You do not need to remove clothing. Hold for 10 seconds.
2. **Call emergency services immediately** — even if symptoms improve after the auto-injector. Biphasic reactions (a second wave) can occur hours later.
3. Lay the person flat with legs raised (unless they have breathing difficulty, in which case sit them up).
4. A second auto-injector dose can be given after 5–15 minutes if symptoms do not improve.
5. If the person loses consciousness and stops breathing, begin CPR.

</Step>

<Step title="Seizures: protecting, not restraining" kind="explain">

A seizure is caused by abnormal, uncontrolled electrical activity in the brain. The most dramatic type is a tonic-clonic (formerly "grand mal") seizure: the person stiffens, falls, then has rhythmic jerking movements.

**What to do during a seizure:**
- **Time it** — note when it starts; most seizures stop within 1–3 minutes.
- **Protect from harm** — cushion the head with something soft; move hard or sharp objects away.
- **Do NOT restrain** limb movements — you cannot stop a seizure this way and you may cause injury.
- **Do NOT put anything in their mouth** — the person cannot swallow their tongue. Foreign objects can cause broken teeth or choking.
- **Stay calm and stay with them.**

**After the seizure — the postictal phase:**
- The person will be confused, exhausted, and disoriented for several minutes to an hour — this is normal.
- Place them in the recovery position once jerking has stopped.
- Speak calmly; reassure them that they have had a seizure and that you are there.

**Call emergency services if:**
- The seizure lasts more than 5 minutes
- There is no return to consciousness after the seizure
- Another seizure follows quickly
- The person was injured during the seizure
- It is their first seizure
- They are pregnant or have diabetes

</Step>

<Step title="Vital signs: reading the body's status" kind="interactive">

In any serious emergency, knowing what normal vital signs look like — and what deviations mean — helps you describe the patient's condition clearly to emergency services and monitor whether they are improving or deteriorating.

When calling emergency services, being able to say "their pulse is very fast and weak, and they're pale and cold" gives the dispatcher critical information. Note the four key vitals: heart rate, blood pressure, temperature, and respiratory rate.

<Step title="The chain of survival" kind="explain">

In cardiac arrest, survival rates roughly double with each link of the **chain of survival**:

1. **Early recognition and call for help** — recognise the emergency and call immediately.
2. **Early CPR** — bystander CPR can triple survival rates for out-of-hospital cardiac arrest.
3. **Early defibrillation** — an AED (automated external defibrillator) used within 3–5 minutes of collapse gives the highest survival rates. AEDs are increasingly available in public spaces; they give spoken instructions and are safe for untrained users.
4. **Advanced life support** — paramedics and emergency physicians.
5. **Post-resuscitation care** — hospital treatment to protect the heart and brain.

You, as a bystander, control the first two or three links. Your action — or inaction — matters enormously.

**Staying calm under pressure**

The biggest barrier to acting in an emergency is not lack of knowledge — it is panic and the fear of doing something wrong. A few anchors:

- **Something is always better than nothing.** Imperfect CPR is far better than no CPR.
- **Call first** — then the dispatcher guides you through whatever needs doing.
- **Use the acronyms** — DRABC, FAST, RICE — they are mental scaffolding that keeps you moving when the mind wants to freeze.
- **Take a course** — the most reliable way to stay calm is rehearsal. Muscle memory from a practice session fires even when the thinking brain is overwhelmed.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Heart attack:** crushing central chest pain ± radiation — call emergency services, sit them comfortably, aspirin if conscious and not allergic, monitor.
- **Stroke:** FAST — Face, Arms, Speech, Time. Any one sign = call immediately; time is brain.
- **Anaphylaxis:** hives + airway or circulation involvement = use adrenaline auto-injector immediately + call emergency services + lay flat or sit up depending on breathing.
- **Seizures:** time it, protect from harm, do not restrain or put anything in the mouth; call services if it lasts more than 5 minutes or is their first seizure.
- **Chain of survival:** early recognition → early CPR → early defibrillation (AED) → advanced care.
- **Staying calm:** call first, use the acronyms, do something — imperfect action beats inaction every time.
- A certified first-aid course is the best investment you can make in being ready when it counts.

</Step>
