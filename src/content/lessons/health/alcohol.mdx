<Step title="The world's most widely used drug" kind="explain">

Alcohol (ethanol) is the most widely consumed psychoactive substance in the world. It is also one of the most thoroughly studied, which means we have unusually good data on both its effects and its harms.

A key concept is the **standard drink** — a unit that makes it easier to track how much alcohol you are consuming, regardless of what you are drinking:

- 1 standard drink ≈ **14 g of pure alcohol** (the US definition)
- This corresponds roughly to: a regular 355 mL beer (5%), a 148 mL glass of wine (12%), or a 44 mL shot of spirits (40%)

Different countries define a standard drink slightly differently (the UK uses 8 g units), but the principle is the same: a common yardstick for comparing amounts.

</Step>

<Step title="Blood-alcohol concentration and its effects" kind="explain">

**Blood-alcohol concentration (BAC)** is expressed as grams of alcohol per 100 mL of blood (a percentage). At 0.08% BAC, 8 g of alcohol are dissolved in every litre of blood.

What does BAC mean in practice?

- **~0.02%** — Minimal effect; slight relaxation for most people.
- **~0.05%** — Mild impairment of coordination and judgment already begins here — well before the legal driving limit in many countries.
- **~0.08%** — Common legal limit for driving; reaction times and decision-making are clearly impaired.
- **~0.15%** — Slurred speech, poor balance, nausea; high accident risk.
- **~0.25%+** — Dangerous; risk of unconsciousness and suppressed breathing (alcohol poisoning).
- **~0.35–0.45%** — Potentially lethal for many people.

Alcohol's main mechanism is enhancing the inhibitory neurotransmitter GABA while suppressing the excitatory glutamate — which is why it slows everything down, from reaction time to inhibitions to breathing at high doses.

</Step>

One critical fact: **the liver clears alcohol at a roughly fixed rate of ~0.015% BAC per hour** — approximately one standard drink per hour. This rate cannot be meaningfully accelerated by coffee, food, or exercise. If you drink faster than the liver can clear, BAC rises.

</Step>

<Step title="Short- and long-term effects" kind="explain">

**Short-term effects** (related to acute BAC):
- Impaired coordination, reaction time, and judgment
- Mood changes — initial relaxation → disinhibition → sometimes aggression or sadness
- Dehydration (alcohol suppresses antidiuretic hormone)
- Disrupted sleep (alcohol sedates but fragments sleep architecture, reducing restorative REM)

**Long-term effects** from regular heavy drinking:
- **Liver**: fatty liver → hepatitis → cirrhosis (irreversible scarring)
- **Brain**: memory impairment, increased dementia risk, Wernicke-Korsakoff syndrome (thiamine deficiency)
- **Heart**: cardiomyopathy (weakened heart muscle), raised blood pressure
- **Cancer**: alcohol is classified as a Group 1 carcinogen; it raises the risk of cancers of the mouth, throat, oesophagus, liver, colon, and breast
- **Mental health**: alcohol is a depressant — regular use worsens anxiety and depression over time despite short-term relief

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **1 standard drink ≈ 14 g of alcohol** — a shared measure across beer, wine, and spirits.
- **BAC rises** with more drinks, lower body weight, and less time; it falls as the liver clears ~0.015%/hour (roughly 1 drink/hour).
- Impairment begins around **~0.05%**, well before the common 0.08% legal limit.
- Short-term: impaired judgment and coordination, disrupted sleep.
- Long-term: liver disease, brain damage, heart disease, and increased cancer risk across several sites.
- The liver cannot be rushed — there is no shortcut to sobriety.

</Step>
