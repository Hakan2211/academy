<Step title="Before you start" kind="explain">

<Callout type="info">
This lesson is educational only — it is not a substitute for hands-on certified first-aid training. If you are in a real emergency, call your local emergency number immediately (999 in the UK, 911 in the US, 112 in the EU). Taking a recognised first-aid course is the best way to build the confidence and muscle memory these skills actually need.
</Callout>

First aid is the immediate care you give someone who is injured or suddenly unwell — before professional medical help arrives. Done well, it can prevent a minor injury from becoming serious, or keep a seriously ill person alive until the ambulance arrives.

The key insight is that first aid does not require medical expertise. A calm, structured approach — following a simple checklist — is what makes the difference.

</Step>

<Step title="The primary survey: DRABC" kind="explain">

Every first-aid response begins with the same checklist: **DRABC**. It covers the things most likely to kill a person in the first few minutes, in the order you tackle them.

- **D — Danger:** Is the scene safe? Can you approach without becoming a second casualty?
- **R — Response:** Is the person conscious? Do they respond to voice or touch?
- **A — Airway:** Is their airway open and unobstructed?
- **B — Breathing:** Are they breathing normally?
- **C — Circulation:** Is there serious bleeding or other signs of poor circulation?

You work through this from top to bottom. If you find a problem at any step, you deal with it before moving on.

The most important rule of DRABC — and of all first aid — is this: **your own safety comes first**. If the scene is dangerous and you cannot make it safe, do not approach. Call emergency services and guide them from a safe distance. A second casualty does not help the first one.

</Step>

<Step title="Calling for help" kind="explain">

Calling emergency services is one of the most important things you do in a medical emergency — and you should do it **early**, not as a last resort.

When you call:

- **Stay calm and speak clearly.** The dispatcher has a checklist too.
- **Give your location first** — they can dispatch help before you finish the rest.
- **Describe what has happened** and the person's current condition.
- **Follow their instructions** — they may guide you through CPR or other steps while help is en route.
- **Do not hang up unless they tell you to.**

If bystanders are present, point to a specific person: "You — in the red jumper — call 999 now." Diffuse instructions like "someone call an ambulance" are less reliable — a named person feels personal responsibility.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **First aid is structured, not expert.** Following DRABC calmly is what matters.
- **D — Danger:** Scene safety first. Never become a second casualty.
- **R — Response:** Check if the person is conscious by tapping and asking.
- **A — Airway:** Open with head-tilt chin-lift if needed.
- **B — Breathing:** Look, listen, feel for up to 10 seconds. Gasps are not normal breathing.
- **C — Circulation:** Control severe bleeding with firm direct pressure.
- **Call early** — give your location first, follow dispatcher instructions, name specific bystanders to help.
- A certified first-aid course is the best way to practise these skills with real confidence.

</Step>
