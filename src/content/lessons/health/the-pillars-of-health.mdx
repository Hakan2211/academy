<Step title="Most health benefit comes from a few things" kind="explain">

After decades of research — large cohort studies, systematic reviews, meta-analyses — a surprisingly short list keeps appearing as the drivers of long, healthy life. Not a perfect diet. Not an elite fitness regimen. Not the right supplement. A handful of everyday behaviours, sustained over time.

The pillars are:

- **Sleep** — 7–9 hours of quality rest, consistently
- **Nutrition** — mostly whole foods, mostly plants, minimal ultra-processing
- **Movement** — 150 minutes or more of moderate activity each week
- **Connection** — genuine social relationships and belonging
- **Mind and stress management** — active practices to down-regulate chronic stress
- **Avoiding harm** — not smoking; moderate or no alcohol

No single pillar is the "secret." The power is in their combination. And here is the genuinely reassuring part: **you don't need perfection in all of them.** Consistent effort across most pillars outperforms perfection in one while neglecting the others.

</Step>

<Step title="They prop each other up" kind="explain">

The pillars are not independent — they form a system. Better sleep makes food choices easier and movement less effortful. Regular movement reduces stress and improves sleep. Strong social ties encourage healthier behaviours and provide stress-buffering. Managing stress protects sleep.

This means two things:

1. **A positive cycle is possible.** Improve one pillar even modestly and the others become easier. The system can move toward health or away from it, and small pushes matter.

2. **The weakest link costs you.** A person who exercises rigorously but sleeps five hours a night, eats poorly, and is chronically isolated will not achieve the health benefits their training promises. A badly lopsided profile limits all the pillars.

The goal is a **reasonably balanced profile** — not peak performance on any single dimension, but adequate support across all of them.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- A short list of pillars — **sleep, nutrition, movement, connection, mind/stress, avoiding harm** — accounts for most of the known benefit of healthy living.
- You don't need perfection in all of them: **consistent effort across most** is more effective than excellence in one while neglecting the others.
- The pillars **reinforce each other** — improving one tends to lift the others through shared biological and behavioural pathways.
- A **balanced profile** (even if imperfect) is healthier than an extreme lopsided one.
- The Wellness Wheel is a practical tool for spotting which dimensions of your own health need attention.

</Step>
