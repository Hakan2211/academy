<Step title="Three levels of prevention" kind="explain">

Prevention is not one thing. Medicine has long divided it into three tiers, each operating at a different stage of disease progression:

**Primary prevention** stops disease from occurring at all. It acts before any pathological process begins:
- Vaccinations against measles, flu, HPV
- Not smoking; eating well; staying active
- Wearing a seatbelt or sunscreen
- Fluoridation of drinking water

**Secondary prevention** detects disease in its early, often symptom-free stage — when it is most treatable. The tools are **screening tests** and **early diagnosis**:
- Mammograms (breast cancer)
- Cervical (Pap/HPV) smears
- Blood-pressure and cholesterol checks
- Bowel cancer stool tests

**Tertiary prevention** manages an existing, diagnosed condition to reduce complications, limit disability, and slow progression:
- Cardiac rehabilitation after a heart attack
- Blood-sugar control in someone with type 2 diabetes
- Physiotherapy after a stroke

<Callout type="info">
Primary prevention is always the cheapest and most powerful tier — it avoids all costs and suffering downstream. But it requires acting early, often before people feel any urgency, which is why it is the hardest to motivate.
</Callout>

<Step title="The logic of screening" kind="explain">

Screening asks a simple question: **can we identify a disease (or the risk of one) in people who do not yet have symptoms?** If we can catch it earlier, treatment is more likely to succeed, less likely to require aggressive intervention, and far less costly — in money, suffering, and life years.

A good screening test must clear four hurdles:

1. **The disease has a detectable pre-symptomatic phase** — there is a window during which it can be found early.
2. **Early treatment improves outcomes** — catching it earlier must actually help; for some conditions, it does not.
3. **The test is accurate enough** — both sensitive (catches most true cases) and specific (avoids flooding the system with false alarms).
4. **The benefits outweigh the harms** — screening is never cost-free (see below).

</Step>

<Step title="The real costs and trade-offs of screening" kind="explain">

Screening is not a free lunch. Every test carries potential harms that must be weighed against benefits:

**False positives** — the test flags a problem that does not exist. This triggers anxiety, follow-up tests, and sometimes unnecessary treatment. Prostate cancer screening (PSA test) and some mammography programmes have been criticised for high false-positive rates.

**False negatives** — the test misses a real problem, giving false reassurance. People may delay seeking help when symptoms eventually appear.

**Overdiagnosis** — the test detects a real abnormality that would never have caused symptoms or death in the person's lifetime. The detected "disease" then receives treatment with genuine side effects, with no benefit.

**Procedural harms** — colonoscopies carry a small perforation risk; CT scans deliver radiation; biopsies are invasive.

This is why screening is not recommended for every disease, and why recommended tests are based on **large randomised trials** showing net population benefit, not just the possibility of early detection.

</Step>

<Step title="Acting on results" kind="explain">

A screening result is the start of a conversation, not a verdict. A raised blood pressure at a routine check does not mean a heart attack is imminent — it means you now have information you can act on:

- **Lifestyle changes first**: for many elevated readings (blood pressure, blood sugar, cholesterol), diet, exercise, and weight management can normalise the number without medication.
- **Monitoring**: some findings warrant watchful waiting and repeat testing before any intervention.
- **Treatment**: when a number is significantly raised or a cancer is detected, evidence-based treatment begins — often with far better outcomes than if found at a symptomatic stage.

The point is empowerment. A screened, informed person has options. An unscreened person discovering symptoms of advanced disease has fewer.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Primary prevention** stops disease before it starts (vaccines, healthy lifestyle, safety measures).
- **Secondary prevention** catches disease in a pre-symptomatic stage through screening and early diagnosis.
- **Tertiary prevention** manages existing disease to limit complications and disability.
- A good screening test requires: detectable pre-symptomatic phase, evidence that early treatment helps, adequate accuracy, and net benefit over harm.
- Screening trade-offs include **false positives**, **false negatives**, **overdiagnosis**, and procedural risks — all must be weighed against lives saved.
- Screening recommendations are age-specific and evidence-based; acting on results with lifestyle changes or treatment dramatically improves outcomes.

</Step>
