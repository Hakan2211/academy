<Step title="The slow epidemic" kind="explain">

In 1900, most deaths in the world were caused by infectious diseases — cholera, pneumonia, tuberculosis, childhood fevers. By 2023, the picture had inverted. Non-communicable diseases (NCDs) now cause **~74% of all global deaths** — and the vast majority of those deaths are from just four conditions:

- **Cardiovascular disease** (heart attacks, strokes) — 17.9 million deaths/year
- **Cancer** — 9.3 million deaths/year
- **Chronic respiratory disease** (COPD, asthma) — 4.1 million deaths/year
- **Type 2 diabetes** — 1.5 million deaths/year (many more in which it is a contributing factor)

These are not random. They cluster around a small set of shared causes — which means preventing one often prevents the others at the same time.

</Step>

<Step title="Modifiable vs non-modifiable risk factors" kind="explain">

A **risk factor** is anything that raises the probability of developing a disease. Risk factors fall into two groups:

**Non-modifiable** — you cannot change them:
- **Age** — the most powerful predictor; risk rises steeply for most NCDs after 50.
- **Biological sex** — men and women have different risk profiles for certain cancers and cardiovascular disease.
- **Family history / genetics** — inherited variants can raise lifetime risk substantially.

**Modifiable** — you can change them, and doing so meaningfully reduces risk:
- **Tobacco use** — largest single preventable cause of cancer and cardiovascular disease.
- **Physical inactivity** — raises risk of heart disease, diabetes, and several cancers.
- **Unhealthy diet** — excess salt, saturated fat, and ultra-processed food; too few fruits, vegetables, and fibre.
- **Excess alcohol consumption** — raises risk of liver disease, stroke, and at least 7 cancer types.
- **High blood pressure (hypertension)** — the "silent killer"; often no symptoms until a heart attack or stroke.

<Callout type="info">
Tobacco, poor diet, physical inactivity, and harmful alcohol use share something important: they are largely shaped by **environment and policy** — what food is affordable and marketed, whether safe spaces for exercise exist, how tobacco is taxed. Personal choice matters, but the environment sets the range of choices.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Risk is probabilistic, not deterministic" kind="explain">

It is tempting to think: "My grandfather smoked until 90 and never got cancer, so it cannot be that risky." This misunderstands how population risk works.

Risk factors raise the **probability** of disease across large groups. Even a factor that doubles your risk does not guarantee you will get ill — it just shifts the odds against you. Think of it like a loaded die. Stacking unhealthy choices loads the die progressively; stacking healthy choices progressively unloads it.

This matters in two directions:
- Do not take comfort in individual "lucky" cases — they do not outweigh population-level evidence.
- Do not catastrophise — having risk factors does not make disease inevitable. The whole point of knowing them is to act.

People sometimes feel fatalistic about family history or age. But even with non-modifiable risks, eliminating modifiable ones still delivers large, measurable reductions in absolute risk.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- NCDs — cardiovascular disease, cancer, chronic respiratory disease, and type 2 diabetes — cause ~74% of global deaths, most of them substantially preventable.
- **Non-modifiable risk factors**: age, sex, family history/genetics — these cannot be changed but inform personalised prevention.
- **Modifiable risk factors**: tobacco, physical inactivity, poor diet, excess alcohol, high blood pressure — changing these substantially lowers risk.
- Risks **stack** — multiple unhealthy habits compound each other; multiple healthy ones also compound.
- Risk is **probabilistic**, not deterministic. Family history loads the die but does not determine the outcome.

</Step>
