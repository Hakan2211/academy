<Step title="From understanding to doing" kind="explain">

We've established the big picture: health is multi-dimensional, it's a continuum you move along, and most of it is shaped by everyday life rather than the clinic. This deep dive turns that picture into action — the mindset and tools for actually *taking charge*.

Three ideas do most of the work: **prevention beats cure**, **health literacy** lets you judge what's true, and **small habits compound** into big outcomes. Get these three working and you've got the operating system for everything else on this island.

</Step>

<Step title="Prevention beats cure" kind="explain">

The cheapest, most powerful health move is to stop problems before they start. Prevention comes in three timings:

- **Primary** — prevent the problem ever arising (vaccines, seatbelts, not smoking, eating well).
- **Secondary** — catch it early, when it's most treatable (screenings, check-ups, blood-pressure checks).
- **Tertiary** — limit the damage once a condition exists (rehab, managing a chronic disease well).

The further *left* you act — toward primary prevention — the more health you buy per unit of effort. An hour spent preventing is worth many hours spent treating.

</Step>

<Step title="Sort the prevention" kind="interactive">

Try placing each action at its level. Notice how primary prevention tends to be the everyday, low-tech stuff — and how it's also the most powerful.

A health system that only does tertiary care is forever bailing out a sinking boat. The smartest individuals — and societies — invest upstream, in primary prevention.

</Step>

<Step title="Health literacy: your nonsense filter" kind="explain">

We're drowning in health *claims* — miracle diets, detox teas, scary headlines, confident influencers. **Health literacy** is the skill of finding, understanding, and judging this information so you can act on what's true and ignore what isn't.

A few quick filters that catch most nonsense:

- **Who's selling something?** A claim attached to a product deserves extra suspicion.
- **Correlation isn't causation.** "People who do X are healthier" rarely means X *caused* it.
- **Anecdote isn't evidence.** One dramatic story ("it cured my cousin!") tells you almost nothing.
- **Extraordinary claims need extraordinary evidence.** "One weird trick" almost never survives a real trial.

You don't need a medical degree — just the habit of pausing and asking, "How would anyone actually *know* that?"

</Step>

<Step title="Small habits compound" kind="explain">

Here's the most encouraging fact in all of health. You don't change your health with one heroic effort — you change it with small actions repeated until they're automatic.

A 1% improvement barely registers today. But repeat it daily and it compounds, the way a tiny interest rate grows a fortune over decades. A 10-minute walk after dinner, one extra glass of water, lights out 20 minutes earlier — each is almost nothing on its own. Done for a year, they reshape your weight, your mood, your sleep, your risk of disease.

<Formula tex="1.01^{365} \approx 37.8 \qquad\text{vs}\qquad 0.99^{365} \approx 0.03" block />

The same maths runs both ways: small *bad* habits compound into disease just as quietly. The lever isn't intensity — it's **consistency** and **direction**.

</Step>

<Step title="Putting it together" kind="explain">

So how do you take charge in practice?

1. **Aim upstream.** Favour primary prevention — the daily habits — over waiting to be fixed.
2. **Filter the noise.** Use basic health literacy so you act on evidence, not marketing.
3. **Start absurdly small.** Pick one tiny habit and make it automatic before adding another.
4. **Watch direction, not perfection.** You don't need peak wellness — you need to be sliding the right way.
5. **Mind the whole wheel.** Strengthen your weakest dimension, not just the easy one.

The rest of the island is the detail: how the body works, what to eat, how to move, sleep, and protect your mind. But the operating system is right here — and it's genuinely within reach.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Prevention beats cure** — primary prevention (stopping problems before they start) buys the most health per effort.
- The three levels are **primary, secondary, tertiary** — act as far upstream as you can.
- **Health literacy** filters the flood of claims: watch for sellers, correlation-not-causation, and anecdotes.
- **Small habits compound** — consistency and direction beat intensity (1.01³⁶⁵ ≈ 38).
- Take charge by aiming upstream, filtering noise, starting small, watching direction, and minding the whole wheel.

</Step>
