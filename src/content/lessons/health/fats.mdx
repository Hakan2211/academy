<Step title="Fat is not the enemy" kind="explain">

Dietary fat has had a bad reputation for decades — much of it unfairly. Fat is not just stored energy waiting to be burned. It is **structurally and biochemically essential**:

- Every cell membrane in the body is made of a **phospholipid bilayer** — two layers of fat molecules that control what enters and leaves each cell.
- The **brain is about 60% fat** by dry weight. Myelin, the insulating sheath around nerve fibres, is largely fat.
- Fat is needed to **absorb fat-soluble vitamins** — A, D, E, and K — none of which can be absorbed without dietary fat.
- Many **hormones**, including oestrogen, testosterone, and cortisol, are built from cholesterol — a type of fat.
- Fat is the body's main **long-term energy store**: roughly 9 kcal per gram, more than twice carbs or protein.

A very low-fat diet risks deficiency of fat-soluble vitamins and disrupts hormone production.

</Step>

<Step title="Types of fat: structure shapes function" kind="explain">

All fats are made of fatty acid chains. Their health effects depend on the chain's **degree of saturation** — how many hydrogen atoms are bonded to the carbon backbone.

**Saturated fats** (no double bonds)
- Solid at room temperature (butter, lard, coconut oil, fatty meat, full-fat dairy).
- High intake raises LDL ("bad") cholesterol in most people, increasing cardiovascular risk.
- Not inherently toxic — context and overall diet matter — but moderation is advised.

**Monounsaturated fats** (one double bond)
- Liquid at room temperature; solidify when cold (olive oil, avocado oil, canola oil, most nuts).
- Associated with improved LDL/HDL balance and reduced heart-disease risk.

**Polyunsaturated fats** (two or more double bonds)
- Omega-3s (oily fish, walnuts, flaxseed) and omega-6s (sunflower oil, corn oil).
- Both are **essential fats** — the body cannot make them. Must come from food.
- Omega-3s are particularly associated with reduced inflammation and cardiovascular benefit.

**Trans fats** (artificially hydrogenated)
- Created industrially by partially hydrogenating vegetable oils → creates unusual molecular shapes.
- Raise LDL *and* lower HDL simultaneously — the worst combination for heart health.
- Most countries have banned or severely restricted industrial trans fats. Avoid "partially hydrogenated oils" on labels.

</Step>

<Step title="Essential fatty acids: the ones you must eat" kind="explain">

Two polyunsaturated fatty acids are **essential** — the body cannot synthesise them:

- **Alpha-linolenic acid (ALA)** — an omega-3; found in flaxseed, chia seeds, walnuts, canola oil. Can be partially converted to EPA and DHA (found in oily fish) but conversion is inefficient (~10%).
- **Linoleic acid (LA)** — an omega-6; found in most vegetable oils, nuts, seeds.

DHA (docosahexaenoic acid) is particularly important for brain and eye development — this is why oily fish or DHA supplements are specifically recommended during pregnancy and for infants.

<Callout type="tip">
If you eat little or no oily fish, an algae-based DHA/EPA supplement provides the long-chain omega-3s directly — without the conversion inefficiency from plant ALA.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Fat's energy density in action" kind="interactive">

Pick a food and notice how high-fat items — almonds, avocado — pack far more calories per 100 g than high-carb foods like rice or apple. Fat's 9 kcal/g density is the reason.

<MacroBreakdown />

This isn't a reason to avoid fat — density can be a feature for endurance athletes and people with high energy needs. But it explains why small amounts of fatty foods contribute a lot to total calorie intake.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- Fat is **essential**: cell membranes, brain tissue, hormone production, fat-soluble vitamin absorption.
- **Unsaturated fats** (mono- and poly-) are healthier; **saturated fat** should be moderate; **trans fat** (industrial) should be avoided.
- **Omega-3 and omega-6** polyunsaturated fatty acids are essential — must come from food.
- Fat provides **9 kcal per gram** — more than twice carbs or protein — making fatty foods very energy-dense.
- Dietary fat quality, not just quantity, is what matters most for health.

</Step>
