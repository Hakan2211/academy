<Step title="Why contraception?" kind="explain">

Contraception refers to methods used to prevent pregnancy. People choose contraception for many reasons — to plan the timing of a family, to space pregnancies, to pursue education or career goals, to protect health, or simply because they do not want to become pregnant.

The right method varies between people and can change over a lifetime. No single method suits everyone, and choosing contraception is a personal health decision best made with accurate information — and where possible, support from a healthcare provider.

Key concepts:
- **Typical-use effectiveness** reflects real-world use, including occasional mistakes. **Perfect-use effectiveness** is higher but less realistic for most people day to day.
- **Reversibility**: most methods are reversible; fertility returns after stopping them. Permanent options (surgical sterilisation) exist but are outside the scope of this lesson.
- **STI protection**: only barrier methods (condoms) significantly reduce the risk of sexually transmitted infections. Other methods do not.

</Step>

<Step title="How the main methods work" kind="explain">

Contraceptive methods work through different mechanisms:

**Barrier methods**
Physically block sperm from reaching the egg. External (male) and internal (female) condoms are the only methods that also protect against most STIs. Diaphragms and cervical caps also act as physical barriers but do not provide STI protection.

**Hormonal methods**
Alter the hormonal environment to prevent pregnancy. The **combined pill** (oestrogen + progestogen) primarily prevents ovulation. The **progestogen-only pill** thickens cervical mucus and may suppress ovulation. Both require daily oral doses. The **patch** and **ring** deliver hormones through the skin or vaginally on a weekly or monthly basis.

**Long-acting reversible contraceptives (LARCs)**
Set-and-forget methods that last years. The **implant** is a small rod inserted under the skin of the upper arm that releases progestogen for up to 3 years. The **hormonal IUS** (intrauterine system) sits in the uterus and releases low-dose progestogen locally for 3–8 years. The **copper IUD** (intrauterine device) uses copper ions, which are toxic to sperm — a hormone-free, highly effective option lasting up to 10 years.

**Emergency contraception**
A backup, not a primary method. The levonorgestrel "morning-after pill" delays or prevents ovulation and is most effective within 72 hours. The copper IUD can also be used as emergency contraception up to 5 days after unprotected sex — and is the most effective emergency option.

</Step>

<Step title="Choosing a method" kind="explain">

There is no universally "best" contraceptive. The right choice depends on:

- **Health factors**: some hormonal methods are not suitable for people with certain medical conditions (e.g., migraines with aura, history of blood clots, some cancers). A healthcare provider can help assess this.
- **Convenience**: daily pills require consistent timing; LARCs remove that burden.
- **Side effects**: hormonal methods affect some people differently — changes in mood, periods, or libido are possible. The copper IUD may cause heavier periods in some people.
- **Future pregnancy plans**: if you want to conceive soon, a method with immediate reversibility (barrier, pill) may suit better than a LARC, though LARCs are also reversible.
- **STI risk**: if there is any risk of an STI, condoms should be used alongside any other method (often called "dual protection").

It is entirely valid to try a method, find it does not suit you, and switch. Discussing options with a GP, sexual health clinic, or pharmacist provides personalised guidance.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- Contraceptive methods fall into three broad types: **barrier** (physical block), **hormonal** (alter ovulation/mucus/lining), and **long-acting reversible** (implant, IUS, IUD).
- **Typical-use effectiveness** is the real-world figure that accounts for occasional user error.
- **LARCs** (implant, IUS, copper IUD) have the highest effectiveness because they remove the need for daily action.
- **Only condoms** (barrier methods) also protect against STIs — all other methods are pregnancy-prevention only.
- Choosing a method depends on health history, lifestyle, future plans, and STI risk; a healthcare provider can help.
- "Dual protection" means using a condom alongside another method for both pregnancy and STI prevention.

</Step>
