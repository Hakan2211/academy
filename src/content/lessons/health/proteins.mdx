<Step title="What proteins actually are" kind="explain">

Proteins are large molecules made from chains of **amino acids** — small chemical units linked end-to-end. There are 20 amino acids the human body uses. Of those:

- **9 are essential** — the body cannot synthesise them; they *must* come from food.
- **11 are non-essential** — the body can make them from other compounds.

A protein's function is entirely determined by the *sequence* of amino acids in its chain and how that chain folds in 3D space. Change one amino acid in haemoglobin and you get sickle-cell anaemia. Same molecule, different fold, completely different consequences.

Dietary protein from food is broken down in the gut into individual amino acids and short peptides, then absorbed and reassembled into the specific proteins your cells need.

</Step>

<Step title="What the body uses protein for" kind="explain">

Protein isn't just for bodybuilders. It plays roles in virtually every biological process:

- **Structural proteins** — collagen (tendons, skin, bone matrix), actin and myosin (muscle fibres), keratin (hair, nails).
- **Enzymes** — nearly every chemical reaction in the body is catalysed by a protein enzyme.
- **Hormones** — insulin, glucagon, growth hormone, and many others are proteins.
- **Transport** — haemoglobin carries oxygen; albumin carries fatty acids and drugs in the blood.
- **Immune defence** — antibodies are proteins; so are complement proteins and many cytokines.
- **Signalling** — receptors on cell surfaces that catch hormones and neurotransmitters are proteins.

Unlike carbs and fat, the body does not have a dedicated storage depot for protein. Excess amino acids can be converted to glucose or fat, but there's no "protein reserve" — which is why regular intake matters.

</Step>

<Step title="Complete vs incomplete protein sources" kind="explain">

A **complete protein** contains all 9 essential amino acids in adequate amounts. Animal sources — meat, fish, poultry, eggs, dairy — are generally complete. Soy and quinoa are among the few plant-based complete sources.

An **incomplete protein** is low in one or more essential amino acids (called the *limiting amino acid*). Most plant proteins are incomplete:

- Grains are typically low in lysine.
- Legumes are typically low in methionine.

However, eating a variety of plant proteins across the day covers all essential amino acids. This is why traditional diets that combine grains and legumes — rice and beans, hummus and bread, dal and chapati — are nutritionally sound.

<Callout type="info">
You don't need to combine complementary proteins within the same meal. Getting all essential amino acids across the day is sufficient.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="How much protein do we need?" kind="explain">

General guidelines (for healthy adults):

- **Minimum to prevent deficiency:** ≈ 0.66 g per kg of body weight per day.
- **General recommendation (RDA):** 0.8 g/kg/day.
- **Active adults / older adults:** 1.2–2.0 g/kg/day is often suggested to support muscle synthesis and prevent age-related loss.

A 70 kg adult needs roughly 56–140 g of protein per day depending on activity. A large chicken breast (150 g) provides about 45 g; a cup of cooked lentils gives about 18 g.

Protein needs rise during pregnancy, lactation, growth, illness recovery, and resistance training.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- Proteins are chains of **amino acids** — 9 essential (must come from food), 11 non-essential.
- Protein roles: structural tissue, enzymes, hormones, oxygen transport, immune defence, cell signalling.
- **Complete sources** (all 9 EAAs): animal products, soy, quinoa. **Incomplete**: most plant proteins — but variety across the day covers all needs.
- Recommended intake: 0.8 g/kg/day minimum; 1.2–2.0 g/kg for active or older adults.
- Protein provides **4 kcal per gram**, the same as carbohydrate.

</Step>
