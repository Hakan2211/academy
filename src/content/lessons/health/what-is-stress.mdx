<Step title="Your body has an alarm system" kind="explain">

Imagine you're crossing a street and a car swerves toward you. Before you've consciously registered what's happening, your heart is hammering, your legs are moving, and your senses are razor-sharp. No decision-making required — your body did it for you.

That automatic reaction is **stress** — or more precisely, your body's **stress response**: a rapid, full-body mobilisation designed to help you survive a threat.

Stress itself is not a disease. It is a deeply wired survival mechanism, and understanding it is the first step toward managing it well.

</Step>

<Step title="What actually triggers stress — stressors" kind="explain">

The thing that sets off the alarm is called a **stressor**: anything your brain interprets as a threat, demand, or challenge.

Stressors come in many forms:

- **Physical** — illness, injury, extreme temperature, hunger
- **Psychological** — a looming deadline, an argument, financial worry, uncertainty
- **Social** — conflict, loneliness, humiliation, peer pressure
- **Environmental** — noise, overcrowding, dangerous surroundings

Notice that the brain doesn't only react to *real* physical threats. It also fires the alarm in response to *perceived* threats — including things that haven't happened yet but that you're *imagining* could. This is why worrying about a presentation can feel physically similar to outrunning a predator.

<Callout type="info">

The stress response evolved to handle short, intense threats. Modern life often serves us *long, ambiguous* stressors — a difficult boss, chronic debt, an uncertain future — for which the same alarm system is less well-suited.

</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Acute vs chronic stress" kind="explain">

Not all stress is the same. The most important distinction is **how long it lasts**:

**Acute stress** is short-lived. The threat appears, the alarm fires, you respond, the threat passes, and your body recovers to its resting state. This is the natural shape of the stress response — brief, purposeful, followed by recovery.

**Chronic stress** is prolonged. The stressor doesn't go away — or you never let your body come down from the alarm state. Instead of a brief spike and recovery, your body is running its stress machinery for hours, days, or months on end.

Chronic stress is the type that causes damage. We'll explore exactly why in later lessons in this world, but the short answer is: the stress system is expensive to run, and running it continuously degrades the machinery.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Stress** is the body's automatic alarm response to a perceived threat or demand.
- The trigger is called a **stressor** — physical, psychological, social, or environmental.
- The brain responds to *perceived* threats just as it does to real ones, including imagined future events.
- **Acute stress** is brief and purposeful — it fires, you respond, and the body recovers.
- **Chronic stress** is prolonged; the body stays in alarm mode with no recovery, which is where the real damage occurs.
- Some stress is not just normal — it's useful. The next lessons explore both sides.

</Step>
