<Step title="When is it time to reach out?" kind="explain">

One of the biggest barriers to seeking help is uncertainty: *Am I bad enough to deserve support? Maybe I should just push through.* The honest answer is: you do not have to be in crisis to deserve care.

Some signs it is worth reaching out:

- Low mood, anxiety, or worry that has lasted **two weeks or more**
- Feelings that are **affecting your daily life** — sleep, work, relationships, eating
- Feeling that things are not improving despite your own efforts
- Increasing use of alcohol or substances to cope
- Thoughts of harming yourself (if this is you right now, please reach out immediately — to a trusted person, a GP, or a crisis helpline)
- A general sense of **not being okay** that you cannot shake

You do not need to meet a diagnostic threshold to ask for support. Asking early — before things escalate — is wisdom, not weakness.

</Step>

<Step title="Kinds of support available" kind="explain">

Support exists on a spectrum from informal to professional:

**A trusted person**
The simplest and often first step. A friend, family member, teacher, or mentor who listens without judgement. Research shows that feeling heard, even without advice, is itself therapeutic.

**A GP or doctor**
Your family doctor is often the gateway to mental health care. They can assess what you are experiencing, rule out physical causes, offer initial support, and refer you to specialist services if needed. No appointment for mental health concerns is "wasting their time."

**Talking therapies**
The most evidence-based talking therapies include:
- **CBT (Cognitive Behavioural Therapy)** — helps identify and change unhelpful thought patterns and behaviours. Recommended first-line for anxiety and depression.
- **Counselling** — provides a safe space to explore feelings and experiences, often around specific events or transitions.
- **ACT (Acceptance and Commitment Therapy)** — focuses on accepting difficult feelings and committing to values-based action.
- **DBT (Dialectical Behaviour Therapy)** — developed for intense emotions, particularly helpful for emotional dysregulation.

**Helplines**
Available around the clock, staffed by trained volunteers, confidential. In most countries there are free mental health helplines for people in distress. A quick search for your country plus "mental health helpline" will find the right number.

**Apps and self-help**
Digital tools like mood diaries, guided meditation, and structured CBT programmes can supplement support or provide a first step. They work best when used alongside — not instead of — human connection.

</Step>

<Step title="What therapy actually is" kind="explain">

Many people have a vague or mythologised idea of therapy. Here is what it actually involves:

- A qualified therapist provides a **confidential, non-judgemental space** to talk about what you are experiencing.
- Together, you explore thoughts, feelings, and patterns — not to dwell in the past, but to understand what is happening and develop skills to change it.
- Therapy is **active**, not passive. Between sessions, most therapies involve exercises — keeping a thought diary, practising new behaviours, gradual exposure to feared situations.
- It typically runs for **6–20 sessions** for common presentations of anxiety and depression, though this varies.
- It **works**. CBT has the largest evidence base of any psychological treatment. The majority of people who complete a course of therapy experience significant improvement.

Therapy is not a sign that you cannot cope alone. It is a skill-building process — like working with a physio for a physical injury.

</Step>

<Step title="Reducing stigma" kind="explain">

Stigma — negative social attitudes toward mental health conditions — is one of the main reasons people delay seeking help. It is worth examining some common myths:

- **"Mental health problems are rare."** — They affect 1 in 8 people worldwide at any time. They are not rare.
- **"People should be able to sort this out on their own."** — We would not say this about a broken bone or diabetes. Mental health conditions involve real biological processes.
- **"Therapy is for weak people."** — Engaging with therapy requires courage, self-honesty, and sustained effort. It is the opposite of weakness.
- **"Things will never get better."** — This is a symptom of depression, not a fact. The evidence is clear: with appropriate support, most people recover significantly.

Normalising conversations about mental health — in families, schools, and workplaces — saves lives.

</Step>

<Step title="How to support a friend" kind="explain">

Supporting a friend who is struggling is one of the most important things we can do. Some guidance:

**Do:**
- Ask directly: "Are you okay? I have noticed you seem a bit down lately." Asking does not plant the idea — it opens the door.
- Listen more than you advise. Often people need to feel heard, not fixed.
- Stay in contact, even when they push back or seem distant. Consistency matters.
- Encourage professional help without pressure: "Have you thought about talking to someone? I could go with you to find out what is available."
- Look after yourself too — supporting someone through difficulty is emotionally demanding.

**Avoid:**
- "Just think positively." "You have so much to be grateful for." These minimise the experience.
- Telling them what they should do before they have asked for that.
- Sharing their situation with others without permission.
- Disappearing because you feel helpless. Your presence itself is valuable.

If you are ever worried a friend is having thoughts of suicide, ask directly. Research consistently shows that asking does not increase risk — it often brings relief.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- You do not need to be in crisis to deserve support — reaching out early is wise.
- Signs it is time to reach out: persistent low mood or anxiety for two-plus weeks, impact on daily life, not improving with your own efforts.
- Support exists on a spectrum: a trusted person, a doctor, talking therapies (CBT, counselling, ACT), helplines, and apps.
- Therapy is an active, evidence-based skill-building process — not a sign of weakness.
- Stigma is built on myths; the reality is that mental health conditions are common, real, and highly treatable.
- Supporting a friend well means listening, staying present, and gently encouraging professional help when appropriate.
- Building a small, consistent mind-care habit through the cue-routine-reward loop is one of the most practical things anyone can do.

</Step>
