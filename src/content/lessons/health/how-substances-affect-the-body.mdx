Humans have used substances that alter the mind — alcohol, caffeine, plant-based medicines, recreational drugs — for as long as recorded history. From a health perspective, the interesting question is not *why* but *how*: what exactly do these substances do inside the body, and why does more of a substance not simply mean more benefit?

</Step>

<Step title="Psychoactive substances and the nervous system" kind="explain">

A **psychoactive substance** is any chemical that crosses the blood-brain barrier and changes the way the brain works — altering mood, perception, cognition, or behaviour. The brain communicates via chemical messengers called **neurotransmitters**: dopamine, serotonin, GABA, glutamate, noradrenaline, and many others.

Psychoactive substances work by hijacking this system:

- **Mimicking neurotransmitters** — fitting into their receptors and switching them on (e.g., heroin mimics the body's own pain-relieving endorphins).
- **Blocking receptors** — preventing the natural signal from landing (some anaesthetics work this way).
- **Flooding the synapse** — blocking the reuptake of a neurotransmitter so it lingers and keeps firing (e.g., cocaine blocks dopamine reuptake, causing a surge).
- **Increasing or decreasing release** — making the sending neuron release more or less of its messenger.

The specific neurotransmitter targeted determines the *type* of effect; the dose determines the *size* of the effect — and how far into risk territory it goes.

</Step>

<Step title="Categories of psychoactive substances" kind="explain">

Substances are loosely grouped by their dominant effect on the central nervous system:

**Depressants** — slow down CNS activity; reduce anxiety and arousal.
Examples: alcohol, benzodiazepines (e.g., diazepam), opioids, cannabis (partially), GHB.

**Stimulants** — speed up CNS activity; increase alertness and energy.
Examples: nicotine, caffeine, amphetamines, cocaine, MDMA.

**Hallucinogens/Psychedelics** — distort perception, thought, and emotion.
Examples: LSD, psilocybin ("magic mushrooms"), mescaline.

**Dissociatives** — produce feelings of detachment from reality.
Examples: ketamine, nitrous oxide, PCP.

**Opioids** — bind to opioid receptors; reduce pain and produce euphoria.
Examples: morphine, codeine, heroin, oxycodone.

Many substances cross categories — cannabis, for instance, has depressant, mild psychedelic, and even some stimulant properties depending on dose and strain.

</Step>

<Step title="The dose–response relationship" kind="interactive">

One of the most fundamental principles in pharmacology and toxicology is: **the dose makes the poison** — a saying often attributed to Paracelsus in the 16th century. Any substance, including water, vitamin D, or paracetamol, becomes harmful at a high enough dose.

Explore how the dose–response curve works. Notice that every substance has a therapeutic or desired range sandwiched between "too little to work" and "too much to be safe." Tolerance shifts the whole curve, meaning a higher dose is needed for the same effect — but the toxic zone moves closer at the same time.

<DoseResponse />

This principle explains why there is no such thing as an inherently "safe" substance — only safe doses in appropriate contexts.

</Step>

<Step title="Your body systems under the influence" kind="interactive">

Psychoactive substances rarely affect only the brain. Because the nervous, circulatory, respiratory, and endocrine systems are all interconnected, substances ripple through the whole body.

<BodySystemMap />

Alcohol, for example, touches the nervous system (slowed reaction time), liver (metabolises it), heart (raised heart rate), kidneys (increased urine output), and stomach (irritation). Understanding which systems are affected — and at what doses — is key to understanding harm and the value of guidelines.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Psychoactive substances** alter brain function by mimicking, blocking, flooding, or modulating neurotransmitter systems.
- **Broad categories**: depressants (slow CNS), stimulants (speed CNS), hallucinogens (distort perception), opioids (pain/euphoria), and dissociatives.
- **The dose–response curve** applies to every substance — desired effects, side effects, and toxicity each occupy a different dose range.
- **Tolerance** shifts the curve: repeated use means needing more for the same effect, while the toxic range moves closer.
- Substances affect **multiple body systems** simultaneously, not just the brain.

</Step>
