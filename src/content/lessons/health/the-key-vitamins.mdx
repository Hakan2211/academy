<Step title="Why some vitamins made history" kind="explain">

Before vitamins were discovered, entire populations died of diseases we now know were just deficiencies. Sailors died of scurvy (Vitamin C deficiency) on long voyages. Children in northern regions were crippled by rickets (Vitamin D deficiency). Mothers died of haemorrhage because Vitamin K was unknown. Millions in South and East Asia went blind from Vitamin A deficiency.

These stories show that specific molecules, each needed in tiny amounts, are not optional. Their absence is catastrophic. The same logic applies today — most of us do not get deficiency diseases, but chronic low intake of certain vitamins quietly degrades function long before a clinical diagnosis appears.

Here we survey the vitamins that matter most clinically and in everyday health.

</Step>

<Step title="The headline vitamins" kind="explain">

**Vitamin A (retinol)** supports vision (especially in low light), immune defence, and the integrity of skin and mucous membranes. Night blindness is the classic early sign of deficiency. Found in liver, eggs, and orange/yellow produce.

**Vitamin C (ascorbic acid)** builds and repairs collagen — the structural protein in skin, tendons, blood vessels, and bone. It also acts as an antioxidant and boosts immune function. Deficiency → **scurvy**: bleeding gums, slow wound healing, and eventually death. Found abundantly in fresh fruit and vegetables.

**Vitamin D (calciferol)** regulates calcium and phosphate absorption, making strong bones possible. Uniquely, most of our Vitamin D is synthesised in the skin on exposure to UVB sunlight — food alone rarely covers needs. Deficiency → **rickets** (children) and **osteomalacia** (adults). Found in oily fish and fortified foods.

**B vitamins** are a family of eight water-soluble vitamins, all involved in energy metabolism (converting food to ATP). The most clinically significant are:
- **B12 (cobalamin)** — red blood cell formation and nerve insulation. Deficiency causes megaloblastic anaemia and neurological damage. Found only in animal foods, making supplementation essential for vegans.
- **Folate (B9)** — DNA synthesis and cell division. Critical in early pregnancy: low folate before conception causes neural tube defects (spina bifida).

<Callout type="warn">
Fat-soluble vitamins (A, D, E, K) can accumulate in body fat to toxic levels with excess supplementation. Hypervitaminosis A causes liver damage and birth defects. Hypervitaminosis D causes dangerous calcium overload. "Natural" is not the same as safe at high doses.
</Callout>

<Step title="More isn't always better" kind="explain">

A common error is treating vitamins like fuel: more in = more health. This is wrong.

For **water-soluble vitamins**, the body discards most of what it cannot use today. High-dose Vitamin C supplements (above ~2 g/day) can cause kidney stones. High-dose B6 causes peripheral neuropathy (nerve damage in hands and feet) with long-term excess.

For **fat-soluble vitamins**, the accumulation risk is real:
- **Vitamin A toxicity** from supplements (not food): headache, nausea, bone pain, liver damage, and serious birth defects if taken during pregnancy.
- **Vitamin D toxicity** from supplements: hypercalcaemia — nausea, weakness, kidney stones, and heart arrhythmia.

The evidence for supplements in people without a diagnosed deficiency is generally weak. A varied, whole-food diet covers needs for most people. Exceptions include: Vitamin D in low-sunlight regions, B12 for vegans, and folate before and during early pregnancy.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Vitamin A** → vision and immunity; deficiency causes night blindness.
- **Vitamin C** → collagen and antioxidant defence; deficiency causes scurvy.
- **Vitamin D** → calcium absorption and bone strength; deficiency causes rickets/osteomalacia; sunlight is the main source.
- **Vitamin B12** → red blood cells and nerve insulation; only in animal foods — vegans must supplement.
- **Folate (B9)** → DNA synthesis; critical before and during pregnancy to prevent neural tube defects.
- **More is not always better** — fat-soluble vitamins accumulate and can become toxic; high-dose supplements carry real risks even for water-soluble vitamins.

</Step>
