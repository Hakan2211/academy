<Step title="The living scaffold" kind="explain">

Bone looks inert — hard, white, solid. But living bone is anything but static. It's a dynamic tissue that continuously remodels itself, houses blood-cell factories in its marrow, stores about 99% of the body's calcium, and adapts its density in response to the loads placed on it.

The adult skeleton has **206 bones** serving several overlapping roles:

- **Support** — the skeleton holds you upright against gravity.
- **Protection** — the skull shields the brain; the ribcage guards the heart and lungs; the vertebral column encases the spinal cord.
- **Movement** — bones act as rigid levers that muscles pull on.
- **Mineral storage** — calcium and phosphate are held in bone and released when blood levels drop.
- **Blood cell production** — red and white blood cells are made in red bone marrow (mainly in the spine, pelvis, and ends of long bones).

</Step>

<Step title="How muscles move bones" kind="explain">

Muscles generate force by contracting — shortening their length. They can only *pull*, never push. This means most movements require muscles arranged in **opposing pairs** across a joint:

- **Biceps** (front of upper arm) flexes the elbow — pulling the forearm up.
- **Triceps** (back of upper arm) extends the elbow — pulling the forearm down.

When one contracts, the other relaxes. This antagonistic arrangement gives fine control over speed and position.

Muscles attach to bones via **tendons** (tough, fibrous cords). Bones connect to other bones at **joints**, stabilised by **ligaments**. Joints come in several designs — ball-and-socket (shoulder, hip) for maximum range, hinge (knee, elbow) for powerful single-plane movement, and pivot (top of neck) for rotation.

</Step>

<Step title="Keeping bones and muscles healthy" kind="explain">

Both bone and muscle follow a simple principle: **use it or lose it**. Under-used bone loses density (osteoporosis); under-used muscle shrinks (atrophy). The good news is that exercise reverses both — and the effects are measurable.

For **bones**:

- Weight-bearing exercise (walking, running, dancing, resistance training) stimulates bone to deposit new mineral — the skeleton literally adapts to become stronger under load.
- Calcium (dairy, leafy greens, fortified foods) and vitamin D (sunlight, oily fish, supplements) are the raw materials.
- Bone density peaks in your late 20s, then slowly declines — building a dense peak early pays dividends for life.

For **muscles**:

- Resistance training (weights, bodyweight exercises) triggers muscle fibres to grow thicker and stronger — a process called hypertrophy.
- Adequate protein (roughly 1.2–2 g per kg body weight for active people) provides the amino acids muscles use to repair and grow.
- Stretching and mobility work maintain range of motion and reduce injury risk.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- The skeleton provides **support, protection, movement, mineral storage, and blood-cell production**.
- Muscles can only **pull** (contract/shorten) — movement in both directions requires **antagonistic pairs**.
- Muscles attach to bones via **tendons**; bones connect to bones via **ligaments** at **joints**.
- Both bone and muscle follow "use it or lose it": **weight-bearing exercise** builds bone; **resistance training** builds muscle.
- Bone density peaks in the late 20s — building it early through exercise and adequate calcium/vitamin D pays off decades later.

</Step>
