<Step title="Why intensity matters" kind="explain">

Not all exercise is equally effective — and more intense isn't always better. The same 30 minutes of activity can be a gentle stroll that barely raises your pulse, or a hard interval run that leaves you breathless. Each has a different effect on the body.

**Intensity** describes how hard your cardiovascular system is working. It determines:
- Which energy systems are used (aerobic vs. anaerobic)
- How much cardiovascular stress is placed on the heart and lungs
- How many calories are burned and how quickly
- How quickly you fatigue and how long you can sustain the effort
- The specific training adaptations your body makes

Getting the intensity right for your goals — not too easy, not too hard — is the core skill of effective exercise programming.

</Step>

<Step title="Maximum heart rate and target zones" kind="explain">

Your heart rate (HR) is the most accessible proxy for exercise intensity. As work increases, the heart beats faster to pump more oxygenated blood to working muscles.

The standard reference point is **maximum heart rate (max HR)** — the fastest your heart can beat. It declines with age. The classic population-level estimate is:

<Formula tex="\text{Max HR} \approx 220 - \text{age (years)}" block />

A 30-year-old has an estimated max HR of 190 bpm; a 50-year-old, 170 bpm.

From max HR, two key **target zones** are defined:

- **Moderate intensity: 50–70% of max HR.** You feel your heart working but can hold a conversation. This zone builds aerobic base and is appropriate for most everyday exercise.
- **Vigorous intensity: 70–85% of max HR.** Harder effort — talking is difficult, sentences are short. More efficient at building cardiovascular fitness per minute spent.

Both zones have health benefits. Vigorous activity counts double toward weekly guidelines because it achieves more in less time.

</Step>

<Step title="The talk test — no equipment needed" kind="explain">

If you don't have a heart rate monitor, the **talk test** gives a surprisingly accurate read of intensity:

- **Light:** You can sing. Heart rate is well below 50% of max.
- **Moderate:** You can speak in complete sentences but wouldn't want to sing. Classic aerobic zone (50–70%).
- **Vigorous:** You can say a few words before needing a breath. You're above 70% of max.
- **Maximum:** Speaking is nearly impossible. Heart rate is near its ceiling.

The talk test works because breathing rate is tightly coupled to cardiovascular demand — you're essentially measuring the same thing as HR but through how often you need to breathe.

<Callout type="tip">
A practical moderate-intensity check: hold a conversation with a training partner. If you can speak but would struggle to hold a long monologue, you're in the moderate zone.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="The FITT principle" kind="explain">

Knowing your zones is useful — but how often should you exercise? How long? At what intensity? The **FITT principle** is the standard framework for answering those questions:

- **F — Frequency:** How many days per week? Current guidelines suggest at least 5 moderate days, or 3 vigorous days, or a mix.
- **I — Intensity:** How hard? Moderate (50–70% max HR) or vigorous (70–85%) depending on your fitness level and goals.
- **T — Time:** How long each session? Moderate sessions: aim for at least 30 minutes. Vigorous: 20+ minutes achieves comparable benefit.
- **T — Type:** What kind of exercise? The type should match your goals: cardio for heart health, resistance for muscle and bone, flexibility for mobility.

FITT gives you a language for designing and adjusting a programme. If progress stalls, you can systematically adjust frequency, intensity, or time rather than guessing.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Max HR ≈ 220 − age** (bpm) — a population-level estimate that declines with age.
- **Moderate zone: 50–70% of max HR** — can hold a conversation; builds aerobic base.
- **Vigorous zone: 70–85% of max HR** — short sentences; more efficient per minute.
- The **talk test** estimates intensity without equipment: moderate = full sentences; vigorous = a few words.
- The **FITT principle** (Frequency, Intensity, Time, Type) is the framework for designing and adjusting any exercise programme.

</Step>
