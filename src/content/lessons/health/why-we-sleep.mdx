<Step title="Sleep is not doing nothing" kind="explain">

It might look like eight hours of idleness, but sleep is one of the most biologically active things your body does. Almost every organ and system uses the overnight window for critical maintenance that simply cannot happen while you are awake and burning energy.

Here is a snapshot of what is happening while you sleep:

- **Physical restoration** — muscle tissue is repaired, growth hormone is released, the immune system ramps up to fight pathogens.
- **Memory consolidation** — the brain replays the day's experiences, transferring information from short-term to long-term storage and pruning connections that are no longer needed.
- **Glymphatic clearance** — a drainage system in the brain flushes out metabolic waste, including amyloid-beta, a protein linked to Alzheimer's disease. This system is almost inactive when you are awake.
- **Hormonal regulation** — appetite hormones (leptin and ghrelin), stress hormones (cortisol), and blood-sugar regulation all reset overnight.

</Step>

<Step title="The architecture of a night's sleep" kind="explain">

Sleep is not a single steady state. Your brain cycles through distinct stages roughly every 90 minutes — four to five complete cycles in a full night. The pattern matters: deep, slow-wave sleep dominates the early part of the night and handles physical restoration, while REM (dreaming) sleep expands toward morning and handles memory and emotional processing.

That means cutting your night short does not merely reduce everything proportionally. **You lose the most REM first** — the late cycles closest to waking — which is exactly when your brain is most busy consolidating learning and regulating mood.

</Step>

<Step title="What sleep deprivation costs" kind="explain">

Modern culture treats lost sleep as a badge of productivity. The biology disagrees.

Even one night of sleeping five or six hours instead of seven to nine produces measurable costs:

- Reaction time slows to a level comparable with mild alcohol impairment.
- Working memory capacity shrinks — you can hold fewer items in mind at once.
- Emotional reactivity increases; the amygdala (threat-detection centre) becomes hyperactive while the prefrontal cortex (rational reasoning) becomes less able to regulate it.
- Calorie intake the next day typically rises by 300–500 kcal as appetite hormones shift.

Chronic short sleep (six hours or less, most nights) is associated with elevated risk of type 2 diabetes, cardiovascular disease, obesity, depression, and impaired immune function. The catch: research consistently shows that sleep-deprived people are **poor judges of their own impairment** — they adapt to feeling tired and no longer notice how degraded their performance has become.

<Callout type="info">
The glymphatic system's waste-clearance function is one reason why adequate sleep may matter for long-term brain health. Accumulation of amyloid-beta, which sleep helps clear, is a hallmark of Alzheimer's pathology.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- Sleep is a biologically active state: the body restores tissue, consolidates memory, clears brain waste (glymphatic system), and resets hormones.
- A full night contains **4–5 cycles of ~90 minutes** each, moving through N1, N2, N3 deep sleep, and REM.
- Deep sleep dominates early cycles; **REM expands toward morning** — so short nights cut REM disproportionately.
- Sleep deprivation impairs reaction time, memory, emotional regulation, appetite control, and immune function.
- People are often unaware of their own impairment — chronic tired becomes the new normal.

</Step>
