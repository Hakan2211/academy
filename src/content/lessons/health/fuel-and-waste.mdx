<Step title="Food is more than fuel" kind="explain">

Every cell in your body is built from, and powered by, molecules that began as food on your plate. Digestion is the process that transforms a ham sandwich into amino acids your muscles can use, glucose your brain can burn, and fatty acids your cells can store or build with. It also disposes of what can't be used.

The digestive system is essentially a long tube — about 9 metres from mouth to anus — lined with specialised machinery that breaks food down in stages. Alongside it, two major accessory organs — the **liver** and **pancreas** — pour in digestive chemicals and later manage what gets absorbed.

</Step>

<Step title="The journey through the gut" kind="explain">

Food moves through distinct zones, each with a job:

- **Mouth** — teeth grind food into small pieces; saliva adds water and amylase (an enzyme that begins breaking down starch).
- **Stomach** — muscular walls churn food with hydrochloric acid and pepsin (a protein-digesting enzyme), turning it into a semi-liquid called chyme. Most bacteria are killed here.
- **Small intestine** (about 6 m) — the main absorption zone. Bile from the liver emulsifies fats; enzymes from the pancreas break carbohydrates, proteins, and fats into their smallest units. These nutrients cross the intestinal wall into the bloodstream and lymphatic system.
- **Large intestine** (about 1.5 m) — absorbs water and minerals from the undigested material; compacts the waste; hosts trillions of gut bacteria (the **microbiome**) that ferment fibre and produce some vitamins.
- **Rectum & anus** — store and expel solid waste.

Most of the nutrition you get from a meal is absorbed in the small intestine within a few hours of eating.

</Step>

<Step title="The liver: the body's chemical factory" kind="explain">

After the small intestine absorbs nutrients, the blood carries them straight to the **liver** via the hepatic portal vein — not to the rest of the body yet. The liver acts as a quality-control and processing hub:

- It **stores glucose** as glycogen when blood sugar is high, and releases it when blood sugar falls.
- It **converts amino acids** from protein into glucose or fat (and produces urea as a waste product).
- It **processes fats** and packages them for transport around the body.
- It **detoxifies** drugs, alcohol, and other chemicals, breaking them down into safer forms.
- It produces **bile**, which is stored in the gallbladder and released into the small intestine to help digest fats.

The liver performs over 500 distinct functions and is so essential that no artificial machine can fully replace it — which is why liver disease is so serious.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- Digestion is a staged process: mouth → stomach → small intestine → large intestine → waste.
- **Small intestine** is the main absorption zone — nutrients cross into blood and lymph here.
- The **liver** receives absorbed nutrients first and acts as a chemical factory: storing glucose, processing fats and proteins, and detoxifying substances.
- The **large intestine** absorbs water and hosts the gut microbiome.
- **Kidneys** filter the blood around 400 times per day, removing urea and other waste as urine while retaining what the body needs.

</Step>
