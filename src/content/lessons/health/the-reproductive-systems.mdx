The reproductive systems are the organs and structures involved in producing offspring. Like all body systems, they develop gradually and are driven by hormones — chemical messengers that coordinate growth and function throughout the body.

Understanding reproductive anatomy is a foundation for understanding contraception, menstrual health, and sexual health more broadly. The language used here refers to structures, not identities — people's bodies vary, and health knowledge benefits everyone.

</Step>

<Step title="The female reproductive system" kind="explain">

The female reproductive system contains the following key structures:

- **Ovaries (×2):** Produce eggs (ova) and secrete the sex hormones oestrogen and progesterone. At birth, ovaries already contain all the eggs a person will ever have — roughly one to two million, though most degenerate before puberty.
- **Fallopian tubes (×2):** Transport eggs from an ovary toward the uterus. Fertilisation — the meeting of egg and sperm — typically occurs in the fallopian tube.
- **Uterus:** A thick-walled, pear-shaped muscular organ where a fertilised egg implants and a pregnancy develops.
- **Cervix:** The narrow lower portion of the uterus, which opens into the vagina and produces mucus that changes across the menstrual cycle.
- **Endometrium:** The inner lining of the uterus. It thickens each cycle and is shed as a period if no pregnancy occurs.
- **Vagina:** A muscular canal connecting the cervix to the outside of the body — the birth canal during labour.

Puberty in people assigned female at birth is driven by rising **oestrogen** from the ovaries, prompted by the pituitary hormones **FSH** (follicle-stimulating hormone) and **LH** (luteinising hormone). Typical changes include breast development, widening of the hips, growth of pubic and underarm hair, and the start of menstrual cycles. Puberty usually begins between ages 8 and 13, though timing varies widely and is influenced by genetics and body composition.

</Step>

<Step title="The male reproductive system" kind="explain">

The male reproductive system's main structures include:

- **Testes (×2):** Produce sperm continuously (from puberty) and secrete testosterone. They are held outside the body in the scrotum because sperm production requires a temperature a few degrees below normal body temperature.
- **Epididymis:** A coiled tube behind each testis where sperm mature and are stored for up to several weeks.
- **Vas deferens:** A muscular duct that carries mature sperm from the epididymis toward the urethra during ejaculation.
- **Seminal vesicles and prostate gland:** Accessory glands that add fluid to sperm to form semen, providing fructose for energy and an alkaline environment to protect sperm.
- **Urethra:** A shared tube for urine and semen (not simultaneously) that opens at the tip of the penis.

Puberty in people assigned male at birth is driven by rising **testosterone** from the testes. Typical changes include growth of the testes and penis, development of pubic and facial hair, deepening of the voice, increases in muscle mass and height, and the start of sperm production. Puberty usually begins between ages 9 and 14.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- The **female reproductive system** includes ovaries (eggs + oestrogen/progesterone), fallopian tubes, uterus, cervix, and vagina. The endometrium thickens each cycle and sheds as a period.
- The **male reproductive system** includes testes (sperm + testosterone), epididymis, vas deferens, seminal vesicles, prostate, and urethra.
- **Puberty** is driven by hormones: oestrogen drives female puberty; testosterone drives male puberty — both triggered by the pituitary hormones FSH and LH.
- Timing of puberty varies widely and is influenced by genetics and body composition.
- Understanding anatomy is a foundation for understanding menstrual health, contraception, and sexual health.

</Step>
