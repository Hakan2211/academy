<Step title="Not all exercise is the same" kind="explain">

When most people picture "exercise," they picture either running or lifting weights. But the body has four distinct physical capacities that need to be trained — and each type of exercise develops different ones.

Exercise scientists organise physical activity into four main categories:

1. **Cardiorespiratory (aerobic) fitness** — the ability of the heart, lungs, and blood vessels to sustain effort over time.
2. **Muscular strength and endurance** — the ability of muscles to produce force and sustain it.
3. **Flexibility and mobility** — the range of motion available at your joints.
4. **Balance and stability** — the ability to control body position and resist falling.

Most popular workout routines focus heavily on one or two and ignore the others. A well-designed programme touches all four — because they interact and support each other.

</Step>

<Step title="Cardio: training your engine" kind="explain">

Cardiovascular or *aerobic* exercise means any sustained activity that raises your heart rate and keeps it elevated — running, cycling, swimming, dancing, brisk walking.

The word "aerobic" means "with oxygen": your muscles are using the aerobic energy pathway, which is efficient, sustainable, and relies on a continuous supply of oxygen from the heart and lungs.

**What regular cardio does:**
- Strengthens the heart muscle (each beat pumps more blood — *cardiac output* increases)
- Grows new capillaries in muscle tissue (more oxygen delivery)
- Lowers resting blood pressure and resting heart rate
- Improves insulin sensitivity (blood glucose management)
- Boosts mood, reduces anxiety, improves sleep quality

Even moderate amounts — say 30 minutes of brisk walking five days a week — produce measurable cardiovascular benefits.

</Step>

<Step title="Strength: training your frame" kind="explain">

Resistance or strength training means working muscles against a load — free weights, machines, bodyweight exercises, or resistance bands. The load forces muscle fibres to contract near their maximum, triggering micro-damage that heals stronger than before (a process called *hypertrophy* for muscle growth, or *neuromuscular adaptation* for strength without size changes).

**What regular strength training does:**
- Increases muscle mass and strength
- Increases bone density (bone remodels under load)
- Raises resting metabolic rate (more muscle burns more calories at rest)
- Improves posture and reduces injury risk
- Protects against age-related muscle loss (*sarcopenia*), which begins around age 30

Health guidelines recommend strength training for all major muscle groups at least **twice per week** — not just for athletes, but for everyone.

</Step>

<Step title="Flexibility and balance: the often-neglected two" kind="explain">

**Flexibility and mobility** describe how freely your joints can move through their full range. Tight muscles and stiff joints don't just feel uncomfortable — they alter movement patterns, increasing injury risk during everything else you do. Regular stretching, yoga, and mobility work counteract the stiffness that builds from sitting and from strength training itself.

**Balance and stability** are often overlooked until a fall makes them impossible to ignore. Balance training strengthens the small stabilising muscles around joints and trains the cerebellum (the brain's movement-coordination centre). It is especially important with age — falls are the leading cause of injury-related death in adults over 65.

These two categories don't require a gym or special equipment. Ten minutes of stretching after any workout, or balance exercises done while waiting for the kettle, add up to meaningful improvement.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- Exercise has **four main types**: cardio (aerobic), strength/resistance, flexibility/mobility, and balance/stability.
- **Cardio** trains the heart and lungs, improves endurance and metabolic health.
- **Strength training** builds muscle and bone density; guards against sarcopenia and osteoporosis.
- **Flexibility** maintains joint range of motion and reduces injury risk.
- **Balance** strengthens stabilising muscles and the neuromuscular system — crucial for fall prevention.
- A complete routine **includes all four** — most people over-focus on one and neglect the rest.
