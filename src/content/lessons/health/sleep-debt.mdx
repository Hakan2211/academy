<Step title="The debt that compounds" kind="explain">

Sleep is not like a bank account where missed nights simply disappear. Every hour of short sleep adds to a running **sleep debt** — a physiological deficit that accumulates in the brain and body, driven by the build-up of adenosine (a chemical that promotes sleepiness) and the disruption of cellular repair processes.

The key numbers:
- Most adults need **7–9 hours** per night; teenagers need 8–10.
- Sleeping 6 hours/night for two weeks produces cognitive impairment **equivalent to two full nights of total sleep deprivation** — yet most people report feeling "only slightly tired."
- Sleeping 5 hours/night for one week leaves you with a ~21-hour deficit.

The insidious part is **adaptation**: after a few weeks of short sleep, people no longer feel as tired as they actually are. They lose access to their own baseline for comparison and conclude they are managing fine — while performance tests tell a very different story.

<Step title="Can you repay it?" kind="explain">

The appeal of "catching up on sleep on the weekend" is understandable — but the biology is less forgiving than the idea suggests.

**What recovery sleep can do:**
- Reduce subjective sleepiness and some of the hormonal disruption.
- Partially restore performance on simple tasks.

**What it cannot do:**
- Fully reverse the cognitive impairment from a week of short sleep in a single weekend.
- Undo the metabolic effects (impaired insulin sensitivity, elevated cortisol) that accumulate with chronic restriction.

Research by Sigrid Veasey and colleagues showed that neurons damaged by extended sleep deprivation may not fully recover, and that some cellular damage is cumulative. The safest strategy is consistent adequate sleep — not accumulating debt and trying to repay it in bulk.

<Callout type="warn">
"Sleeping in" on the weekend — more than an hour later than your weekday wake time — also disrupts your circadian rhythm for the week ahead, producing a "social jet lag" that makes Monday morning harder, not easier.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Five things that wreck sleep" kind="explain">

Understanding what undermines sleep quality is half the battle:

**1. Caffeine — the adenosine blocker**
Caffeine works by blocking adenosine receptors. Its half-life is 5–7 hours — meaning half of a 3 pm coffee is still active at 9 pm, and a quarter remains at midnight. For people who metabolise caffeine slowly (a genetic variation in the CYP1A2 gene), the effect lasts even longer. The result: lighter, more fragmented sleep even if you fall asleep normally.

**2. Evening screens and blue light**
Covered in the body-clock lesson — blue-spectrum light delays melatonin onset by up to 3 hours, shifting the entire sleep window later. Evening screen use is now among the most common circadian disruptors in modern life.

**3. Alcohol**
Alcohol is a sedative that helps you fall asleep but fragments the second half of the night. It dramatically suppresses REM sleep and increases light-sleep arousals. The popular "nightcap" actually worsens sleep quality — more so as blood alcohol falls during the night.

**4. Irregular schedule**
Your circadian clock is a *precise* timing system. A variable wake time — especially sleeping in substantially on weekends — is the equivalent of flying between time zones twice a week. The clock cannot anchor, melatonin timing drifts, and sleep pressure is disrupted.

**5. Stress and hyperarousal**
The physiological arousal of chronic stress (elevated cortisol, a racing mind) is incompatible with the relaxed drowsiness needed for sleep onset. High bedtime cortisol keeps the brain in alert mode, making it harder to initiate sleep and easier to wake during the night.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Sleep debt accumulates** when you consistently sleep below your need (~7–9 h for adults); two weeks of 6-hour nights equals the impairment of two sleepless nights.
- People adapt to feeling tired and **underestimate their own impairment** — the subjective feeling does not track the objective performance loss.
- **Recovery sleep helps but cannot fully repay** chronic debt; cumulative cellular damage may be lasting.
- Weekend lie-ins beyond ~1 hour worsen the following week's sleep (social jet lag).
- Key sleep wreckers: **caffeine** (long half-life, still active at bedtime), **evening screens** (delay melatonin), **alcohol** (suppresses REM), **irregular schedule** (disrupts the clock), **chronic stress** (raises cortisol, maintains arousal).

</Step>
