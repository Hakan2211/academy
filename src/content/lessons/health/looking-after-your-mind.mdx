<Step title="Mental fitness is a practice" kind="explain">

Physical fitness does not happen by accident. You build it gradually, through repeated effort, and you maintain it with ongoing practice. Mental fitness works the same way.

There is now strong evidence that certain everyday habits **protect mental wellbeing**, reduce the risk of anxiety and depression, and help us recover from setbacks more quickly. These are not cures for mental illness — they are the equivalent of regular exercise and a balanced diet for the mind.

The five ways to wellbeing (developed from large-scale research by the New Economics Foundation) are:

1. **Connect** — nurture relationships
2. **Be active** — move your body
3. **Take notice** — pay attention to the present
4. **Keep learning** — stay curious
5. **Give** — help others

We will explore these and a few more. The goal is not to do all of them perfectly — even one or two, practised consistently, make a real and measurable difference.

</Step>

<Step title="A closer look at each factor" kind="explain">

**Social connection** is one of the strongest predictors of both mental and physical health. Loneliness has health effects comparable to smoking 15 cigarettes a day. Even one close, trusting relationship is profoundly protective.

**Movement** releases endorphins and reduces cortisol. Even a 20-minute walk has measurable effects on mood. The type and intensity matter less than consistency — finding movement you actually enjoy is the key.

**Sleep** is when the brain consolidates memory, clears metabolic waste, and regulates emotion. Poor sleep worsens anxiety, impairs decision-making, and lowers mood. Good sleep is one of the highest-leverage habits available.

**Sense of meaning** — having a "why" — buffers against stress and low mood. This can come from work, relationships, creativity, faith, service, or anything that connects you to something larger than your daily concerns.

**Time in nature** reduces activity in the part of the brain associated with rumination (the medial prefrontal cortex) and lowers cortisol. Even urban green space counts.

**Helping others** activates reward circuits in the brain — giving is neurologically pleasurable. Volunteering and small acts of kindness both boost wellbeing for the giver as well as the recipient.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- Mental fitness is built gradually through consistent habits, just like physical fitness.
- The core protective factors are: social connection, movement, consistent sleep, sense of meaning, time in nature, and helping others.
- Limiting passive scrolling and doomscrolling also protects mood.
- You do not need all of these at once — even one or two, practised regularly, make a meaningful difference.
- Controlled breathing is a fast, direct tool for calming the nervous system in moments of stress.
