<Step title="How your body gets stronger" kind="explain">

After a challenging workout, your muscles don't immediately emerge stronger. The opposite happens first: they're mildly damaged — microscopic tears in muscle fibres trigger inflammation and soreness. This is the familiar DOMS (delayed onset muscle soreness) that peaks 24–48 hours after exercise.

But during the following hours and days, something remarkable happens. The body repairs those fibres and makes them **slightly thicker and stronger** than before — an adaptation called *hypertrophy* (for size) or *neural adaptation* (for strength before muscle mass has grown much). The musculoskeletal system has concluded: "we were challenged beyond our current capacity, so we need to be able to handle that load next time."

This process — **stress → micro-damage → repair → stronger** — is the fundamental mechanism behind all strength and endurance training. It applies to:

- Muscles (hypertrophy and strength)
- Bones (increased density under load)
- Cardiovascular system (heart becomes a more powerful pump with aerobic training)
- Tendons and ligaments (gradually strengthen to support greater loads)

</Step>

<Step title="Progressive overload: the key principle" kind="explain">

The body only adapts to a *new* challenge. Once it has adapted to a given load, doing the same thing produces no further improvement — you've plateaued.

**Progressive overload** is the principle of gradually increasing the training stimulus over time so the body keeps adapting. You can increase:

- **Load** — heavier weights or higher resistance
- **Volume** — more sets, more repetitions, or more sessions per week
- **Difficulty** — harder exercise variations (e.g. progression from knee push-ups → full push-ups → decline push-ups)
- **Reduced rest** — shorter recovery between sets

A common rule of thumb: increase load by no more than **5–10% per week** for strength training. This is enough to keep providing a new stimulus, but small enough to be manageable and safe.

</Step>

<Step title="Why rest days are not optional" kind="explain">

Training is a controlled stressor. The adaptation (getting stronger, fitter) happens in the gaps between sessions. Skipping rest turns beneficial stress into accumulating damage.

**Physiological reasons rest is essential:**

- **Muscle protein synthesis** (the repair and growth process) peaks 24–48 h after training and requires adequate sleep, protein, and recovery time.
- **Glycogen replenishment** — muscles store glucose as glycogen for energy. Depleted glycogen takes 24–48 h to fully restore, even with good nutrition.
- **Nervous system recovery** — heavy lifting is not just muscular; the nervous system is under significant stress and needs recovery time to maintain coordination and power output.
- **Connective tissue** — tendons and ligaments adapt more slowly than muscles. Overloading them before they've recovered is a common source of injury.

Most training programmes build in at least **1–2 rest or light activity days per week**. Beginners often need more rest than advanced athletes because the body's adaptation mechanisms are less efficient early on.

<Callout type="info">
"Active recovery" — a light walk, gentle swimming, or easy cycling — on rest days maintains blood flow and can actually speed muscle repair compared with complete inactivity.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="The danger of doing too much too soon" kind="explain">

One of the most common training mistakes — especially among motivated beginners — is dramatically increasing volume or intensity in the first weeks. It feels productive. It usually backfires.

**Overtraining syndrome** occurs when training load consistently exceeds the body's ability to recover. Signs include:

- Declining performance (getting weaker or slower despite training hard)
- Persistent fatigue that doesn't resolve with sleep
- Increased injury rates, especially in tendons and joints
- Mood disturbance, irritability, and loss of motivation
- Suppressed immune function (frequent illness)

This is why experienced coaches emphasise patience. A beginner who starts with three moderate sessions a week, increases gradually, and trains consistently for a year will almost always outperform someone who trains furiously for six weeks and then gets injured or burns out.

The mantra: **gradual and consistent beats intense and unsustainable.**

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- Muscles (and bones, tendons, and the cardiovascular system) grow stronger via a **stress → micro-damage → repair → stronger** cycle.
- **Progressive overload** — gradually increasing load, volume, or difficulty — is the mechanism that keeps the body adapting. Aim for ≤5–10% increase per week.
- **Fitness gains happen during recovery**, not during the workout itself. Rest days are when adaptation occurs.
- **Overtraining** (doing too much too soon) leads to declining performance, injury, fatigue, and burnout — the opposite of the goal.
- The sustainable path: **gradual, consistent progress** over months and years beats intense short bursts.

</Step>
