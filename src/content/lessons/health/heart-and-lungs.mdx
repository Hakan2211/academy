<Step title="Two systems, one mission" kind="explain">

Every living cell in your body needs a constant supply of oxygen and a way to get rid of carbon dioxide — the waste gas produced when cells make energy. Two systems share this job so tightly that they're often discussed together:

- The **cardiovascular (circulatory) system** — the heart plus roughly 100,000 km of blood vessels — acts as the delivery network.
- The **respiratory system** — lungs, airways, and the diaphragm — acts as the gas exchange station where oxygen enters the blood and CO₂ leaves.

Neither is useful without the other. The lungs can load oxygen into the blood, but without the heart pumping that blood around, the oxygen never arrives at your muscles. The heart can pump all day, but without the lungs refreshing the blood with oxygen, it quickly runs out of fuel to deliver.

</Step>

<Step title="The journey of a breath" kind="explain">

Here is what happens between one breath and the next:

1. **Inhale** — the diaphragm contracts and pulls down, expanding the chest. Air rushes in through the nose and mouth, down the trachea, into branching bronchi, and reaches the tiny air sacs called **alveoli** in the lungs.
2. **Gas exchange at the alveoli** — oxygen diffuses across the paper-thin alveolar walls into surrounding capillaries, where it binds to haemoglobin in red blood cells. CO₂ moves the other way — out of the blood and into the alveolar air.
3. **Pulmonary circulation** — freshly oxygenated blood travels from the lungs to the **left side of the heart**, which pumps it out through the aorta to the rest of the body.
4. **Systemic circulation** — arteries carry oxygenated blood to every tissue. Cells use the oxygen and release CO₂ into the blood. Deoxygenated blood returns through veins to the **right side of the heart**, which pumps it back to the lungs to reload.
5. **Exhale** — the diaphragm relaxes, the chest recoils, and CO₂-laden air is pushed out.

The heart runs this circuit twice: once through the lungs (pulmonary circuit), once through the body (systemic circuit).

</Step>

<Step title="See how activity changes both rates" kind="interactive">

Heart rate and breathing rate always rise and fall together — because the demand for oxygen and the production of CO₂ go hand in hand with how hard muscles are working.

<HeartRateDemo />

At rest, the heart beats about 60–70 times a minute and you breathe about 12–16 times. During hard exercise, heart rate can exceed 150 bpm and breathing can double — all to keep up with muscles burning through oxygen many times faster than at rest.

</Step>

<Step title="Why they're central to health" kind="explain">

The cardiovascular system is the most common site of serious disease in wealthy nations — heart disease and stroke together remain the world's leading causes of death. But it's also one of the most *responsive* to lifestyle:

- **Regular aerobic exercise** (brisk walking, cycling, swimming) strengthens the heart muscle, lowers resting heart rate, and keeps blood vessels elastic and clear.
- **Diet** matters too — eating less saturated fat, salt, and ultra-processed food lowers blood pressure and reduces arterial plaque.
- **Not smoking** is the single biggest lung-and-heart protection you can make. Cigarette smoke damages the alveolar walls, narrows blood vessels, and accelerates arterial disease.
- **Staying at a healthy weight** reduces the workload on both systems.

A strong heart doesn't mean a heart that never rests — it means a heart that pumps more blood per beat at rest, so it *doesn't need* to beat as often. Trained athletes can have resting heart rates below 50 bpm because their hearts are so efficient.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- The **cardiovascular** and **respiratory** systems work together to deliver oxygen and remove CO₂.
- Gas exchange happens at the **alveoli**: O₂ enters blood, CO₂ leaves.
- The heart runs two circuits: **pulmonary** (heart → lungs → heart) and **systemic** (heart → body → heart).
- Heart rate and breathing rate **rise together** during exercise to meet increased oxygen demand.
- Aerobic exercise, a good diet, not smoking, and healthy weight are the foundations of cardiovascular health.

</Step>
