<Step title="Why willpower alone doesn't work" kind="explain">

Most people try to change health behaviour through willpower — deciding harder, trying harder. It works briefly, then collapses. The reason is not weakness of character. It is neuroscience.

The prefrontal cortex, which handles deliberate decisions, is a finite resource. It depletes under stress, fatigue, and decision overload — exactly the conditions in which we most need to make healthy choices. Willpower cannot be the primary mechanism for lasting behaviour change.

What actually works, according to decades of behaviour-change research, is **designing the environment** and **working with the brain's habit system**, not against it.

Charles Duhigg's *The Power of Habit* (drawing on MIT neuroscience research) describes the habit loop: a **cue** triggers a **routine**, which delivers a **reward**. Once the loop is run enough times, the basal ganglia automates it — it no longer requires deliberate thought. That is the goal: making healthy behaviour automatic.

</Step>

<Step title="Starting small and stacking" kind="explain">

BJ Fogg's research at Stanford identified a key insight: **behaviour change fails when the target behaviour is too large.** The brain resists big changes. It readily accepts small ones.

The strategy of **starting tiny** — smaller than feels meaningful — bypasses this resistance. A "two-minute rule" (start with just two minutes of any new habit) takes the psychological threat out of beginning. You can always do more. The critical thing is showing up.

**Habit stacking** (a term from James Clear's *Atomic Habits*) uses existing habits as cue anchors for new ones:

> "After [current habit], I will [new tiny habit]."

Examples:
- After I make my morning coffee, I will do five minutes of stretching.
- After I sit down at my desk, I will write one sentence in my journal.
- After I brush my teeth, I will take my vitamins.

The existing habit provides the cue reliably — no separate reminder system needed.

<Callout type="tip">
Design your environment to make the healthy choice the easy choice. Want to eat more fruit? Put it on the counter, not in the fridge drawer. Want to exercise in the morning? Put your trainers by the bed. Friction is the enemy of habit — remove it.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Identity-based habits" kind="explain">

James Clear's most powerful insight in *Atomic Habits*: the most durable habit change is **identity-based**, not outcome-based.

- **Outcome-based:** "I want to lose 5 kg."
- **Identity-based:** "I am someone who moves their body every day."

The difference matters. An outcome goal gives you a destination to reach and then stop. An identity goal is ongoing — it changes how you make decisions. When a choice arises, the question becomes: *"What would someone like me do?"*

This is not self-delusion. Every action you take is a vote for the kind of person you are. Running once doesn't make you a runner. Running a hundred times does — and, crucially, thinking of yourself as a runner makes you more likely to keep running.

The pathway is:
1. Decide the type of person you want to be.
2. Take tiny actions that are consistent with that identity.
3. Accumulate evidence (repetitions) until the identity feels true.

<Callout type="info">
You don't have to feel like a healthy person before acting like one. The feeling follows the behaviour, not the other way around. Start with the action; let the identity catch up.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Willpower depletes** under stress and fatigue — it cannot be the main mechanism for lasting health behaviour change.
- The **habit loop** (cue → routine → reward) automates behaviour over time, removing the need for deliberate effort.
- **Start tiny** — smaller than feels meaningful — to bypass the brain's resistance to change. You can always do more once started.
- **Habit stacking** anchors new habits onto existing ones ("After X, I will Y"), providing a reliable cue without a separate reminder system.
- **Design your environment** to make healthy choices the low-friction default.
- **Identity-based habits** are more durable than outcome-based goals: "I am someone who moves every day" outlasts "I want to lose 5 kg."

</Step>
