<Step title="A judgement-free space" kind="explain">

<Callout type="info">
This lesson approaches substance use with the same openness as any other health topic — factual, compassionate, and free of judgement. Whether you are here out of curiosity, concern for someone you know, or your own experience, this is a place for information. If you are affected by substance use — your own or someone else's — help exists and you deserve it. Reaching out is a sign of self-awareness, not weakness.
</Callout>

Most people who use substances — including alcohol, caffeine, and tobacco as well as others — do not develop addiction. But every substance carries some risk, and that risk is shaped by what you use, how much, how often, and the circumstances. This lesson is about understanding risk and making more informed decisions — without pretending the choices are simple or that the context does not matter.

</Step>

<Step title="The risk spectrum" kind="interactive">

No substance is completely without risk, and no use is uniformly dangerous. Risk depends on:

- **The substance itself** — its mechanism, potency, and how rapidly it causes tolerance or dependence.
- **Dose and frequency** — one cup of coffee is not the same as ten; one drink occasionally is not the same as ten drinks daily.
- **The individual** — genetics, mental health, age (adolescent brains are more vulnerable), and family history all modulate risk.
- **The context** — using alone, mixing substances, driving, or being in an unsafe environment all increase risk.

<Step title="Harm reduction as a principle" kind="explain">

**Harm reduction** is a public health approach that prioritises reducing the harms of substance use rather than demanding abstinence as the only acceptable goal. It is an evidence-based, non-judgemental framework used by health services worldwide.

Examples of harm-reduction thinking:

- **Low-risk drinking guidelines** — e.g., no more than 14 units/week (UK), spread over several days, with alcohol-free days. Not zero, but substantially lower risk than heavier use.
- **Never drink and drive** — a specific harm that is entirely avoidable.
- **Not mixing substances** — alcohol and benzodiazepines both depress the CNS; combined, the risk of respiratory depression rises dramatically.
- **Needle exchanges** — providing clean injecting equipment to people who use drugs reduces transmission of blood-borne viruses like HIV and hepatitis C, even if they continue using.
- **Supervised consumption sites** — allow people to use in a medically supervised environment, reducing overdose deaths.
- **Naloxone availability** — a fast-acting opioid-overdose reversal drug, increasingly available without prescription in many countries.

Harm reduction acknowledges the reality that some people will use substances regardless of legal status or social pressure. The goal is to keep them alive and healthier while pathways to treatment remain open.

</Step>

<Step title="Recognising when use has become a problem" kind="explain">

There is no bright line between use and a problem — it is a continuum. Some helpful questions to consider (for yourself or with concern for someone else):

**The CAGE questions** (originally designed for alcohol, applicable broadly):
- **C** — Have you ever felt you should **Cut** down?
- **A** — Have people **Annoyed** you by criticising your use?
- **G** — Have you ever felt **Guilty** about your use?
- **E** — Have you ever needed a drink or substance first thing in the morning (**Eye-opener**)?

Two or more "yes" answers suggest talking to a healthcare provider is worthwhile.

Other indicators that use may have become a problem:
- Using more than intended, or for longer than intended.
- Repeated unsuccessful attempts to cut down.
- Spending significant time obtaining, using, or recovering.
- Use interfering with work, relationships, or responsibilities.
- Continuing despite knowing it is causing harm.
- Experiencing craving or withdrawal.

Recognising a problem is the first and often hardest step. It does not mean you are broken — it means you have identified something worth addressing.

</Step>

<Step title="Getting help — and what works" kind="explain">

Addiction and problematic substance use are **treatable**. Outcomes improve substantially with support. What is available:

**Talking to a GP or doctor** — a good first step. GPs can assess severity, prescribe medications, and refer to specialist services. There is no obligation to report substance use to authorities; medical consultations are confidential.

**Medications:**
- For alcohol: naltrexone (reduces craving), acamprosate (supports abstinence), disulfiram (creates nausea if alcohol is consumed).
- For opioids: methadone and buprenorphine (opioid agonist therapies) are evidence-based and life-saving; they reduce illicit use and overdose risk dramatically.
- For nicotine: varenicline (Champix/Chantix), bupropion, and NRT (patches, gum, lozenges) all improve quit rates.

**Psychological and behavioural support:**
- Cognitive Behavioural Therapy (CBT) — addresses the thoughts and habits that maintain use.
- Motivational interviewing — helps explore ambivalence about change.
- Peer support groups — Alcoholics Anonymous, SMART Recovery, and many others provide community and accountability.

**Withdrawal management:** For alcohol and benzodiazepines in particular, sudden cessation can be medically dangerous. Supervised withdrawal ("detox") is available and much safer than stopping abruptly alone.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- Risk depends on **substance, dose, frequency, individual factors, and context** — it is not binary.
- **Harm reduction** is an evidence-based public health approach: reduce harms in practical ways without requiring abstinence.
- Key risk-reduction behaviours: following low-risk guidelines, never mixing CNS depressants, never driving impaired, using in safer contexts.
- The **CAGE questions** and other indicators help identify when use may have become problematic.
- Effective treatments exist: **medications** (naltrexone, buprenorphine, varenicline, NRT) and **psychological therapies** (CBT, motivational interviewing) both improve outcomes substantially.
- Supervised withdrawal is available and important for alcohol/benzodiazepine dependence.
- **Reaching out for help is a sign of self-awareness.** Recovery is possible — and common.

</Step>
