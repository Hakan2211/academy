<Step title="What minerals do" kind="explain">

Minerals are inorganic elements the body cannot make — they must come from food. Unlike vitamins, they cannot be destroyed by heat or oxygen, but they can be washed out of vegetables during boiling or excreted in sweat and urine if intake is too low.

The most important dietary minerals fall into two tiers:

**Macro-minerals** (needed in hundreds of milligrams per day): calcium, phosphorus, magnesium, sodium, potassium, chloride, sulphur.

**Trace minerals** (needed in micrograms to low milligrams): iron, iodine, zinc, selenium, fluoride, manganese, copper.

Each has specific, non-interchangeable roles. The body cannot substitute potassium for sodium, or zinc for iron. They are more like specialist tools than interchangeable fuel.

</Step>

<Step title="The five minerals you need to know" kind="explain">

**Iron** — the core of haemoglobin, the protein that carries oxygen in red blood cells. Without enough iron, red blood cells shrink and pale, carrying less oxygen — the condition called iron-deficiency anaemia (fatigue, pallor, shortness of breath, poor concentration). Women of reproductive age and vegetarians are at highest risk. Haem iron from meat is far more absorbable than non-haem iron from plants; Vitamin C eaten alongside plant iron dramatically boosts absorption.

**Calcium** — 99% of the body's calcium is in bone and teeth, providing structure. The remaining 1% is in blood, where it enables muscle contraction (including the heartbeat), nerve signalling, and clotting. Blood calcium is tightly regulated — if dietary intake is too low, the body dissolves bone to maintain it. Long-term low intake → osteoporosis.

**Sodium & potassium** — the great ionic partnership. Sodium dominates outside cells; potassium dominates inside. The balance between them drives nerve impulses and muscle contractions via the sodium-potassium pump. Most people eat far too much sodium (processed food) and too little potassium (not enough fruit and vegetables) — the combination raises blood pressure.

**Iodine** — one job: the thyroid gland uses iodine to make thyroid hormones (T3 and T4), which control metabolic rate. Deficiency in adults causes goitre (an enlarged thyroid gland attempting to capture more iodine) and hypothyroidism. In foetal and infant life, iodine deficiency can cause severe, irreversible intellectual disability (cretinism) — making it one of the most consequential single-nutrient deficiencies globally.

</Step>

<Step title="Water — the forgotten nutrient" kind="explain">

Water is technically a nutrient: you need it to survive and you must consume it. Yet it is often left off nutrient lists. It is:

- The **solvent** for every biochemical reaction in the body.
- The **transport medium** for blood (water is ~90% of plasma).
- The **coolant** — sweating is evaporative cooling, your body's primary heat-management system.
- A **lubricant** for joints and digestive surfaces.
- The **medium** for waste removal in urine.

Adults lose 2–3 litres of water per day just from breathing, urinating, and skin evaporation — more during heat and exercise. The classic guidance of "8 glasses (~ 2 litres) per day" is a reasonable baseline, but real needs vary.

**Thirst is a late signal.** By the time the thirst mechanism fires, you are already mildly dehydrated — blood osmolality has risen enough to trigger the brain's thirst centre. Pale, straw-coloured urine is a more reliable real-time indicator than waiting to feel thirsty.

<Callout type="tip">
Urine colour is a practical hydration guide: pale yellow = well hydrated; dark yellow or amber = drink more; colourless = you may have overdone it (but overhydration is rare in everyday life).
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Iron** → oxygen transport (haemoglobin); deficiency causes anaemia — fatigue, pallor, breathlessness.
- **Calcium** → bone/teeth structure + muscle/nerve signalling; long-term low intake → osteoporosis.
- **Sodium & potassium** → fluid balance and nerve/muscle function; most people have too much sodium and too little potassium.
- **Iodine** → thyroid hormone production; deficiency causes goitre, hypothyroidism, and foetal brain damage.
- **Water** is a nutrient — adults need ~2 L/day baseline, more with heat and exercise.
- **Thirst lags dehydration** — use urine colour as a practical guide; pale yellow = on track.

</Step>
