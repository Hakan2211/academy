<Step title="CPR: keeping blood moving" kind="explain">

When someone's heart stops, blood stops delivering oxygen to the brain. Brain damage can begin within **4–6 minutes**. CPR (cardiopulmonary resuscitation) does not restart the heart — it **buys time** by pumping enough blood to keep the brain and heart tissue alive until a defibrillator or medical team can arrive.

The key numbers for adult CPR:

- **Rate:** 100–120 compressions per minute — roughly to the beat of *Stayin' Alive* by the Bee Gees.
- **Depth:** 5–6 cm — about a third of the chest depth. This requires real force; do not be timid.
- **Ratio:** **30 compressions : 2 rescue breaths.** If you are not trained in rescue breaths or unwilling to give them, continuous hands-only compressions are still highly effective — especially in the first few minutes.
- **Recoil:** Allow the chest to fully rise between compressions so the heart can refill.
- **Minimal interruptions:** Switch compressors every 2 minutes to maintain quality. Fatigue sets in fast.

Push hard, push fast, push in the centre of the chest (lower half of the breastbone).

</Step>

<Step title="Recovery position and choking" kind="explain">

**The recovery position**

If someone is unconscious but **breathing normally**, do not start CPR. Instead, roll them into the **recovery position** to keep the airway open and prevent choking on vomit or fluids:

1. Kneel beside them. Place the near arm at a right angle, elbow bent upward.
2. Bring the far arm across the chest and hold the back of the hand against their near cheek.
3. Pull up the far knee, then roll them towards you onto their side.
4. Tilt the head back slightly to keep the airway open.
5. Monitor their breathing continuously and call emergency services.

**Choking**

Choking occurs when a foreign object partially or completely blocks the airway. Signs: sudden inability to speak or breathe, high-pitched noise when inhaling, hands clutching the throat.

For a conscious adult:

1. **Encourage coughing** if the cough is effective (they can speak and breathe).
2. **Five back blows:** lean them forward and give five firm strikes between the shoulder blades with the heel of your hand.
3. **Five abdominal thrusts (Heimlich manoeuvre):** stand behind them, make a fist above the navel, cover with your other hand, and pull sharply inward and upward. Alternate five back blows and five abdominal thrusts until the object is cleared.
4. If they become unconscious, lower them to the floor and begin CPR — compressions can sometimes dislodge objects.

Do not perform abdominal thrusts on infants, pregnant people, or the very obese — use back blows and chest thrusts instead. Always seek medical assessment after abdominal thrusts.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **CPR rate:** 100–120 compressions per minute; **depth:** 5–6 cm; **ratio:** 30 : 2.
- Push hard and allow full chest recoil — both are essential for blood flow.
- Hands-only CPR (no breaths) is effective and appropriate if you are untrained in rescue breaths.
- **Recovery position** — for unconscious but breathing people — keeps the airway open and prevents aspiration.
- **Choking:** encourage coughing → five back blows → five abdominal thrusts, alternating until cleared.
- Always call emergency services early. A certified course is needed to perform these skills with real confidence.

</Step>
