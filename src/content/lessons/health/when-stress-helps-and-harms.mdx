<Step title="Not all stress is the enemy" kind="explain">

The word "stress" has collected so much negative baggage that it can be easy to forget: **a life with no stress at all would be worse, not better.**

Psychologist Hans Selye introduced a useful distinction in the 1970s:

- **Eustress** (*eu-* from Greek for "good") — the kind of stress that motivates and energises. A challenging deadline that gets you focused, a competition that sharpens your performance, a workout that strengthens your muscles. The body is stressed, and the outcome is growth.
- **Distress** — stress that is overwhelming, uncontrollable, or prolonged. It exceeds your capacity to cope, and the outcome is damage: physical, mental, or both.

The difference is not always in the *size* of the stressor. It's in whether the challenge feels manageable, whether you feel some control, and — critically — whether you get to recover.

</Step>

<Step title="The Yerkes-Dodson curve" kind="explain">

In 1908, psychologists Robert Yerkes and John Dodson described a relationship between arousal and performance that has been replicated ever since:

**Performance follows an inverted-U as arousal increases.**

- Too little arousal (bored, unstimulated) → performance is flat. You're disengaged; effort and focus are low.
- Moderate arousal (energised, focused, slightly challenged) → **peak performance**. This is the sweet spot where eustress lives.
- Too much arousal (overwhelmed, panicked) → performance collapses. The prefrontal cortex — your thinking brain — is overridden by the emotional alarm system. Decision-making, memory, and creativity all suffer.

This is why a small amount of pressure before a presentation makes you sharper, while *too much* anxiety makes you blank.

</Step>

The practical takeaway: the goal isn't to eliminate all pressure. It's to **calibrate** arousal toward the peak. Sometimes that means adding challenge (when you're under-aroused) and sometimes it means bringing the stress response down (when you're overwhelmed).

</Step>

<Step title="How chronic stress harms the body" kind="explain">

Acute stress is fine. Chronic stress — the kind that never fully switches off — is another story. Sustained high cortisol, adrenaline, and inflammation affect nearly every system:

**Sleep** — cortisol and adrenaline keep the brain alert. Chronic stress delays sleep onset, reduces deep sleep, and fragments the night. Poor sleep then *worsens* stress reactivity, creating a vicious cycle.

**Heart and blood vessels** — chronically elevated blood pressure and inflammation damage artery walls. Over years, chronic stress is an independent risk factor for heart attack and stroke.

**Immune system** — short-term stress briefly boosts immune function; long-term stress suppresses it. Chronic stress increases susceptibility to colds and infections, and can worsen inflammatory conditions.

**Mood and mental health** — chronically elevated cortisol shrinks the hippocampus (memory and mood regulation) and is strongly linked to depression and anxiety disorders.

**Metabolism** — sustained cortisol encourages fat storage, particularly around the abdomen, and raises the risk of type 2 diabetes.

<Callout type="warn">

These harms accumulate gradually — chronic stress doesn't announce itself with a single dramatic event. This is why managing it *before* you feel seriously unwell matters.

</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Eustress** (good stress) is motivating and growth-producing; **distress** is overwhelming or prolonged and causes harm.
- The **Yerkes-Dodson inverted-U**: performance peaks at moderate arousal and drops at both extremes (too bored or too overwhelmed).
- The goal isn't zero stress — it's **calibrated arousal** in the sweet spot.
- Chronic stress causes measurable damage to **sleep, heart health, immunity, mood, and metabolism** through sustained cortisol and inflammation.
- The harms accumulate gradually, which is why early management matters more than waiting for a crisis.

</Step>
