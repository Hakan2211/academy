<Step title="Why you only get measles once" kind="explain">

Before vaccines, parents knew that a child who survived measles would never get it again. Somehow, the body remembered. The same is true for chickenpox, mumps, and dozens of other diseases: survive it once, and you are protected for life or for many years.

This is **immunological memory** — the defining feature that separates adaptive immunity from the innate system, and the biological foundation on which all vaccination rests.

When your adaptive immune system defeats a pathogen for the first time, most of the B and T cells that were activated die off — the battle is won and an army is no longer needed. But a small population survives as **memory cells**, quietly persisting in your blood and tissues for years or even decades.

</Step>

<Step title="The memory response: faster, stronger, better" kind="explain">

When the same pathogen appears a second time, memory cells recognise it immediately. Instead of the 4–7 day build-up required for a first response, the memory response kicks in **within hours**. The antibody level surges far higher than the first time, and the pathogen is cleared before it can replicate enough to make you ill.

Three ways the second response differs:

- **Speed** — memory cells activate in hours, not days.
- **Magnitude** — far more antibodies are produced, reaching higher peak levels.
- **Affinity** — memory B cells produce antibodies that bind the pathogen more tightly than first-exposure antibodies (the immune system "learns" during the first encounter).

The result: the second encounter is often completely asymptomatic. Your immune system eliminates the threat before you even know it arrived.

</Step>

<Step title="What a vaccine actually does" kind="explain">

A vaccine is a way to give your immune system a **safe first exposure** — enough to build memory, without causing the disease itself.

Different vaccines achieve this in different ways:

- **Live attenuated vaccines** (e.g. MMR, chickenpox) — weakened live pathogen; very close to natural infection, produces strong memory.
- **Inactivated vaccines** (e.g. flu shot, polio IPV) — killed pathogen; safe but sometimes needs booster doses.
- **Subunit / protein vaccines** (e.g. hepatitis B, whooping cough component) — just a piece of the pathogen (surface protein); very safe.
- **mRNA vaccines** (e.g. COVID-19 Pfizer/Moderna) — mRNA instructions tell your cells to make one pathogen protein temporarily; immune system responds, mRNA is broken down.

In every case, the **antigen** (the pathogen piece your immune system responds to) triggers B and T cell activation and memory formation — without you ever having to get sick.

<Callout type="info">
The mRNA in mRNA vaccines is broken down within days. It does not enter the cell nucleus and cannot alter your DNA.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- After the first infection, **memory B and T cells** persist for years and provide long-term protection.
- The second response is **faster** (hours not days), **stronger** (more antibodies), and **tighter-binding** (higher affinity).
- A **vaccine** triggers memory formation using a harmless piece of the pathogen — you get protection without getting ill.
- Types include live attenuated, inactivated, subunit/protein, and mRNA vaccines — all producing the same memory outcome.
- mRNA vaccines provide temporary instructions only; they do not alter DNA.

</Step>
