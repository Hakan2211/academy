<Step title="More than the sum of its parts" kind="explain">

Your body contains about 37 trillion cells, hundreds of distinct tissues, and more than 70 distinct organs — yet it runs as a single, coordinated machine. How? Because organs don't work in isolation. They're organised into **body systems**, each with a shared job, and those systems talk to each other constantly.

Think of it like a city. The roads (circulatory system) are useless without fuel depots (digestive system), communication lines (nervous system), or maintenance crews (immune system). Pull out one piece and everything else slows down. Put them all together and you get a living city that repairs itself, adapts to weather, and keeps the streets running at 3 a.m.

The body has about **11 major systems**. Six of the most health-relevant ones are:

- **Circulatory** — heart and blood vessels delivering oxygen and nutrients
- **Respiratory** — lungs exchanging oxygen and carbon dioxide
- **Digestive** — breaking food into fuel and raw materials
- **Musculoskeletal** — bones and muscles for structure and movement
- **Nervous** — the brain-and-nerve network that coordinates everything
- **Immune / Lymphatic** — defence and fluid balance

<Step title="Homeostasis: keeping the inside steady" kind="explain">

All this coordination has one overriding goal: keep your internal environment stable, no matter what the outside world throws at you. This is called **homeostasis** — from the Greek for "standing still."

Your body temperature stays near 37 °C whether you're in a snowstorm or a sauna. Your blood sugar stays near 5 mmol/L whether you've just eaten or just exercised. Your blood pH barely shifts a fraction of a unit your entire life.

These aren't lucky accidents — they're the result of thousands of continuous, automatic adjustments happening right now, inside you. Homeostasis is what makes life possible at the molecular level: enzymes, proteins, and cells only work within narrow ranges. Stray too far from the set point and things stop working properly.

</Step>

<Step title="Negative feedback: the body's thermostat" kind="explain">

The engine of homeostasis is **negative feedback**: a loop that detects when something is off and corrects it *in the opposite direction*.

Every feedback loop has three parts:

- **Sensor** — detects the current value (e.g. thermoreceptors in the skin)
- **Control centre** — compares the value to the set point (e.g. the hypothalamus in the brain)
- **Effector** — takes action to restore balance (e.g. sweat glands, shivering muscles)

The word "negative" doesn't mean bad — it means the response *negates* (reverses) the deviation. Get too hot → sweat to cool down → temperature falls back to 37 °C. Get too cold → shiver to generate heat → temperature rises back to 37 °C. The loop keeps running, quietly, every moment of your life.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- The body is organised into **systems** — groups of organs working together toward a shared function.
- Six key systems: circulatory, respiratory, digestive, musculoskeletal, nervous, and immune/lymphatic.
- All systems cooperate; disrupting one affects others.
- **Homeostasis** keeps the internal environment stable — temperature, blood sugar, pH, and more all have set points.
- **Negative feedback loops** (sensor → control centre → effector) are the mechanism: they detect a deviation and correct it in the opposite direction.

</Step>
