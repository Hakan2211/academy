<Step title="We are drowning in health claims" kind="explain">

Open any social media feed and you will find a stream of health claims — foods that cure cancer, supplements that reverse ageing, practices that "toxins" from your body. Almost all of them are wrong, misleading, or wildly overstated.

This is not a minor annoyance. Acting on false health beliefs has real costs: wasted money, missed effective treatments, unnecessary anxiety, and occasionally direct harm. The ability to evaluate health claims is one of the most practically useful skills a person can develop.

The good news is that the reasoning errors behind most health myths are surprisingly predictable. A small number of thinking traps generate the vast majority of misinformation. Learn to spot the traps, and you can evaluate new claims quickly — without needing a medical degree.

</Step>

<Step title="The core reasoning traps" kind="explain">

**1. Correlation treated as causation**

Two things vary together — they are correlated. From this, people conclude one causes the other. Classic examples: ice cream sales and drowning rates both peak in summer (cause: hot weather, not ice cream); people who carry lighters are more likely to get lung cancer (cause: smoking, not the lighter).

In health research, confounders are everywhere. People who eat more vegetables also tend to exercise more, sleep better, and have higher incomes. Attributing their health to any one of these variables is extremely hard. Randomised controlled trials (RCTs) exist precisely to control for this — but most health journalism reports observational correlations as if they were causal.

**2. Anecdote as evidence**

"My uncle smoked until he was 90 and was fine" does not refute the evidence that smoking increases lung-cancer risk. One person's experience is n=1 — it cannot speak to population-level probability. Human memory also actively selects confirming cases. We remember the healthy smoker; we do not equally remember the many who died young.

**3. Marketing language as medical claim**

"Natural," "detox," "cleanse," "superfood," "alkaline," "boosts immunity" — these are marketing terms with no agreed scientific definition. They are designed to trigger positive associations, not convey information. Their presence in a health claim is a reliable warning sign.

**4. The appeal to authority without credentials**

A celebrity endorsing a supplement, a wellness influencer recommending a protocol, a testimonial from someone who "reversed their condition" — none of these are evidence. The question is not who says it, but what the systematic evidence says.

<Callout type="warn">
The absence of evidence is not the same as evidence of absence — but it is also not a reason to believe a claim. For new or implausible health interventions, the burden of proof is on the claim-maker.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Finding trustworthy sources" kind="explain">

Not all health information is equal. Some simple heuristics for navigating the landscape:

**Higher-quality sources:**
- Systematic reviews and meta-analyses (compilations of multiple well-designed studies) — found on PubMed, Cochrane Library
- National health bodies (NHS, CDC, WHO) — they are conservative but based on consensus evidence
- Academic medical centres with no product to sell

**Questions to ask about any source:**
- Does this person or organisation have a financial interest in my believing this claim?
- Is a specific mechanism proposed, or just an association?
- Has this been replicated by independent researchers?
- Is the study in humans, with an adequate sample, for a relevant duration?

**What to do with uncertainty:**
Genuine scientific uncertainty exists — and is different from manufactured doubt. "The evidence on X is mixed" is a legitimate finding. "Scientists don't know anything, so my anecdote is as valid as their data" is not.

<Callout type="tip">
For personal health decisions, the most reliable single source is a conversation with a GP or specialist who knows your individual circumstances. Population-level statistics tell you about average risk — your doctor can apply them to you.
</Callout>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- Health misinformation is pervasive — and the reasoning errors behind it are predictable and learnable.
- The core traps: **correlation ≠ causation**, **anecdote as evidence**, **marketing language as claim**, and **appeal to unqualified authority**.
- Most persistent health myths contain a grain of truth — enough to make them plausible — wrapped in an overstatement or reasoning error.
- Trustworthy sources ask: is there a mechanism? Is it replicated? Who funded it? Is it in humans, at adequate scale?
- **Genuine uncertainty** is different from manufactured doubt. "Mixed evidence" is a legitimate finding; "anecdotes are as good as studies" is not.
- The most reliable source for personal decisions is a GP who knows your circumstances and can apply population statistics to you.

</Step>
