<Step title="Why 'just sleep more' is not a strategy" kind="explain">

Most people know they should sleep more. Knowing is the easy part. The harder part is understanding *why* sleep is difficult and *what specifically* changes it — because vague intentions rarely survive contact with a stressful evening, a bright phone screen, or a midnight racing mind.

Sleep hygiene is not a list of rules to follow perfectly. It is a set of evidence-based practices that work by addressing the actual mechanisms that govern sleep: the circadian clock, the build-up of sleep pressure (adenosine), arousal level, and the brain's associative link between bed and sleep. Each practice targets one or more of these levers.

This lesson covers the major ones in depth.

</Step>

<Step title="The single most powerful habit: a fixed wake time" kind="explain">

If you could only change one thing, sleep researchers consistently name the same answer: **get up at the same time every day — including weekends — no matter how poorly you slept.**

This works because a fixed wake time anchors the circadian clock, prevents the social jet lag of sleeping in, and gradually increases adenosine-driven sleep pressure by the time you go to bed the following night. Variable wake times prevent the clock from ever settling on a stable rhythm, which perpetuates difficulty falling asleep.

The counterintuitive implication: if you slept badly, do not sleep in to compensate. Getting up at your fixed time, however tired you feel, rebuilds sleep pressure for the next night and re-anchors the clock. One bad night followed by a good fixed-schedule night is far better than two bad nights caused by compensatory sleeping in.

</Step>

<Step title="Light: the most underrated lever" kind="explain">

You have already seen how melatonin and the circadian clock respond to light. Here is the practical prescription:

**Morning** (within the first hour of waking):
- Get outside or open bright indoor lights. Even on a cloudy day, outdoor light (~10,000 lux) is far more powerful than indoor lighting (~200–500 lux).
- This suppresses residual melatonin, triggers the cortisol awakening response, and sets the clock's timer for that night's melatonin release (~12–16 hours later).
- Just 10–20 minutes of morning bright light has measurable effects.

**Evening** (2–3 hours before target sleep time):
- Dim indoor lighting. Candles, table lamps, and warm-coloured bulbs are all better than overhead lighting.
- Avoid screens entirely, or use a hardware blue-light filter (not just a "night mode" software filter — the absolute brightness matters as much as colour temperature).
- Let the environment signal "darkness is coming" — this is the most reliable way to allow melatonin to rise on schedule.

</Step>

<Step title="Temperature, darkness, and quiet" kind="explain">

The bedroom environment directly supports or sabotages the physiology of sleep onset:

**Temperature**
Core body temperature must drop by approximately 1°C to initiate and maintain sleep. You shed heat from your hands, feet, and face — which is why people stick a foot out of the bed. A cool bedroom (roughly 16–19°C / 60–67°F) supports this process; a hot room fights it. Hot showers or baths an hour before bed are paradoxically helpful — the rebound cooling after the bath accelerates the drop.

**Darkness**
Even low-level light in the bedroom suppresses melatonin and shifts sleep into lighter stages. Blackout curtains or a sleep mask are not luxuries — if your bedroom has street-light intrusion, they make a real difference to sleep depth.

**Noise and vibration**
Sound does not have to wake you to disrupt sleep — it can pull you from N3 or REM into lighter stages without you remembering. Consistent low-level noise (fans, white noise, earplugs) masks variable intrusive noise more effectively than silence against a noisy background.

</Step>

<Step title="Caffeine, alcohol, and the wind-down routine" kind="explain">

**Caffeine cutoff**
Aim to stop caffeine intake by 2 pm (or earlier if you are a slow metaboliser). If you want an afternoon beverage, decaf or herbal tea work well. The effect is not dramatic from day one — adenosine receptors take a few days to downregulate — but the consistent shift in sleep quality across a week is usually noticeable.

**Alcohol**
Avoid alcohol within 3–4 hours of bedtime. Even one drink suppresses REM and increases sleep fragmentation in the second half of the night. If you drink socially, earlier in the evening is far better for sleep quality than drinking near bedtime.

**Wind-down routine (30–60 min)**
The nervous system does not switch off instantly. A consistent pre-sleep routine signals to the brain that sleep is imminent and helps lower physiological arousal. This could be reading (paper, not screen), light stretching, a warm bath, journalling, or anything that is genuinely calming and not mentally activating. The key is *consistency* — over time, the routine itself becomes a conditioned trigger for drowsiness.

<Callout type="tip">
If you find yourself lying awake with a racing mind, do not force yourself to stay in bed. After about 20 minutes of wakefulness, get up, go to a dim room, and do something quiet until you feel sleepy, then return to bed. This is called stimulus control and prevents the brain from learning to associate bed with wakefulness and frustration.
</Callout>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Fixed wake time every day** (including weekends) is the single most effective sleep-hygiene habit — it anchors the clock and rebuilds adenosine pressure.
- **Morning bright light** (10–20 min) within the first hour of waking sets the circadian timer; **dim evenings** allow melatonin to rise on schedule.
- A **cool** (~16–19°C), **dark**, and **quiet** bedroom supports the physiology of sleep onset and maintenance.
- **Caffeine**: cut off by ~2 pm; **alcohol**: avoid within 3–4 hours of bed; both fragment sleep.
- A **wind-down routine** (30–60 min) lowers arousal and becomes a conditioned sleep trigger.
- **If you can't sleep**: get up after ~20 min, do something calm in dim light, return when sleepy — this is stimulus control, the bed-is-for-sleep rule.
- No single habit is magic; the gains compound when several work together.

</Step>
