<Step title="Four stages, one cycle" kind="explain">

Every sleep cycle travels through four stages. Three are NREM (non-rapid eye movement) sleep; one is REM. The whole loop takes roughly **90 minutes** and repeats four to five times across a healthy night.

**N1 — the doorway**
The lightest stage, lasting just a few minutes. You are drifting off: muscles twitch occasionally (the famous hypnic jerk), and you are easily pulled back to waking. The brain produces slow theta waves as consciousness fades.

**N2 — the bulk of the night**
You spend about **50% of total sleep time** here. Heart rate and body temperature fall. The brain emits brief bursts called *sleep spindles* and isolated *K-complexes* — both thought to play a role in memory consolidation and protecting sleep from outside noise. You are harder to wake than in N1.

**N3 — deep, restorative sleep**
Also called slow-wave sleep (SWS). Large, slow delta waves dominate. This is the hardest stage to wake someone from — if you are roused, you feel groggy and confused (sleep inertia). N3 is when the body releases the most growth hormone, repairs tissue, and runs the glymphatic system at full capacity. It dominates the **first half of the night**.

**REM — the dream stage**
Your eyes flicker behind closed lids; the brain is nearly as active as in waking. The body's major muscles are temporarily paralysed (so you cannot act out dreams). REM is critical for **emotional memory consolidation** and creativity. REM periods are short in the early cycles but lengthen dramatically as morning approaches — a 20-minute first REM can grow to 45–60 minutes in the final cycle.

</Step>

<Step title="What changes across the night" kind="explain">

The ratio of stages is not fixed — it shifts in a predictable and important way.

In the **first half of the night**, cycles are heavy on N3 deep sleep. This is the brain and body running their most critical repair work while energy is available.

As the night progresses, N3 shrinks and **REM expands**. The final cycle before waking might consist almost entirely of REM. This means the last hour or two of sleep is rich in dreaming and emotional processing.

Practical consequence: someone who sleeps six hours instead of eight does not lose 25% of everything. They lose a disproportionate chunk of the late, REM-heavy cycles — the cycles most valuable for learning, creativity, and mood regulation.

</Step>

<Step title="Why each stage matters" kind="explain">

It is tempting to think of N3 as the "good" sleep and everything else as filler. In reality, each stage has a distinct role:

- **N2** cements procedural memories (skills, sequences) via sleep spindles; this is why sleeping after practising a new skill improves performance the next day.
- **N3** handles declarative memory (facts, events), physical repair, and immune activity. Growth hormone release is tightly coupled to these deep waves.
- **REM** processes emotional memories — dampening the emotional charge attached to difficult experiences — and supports creative thinking by linking distantly related ideas.

Disrupting any stage has specific costs. People with sleep apnoea, for example, are repeatedly pulled out of N3 and REM, which is why they wake feeling unrefreshed even after eight hours in bed.

<Callout type="tip">
Alcohol before bed sends you to sleep faster but dramatically suppresses REM and fragments the second half of the night — so the popular idea of a "nightcap" aiding sleep is misleading. You may fall asleep faster but sleep worse overall.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- A sleep cycle ≈ 90 minutes, cycling through **N1 → N2 → N3 → REM**, repeated 4–5 times per night.
- **N1**: brief light-sleep doorway; **N2**: ~50% of total sleep, sleep spindles; **N3**: deep slow-wave, growth hormone, glymphatic clearance; **REM**: dreaming, emotional memory, body paralysed.
- **N3 dominates early cycles; REM dominates later cycles** — so the night has a clear architecture that shifts from physical repair to memory/emotional processing.
- Cutting sleep short removes the late REM-rich cycles disproportionately.
- Every stage serves a distinct purpose; disrupting any one of them carries specific costs.

</Step>
