Healthy relationships — romantic, sexual, or otherwise — are built on **mutual respect, trust, and communication**. Understanding consent is central to this, and it applies far more broadly than just physical intimacy: it is about respecting other people's autonomy and having your own autonomy respected in return.

This lesson introduces the **FRIES model** of consent, explores what healthy and unhealthy relationship patterns look like, and covers where to find help and support.

</Step>

<Step title="What is consent?" kind="explain">

**Consent** means agreeing to something freely and knowingly. In the context of sexual activity, consent means that everyone involved has clearly and willingly agreed to what is happening.

Consent is **not:**
- Silence or not saying no
- Agreeing because of pressure, fear, or obligation
- Assuming that a previous yes means yes to everything in the future
- Consenting under the influence of alcohol or drugs to a degree that affects judgment

Consent **is:**
- An active, positive agreement — a clear yes
- Ongoing — it can be withdrawn at any time, and must be re-confirmed for new activities
- Specific — agreeing to one thing does not mean agreeing to everything
- Given freely, without pressure or manipulation

**Age of consent** exists in law to protect young people from exploitation by those with more power. Consent between people with large age or power differences raises additional concerns beyond simple willingness.

</Step>

<Step title="The FRIES model" kind="explain">

The **FRIES model** is a practical framework for understanding what makes consent genuine. It was developed by Planned Parenthood and is widely used in health education.

- **F — Freely given:** Consent must come from a genuine free choice. It cannot be the result of pressure, coercion, threats, or fear of consequences for saying no.
- **R — Reversible:** Anyone can change their mind at any time — even during an activity that has already started. Prior consent does not lock anyone in.
- **I — Informed:** Consent must be based on honest information. Deceiving someone — about who you are, about protection, about circumstances — undermines their ability to genuinely consent.
- **E — Enthusiastic:** Look for a willing, positive yes — not just the absence of a no. Silence, going along with something, or freezing are not consent.
- **S — Specific:** Saying yes to one activity does not mean yes to a different activity. Each act needs its own agreement.

All five criteria must be present. If any one is missing, genuine consent is absent.

</Step>

<Step title="Healthy vs unhealthy relationships" kind="explain">

Healthy and unhealthy relationships exist on a spectrum — unhealthy patterns often develop gradually, making them harder to notice from the inside.

**Signs of a healthy relationship:**
- Both people communicate openly and honestly
- Both people feel safe expressing disagreement or saying no
- Boundaries are respected without argument
- Both people maintain their own friendships, interests, and independence
- Disagreements are resolved with respect, not threats or punishment
- Both people feel better — not worse — about themselves in the relationship

**Warning signs of an unhealthy relationship:**
- **Pressure and control:** one person controls what the other wears, who they see, where they go, or their money
- **Jealousy framed as love:** extreme jealousy presented as caring, combined with checking up or monitoring
- **Isolation:** one person pulls the other away from friends and family
- **Blame and criticism:** one person frequently puts the other down, blames them for the relationship's problems, or makes them feel inadequate
- **Manipulation:** guilt-tripping, emotional blackmail, or threats to hurt themselves if the relationship changes
- **Disregarding consent:** pressure to do things sexually that have not been agreed to, or ignoring a stated boundary

An unhealthy relationship does not have to include physical violence to be harmful. Emotional and psychological control are serious and valid concerns.

</Step>

<Step title="Communication and respect" kind="explain">

Good communication is not just about avoiding conflict — it is the active practice of making a relationship safe and honest for both people.

**Practical principles:**
- **Use "I" statements:** "I feel uncomfortable when..." rather than "You always..." reduces defensiveness and opens conversation.
- **Listen to understand, not just to respond:** give the other person space to say what they mean without interrupting or dismissing.
- **Respect "no" without negotiation:** if someone declines something, accepting that answer without arguing, sulking, or retrying later is a sign of respect.
- **Check in:** consent is not a one-time gate. Regularly and naturally checking that a partner is comfortable is a positive, not a burden.
- **Accept that feelings can change:** what felt fine before may not feel fine now. That is not a contradiction — it is people being honest.

These principles apply in all relationships, not just romantic or sexual ones.

</Step>

<Step title="Getting support" kind="explain">

If you or someone you know is in a relationship that does not feel safe, or has experienced a breach of consent, there is help available.

**What to know:**
- You are not alone, and it is not your fault.
- Support is available confidentially — you do not have to give your name.
- Reaching out to a trusted adult, counsellor, or helpline is a sign of strength.

**Types of support:**
- **School or university counsellors:** safe, confidential conversations with trained support staff.
- **GP or sexual health clinic:** non-judgemental care, including advice after incidents affecting sexual health.
- **National helplines:** most countries have dedicated helplines for sexual violence, relationship abuse, and mental health crises. A search for your country's name and "relationship abuse helpline" or "sexual violence support" will find local resources.
- **Online text services:** for those who are not ready to speak on the phone.

You have the right to be safe, to say no, and to be respected in every relationship. Knowing that — and knowing where to turn — is part of your health.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Consent** is an active, positive agreement — not the absence of a no. It must be ongoing and can be withdrawn at any time.
- The **FRIES model:** Freely given, Reversible, Informed, Enthusiastic, Specific. All five must be present.
- Silence, freezing, or going along under pressure are **not** consent.
- **Healthy relationships** feature open communication, mutual respect, maintained independence, and the freedom to say no.
- **Warning signs** of unhealthy relationships include control, isolation, manipulation, and disregarding consent — even without physical violence.
- Good communication means using "I" statements, listening properly, and accepting no without negotiation.
- **Support is available** — school counsellors, GPs, sexual health clinics, and national helplines offer confidential help.

</Step>
