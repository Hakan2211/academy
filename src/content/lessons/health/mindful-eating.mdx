<Step title="What mindful eating is — and isn't" kind="explain">

"Mindful eating" sounds like a wellness buzzword, but the idea is grounded in straightforward physiology and behaviour science. It simply means **eating with attention rather than on autopilot** — noticing hunger before you eat, noticing fullness while you eat, and noticing what drives you to eat in the first place.

The opposite is distracted eating: scrolling through a phone, watching TV, or eating at a desk while your plate disappears without you registering a single bite. Studies consistently show that distracted eating leads to larger portions, less satisfaction, and more eating later in the day.

Mindful eating is not a diet. It does not restrict which foods you eat. It does not count calories. It is about reconnecting with signals your body is already sending — signals that modern eating culture encourages you to ignore.

**Three core practices:**
- Check in with your hunger before eating — are you physically hungry, or driven by boredom, stress, or habit?
- Eat slowly — put cutlery down between bites; chew thoroughly; take at least 20 minutes per main meal.
- Stop at satisfaction, not at the point of being stuffed.

</Step>

<Step title="The 20-minute signal" kind="explain">

One of the most important (and most violated) facts about eating is the **gut-brain delay**. When you begin eating, the gut releases hormones — GLP-1, peptide YY, CCK — that signal fullness to the brain. But this signalling cascade takes **roughly 20 minutes** to fully register.

If you eat a full meal in 8 minutes (a common pace for a rushed lunch), you will not feel full until 12 minutes *after* you have already finished — by which time you may have already gone back for more.

This delay is why:
- Eating slowly is one of the most consistently evidence-backed eating behaviours for avoiding over-eating.
- Serving yourself a modest amount, waiting 20 minutes, and *then* deciding if you want more is far more accurate than eating until you feel full.
- Buffets, large bowls, and distraction-eating all exploit this lag window.

<Callout type="tip">
A simple trick: eat the vegetables on your plate first. They take longer to chew, slow the meal down, and trigger satiety signals before you reach the denser, calorie-rich parts of the meal.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Physical vs. emotional hunger" kind="explain">

Not every urge to eat is physical hunger. Distinguishing between the two is a central mindful-eating skill:

**Physical hunger:**
- Builds gradually over hours since the last meal.
- Feels like stomach emptiness, low energy, mild headache, or difficulty concentrating.
- Any food sounds appealing — you would eat a plain apple.
- Eating resolves it.

**Emotional hunger:**
- Can appear suddenly, even after a recent meal.
- Is often triggered by stress, boredom, loneliness, or reward-seeking.
- Is specific — you want *that* particular food (comfort food, sweets, crisps).
- Eating does not fully resolve it; guilt often follows instead.

Neither type of hunger is "bad" — eating for comfort or pleasure is normal and part of a healthy relationship with food. The goal is simply awareness: knowing *why* you're eating helps you make a conscious choice rather than a reactive one.

</Step>

<Step title="Where on the hunger scale?" kind="interactive">

The hunger-fullness scale gives you a practical language for your body's signals. The sweet spot: begin eating around 3–4 (hungry but not ravenous); stop around 6–7 (satisfied but not stuffed). Avoiding the extremes in both directions is the single most practical habit in mindful eating.

<HungerScale />

Try checking in with this scale before each meal for a week. Most people find they were eating at 5 or 6 (not actually hungry) more often than they expected.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Mindful eating** means eating with attention: checking hunger before, slowing the pace during, and stopping at satisfaction.
- The **20-minute gut-brain delay** means fullness signals lag behind eating — eating slowly prevents over-eating.
- **Physical hunger** builds gradually; **emotional hunger** is sudden, specific, and not resolved by eating.
- The **hunger-fullness scale** (1–10): start eating at 3–4, stop at 6–7; avoid the extremes at both ends.
- Mindful eating is not a diet — it is a framework for listening to the body's own feedback.
