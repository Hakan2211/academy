<Step title="The brain's alarm — the amygdala" kind="explain">

Deep inside each hemisphere of the brain sits a small, almond-shaped structure called the **amygdala**. It is the brain's threat detector — scanning your environment moment by moment for anything that looks dangerous. When it senses a threat, it fires an alarm signal to the **hypothalamus**, which acts as the body's command centre.

The hypothalamus doesn't debate the threat. It immediately activates two overlapping systems:

1. **The fast track (SAM axis):** The sympathetic nervous system sends a signal straight to the adrenal glands atop the kidneys. Within *seconds* they release **adrenaline** (epinephrine) into the bloodstream.

2. **The slow track (HPA axis):** The hypothalamus triggers a hormonal cascade — CRH → pituitary → ACTH → adrenal cortex — that releases **cortisol**. This unfolds over *minutes*, sustaining and deepening the response.

Both happen together during a stress response. Adrenaline is the spark; cortisol is the fuel.

</Step>

<Step title="What changes — and why it made sense" kind="explain">

Once adrenaline and cortisol flood the body, almost every organ shifts:

- **Heart rate rises** — pumps more oxygenated blood to muscles for running or fighting.
- **Breathing quickens** — lungs take in more oxygen per minute.
- **Pupils dilate** — letting in more light for sharper vision.
- **Glucose floods the blood** — the liver releases stored glycogen for instant energy.
- **Digestion pauses** — blood and energy are redirected away from the gut (no time for lunch during a predator encounter).
- **Sweat increases** — the body pre-cools itself for the physical exertion ahead.

Every change is a performance optimisation for the immediate task: escape or fight. The body is sacrificing non-urgent functions (digestion, reproduction, immune surveillance) to redirect every resource toward the crisis.

Evolutionarily, this was brilliant. The problem is that modern stressors rarely require physical exertion — you can't sprint away from a difficult conversation or punch a looming debt.

</Step>

<Step title="Stress and homeostasis" kind="interactive">

The stress response deliberately disrupts your body's steady state. Heart rate, blood pressure, and blood glucose all rise sharply — they're pushed off their normal set points. This is by design: the emergency justifies the disturbance.

Under acute stress, the system resets after the threat passes. Under chronic stress, the body is trapped in a disturbed state — which is exactly what the homeostasis model below shows. Push the blood glucose to a high level and watch what happens if the system never gets to correct:

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- The **amygdala** detects threats and fires the alarm; the **hypothalamus** commands the response.
- **Adrenaline** (fast track, seconds) causes the immediate surge — heart rate, breathing, glucose, pupils, sweat.
- **Cortisol** (slow track, minutes via the HPA axis) sustains the mobilised state.
- Every bodily change is a performance trade-off: non-urgent systems (digestion) pause so urgent ones (muscles, heart) can excel.
- Stress deliberately pushes the body **out of homeostatic balance** — this is fine briefly, destructive chronically.

</Step>
