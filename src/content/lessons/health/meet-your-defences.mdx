<Step title="You are constantly under attack" kind="explain">

Bacteria, viruses, fungi, and parasites are everywhere — on every surface you touch, in every breath you take, in the food you eat. Yet most of the time you are not ill. That is not luck. It is the result of an extraordinarily well-organised defence system that has been refined over hundreds of millions of years.

Your immune system is not one thing. It is **three overlapping lines of defence**, each one harder to breach than the last:

1. **Physical and chemical barriers** — keep most threats out in the first place.
2. **Innate immunity** — responds fast and broadly to anything that gets through.
3. **Adaptive immunity** — responds slowly but with pinpoint accuracy, and *remembers* what it has seen.

Understanding these layers is the foundation of everything else in this world — vaccines, infections, allergies, all of it.

</Step>

<Step title="Layer 1 — Barriers: the body's walls" kind="explain">

The first line of defence never lets pathogens reach the interior at all.

- **Skin** — a tough, layered physical wall; most pathogens cannot penetrate intact skin.
- **Mucus membranes** — the lining of your nose, mouth, lungs, and gut traps pathogens in sticky mucus; tiny hairs (cilia) sweep it out.
- **Stomach acid** — destroys most bacteria swallowed with food (pH ≈ 1.5–3.5).
- **Tears and saliva** — contain lysozyme, an enzyme that breaks down bacterial cell walls.
- **Normal bacteria on skin and in the gut** — outcompete pathogens for space and resources.

Barriers do not discriminate — they block *everything*. They are not part of the "immune system" in the narrow sense, but they prevent the vast majority of infections before they begin.

</Step>

<Step title="Layer 2 — Innate immunity: fast and general" kind="explain">

When a pathogen breaks through — through a cut, an inhaled virus, contaminated food — the innate immune system acts within **minutes to hours**. It does not need to identify the exact pathogen; it responds to broad danger signals.

Key players:

- **Macrophages** — patrol tissues and engulf (phagocytose) invaders; release chemical alarm signals (cytokines).
- **Neutrophils** — rush to infected sites in huge numbers and engulf or chemically destroy pathogens.
- **Natural killer cells** — destroy cells already infected with viruses before adaptive immunity arrives.
- **Inflammation** — increased blood flow, heat, swelling — brings immune cells to the site and creates a hostile environment for pathogens. Fever raises body temperature enough to slow bacterial growth.

The innate system buys time. It cannot distinguish between a flu virus and a cold virus — but it does not need to. Its job is to hold the line.

</Step>

<Step title="Layer 3 — Adaptive immunity: slow, specific, and remembers" kind="explain">

If the innate system cannot clear an infection on its own, adaptive immunity engages after **4–7 days**. This is the system that makes vaccines work.

- **B cells** produce **antibodies** — proteins shaped specifically to lock onto one pathogen's surface proteins (antigens). Antibodies tag the pathogen for destruction.
- **T-helper cells** coordinate the response — they activate B cells and cytotoxic T cells.
- **Cytotoxic (killer) T cells** destroy your own cells if a virus has infected them — stopping viral replication at the source.
- **Memory B and T cells** remain after the infection clears. If the same pathogen returns, these cells mount a response in **hours rather than days**, often clearing the infection before you feel ill at all.

This memory is why you only get chickenpox once, and why vaccines work.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- The immune system has **three layers**: physical/chemical barriers, fast innate immunity, and targeted adaptive immunity.
- **Barriers** (skin, mucus, stomach acid, tears) block most pathogens before they enter the body.
- **Innate immunity** (macrophages, neutrophils, inflammation, fever) responds in minutes to hours without specificity.
- **Adaptive immunity** (B cells, T cells, antibodies) takes days but is precise and leaves **memory cells**.
- Memory cells are why the second encounter with a pathogen is far milder — and why vaccines work.

</Step>
