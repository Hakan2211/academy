<Step title="The chain of infection" kind="explain">

Infectious disease does not jump magically from one person to another. It follows a chain — a series of links that must all hold for transmission to succeed:

1. **Pathogen** — the agent (virus, bacterium, etc.)
2. **Reservoir** — where it lives between hosts (a person, animal, or surface)
3. **Exit** — how it leaves the reservoir (respiratory secretions, blood, faeces, bites)
4. **Transmission** — the route it travels
5. **Entry** — how it enters a new host (airways, mouth, broken skin, mucous membranes)
6. **Susceptible host** — someone who lacks immunity

Break any single link and you stop the infection. That is the logic behind everything from hand-washing to vaccines.

</Step>

<Step title="Six transmission routes" kind="explain">

Most infectious diseases travel by one of six main routes:

- **Airborne** — tiny droplet nuclei drift in the air for minutes to hours (measles, tuberculosis, COVID-19 in poorly ventilated rooms).
- **Droplet** — larger droplets fall within ~1 metre of a cough or sneeze (influenza, common cold, pertussis).
- **Direct/indirect contact** — touching an infected person or a surface they contaminated and then touching your face (cold viruses, norovirus, ringworm).
- **Fecal-oral** — contaminated food or water, or unwashed hands after using the toilet (cholera, typhoid, hepatitis A).
- **Vector** — an insect bite carries the pathogen from one host to another (malaria via mosquito, Lyme disease via tick).
- **Bloodborne** — exposure to infected blood or body fluids (HIV, hepatitis B and C).

One disease can use more than one route — COVID-19 is mainly airborne but also travels on contaminated surfaces.

</Step>

Notice that the intervention always targets the **weakest link** in the chain for that route. Hand-washing blocks contact and fecal-oral transmission; insect repellent blocks the vector; sterile needles block bloodborne spread.

<Step title="R₀ — the basic reproduction number" kind="explain">

Epidemiologists measure how fast a disease spreads with a single number: **R₀** (pronounced "R-naught"), the average number of people one infectious person infects in a population where nobody is immune.

- R₀ < 1 → each case causes less than one new case → the outbreak fizzles out on its own.
- R₀ > 1 → each case causes more than one new case → the outbreak grows.
- R₀ = 1 → steady spread — disease is endemic, neither growing nor shrinking.

Some reference values: seasonal flu ≈ 1.1–2; COVID-19 original strain ≈ 2–3; measles ≈ 12–18. Measles is one of the most contagious diseases known — one infectious person can seed a dozen or more cases.

<Callout type="info">
R₀ is a property of the pathogen **and** the setting — it assumes no immunity and no control measures. Real-world spread is tracked by the **effective reproduction number Rₑ**, which accounts for immunity and interventions.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- The **chain of infection** has six links: pathogen → reservoir → exit → transmission → entry → susceptible host. Breaking any link stops spread.
- Six main **transmission routes**: airborne, droplet, contact (direct/indirect), fecal-oral, vector, bloodborne.
- **R₀** is the average number of secondary cases from one index case in a fully susceptible population; R₀ > 1 means an outbreak can grow.
- R₀ is a theoretical baseline — the **effective Rₑ** falls as immunity builds or interventions kick in.
- Interventions are designed to target the weakest link in each disease's specific chain.

</Step>
