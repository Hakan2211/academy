<Step title="Prevention beats first aid" kind="explain">

The best first aid is the kind you never need to give. Most accidents are not random — they are the predictable result of identifiable hazards that could have been removed or reduced.

Prevention matters for a deeper reason too: not every injury has a good first-aid fix. A traumatic brain injury from a fall, a drowning, a house fire — these can cause irreversible harm in seconds. No amount of first-aid skill can fully undo them.

This lesson looks at the four areas where most preventable accidents happen: the home, the road, water, and the sun.

</Step>

<Step title="Home safety" kind="explain">

The home is where most injuries occur — especially for the very young and the elderly.

**Falls**
- Keep floors clear of cables, rugs, and clutter
- Install grab rails in bathrooms; use non-slip mats in baths and showers
- Ensure stairways are well lit; consider stair gates for young children
- Store frequently used items at reachable heights — avoid climbing on chairs

**Fire**
- Fit smoke alarms on every level; test them monthly
- Have a fire escape plan and practise it
- Never leave candles, stoves, or open fires unattended
- Keep a fire blanket in the kitchen

**Poisons and medications**
- Store all medicines, cleaning products, and chemicals in locked or child-proof cupboards — children are fast and curious
- Check expiry dates; dispose of unused medications safely (many pharmacies accept them)
- Carbon monoxide is invisible and odourless — fit a CO detector near sleeping areas

**Choking hazards**
- Keep small objects, coins, batteries, and balloons out of reach of children under 3
- Cut food (especially grapes, nuts, raw carrots) into small pieces for young children

</Step>

<Step title="Road and water safety" kind="explain">

**Road safety**

Road traffic accidents are a leading cause of injury and death worldwide — and most are preventable.

- **Always wear a seatbelt** — it reduces fatal injury risk by around 45%.
- **Never use a phone while driving** — reaction time is impaired comparably to being over the drink-drive limit.
- **Respect speed limits** — stopping distance increases with the square of speed.
- **Pedestrians:** use crossings, make eye contact with drivers before stepping out, be especially careful at night or when wearing headphones.
- **Cyclists and motorcyclists:** wear a properly fitted helmet; use lights and high-visibility clothing.

**Water safety**

Drowning is the third leading cause of unintentional injury death globally — and it is **silent**. A drowning person typically cannot shout for help; they are vertical in the water, head tilted back, gasping, unable to wave.

- Never swim alone
- Know your limits and respect water conditions; cold water shock can incapacitate a strong swimmer in seconds
- Wear a buoyancy aid when boating or kayaking
- If someone is in trouble in water, **throw, don't go** — throw a rope, buoyancy aid, or anything that floats. Entering the water yourself risks two casualties
- Fence garden ponds and pools; empty paddling pools when not in use

</Step>

<Step title="Sun safety and DRABC at any scene" kind="explain">

**Sun safety**

UV radiation causes sunburn, accelerated skin ageing, and is the main cause of skin cancer — yet most UV damage is preventable.

- **Apply SPF 30+ broad-spectrum sunscreen** to exposed skin 15–30 minutes before going out; reapply every 2 hours and after swimming
- **Seek shade between 11 am and 3 pm** (the peak UV hours in most climates)
- **Cover up:** a wide-brimmed hat, UV-protective clothing, and sunglasses (wraparound lenses block UVA and UVB)
- **Check the UV index** before outdoor activities — UV can be high even on cool or cloudy days

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Prevention is the most effective first aid** — identify and remove hazards before accidents occur.
- **Home:** clear floors, fit smoke and CO alarms, lock away medicines and poisons, cut food for young children.
- **Road:** wear a seatbelt, never phone while driving, use helmets and lights when cycling.
- **Water:** drowning is silent — never swim alone, throw a buoyancy aid rather than entering the water yourself, fence home ponds and pools.
- **Sun:** SPF 30+ sunscreen, shade between 11 am and 3 pm, hat and sunglasses — UV is high even on cloudy days.
- When an accident does happen despite precautions, DRABC and the correct injury response give you the best chance of a good outcome.

</Step>
