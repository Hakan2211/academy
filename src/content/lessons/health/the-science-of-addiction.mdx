<Step title="Addiction is a brain condition" kind="explain">

Addiction is defined by compulsive substance use despite harmful consequences and a difficulty stopping despite wanting to. It is classified by leading health organisations — including the World Health Organization and the American Medical Association — as a **chronic, treatable health condition**, not a moral failing or a sign of weak character.

Understanding *why* addiction is so hard to overcome requires understanding what addictive substances do to the brain's reward system — and how that changes with repeated exposure.

</Step>

<Step title="The reward pathway" kind="explain">

Deep within the brain lies a circuit called the **mesolimbic dopamine pathway** — sometimes called the "reward pathway" or "pleasure centre." Its key structures include:

- **Ventral tegmental area (VTA)** — produces dopamine
- **Nucleus accumbens** — receives the dopamine signal; mediates pleasure and motivation
- **Prefrontal cortex** — modulates decision-making and impulse control

Normally, this pathway fires in response to naturally rewarding activities: food, social connection, exercise, creative achievement. Dopamine tells the brain "that was good — remember it, want more."

Addictive substances short-circuit this system. Many release dopamine at 2–10 times the level of natural rewards, and they do so immediately and reliably. The brain cannot easily distinguish this artificial dopamine flood from genuine achievement — it records it as extraordinarily important.

</Step>

<Step title="Tolerance vs dependence vs addiction" kind="explain">

These three terms are often confused — they describe related but distinct phenomena:

**Tolerance** — the brain adapts to repeated substance exposure by reducing receptor sensitivity or numbers. The same dose produces less effect. More is needed to achieve the same result. Tolerance can develop to a substance without dependence or addiction.

**Physical dependence** — the body has adapted so thoroughly to the substance's presence that removing it causes a withdrawal syndrome (sweating, tremors, anxiety, pain, nausea). Dependence can occur with substances like beta-blockers or antidepressants that are not addictive — the body simply adapted to their presence.

**Addiction (substance use disorder)** — characterised by compulsive use, loss of control, continued use despite harm, and intense craving. It involves changes in the prefrontal cortex (impairing the "brakes" on impulse), not just tolerance or dependence. Addiction is the most complex of the three, involving biology, psychology, and social context.

</Step>

<Step title="Habit loops and why stopping is so hard" kind="interactive">

Addiction also builds powerfully on habit. The cue-routine-reward loop that forms all habits applies with particular force to addictive substances — because the reward (dopamine) is so strong and the relief of withdrawal so immediate.

<HabitLoop />

Environmental cues — the smell of cigarettes, passing a familiar bar, stress — become deeply wired triggers that fire craving even years after quitting. This is why a brief lapse does not mean recovery is impossible; the brain's habit architecture is very powerful, but it can be retrained over time with the right support.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- Addiction is a **chronic health condition** — defined by compulsive use, loss of control, and continued use despite harm.
- Addictive substances hijack the **mesolimbic dopamine pathway**, producing dopamine surges far beyond natural rewards.
- **Tolerance** = reduced effect at the same dose. **Dependence** = withdrawal when stopping. **Addiction** = compulsive use with loss of control — these are related but distinct.
- With tolerance, users often chase relief from withdrawal, not pleasure — they use to feel normal.
- **Environmental cues** become powerful triggers; quitting is a biological challenge, not a willpower contest.
- Effective, evidence-based treatments exist: medications, behavioural therapies, and support networks all improve outcomes.

</Step>
