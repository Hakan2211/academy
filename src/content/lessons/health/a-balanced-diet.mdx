<Step title="No single perfect food" kind="explain">

A persistent myth in nutrition is the "superfood" — one magical ingredient that, if you eat enough of it, unlocks health. Blueberries, kale, turmeric, açaí, green tea: every decade has its candidate. The research invariably shows these foods have genuinely good properties — but no single food can do what variety does.

Here is why. Each food group and each colour of produce delivers a different set of nutrients and bioactive compounds. Broccoli gives you sulforaphane and folate. Salmon gives you omega-3 fatty acids and Vitamin D. Lentils give you iron and resistant starch. Yoghurt gives you calcium, probiotics, and B12. No overlap: they each do something others do not.

The nutritional strategy that has the strongest evidence behind it is not "eat more of X" — it is **eat a wide variety of minimally processed whole foods across all the food groups**. Variety is the insurance policy: if one food is low in a nutrient you need, another probably has it covered.

</Step>

<Step title="Eat the rainbow — phytonutrients beyond vitamins" kind="explain">

Beyond the thirteen vitamins and the essential minerals, plants contain thousands of bioactive compounds called **phytonutrients** (or phytochemicals). They are not vitamins — the body does not die without them — but decades of research link high phytonutrient intake to lower rates of cardiovascular disease, certain cancers, neurodegenerative diseases, and type 2 diabetes.

The colour of a fruit or vegetable is largely determined by the phytonutrients inside it:

- **Red** (tomatoes, watermelon) → lycopene (cardiovascular and prostate protection).
- **Orange/yellow** (carrots, sweet potato, oranges) → carotenoids including beta-carotene (pro-Vitamin A, eye health).
- **Green** (broccoli, spinach, kale) → chlorophyll, lutein, sulforaphane, folate.
- **Blue/purple** (blueberries, red grapes, beetroot) → anthocyanins (potent antioxidants, cognitive health).
- **White/brown** (garlic, onion, mushrooms) → allicin, quercetin, selenium.

"Eat the rainbow" is not a cute slogan — it is the practical strategy for collecting this full spectrum of compounds through food rather than supplements.

</Step>

<Step title="Deficiency vs excess — both harm" kind="explain">

Nutrition problems in wealthy countries are less about classic deficiency diseases and more about chronic imbalance in both directions.

**Too little** of micronutrients is the familiar story — anaemia, bone loss, fatigue. But it is surprisingly common even in high-income populations:
- Vitamin D deficiency affects an estimated 40% of Europeans due to limited sunlight.
- Iron deficiency is the most common single-nutrient deficiency worldwide, affecting menstruating women and vegetarians disproportionately.
- Iodine and folate are routinely low in groups who avoid seafood or processed fortified foods.

**Too much** is the less-discussed side:
- **Sodium excess** (from ultra-processed food) raises blood pressure and drives cardiovascular disease.
- **Saturated fat and refined sugar** excess drives insulin resistance and weight gain without providing micronutrients.
- **Supplement megadosing** — taking fat-soluble vitamins far above recommended levels — accumulates in tissue. Hypervitaminosis D (from supplement overuse) causes kidney damage and dangerous calcium overload.

Balance is genuinely the goal — not maximum of every nutrient, not restriction, but a wide enough variety that adequacy comes naturally.

</Step>

<Step title="Who needs supplements — and who does not?" kind="explain">

The supplement industry is worth hundreds of billions of dollars annually, with marketing that implies most people are dangerously deficient without daily pills. The evidence is more nuanced.

**Strong evidence supports supplementation for:**
- **Vitamin D** — for people with limited sun exposure (indoor workers, dark-skinned people in northern latitudes, the elderly): 10–25 μg/day.
- **Vitamin B12** — for vegans and strict vegetarians: B12 is only in animal foods. Deficiency is almost inevitable without supplementation over time.
- **Folate (400 μg/day)** — for all people planning pregnancy or in the first 12 weeks: reduces neural tube defect risk significantly.
- **Iron** — for people diagnosed with iron-deficiency anaemia: always worth confirming with a blood test first.

**Weak or no evidence for:**
- Multivitamins in healthy adults eating varied diets — most large trials show no mortality or disease benefit.
- High-dose antioxidant supplements (Vitamin E, beta-carotene) — some trials showed *increased* cancer risk in smokers.
- Most "wellness" supplements without a confirmed deficiency.

The principle: **test before you supplement if possible** (a blood test for Vitamin D, B12, iron, and ferritin covers most common needs). Supplements can correct genuine deficiencies effectively — but they are not a substitute for a varied diet and they can cause harm at high doses.

<Callout type="warn">
If you take a blood thinner (warfarin/Coumadin), Vitamin K supplements and even large amounts of green vegetables can interfere with your dosing. Always tell your prescriber what you take. This is one case where dietary changes genuinely interact with medication.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Building sustainable balance" kind="explain">

Knowing the ideal plate is not the hard part. The hard part is making it your default over months and years — without turning eating into a source of anxiety.

A few principles from behavioural nutrition:

**Crowd out, don't cut out.** Adding more vegetables naturally displaces less nutritious foods. Strict elimination triggers craving and rebound; abundance does not.

**Consistency over perfection.** One "bad" meal does not derail a healthy pattern; a consistently poor pattern over years does the damage. The 80/20 idea (eating well 80% of the time leaves comfortable room for enjoyment) has practical merit.

**Make the default healthier.** What you keep at home, what comes first on your plate, what you order first at a restaurant — these defaults drive most eating decisions with little willpower. Redesigning the environment is more reliable than relying on discipline.

**Variety reduces boredom.** The monotony of restrictive diets is a major reason they fail. A varied diet of whole foods is interesting — there are hundreds of vegetables, dozens of whole grains, infinite combinations.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Variety is the strategy** — no single food does what a diverse whole-food diet does.
- **"Eat the rainbow"** collects phytonutrients beyond vitamins: lycopene (red), carotenoids (orange), sulforaphane (green), anthocyanins (blue/purple), allicin (white).
- **Both deficiency and excess harm** — common deficiencies include Vitamin D, iron, and folate; sodium and saturated fat excess drive chronic disease.
- **Supplements with strong evidence**: Vitamin D (low-sunlight populations), B12 (vegans), folate (pregnancy). Most other routine supplementation lacks evidence in people already eating well.
- **Sustainable balance** beats short-term perfection: crowd out rather than cut out, make the default healthier, embrace variety to avoid monotony.

</Step>
