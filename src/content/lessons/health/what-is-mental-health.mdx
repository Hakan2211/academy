<Step title="What mental health actually means" kind="explain">

When people hear "mental health" they often think: *Does this person have a mental illness?* But that framing misses something important. Mental health is not a binary — sick or well. It is a spectrum of wellbeing that **every single person** sits on, all the time.

The World Health Organization defines mental health as:

> **A state of well-being in which a person realises their own potential, can cope with the normal stresses of life, can work productively, and is able to contribute to their community.**

Notice what that definition is *for*, not just *against*. Mental health is not simply the absence of a diagnosis. It is the presence of something good: resilience, connection, a sense of purpose, the ability to feel your feelings and recover from setbacks.

Just as we all have physical health — regardless of whether we have a physical illness — we all have mental health. And just as we look after our bodies, we can look after our minds.

</Step>

<Step title="The continuum — everyone moves along it" kind="interactive">

Mental health exists on a continuum. At one end, people are thriving; at the other, they are in real difficulty. Most of us spend most of our lives somewhere in the middle — coping, mostly okay, with harder patches along the way.

**Nobody stays in the same place forever.** A bereavement, job loss, illness, or sustained stress can move someone from thriving to struggling quickly. Recovery, support, and changed circumstances can move them back.

<Step title="Mind and body are one system" kind="explain">

Mental and physical health are deeply intertwined — not separate categories. When you are physically unwell, your mood often drops. When you are anxious or depressed, your body feels it: tense muscles, disrupted sleep, changes in appetite.

The nervous system, immune system, and endocrine (hormone) system are in constant conversation. Chronic stress raises cortisol, which over time affects the heart, digestion, and immune function. Exercise releases neurotransmitters that lift mood. Sleep knits both together.

This is why modern medicine speaks of **whole-person health** — tending to the mind is tending to the body, and vice versa.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- Mental health is a spectrum of wellbeing that everyone sits on — not just a label for illness.
- The WHO definition frames it positively: realising potential, coping with stress, connecting with others.
- Everyone moves along the continuum over their lifetime — struggling at a point in time is normal and does not define anyone.
- Mind and body are one system: physical and mental health constantly influence each other.
- Tending to mental health is as important and as possible as tending to physical health.

</Step>
