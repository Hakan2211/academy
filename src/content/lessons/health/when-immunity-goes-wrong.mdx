<Step title="The immune system as a balance" kind="explain">

Everything you have learned so far has described an immune system working beautifully — identifying threats, launching measured responses, clearing infections, and remembering enemies. But the same system that protects you can also turn against you.

Immune dysfunction falls into **two broad categories**:

- **Too much** — the immune system attacks things it should tolerate: harmless allergens (allergy), or the body's own tissues (autoimmunity).
- **Too little** — the immune system cannot mount an adequate defence: immunodeficiency.

These are not rare edge cases. Allergies affect roughly 1 in 4 people in high-income countries. Autoimmune diseases collectively affect about 1 in 20. And temporary immunodeficiency (from stress, poor sleep, or medication) is universal. Understanding these failures illuminates what the healthy system actually has to get right.

</Step>

<Step title="Allergies: overreacting to the harmless" kind="explain">

An allergy is an inappropriate immune response to a substance that poses no real threat. Pollen, peanuts, dust mites, cat dander, penicillin — all harmless, but in some people the immune system treats them as dangerous invaders.

The mechanism involves **IgE antibodies** — a class of antibody normally used to fight parasites. In allergic individuals, the immune system mistakenly produces IgE targeting the allergen. IgE binds to **mast cells** in tissues and **basophils** in blood. On re-exposure, the allergen cross-links IgE molecules on these cells, triggering mass release of **histamine** and other mediators — causing the familiar symptoms.

- **Hay fever / allergic rhinitis** — runny nose, itchy eyes, sneezing; IgE targeting pollen.
- **Food allergy** — from hives and swelling to anaphylaxis; can be life-threatening.
- **Asthma** — allergen-driven airway inflammation; bronchospasm; wheezing.
- **Anaphylaxis** — systemic histamine release; can cause airway closure and circulatory collapse; treated with adrenaline (epinephrine).

Why do some people develop allergies and others do not? A mix of **genetics** (certain immune gene variants) and **environment** (the "hygiene hypothesis" suggests reduced early microbial exposure leaves the immune system less well-calibrated). Rates of allergy are higher in wealthier, more sanitised societies.

<Callout type="info">
The hygiene hypothesis does not mean being "too clean" causes allergies. It refers to reduced *microbial diversity* in early life — fewer infections, less farm exposure, more urban environments — which may affect how the immune system calibrates its tolerances.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Autoimmunity: attacking the self" kind="explain">

In autoimmune disease, the adaptive immune system loses its ability to distinguish "self" from "non-self" — and attacks the body's own tissues. There are over 80 recognised autoimmune diseases.

Normally, T cells that react to self-proteins are eliminated during development in the thymus (central tolerance), and further checkpoints exist in tissues (peripheral tolerance). In autoimmunity, these checkpoints fail.

Some common examples:

- **Type 1 diabetes** — beta cells in the pancreas are destroyed; the person can no longer produce insulin.
- **Rheumatoid arthritis** — the synovium (joint lining) is attacked; causes chronic joint inflammation, pain, and damage.
- **Multiple sclerosis** — the myelin sheath insulating nerve fibres is degraded; disrupts nerve signal transmission.
- **Lupus (SLE)** — antibodies against the body's own DNA and other proteins; can damage kidneys, skin, joints, and more.
- **Hashimoto's thyroiditis** — the thyroid gland is attacked, impairing thyroid hormone production.

Triggers are complex: genetics sets the susceptibility, but often an **environmental trigger** (infection, hormonal change, stress) initiates the disease in a predisposed person. Many autoimmune conditions are more common in women, suggesting hormonal influences.

</Step>


<Step title="Immunodeficiency: too little protection" kind="explain">

Immunodeficiency means the immune system cannot mount an adequate defence. It ranges from temporary and mild to permanent and life-threatening.

**Primary immunodeficiencies** are inherited. Severe combined immunodeficiency (SCID) — "bubble boy disease" — leaves infants with virtually no functional T or B cells; previously fatal without bone marrow transplant. Most primary immunodeficiencies are less extreme but still significantly impair immunity.

**Secondary immunodeficiencies** are acquired:

- **HIV/AIDS** — HIV destroys CD4 T-helper cells; as their count falls, opportunistic infections and cancers that a healthy immune system would suppress become life-threatening. Modern antiretroviral therapy prevents progression to AIDS.
- **Immunosuppressive medication** — drugs used after organ transplant or for autoimmune disease deliberately suppress immunity (to prevent rejection or reduce self-attack); this increases infection risk as a side effect.
- **Malnutrition** — severe protein and micronutrient deficiency impairs immune cell production; a leading cause of immune compromise globally.
- **Chronic stress and sleep deprivation** — significant but reversible suppression of immune function.

People with immunodeficiency are susceptible to **opportunistic infections** — pathogens that a healthy immune system would handle without symptoms, but which become dangerous when defences are lowered.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- Immune dysfunction falls into **too much** (allergy, autoimmunity) or **too little** (immunodeficiency).
- **Allergies** — IgE antibodies against harmless substances trigger mast cell histamine release; causes hay fever, food allergy, asthma, and anaphylaxis.
- **Autoimmunity** — tolerance checkpoints fail; the immune system attacks self-tissue (e.g. type 1 diabetes, rheumatoid arthritis, lupus).
- **Immunodeficiency** — primary (inherited, e.g. SCID) or secondary (HIV, immunosuppressants, malnutrition, stress).
- The immune system must walk a narrow line: responsive enough to clear real threats, tolerant enough to ignore self and harmless antigens.

</Step>
