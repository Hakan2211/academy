<Step title="A note on this lesson" kind="explain">

<Callout type="info">
This lesson covers sexually transmitted infections (STIs) in a factual, non-judgemental, and inclusive way. STIs are common health conditions — having one is not a moral failing. The aim here is accurate health knowledge so that everyone can make informed decisions about their sexual health, access testing and treatment without shame, and understand how to protect themselves and others.
</Callout>

A **sexually transmitted infection (STI)** — also called a sexually transmitted disease (STD) — is an infection passed from one person to another primarily through sexual contact. STIs are caused by bacteria, viruses, or parasites, and many are very common worldwide.

Having an STI does not reflect a person's character or worth. STIs are health conditions, like any other, that can be tested for, treated, and in many cases prevented.

</Step>

<Step title="How STIs spread" kind="explain">

Different STIs spread through different routes:

**Through genital, anal, or oral sex (skin-to-skin or fluid contact):**
- **Chlamydia** (bacterial): one of the most common STIs; often has no symptoms. Curable with antibiotics.
- **Gonorrhoea** (bacterial): increasingly antibiotic-resistant; can cause discharge and pain. Curable with antibiotics.
- **Syphilis** (bacterial): progresses in stages; very curable if caught early. Symptoms include a painless sore, then rash.
- **Genital herpes** (viral, HSV-1 or HSV-2): causes occasional sore outbreaks; cannot be cured, but symptoms can be managed with antivirals. Very common — most people with HSV have no or mild symptoms.
- **HPV** (human papillomavirus, viral): the most common STI; most infections clear on their own. Some strains cause genital warts; others are linked to cervical and other cancers. A vaccine is available.
- **HIV** (viral): spread through blood, semen, vaginal fluid, and breast milk. Not curable but highly manageable with antiretroviral therapy (ART) — people on effective treatment live long, healthy lives and cannot transmit the virus.

**Through blood (sharing needles, transfusions):**
HIV and hepatitis B and C can also be transmitted this way.

**Key point:** many STIs have **no noticeable symptoms**, especially early on. This is why regular testing is important for sexually active people.

</Step>

<Step title="Protection" kind="explain">

The most reliable ways to reduce STI risk:

**Condoms (barrier methods)**
Using external or internal condoms correctly and consistently is the single most effective behaviour for reducing STI transmission during sex. Condoms protect against most STIs spread through bodily fluids (chlamydia, gonorrhoea, HIV) and reduce (though do not eliminate) risk for skin-contact infections like herpes and HPV.

**Vaccination**
- **HPV vaccine:** highly effective against the strains most associated with cervical cancer and genital warts. Recommended for young people before sexual activity begins; also beneficial for adults who have not been vaccinated.
- **Hepatitis B vaccine:** part of the standard childhood schedule in many countries; highly effective.

**Testing and treatment**
Knowing your status and treating infections promptly:
- Breaks the chain of transmission.
- Prevents the complications of untreated infections (e.g., infertility from untreated chlamydia, serious illness from late-stage syphilis).
- Allows partners to be informed and tested.

**PrEP (pre-exposure prophylaxis)**
A daily medication for people at higher risk of HIV that reduces the risk of transmission by over 99% when taken consistently.

**Communication**
Open, honest conversations with partners about testing history and protection choices are part of healthy sexual health practice.

</Step>

<Step title="Testing and getting help" kind="explain">

**Who should test?**
Anyone who is sexually active — especially with new or multiple partners, or if a condom was not used. Testing is recommended routinely once or twice a year for people with multiple partners, and immediately if you have symptoms or think you may have been exposed.

**What testing involves**
Testing is straightforward and largely painless — depending on the STI, a urine sample, a blood test, or a swab is used. Sexual health clinics, GPs, and many pharmacies offer confidential testing.

**Treatment**
- Bacterial STIs (chlamydia, gonorrhoea, syphilis): treated with antibiotics, usually effectively.
- Viral STIs (herpes, HIV): cannot be cured but are managed with antiviral medications that reduce symptoms and transmission.
- HPV: most infections clear naturally; cervical screening (smear tests) detects cell changes early.

**Telling a partner**
Being told a partner has an STI can feel difficult, but it allows them to get tested and treated. Sexual health clinics can help with partner notification confidentially.

Getting tested regularly and treating infections promptly is simply responsible healthcare — the same as any other aspect of maintaining your health.

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **STIs** are common health conditions passed through sexual contact, blood, or other fluids — having one is not a moral issue.
- Common examples: chlamydia, gonorrhoea, syphilis (bacterial, curable); herpes, HPV, HIV (viral, manageable).
- **Many STIs have no symptoms** — you cannot tell by looking or feeling. Testing is the only way to know.
- **Condoms** (barrier methods) are the most effective protection against most STIs. No other contraceptive method provides STI protection.
- **Vaccination** against HPV and hepatitis B provides strong protection.
- Regular **testing**, prompt treatment, and open communication with partners are the pillars of good sexual health.
- Sexual health clinics and GPs offer confidential, non-judgemental testing and treatment.

</Step>
