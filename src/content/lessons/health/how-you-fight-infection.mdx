<Step title="The moment a pathogen breaks in" kind="explain">

Most of the time your barriers hold. But sometimes a pathogen slips through — via a small cut, a sneeze that reaches your respiratory tract, contaminated food, or a virus particle that lands on a mucus membrane. What happens next unfolds over hours and days in a remarkably choreographed sequence.

The story has three overlapping acts: the **alarm** (innate immunity recognises danger), the **battle** (phagocytes, inflammation, and fever do the fighting), and the **targeted campaign** (adaptive immunity arrives with antibody weapons and cleans up).

</Step>

<Step title="Hours 0–6: the alarm goes off" kind="explain">

Tissue cells and patrol macrophages have surface receptors that detect molecular patterns common to pathogens — patterns your own cells do not display. The moment they sense an invader, they release **cytokines**: chemical alarm signals that spread through the tissue.

Cytokines trigger a cascade:

- Blood vessels near the infection widen and become more permeable (leaky) — this is **inflammation**. The area becomes red, warm, and swollen.
- The wider vessels let immune cells exit the bloodstream and pour into the tissue.
- Macrophages begin **phagocytosis** — engulfing pathogens and debris.
- Neutrophils are recruited in huge numbers (they are the most abundant white blood cell for a reason).

You might not feel anything yet. Or you might notice a slight soreness — that is the inflammation beginning.

</Step>

<Step title="Days 1–3: fever and the battle intensifies" kind="explain">

As the innate response scales up, one of the cytokines — interleukin-1 and others — signals the hypothalamus in the brain to raise the body's thermostat. **Fever** results.

Fever is not a malfunction — it is a deliberate immune strategy:

- Most bacteria and viruses replicate more slowly at 38–39 °C than at 37 °C.
- Fever speeds up immune cell activity — white blood cells become more active and move faster.
- Phagocytes and natural killer cells are killing infected cells and engulfing pathogens around the clock.

You feel ill because **cytokines themselves cause symptoms**: fatigue, muscle aches, loss of appetite. These are side effects of the immune battle, not direct damage from the pathogen (though pathogens cause harm too).

<Callout type="info">
Loss of appetite during infection is now thought to be partly adaptive — it shifts resources away from digestion toward immune activity, and may deprive some bacteria of key nutrients.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Days 4–10: adaptive immunity takes over" kind="explain">

While the innate system has been fighting, antigen-presenting cells (macrophages and dendritic cells) have been capturing pathogen fragments and carrying them to the **lymph nodes**, where B and T cells wait.

Here, B cells that happen to have a receptor shape matching the pathogen's antigens are activated, multiply rapidly, and begin producing **antibodies** in enormous quantities. T-helper cells coordinate the process; cytotoxic T cells hunt down infected body cells.

Antibodies work in several ways:

- **Neutralisation** — directly block the pathogen from entering cells.
- **Opsonisation** — coat the pathogen, marking it for phagocytes to devour.
- **Complement activation** — trigger a cascade that punches holes in bacterial membranes.

The combination of antibodies, phagocytes, and T cells overwhelms the pathogen. Pathogen numbers plummet. Symptoms fade. Recovery begins.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- When a pathogen breaches barriers, **macrophages and tissue cells detect it** via pattern recognition within minutes.
- **Cytokines** trigger inflammation — widened vessels, immune cell influx, phagocytosis.
- **Fever** is a deliberate immune strategy: it slows pathogen replication and speeds up immune cell activity.
- Symptoms (fatigue, aches, fever) are caused by **cytokines**, not purely by the pathogen itself.
- **Adaptive immunity** (B cells, antibodies, T cells) arrives after 4–7 days, clears the infection, and leaves memory cells.

</Step>
