<Step title="How common is this?" kind="explain">

Anxiety disorders and depression are among the most widespread health conditions on the planet — more common than diabetes, more common than asthma. According to the World Health Organization:

- **1 in 8 people worldwide** lives with a mental disorder at any given moment.
- **Depression affects around 280 million people** globally.
- **Anxiety disorders affect roughly 301 million people** globally.

These are not rare edge cases. In any classroom, office, or family, people around you — people who appear fine — may be managing these conditions right now. Understanding them reduces stigma and makes it easier for everyone to seek help.

</Step>

<Step title="What anxiety actually feels like" kind="explain">

Anxiety is the body's threat-response system running at the wrong time, in the wrong amount, or for too long. A certain level of anxiety is normal and useful — it sharpens focus before an important event. An anxiety *disorder* is when this system becomes dysregulated.

It can feel like:

- A constant low-level worry that is hard to switch off
- Physical sensations: tight chest, racing heart, shallow breathing, nausea
- Avoiding situations that trigger the feeling (which, over time, makes the feeling worse)
- Difficulty sleeping because the mind races at night
- A sense of impending dread without a clear cause

Anxiety is not "being nervous" or "overthinking as a personality trait." It is a medical condition involving changes in how the brain processes threat signals, and it responds well to treatment — talking therapies, lifestyle approaches, and sometimes medication.

</Step>

<Step title="What depression actually feels like" kind="explain">

Depression is more than feeling sad. Sadness is a natural emotion that comes and goes. Depression is a sustained change in mood, thinking, and physical functioning that affects every area of life.

It can feel like:

- A persistent flatness, emptiness, or heaviness — not necessarily crying, sometimes just numbness
- Loss of interest or pleasure in things that used to matter (called anhedonia)
- Exhaustion that rest does not fix
- Difficulty concentrating, making decisions, or remembering things
- Negative thoughts about oneself, the world, and the future that feel like facts rather than thoughts
- Changes in sleep, appetite, and energy

Depression is not laziness, weakness, or a bad attitude. It involves measurable changes in brain chemistry and neural circuitry. It is a medical condition, and it is highly treatable — most people who receive appropriate support recover significantly.

</Step>

<Step title="How thought patterns feed the loop" kind="interactive">

Both anxiety and depression are maintained, in part, by patterns of thinking. In anxiety, the mind catastrophises — jumping to worst-case interpretations. In depression, the mind globalises — one bad event becomes evidence that everything is always bad.

CBT works precisely because it targets these patterns. Recognising and gently questioning automatic thoughts is a core skill.

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- Anxiety and depression are among the most common health conditions worldwide — 1 in 8 people is affected at any given time.
- Anxiety is not just nervousness; it is a dysregulated threat-response system that causes real physical and mental distress.
- Depression is not just sadness; it is a sustained change in mood, thinking, energy, and functioning — a medical condition.
- Both conditions are maintained partly by thought patterns, which is why psychological therapies are effective.
- Both are real, common, and treatable. Seeking help is a sign of self-awareness and strength, not weakness.
