<Step title="What is the menstrual cycle?" kind="explain">

The **menstrual cycle** is a recurring sequence of hormonal and physical changes that prepares the body for a potential pregnancy. It runs on average about 28 days — but cycles of 21 to 35 days are all within normal range, and even healthy cycles vary from month to month in the same person.

The cycle is orchestrated by four key hormones:

- **FSH** (follicle-stimulating hormone): released by the pituitary gland; stimulates a follicle in an ovary to grow and mature.
- **Oestrogen:** produced by the developing follicle; rises through the first half of the cycle, triggering growth of the uterine lining and ultimately causing the LH surge.
- **LH** (luteinising hormone): a sharp surge around mid-cycle that triggers ovulation.
- **Progesterone:** produced after ovulation by the corpus luteum (the empty follicle); maintains the uterine lining and supports a potential pregnancy.

Day 1 of the cycle is defined as the first day of menstrual bleeding.

</Step>

<Step title="The four phases" kind="explain">

The cycle is divided into four overlapping phases:

**1. Menstrual phase (days 1–5 typically)**
Oestrogen and progesterone both fall, causing the uterine lining (endometrium) to break down and shed — this is a period. Flow typically lasts 2–7 days. Some cramping as the uterus contracts is normal.

**2. Follicular phase (days 1–13)**
Overlapping with menstruation, FSH stimulates several follicles to grow. The dominant follicle secretes rising oestrogen, which thickens the endometrium in preparation for a potential implantation.

**3. Ovulation (around day 14)**
The oestrogen peak triggers a sharp surge of LH from the pituitary. This causes the dominant follicle to rupture and release a mature egg. The egg travels down the fallopian tube, where it remains viable for roughly 12–24 hours. Sperm can survive in the reproductive tract for up to 5 days, so the fertile window extends a few days before ovulation.

**4. Luteal phase (days 15–28)**
The empty follicle transforms into the **corpus luteum**, which produces progesterone. This keeps the endometrium thick and receptive. If no fertilisation occurs, the corpus luteum breaks down around day 24–26, progesterone falls, and the lining begins to shed — starting a new cycle.

</Step>

<Step title="What is typical — and what to discuss with a doctor" kind="explain">

Most variation in periods is normal. Here is a guide:

**Typical:**
- Cycles of 21–35 days
- Periods lasting 2–7 days
- Some cramping, especially on day 1–2
- Flow that varies between cycles
- A few days of spotting or lighter bleeding

**Worth discussing with a doctor:**
- Cycles consistently shorter than 21 days or longer than 35 days
- Periods lasting longer than 7 days
- Very heavy flow (soaking through a pad or tampon every 1–2 hours for several hours)
- Severe pain that prevents normal activities (may indicate conditions like endometriosis)
- Absent periods for 3 or more months when not pregnant (amenorrhoea)
- Sudden significant changes in the pattern

Periods can also be affected by stress, significant weight changes, intense exercise, illness, and certain medications — all of which can temporarily alter cycle length or flow.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- The menstrual cycle is typically ~28 days but 21–35 days is normal; variation is common.
- **Day 1** = first day of bleeding. **Four phases:** menstrual, follicular, ovulation, luteal.
- **Key hormones:** FSH (grows follicle) → oestrogen (builds lining, triggers LH surge) → LH surge (causes ovulation) → progesterone (maintains lining post-ovulation).
- **Oestrogen** peaks just before ovulation; **progesterone** peaks in the luteal phase.
- The fertile window spans a few days before ovulation through the day of ovulation.
- Symptoms like mild cramping and variable flow are typical; severe pain or very heavy bleeding is worth discussing with a doctor.

</Step>
