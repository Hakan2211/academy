<Step title="Health has many sides" kind="explain">

When we say "health," we usually picture the body — muscles, blood pressure, the number on a scale. But a person is more than a body, and so is their health. Researchers usually describe several **dimensions of health** that together make up the whole:

- **Physical** — how your body works: fitness, nutrition, sleep, freedom from pain and disease.
- **Mental** — how your mind works: thinking clearly, managing thoughts, learning, focus.
- **Emotional** — how you feel and handle feelings: recognising emotions, coping, self-worth.
- **Social** — your relationships: friendship, family, belonging, support.
- **Purpose / spiritual** — meaning, values, goals, a sense that your life points somewhere.
- **Environmental** — the spaces you live in: a safe home, clean air and water, access to nature.

</Step>

<Step title="The dimensions hold each other up" kind="explain">

These aren't separate boxes — they're deeply tangled, and they prop each other up:

- A bad night's **sleep** (physical) wrecks your **mood** (emotional) and **focus** (mental) the next day.
- **Loneliness** (social) raises stress hormones and harms the **heart** (physical) as much as smoking does.
- A sense of **purpose** (spiritual) predicts how long and how well people live.

This is why you can't fix health one silo at a time. Pour everything into the gym while your relationships and sleep collapse, and your overall health still suffers. The strongest move is usually to shore up your *weakest* dimension, not to max out your strongest.

</Step>

<Step title="Build your wellness wheel" kind="interactive">

Picture your health as a wheel with a spoke for each dimension. Rate each one and watch the wheel take shape. A balanced life fills the wheel evenly — a life that's all high on one spoke and low on another makes a lopsided wheel that rolls badly.

<WellnessWheel />

There's no "right" shape, and it changes week to week. The value is in *noticing*: which spoke is shortest right now, and what's one small thing that would lengthen it?

</Step>

<Step title="Why bother splitting it up?" kind="explain">

Naming the dimensions does something useful: it widens what counts as "working on your health."

Feeling run-down isn't always a nutrition problem you can fix with a supplement. Sometimes the shortest spoke is **social** (you're isolated) or **purpose** (you're drifting) or **emotional** (you're carrying something heavy). The dimensions give you a map so you look in the right place instead of grinding harder on a spoke that's already long.

The rest of this island walks through the big spokes one by one — the body, food, movement, sleep, the mind, stress — so you can lengthen whichever needs it.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- Health has several **dimensions**: physical, mental, emotional, social, purpose, and environmental.
- The dimensions are **interconnected** — weakness in one drags the others down.
- A **wellness wheel** is a quick way to spot your shortest spoke.
- The smartest move is usually to strengthen your **weakest** dimension, not max out your strongest.
- Naming the dimensions widens what counts as "working on your health."

</Step>
