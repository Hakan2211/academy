<Step title="Two kinds of sugar" kind="explain">

Sugar is not one thing, and the distinction matters enormously:

**Naturally occurring sugars** are found inside whole foods — the fructose in an apple, the lactose in plain milk. These come packaged with fibre, water, protein, and micronutrients that slow absorption and provide genuine nutrition. The body handles these well.

**Added sugars** are extracted, concentrated, and added to food during processing or cooking — table sugar, high-fructose corn syrup, honey in a commercial product, dextrose in a sauce. They arrive without fibre, without protein, without the buffering matrix of a whole food. They are absorbed quickly, spike blood glucose, and deliver energy with no accompanying satiety signals.

Health authorities (WHO, NHS, Heart Foundation) recommend limiting **added sugar** to around **6 teaspoons (25 g) per day** for adults. They do not set limits on naturally occurring sugars in whole foods.

The challenge: **most people have no idea how much added sugar they consume**, because it is hidden across dozens of everyday products — and food labels often use 50+ different names for it (dextrose, maltose, evaporated cane juice, brown rice syrup…).

<Callout type="info">
A practical label rule: ingredients are listed in order of quantity. If any form of sugar appears in the first three ingredients, the product is high in added sugar.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Salt, sodium, and hidden sources" kind="explain">

**Salt** is sodium chloride (NaCl). The health concern is the **sodium** component — excess dietary sodium raises blood pressure in many people, increasing risk of heart disease and stroke.

Recommended daily limit: **around 6 g of salt (roughly one teaspoon)**, which contains about 2.4 g of sodium.

The problem is the same as sugar: most salt in the modern diet is **hidden in processed and restaurant food**, not added at the table. Research consistently finds that 70–75% of salt intake comes from packaged and prepared foods — bread, breakfast cereals, soups, sauces, deli meats, and cheese — long before anyone reaches for a salt shaker.

The result: people who think they "don't add much salt" may still be consuming two to three times the recommended daily amount.

Key label skills:
- **High in sodium:** >600 mg per 100 g (>1.5 g salt).
- **Low in sodium:** under 120 mg per 100 g (under 0.3 g salt).
- "Reduced salt" means less than the standard version — not necessarily low.

</Step>

<Step title="What is ultra-processed food?" kind="explain">

The NOVA classification system divides foods into four groups by level of processing. The most useful distinction is **Group 4: ultra-processed**.

Ultra-processed foods are industrial formulations made mostly from extracted substances — refined starch, hydrogenated oils, protein isolates, sugar — combined with additives not used in home cooking: emulsifiers, artificial flavours, colour enhancers, texturisers, and preservatives. They typically have more than five ingredients, and most of those ingredients are unrecognisable to a non-specialist.

Examples: soft drinks, packaged salty snacks, commercial biscuits, breakfast cereals with added sugar, instant noodles, flavoured yoghurts, processed meats, most fast food.

Why do they matter? Converging evidence links high ultra-processed food intake to:
- Higher total calorie intake (they are engineered to be hyper-palatable and easy to over-eat).
- Higher risk of obesity, type 2 diabetes, cardiovascular disease, and certain cancers.
- Displacement of whole foods that carry fibre, micronutrients, and protective compounds.

The "engineering to override" point is key: food companies spend billions optimising the **bliss point** — the precise combination of sugar, salt, fat, and texture that maximises repeated consumption and overrides satiety signals. This is not accidental; it is the business model.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Added sugar** (limit: ~6 tsp/day) is very different from naturally occurring sugar in whole foods — it arrives without fibre, protein, or satiety signals.
- **Hidden salt** is the bigger concern: 70–75% of sodium comes from processed foods, not the salt shaker. Daily limit: ~6 g salt.
- **Ultra-processed foods** (NOVA Group 4) are industrial formulations engineered to be hyper-palatable and easy to over-eat, displacing whole-food nutrition.
- **Label literacy**: ingredients in order of quantity; >600 mg sodium per 100 g = high in salt; sugar has 50+ names on labels.
- Eating more whole foods and fewer packaged products automatically reduces added sugar, salt, and ultra-processing.

</Step>
