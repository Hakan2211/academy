<Step title="Opening the brain" kind="explain">

The CPU — the **central processing unit** — is the part everyone calls the "brain" of a computer. It is a single chip, often smaller than a fingernail, holding billions of the transistors and gates we met earlier.

But what is *inside* it? When you peel back the lid, the brain turns out to be a few simple parts cooperating. None of them is mysterious. In fact, you have already built the most important one.

</Step>

<Step title="The parts inside" kind="explain">

A simple CPU has four kinds of part:

- The **ALU** (Arithmetic Logic Unit) — the calculator we built from gates. It adds, subtracts, compares and runs logic. This is the part that *does* the work.
- **Registers** — a tiny handful of ultra-fast storage slots, right next to the ALU, holding the numbers the CPU is using this very instant.
- The **control unit** — the conductor. It reads each instruction and sends signals telling every other part what to do and when.
- Two special registers: the **program counter (PC)**, which remembers the address of the next instruction, and the **instruction register (IR)**, which holds the instruction being worked on now.

</Step>

The registers are nothing exotic either: each is a row of **latches**, the feedback loops of gates that store a bit. Everything you see here is built from the logic of the previous world — there is no new magic, only new arrangement.

</Step>

<Step title="What the control unit really does" kind="explain">

The **control unit** is the part that turns a CPU from a calculator into a *programmable* machine. The ALU can add two numbers, but it has no idea *which* numbers, or whether you wanted to add at all. The control unit answers those questions.

It reads the instruction in the IR, decodes the opcode, and then flips exactly the right control signals: open this register onto the ALU's input, set the ALU to "add", route the result back to that register. Then it nudges the **program counter** forward so the next instruction is ready.

<Callout type="info" title="Why registers are so fast">
Registers sit *inside* the CPU, beside the ALU. Reading one takes essentially a single clock tick — far faster than reaching out to RAM. The catch is there are only a few of them, so the CPU constantly juggles values between registers and memory.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- The **CPU** is a chip of billions of gates, made of a few cooperating parts.
- The **ALU** does the arithmetic and logic — it is the calculator built from gates in the last world.
- **Registers** are a few ultra-fast storage slots beside the ALU; each is a row of latches.
- The **control unit** decodes instructions and conducts the other parts; the **program counter** tracks the next instruction and the **instruction register** holds the current one.

</Step>
