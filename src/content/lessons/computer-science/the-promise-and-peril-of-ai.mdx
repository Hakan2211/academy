<Step title="A tool of extraordinary reach" kind="explain">

Step back and take in what you've built across this world: search that explores vast spaces, models that learn patterns from data, neural networks that approximate almost any function, language models that write and reason, robots that perceive a noisy world. Together they form the most powerful, general-purpose technology since electricity.

The promise is genuine and already arriving. AI systems detect tumours in scans earlier than radiologists, predicted the 3D structure of nearly every known protein, tutor students one-to-one, draft and debug code, translate between languages in real time, and accelerate scientific discovery from materials to medicine. Used well, this is a tool that amplifies human capability on a historic scale.

But a technology this powerful demands clear eyes. The same systems have sharp limits and real dangers, and the goal of this final lesson is neither hype nor doom — it's **understanding well enough to use them wisely**.

</Step>

<Step title="What AI is — and isn't — good at" kind="explain">

It helps to be precise about the strengths. Modern AI excels at **pattern recognition** in huge datasets, at fluent **generation** of text, images and code, at tireless **scale and speed**, and at finding signals in noise that humans miss. Within a domain it has trained on, it can be superhuman.

Yet the limits are just as real. An AI has no genuine **understanding** of meaning — it manipulates patterns, not concepts. It has no **common sense** beyond what statistics imply, struggles with truly **novel** situations outside its training, and cannot explain *why* it reached an answer (the **black-box** problem). It does not know what it doesn't know. Treating a brilliant pattern-matcher as if it were a reasoning, accountable mind is the source of most AI mishaps.

</Step>

<Step title="Bias: the model learns our flaws" kind="interactive">

Recall the central lesson of machine learning: a model learns whatever patterns are in its **data**. That cuts both ways. If the data reflects historical bias, the model absorbs and can *amplify* it — a hiring AI trained on past hires may quietly penalise groups who were underrepresented; a facial system trained mostly on one skin tone performs worse on others.

<MlClassifier />

Notice how the boundary you draw depends entirely on the points you're given. Feed it a skewed sample and it confidently learns a skewed rule — then applies that rule at scale, to millions of people, with the false authority of a machine. **Bias in, bias out**, but faster and harder to question. Fairness isn't an afterthought; it's a property of the data and the choices behind it.

</Step>

<Step title="Hallucination: confidently wrong" kind="interactive">

Because a language model **generates** the next likely token rather than retrieving a fact, it can produce statements that are fluent, plausible, and completely false — invented citations, fake quotes, confident wrong answers. This is called **hallucination**, and it's not a bug to be fully patched away; it's a direct consequence of how the model works.

<TokenPredictor />

The model picks whatever continuation is statistically likely — and "likely-sounding" is not the same as "true." Raise the temperature and it strays further from safe ground. This is why the golden rule of using AI is to **verify**: treat its output as a fast, fallible draft from a confident assistant who sometimes makes things up, never as a final authority. The fluency is exactly what makes the errors dangerous.

</Step>

<Step title="Jobs, society, and concentration" kind="explain">

Every major automation wave has reshaped work, and AI is no exception. It won't simply "replace humans"; more often it shifts *which* tasks people do — automating the routine parts of a job while raising the value of judgement, creativity, and oversight. Some roles shrink, new ones appear, and the transition can be painful and uneven. The honest answer is that the *net* effect is genuinely uncertain and depends heavily on the choices societies make.

There are quieter concerns too: the enormous **energy and compute** that training takes; the **concentration of power** in the few organisations that can afford it; the flood of cheap synthetic text, images and **deepfakes** that erode our shared sense of what's real. None of these are reasons to abandon the technology — they're reasons to govern it thoughtfully.

</Step>

<Step title="Alignment: making powerful systems do what we mean" kind="explain">

As systems grow more capable, a deeper question moves to centre stage: the **alignment** problem. How do we ensure an AI pursues what we actually *intend*, not a literal or distorted version of it? A system optimising hard for the wrong objective — engagement, clicks, a misread goal — can cause real harm precisely *because* it's effective.

This is why **AI safety** has become a serious field: not the cinematic fear of conscious robots, but the down-to-earth challenge of building systems that are reliable, honest, controllable, and steered toward human values even as they grow more powerful. The harder problem isn't making AI smart; it's making powerful AI *trustworthy*.

<Callout type="info" title="Neither hype nor doom">
The most useful stance is sober optimism. AI is a transformative tool, not a magic oracle and not an impending apocalypse. Its impact — for good or ill — will be decided less by the technology itself than by the wisdom, care, and intentions of the people who build and wield it.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Using AI well" kind="explain">

So how should *you* engage with all this? A few principles fall out of everything you've learned. **Verify** what AI tells you, especially facts and figures — it is fluent, not infallible. Understand it as a **pattern-matcher**, powerful within its training and unreliable beyond it. Stay alert to **bias** and ask what data shaped a system's behaviour. Keep a **human in the loop** for decisions that carry real weight — medical, legal, financial, moral.

Most of all, treat AI as an **amplifier of human intent**. It will make a careful, knowledgeable person dramatically more capable — and a careless one dramatically more dangerous. The intelligence in the loop is still, and for the foreseeable future, *yours*.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- AI is a **general-purpose amplifier** with real, arriving benefits — from medicine and science to coding and education.
- It excels at **pattern recognition and generation** but lacks true understanding, common sense, and explainability (the **black-box** problem).
- **Bias in, bias out**: a model learns the flaws in its data and applies them at scale with false authority.
- **Hallucination** is intrinsic to next-token generation — fluent output can be confidently false, so always **verify**.
- AI reshapes **jobs** unevenly and raises concerns about energy, power concentration, and deepfakes; the net effect depends on human choices.
- The deep challenge is **alignment and safety** — making powerful systems trustworthy — and the wise stance is **sober optimism**: AI amplifies the intent of whoever wields it.

</Step>
