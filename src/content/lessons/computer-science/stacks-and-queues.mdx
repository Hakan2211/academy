<Lesson>

<Step title="Adding a rule changes everything" kind="explain">

An array lets you poke at any slot you like. But sometimes the *discipline* of restricting **where** you can add and remove is exactly what makes a structure useful. Two such structures show up everywhere in computing: the **stack** and the **queue**.

Both hold a sequence of items. The only difference is **which end** you're allowed to touch — and that single rule completely changes their behaviour.

</Step>

<Step title="Stack: last in, first out" kind="explain">

A **stack** works like a pile of plates: you add to the **top** and take from the **top**. The last thing you put on is the first thing you take off. We call this **LIFO** — **L**ast **I**n, **F**irst **O**ut.

Its two operations have classic names:

- **push** — put an item on top.
- **pop** — take the top item off.

You can't reach into the middle of the pile, and that limitation is the point — it keeps things simple and fast.

</Step>

<Step title="Queue: first in, first out" kind="interactive">

A **queue** works like a line at a shop: you join at the **back** and you're served from the **front**. The first to arrive is the first to leave — **FIFO**, **F**irst **I**n, **F**irst **O**ut. Its operations are **enqueue** (add at the back) and **dequeue** (remove from the front).

Flip between the two below. Add several items, then remove them and watch *which* item leaves.

<StackQueue />

Same items, opposite order out. With the **stack** the newest leaves first; with the **queue** the oldest leaves first.

</Step>

<Step title="Where they show up" kind="explain">

These two patterns are quietly everywhere:

- **Stacks** — the **undo** history in your editor (undo the most recent change first); the **call stack** that tracks which function called which; the back button in a browser.
- **Queues** — a **print queue** (documents print in the order sent); tasks waiting for a server; and the heart of **breadth-first search**, the graph traversal we'll meet soon.

<Callout type="info" title="Recursion runs on a stack">
Every time a function calls another, the computer **pushes** a frame onto the call stack; when the function returns, it **pops** back off. That LIFO discipline is exactly why recursion unwinds in reverse order — the most recent call finishes first.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- A **stack** is **LIFO**: **push** and **pop** only at the **top** — newest out first.
- A **queue** is **FIFO**: **enqueue** at the back, **dequeue** at the front — oldest out first.
- The restriction on *where* you add/remove is the feature, not a limitation.
- Stacks power **undo**, the **call stack**, and back buttons; queues power **print spools**, task lists, and **BFS**.

</Step>
