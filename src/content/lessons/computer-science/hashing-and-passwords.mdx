<Step title="A fingerprint for data" kind="explain">

Encryption is two-way: you scramble data so you can later unscramble it. But sometimes you want the opposite — a calculation that is deliberately **impossible to reverse**. That is a **hash function**: it takes any input, of any length, and squeezes it into a short, fixed-length **digest** that acts like a fingerprint.

Two properties make hashes useful. The same input always gives the same digest, so a fingerprint is repeatable. Yet from the digest alone you cannot recover the input — the function is **one-way**. These twin facts turn out to solve two very different security problems: checking that data is unchanged, and storing passwords safely.

</Step>

<Step title="Hashing in action" kind="interactive">

Type something and watch it collapse into a fixed-length digest. Then notice what happens when you change a single character.

<HashViz />

That dramatic, total change from a tiny tweak is the **avalanche effect**, and it is on purpose: a good hash spreads every bit of input across the whole output. It means you cannot nudge your way backwards toward the original, and even a one-character difference produces a completely unrelated fingerprint.

</Step>

<Step title="Never store the password" kind="explain">

Here is the security payoff. A responsible website **never stores your actual password**. Instead it stores the *hash* of your password. When you log in, it hashes what you typed and compares the two digests. Match? You are in — and the site never had to keep your secret around.

The benefit is enormous: if attackers steal the database, they get a pile of one-way digests, not passwords. They cannot reverse a hash to recover the original. (They can still *guess* common passwords and hash each guess to see if it matches — which is exactly why a strong, uncommon password matters, as we will see next.)

<Callout type="warn" title="If a site emails you your password">
A site that can show you your *original* password stored it in plaintext — a serious red flag. Proper systems only ever keep the hash and literally cannot recover what you typed.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Salting: defeating the lookup table" kind="explain">

There is a clever attack on hashed passwords: precompute the hashes of millions of common passwords once, then look up any stolen digest in that table instantly. The defence is a **salt** — a unique random string added to each password *before* hashing.

Because every user's salt is different, identical passwords produce different digests, and the attacker's giant precomputed table becomes useless — they would have to redo all that work for every single salt. Salting does not make a weak password strong, but it forces attackers to attack each account separately, the slow way.

</Step>

<Step title="Integrity checks" kind="explain">

Hashes have a second life far from passwords. Download a large file and the site often publishes its hash. Hash your downloaded copy; if the digests match, your file is **bit-for-bit identical** to the original — uncorrupted and untampered. A single flipped bit would avalanche into a totally different digest, exposing the change at once.

This is **integrity** — the "I" of the CIA triad — built directly from one-wayness and the avalanche effect. The same idea secures software updates, verifies backups, and links the blocks of a blockchain. One humble fixed-length fingerprint, quietly guarding trust everywhere.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- A **hash function** maps any input to a short, fixed-length **digest** that is repeatable but **one-way** (cannot be reversed).
- The **avalanche effect** means one changed character produces a completely different digest.
- Safe systems store the **hash** of a password, never the password itself — a stolen database yields unreversible digests, not secrets.
- A unique **salt** per password defeats precomputed lookup tables, and matching hashes verify **integrity** for downloads, updates, and backups.

</Step>
