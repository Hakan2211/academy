<Step title="Assembling the whole pipeline" kind="explain">

You now hold every piece of computer graphics. This deep dive bolts them together into the machine that builds entire virtual worlds — the worlds of games, films, simulations and VR.

Recall the data-representation island, where an image was a **grid of numbers**. Everything since has been one long answer to a single question: *how do you turn numbers and maths into believable images and motion?* Pixels gave us a canvas; transforms let us move shapes; meshes gave us 3D form; lighting made it look solid; animation made it move. The **rendering pipeline** is the assembly line that runs all of it, dozens of times a second.

</Step>

<Step title="The rendering pipeline, stage by stage" kind="explain">

To draw a single frame, the computer marches every model through a fixed sequence of stages:

- **Model** — start with a mesh: vertices, edges and faces in their own local space.
- **Transform** — translate, rotate and scale each model into the shared **world**, then into the camera's view (the matrix maths from the 2D lesson, now in 3D).
- **Project** — flatten the 3D scene onto the 2D screen with perspective, so distant things shrink toward a vanishing point.
- **Rasterize / shade** — work out which pixels each triangle covers and compute each pixel's colour from the lighting (the `N · L` dot product), resolving which surfaces are in front with a depth buffer.
- **Frame** — the finished grid of pixels is shown, then the whole pipeline runs again for the next frame.

Model, transform, project, shade, repeat — that loop, running fast enough, *is* a virtual world.

</Step>

<Step title="The GPU: doing it a million times at once" kind="explain">

Here's the catch that shapes everything: a single frame might need the *same* calculation — transform this vertex, shade this pixel — run for **millions** of points. A normal CPU, with a few powerful cores, would crawl.

So graphics runs on a **GPU** (graphics processing unit): a chip with **thousands** of small cores built to do the same simple operation on heaps of data simultaneously. This is **parallelism**. Because every vertex transform and every pixel's `N · L` is independent, they can all be computed at the same time. The GPU is why your phone can render a 3D world sixty times a second — and, as it happens, why the very same chips now power machine learning, which is also drowning in identical parallel maths.

<Callout type="tip" title="Why graphics drove parallel hardware">
Computer graphics is *embarrassingly parallel* — a sea of identical, independent calculations. That demand is exactly what pushed the industry to build massively parallel GPUs, hardware that has since transformed far more than games.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Real-time vs offline: games and films" kind="explain">

The same pipeline runs under two very different deadlines.

**Real-time rendering** powers games and VR: each frame must be finished in a few **milliseconds**, live, in response to your input. It leans on fast **rasterization** and clever shortcuts — pre-baked shadows, approximate reflections — to stay smooth. The goal is *fast enough to fool you in motion*.

**Offline rendering** powers feature films: there's no rush, so each frame can take **hours**, computed long before you watch. It uses **ray tracing**, following light rays as they bounce, refract and scatter through a scene to produce reflections and shadows of near-photographic accuracy. The goal is *perfect, whatever it costs*.

The two are converging fast. Modern GPUs now do **real-time ray tracing** — bringing film-grade lighting into live games — and trained neural networks **upscale and denoise** frames so the hardware can render less and still look superb.

</Step>

<Step title="Where graphics is going" kind="explain">

Three currents are pulling the field forward. **Real-time ray tracing** is erasing the old gap between game and film quality. **Virtual and augmented reality** demand two high-resolution frames at very high fps with near-zero lag — graphics' most punishing challenge yet. And **AI-assisted rendering** uses neural networks to generate, upscale and even invent frames, blurring the line between *computing* an image and *learning* one.

The constant through all of it is the idea you've carried since the first island: **everything is numbers, and clever maths turns those numbers into worlds.** A glowing planet, a rushing river, a character's smile — each is, underneath, vertices transformed, points projected, and dot products of light against surface, computed millions of times a second.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- The **rendering pipeline** runs every model through **model → transform → project → rasterize/shade → frame**, repeated each frame.
- Each stage is the maths from earlier lessons: transforms, perspective projection, and `N · L` shading with a depth buffer.
- A **GPU** finishes frames fast by **parallelism** — thousands of cores doing the same independent calculation at once.
- **Real-time** rendering (games/VR, milliseconds, rasterization) trades accuracy for speed; **offline** rendering (films, hours, ray tracing) trades speed for photorealism.
- The frontier is **real-time ray tracing, VR/AR, and AI-assisted rendering** — but it all stays *numbers turned into worlds by maths*.

</Step>
