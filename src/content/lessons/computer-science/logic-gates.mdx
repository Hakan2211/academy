<Step title="Logic made physical" kind="explain">

So far logic has been abstract — true and false. A **logic gate** makes it real: a tiny electronic circuit that takes one or two bits as input (as voltages) and outputs a bit, following one fixed rule.

Gates are built from **transistors** — microscopic switches where one electrical signal controls another. A few transistors wired together make a gate; that's the bridge from the physics of silicon up to the logic of true and false.

</Step>

<Step title="The gate symbols" kind="interactive">

Notice the small **bubble** on the output of NOT, NAND and NOR — that bubble always means "invert." A NAND is literally an AND with an inverting bubble stuck on the end.

</Step>

<Step title="Why gates are the perfect building block" kind="explain">

Gates have three properties that make them ideal to build with:

- **They're fast** — a modern gate switches in well under a *billionth* of a second.
- **They're tiny** — billions fit on a chip the size of a fingernail.
- **They snap to clean 1s and 0s** — each gate restores the signal to a crisp high or low, so errors don't accumulate as signals pass through millions of them.

<Callout type="info" title="From switch to thought">
A single transistor is just a switch. A few make a gate. Thousands of gates make an adder or a memory cell. Billions make a processor that can run a video game or a language model. It's abstraction stacked dizzyingly high — and it all starts here.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Gates have no memory (yet)" kind="explain">

One crucial limitation: a basic gate's output depends *only* on its current inputs. Change the inputs and the output instantly follows. This is called **combinational** logic — it computes, but it can't *remember*.

Yet computers clearly remember things. The trick is to wire gates' outputs back into their own inputs in a loop, creating a circuit that holds onto a value — a **latch**, the seed of memory. We'll meet that idea in this world's capstone. First, let's connect gates together to do something genuinely useful: arithmetic.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- A **logic gate** is a small circuit (built from **transistors**) that computes a logic operation on bits.
- Each operation has a **standard symbol**; an output **bubble** means "invert" (NOT, NAND, NOR).
- Gates are **fast, tiny, and self-correcting** to clean 1s and 0s — perfect to stack by the billion.
- Basic **combinational** gates compute but can't remember; feeding outputs back creates memory (a latch).

</Step>
