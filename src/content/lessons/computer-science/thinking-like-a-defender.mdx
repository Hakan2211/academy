<Lesson>

<Step title="What are we actually protecting?" kind="explain">

Before you can defend a system, you need a clear answer to a deceptively simple question: protect it from *what*? Security people answer with three goals, known together as the **CIA triad** — and it has nothing to do with spies.

- **Confidentiality** — only the right people can *read* the data. (Your messages stay private.)
- **Integrity** — the data cannot be *altered* without detection. (Your bank balance is not silently edited.)
- **Availability** — the system is *there when you need it*. (The site does not go down.)

Every attack in the last lesson maps onto these. Eavesdropping breaks confidentiality, tampering breaks integrity, a DDoS flood breaks availability. Defence means deciding which of the three matters most for *your* data, and guarding it.

</Step>

<Step title="The same threats, through a defender's eyes" kind="interactive">

Revisit the threat gallery — but this time, for each attack, ask which corner of the CIA triad it breaks, and notice how the defence restores it.

<ThreatGallery />

A man-in-the-middle attacker reading your traffic is a **confidentiality** failure that encryption fixes. Ransomware encrypting your files is an **availability** failure that backups fix. The triad gives you a checklist for thinking about any new system.

</Step>

<Step title="Defence in depth & least privilege" kind="explain">

No single wall is unbreakable, so good defenders never rely on one. **Defence in depth** stacks independent layers — a firewall, *and* encryption, *and* logging, *and* trained staff — so that breaching one still leaves the attacker facing the next. A castle has a moat *and* walls *and* guards.

The second great principle is **least privilege**: give every user, program, and process exactly the access it needs to do its job, and not one drop more. If a hijacked account can only read one folder, the damage is contained to that folder. Most catastrophic breaches are really small breaches that were allowed to spread.

<Callout type="tip" title="Fail safe, not open">
Well-designed systems fail *closed*: when something goes wrong, the default is to deny access, not grant it. A locked door that jams should stay locked, not spring open.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Humans are the weakest link" kind="explain">

The most sophisticated attacks often skip the technology entirely. **Social engineering** — phishing, pretexting, a confident phone call from "IT" — manipulates people into opening the door themselves. No firewall blocks a freely given password.

So a defender's job includes the human layer: training people to pause and verify, designing systems that do not *ask* for risky actions, and assuming that someone, somewhere, will eventually be fooled. Security is a property of people and processes, not just code.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- The **CIA triad** names what we protect: **Confidentiality** (only the right people read it), **Integrity** (it cannot be secretly altered), **Availability** (it is there when needed).
- **Defence in depth** stacks independent layers so breaching one still leaves more to defeat.
- **Least privilege** gives each user and program only the access it truly needs, containing damage when an account is compromised.
- **Humans are the weakest link** — social engineering bypasses technology, so defence must include people and processes, and systems should **fail closed**.

</Step>
