<Lesson>

<Step title="The screen is a grid of squares" kind="explain">

Back in the data-representation world you saw that an image is a **grid of pixels**, each a number naming a colour. Computer graphics starts from that same fact and asks a harder question: how do you *draw* onto such a grid?

The trouble is that the things we want to draw — circles, curves, smooth diagonal lines — are continuous and infinitely detailed. The screen is not. It's a fixed mesh of little square tiles, and each tile can only be one solid colour. This kind of image, built from a grid of coloured cells, is called **raster graphics** (from the Latin for "rake", because the screen is scanned row by row).

So drawing a shape really means **deciding, for every square, what colour it should be** to best approximate the smooth ideal.

</Step>

<Step title="Where the jaggies come from" kind="interactive">

Try to draw a circle on a coarse grid and the all-or-nothing choice — "is this square inside the circle or not?" — produces a staircase of blocky steps along the edges. These steps are called **aliasing**, and the jagged result is affectionately known as the **jaggies**.

<RasterCanvas />

Two things fight the jaggies. **Resolution** — packing in more, smaller pixels — shrinks each step until your eye can't see it. And **anti-aliasing** cheats: instead of every edge pixel being fully on or off, it greys them in proportion to how much of the square the shape actually covers. A partly-covered pixel becomes a soft in-between shade, and the staircase melts into a smooth-looking edge.

</Step>

<Step title="Raster vs vector" kind="explain">

A raster image is a fixed grid of pixels, so it has a built-in **resolution**. Blow it up beyond that and there's no extra detail to show — you just see the squares get bigger. Photos, screenshots and camera output are all raster.

The alternative is **vector graphics**: instead of storing pixels, store the *shapes* themselves as math — "a circle of radius 5 centred here", "a line from A to B". Because it's a recipe rather than a picture, the computer can re-draw it crisply at any size. Logos, fonts and icons are usually vectors. The catch: vectors can't easily capture the messy detail of a photograph. The next lesson dives into how those shapes are stored and transformed.

<Callout type="tip" title="Two jobs, two formats">
Use **raster** (PNG, JPEG) for photos and rich, detailed images. Use **vector** (SVG) for logos and diagrams that must stay sharp at every size. Picking the right one is half of looking professional.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Raster graphics** represent an image as a fixed **grid of square pixels**, each one solid colour.
- Smooth shapes must be **approximated** square-by-square, producing jagged **aliasing** ("jaggies").
- **Higher resolution** (more, smaller pixels) and **anti-aliasing** (greyed edge pixels) both hide the steps.
- **Vector graphics** instead store shapes as math, so they stay crisp at any size — great for logos and type, poor for photos.

</Step>
