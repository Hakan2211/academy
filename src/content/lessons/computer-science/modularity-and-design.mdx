<Lesson>

<Step title="No one can hold it all in their head" kind="explain">

A serious piece of software has millions of lines of code — far more than any human can keep in mind at once. So how does anyone build, or even understand, something that big?

The same way we tamed the complexity of a computer itself, back in the abstraction world: by **breaking the whole into parts**, where each part hides its messy insides behind a simple promise. In software we call those parts **modules**, and the art of dividing the system well is **design**.

</Step>

<Step title="Monolith vs modular" kind="interactive">

Picture the same app built two ways. Toggle between them, then "break" a component and watch how far the damage spreads.

<ModularityViz />

In the **monolith**, every part reaches into every other part. There's no boundary, so one failure cascades everywhere and every change is risky. In the **modular** version, each box talks only through a defined **interface** — so a broken component is contained, and you can rework one box without disturbing the rest.

</Step>

<Step title="Coupling and cohesion" kind="explain">

Two words capture good design.

**Coupling** is how tightly modules depend on each other. The monolith was *tightly coupled* — everything tangled together. We want **loose coupling**: modules connected only through narrow, well-defined interfaces, so each can change independently.

**Cohesion** is how focused a single module is. A module with *high cohesion* does one clear job (handle payments, and only payments). A *low-cohesion* module is a junk drawer of unrelated tasks. The mantra:

```
Good design = loose coupling + high cohesion.
```

Each module is a little abstraction: it offers a simple interface and hides how it works inside — exactly the idea from World 1, now applied to your own code.

</Step>

<Step title="Interfaces and reusable patterns" kind="explain">

The **interface** is the contract between modules: *call me this way, and I promise this result* — without revealing how I do it. As long as the promise holds, you can completely rewrite a module's insides and nothing else needs to change. That's what makes large teams possible: each person owns a module and trusts the interfaces around it.

Over decades, engineers noticed the *same shapes* of problems coming up again and again, and named reusable solutions for them — **design patterns**. You don't need them yet, but the instinct behind them is everything: spot the recurring structure, and reuse a proven design instead of reinventing a tangled one.

<Callout type="tip" title="Decomposition, again">
Breaking a problem into well-chosen modules is just **decomposition** — one of the four pillars of computational thinking — applied to building software. The big ideas keep coming back.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- Big systems are tamed by splitting them into **modules**, each hiding its internals behind an **interface**.
- A **monolith** tangles everything together; modular design **contains** failures and changes.
- Aim for **loose coupling** (modules barely depend on each other) and **high cohesion** (each does one clear job).
- Modularity is **abstraction and decomposition** applied to your own code — and **design patterns** are reusable solutions to recurring shapes of problems.

</Step>
