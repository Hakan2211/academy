<Lesson>

<Step title="Standing at the summit" kind="explain">

You have reached the top of the island. From here, look back at the whole climb: a single **bit**, two states on a wire; **gates** computing logic on bits; gates wired into an adder, a CPU, a whole **computer**; then **algorithms**, **data structures**, **languages**, **operating systems**, **networks**, the **web**, **databases**, **security**, **graphics**, **AI**. Layer upon layer, each built honestly from the one below.

This final world asked the deepest question of all — not *how* to compute, but *what computation is*, and where its **absolute edges** lie. Let's draw the complete map.

</Step>

<Step title="The map of all problems" kind="interactive">

Every problem you could ever pose to a computer falls somewhere on this map. The first, hardest line divides **decidable** (an algorithm exists) from **undecidable** (none can, ever). Inside decidable, a second line splits **tractable** (efficient) from **intractable** (correct but exponential). Click each zone.

<DecidabilityMap />

Three tiers, in widening circles of difficulty:

- **Tractable** — adding numbers, sorting, routing. The bright, comfortable home of everyday software.
- **Intractable** — optimal n×n chess, exact travelling salesman. Solvable in principle, hopeless at scale.
- **Undecidable** — does this program halt? No algorithm, on any machine, forever.

Most of computing — and almost all of what feels magical about it — happens in that thin, bright tractable band.

</Step>

<Step title="One thesis ties it together" kind="explain">

What lets us speak of "all computers at once" is the **Church–Turing thesis**: every reasonable model of computation — Turing machines, lambda calculus, your laptop, every programming language — has **exactly the same computational power**. They differ in speed and elegance, never in what is *possible*.

So the map isn't a map of one machine's limits. It is the map for **every** machine, because they are all the same machine in disguise. When we proved the halting problem undecidable for a Turing machine, we proved it for your phone, the largest supercomputer, and every computer not yet invented.

<Callout type="info" title="The boundaries are made of logic, not silicon">
These edges were charted by **pure reason** — Turing in 1936, before a single electronic computer ran. They are theorems, not engineering limits. No future technology relocates them, any more than a faster engine lets you reach the end of the number line.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Back to the gates we built" kind="explain">

Remember the gates-to-a-computer world? A real computer, you saw, is **logic and memory marching to a clock** — gates wired into an ALU, latches wired into RAM, a fetch–decode–execute loop. That machine is, in the precise sense of this world, a **Turing machine** with finite memory: a head (the CPU) reading and writing cells (RAM) by a rule table (its instructions).

So the abstract tape from this world and the silicon you built earlier are the **same idea**, seen from two ends. Turing imagined the machine in 1936; engineers built it from transistors a decade later. The theory came *first*, and it told us — exactly — what the hardware could and could not ever do.

</Step>

<Step title="The open frontiers" kind="interactive">

The map is mostly drawn, but two great regions remain unsettled — and they keep this the most alive field in computing.

The first is the boundary *within* the decidable: **P =? NP**. Are problems that are easy to check also easy to solve? Revisit the contradiction at the heart of undecidability, then sit with the fact that the P-vs-NP line is still, after fifty years, undrawn.

<HaltingProblem />

The second frontier is **quantum computing**. Quantum machines exploit superposition to speed up *certain* problems — most famously factoring, which threatens some of today's cryptography. But be precise: quantum computers are **not** believed to solve NP-complete problems efficiently, and they compute exactly the same *set* of functions as Turing machines. They may shift the **tractable/intractable** line for special problems — they do **not** cross the **decidable/undecidable** line. The undecidable stays undecidable.

</Step>

<Step title="A closing reflection" kind="explain">

So here is the whole island in one breath. **Bits** become **logic**; logic becomes a **machine**; the machine runs **algorithms** on **data**, spoken in **languages**, organised by **operating systems**, connected through **networks** into a **web**, remembered in **databases**, defended by **security**, painted by **graphics**, and lately, learning through **AI**. And all of it — every layer — is the patient repetition of one humble act: a switch flipping, a symbol read, a rule obeyed.

The summit's gift is **perspective**: this boundless tower of capability rests on a foundation we fully understand, *and* it has edges we have proved it can never cross. Computing is neither magic nor limitless. It is the art of doing the **possible** beautifully — while knowing, with mathematical certainty, exactly where the possible ends.

<Callout type="tip" title="The end of the climb">
You began with one bit and two states. You end knowing what *every* computer can do, what none can do quickly, and what none can ever do at all. That is the beautiful, hard edge of computation — and you can see all the way to it now.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- All problems split into **decidable vs undecidable**, and decidable into **tractable vs intractable** — the complete map of computability.
- The **Church–Turing thesis** makes that map universal: every computer has the same computational power, so the limits hold for all of them.
- A real computer is a **Turing machine** in silicon — the gates-to-a-computer build and Turing's tape are the same idea from two ends.
- Open frontiers remain: **P =? NP**, and **quantum computing** (which shifts *tractability* for some problems but never crosses into the undecidable).
- The island in one line: **bits → gates → machine → algorithms → … → AI** — boundless capability on understood foundations, with edges proven by **pure reason**.

</Step>
