<Lesson>

<Step title="The output of one is the input of the next" kind="explain">

A single gate is limited. The power comes from **wiring gates together**: feed the output of one gate into the input of another, and you can build circuits that compute far more than any basic operation alone.

This is just the **composition** of Boolean logic made physical. Where the logician writes `(A OR B) AND NOT(A AND B)`, the engineer wires an OR, an AND and a NOT together so the electricity computes the same thing.

</Step>

<Step title="Building XOR from the basics" kind="interactive">

Let's build something useful: an XOR ("differ") behaviour, using only AND, OR and NOT. The recipe is `(A OR B) AND NOT(A AND B)` — "at least one is on, but not both." Flip A and B and trace each green signal through the circuit.

<GateCircuit />

The whole tangle behaves as a *single* XOR gate. We can now draw a box labelled "XOR" and forget the wiring inside — a new, higher-level building block. That's abstraction, growing right before your eyes.

</Step>

<Step title="Layers of building blocks" kind="explain">

This is how all digital hardware is designed. You never reason about billions of gates at once. Instead:

- Wire a few gates into a small block (an adder, a comparator, a selector).
- Treat that block as a single component with its own "truth table."
- Wire those blocks into bigger blocks (an arithmetic unit, a memory).
- Repeat, until you've built a processor.

<Callout type="tip" title="Hide the wiring, keep the promise">
Each block hides its internal gates behind a clean interface, exactly like the abstraction layers from the foundations world. A chip designer thinks in adders and registers, not individual transistors — the same way a programmer thinks in functions, not machine code.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Combinational logic, recapped" kind="explain">

Everything in this lesson is still **combinational**: the outputs depend only on the current inputs, with no memory of the past. But that's already enough to build the entire *calculating* part of a computer — anything that transforms inputs into outputs.

Adders, multipliers, comparators, the circuit that decodes an instruction, the unit that picks one of many inputs (a multiplexer) — all are just clever arrangements of gates. In the next lesson we wire gates into the most important block of all: one that adds.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- Gates **compose**: one gate's output feeds another's input, building richer circuits.
- Example: **XOR** can be built from `(A OR B) AND NOT(A AND B)`.
- Designers wire gates into **blocks**, treat each as one component, and stack blocks into a processor.
- This is all **combinational** logic (no memory), yet enough to build a computer's entire calculating core.

</Step>
