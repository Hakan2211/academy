<Step title="From 2D points to 3D points" kind="explain">

In 2D every point had two numbers, `(x, y)`. Step into 3D and we add a third, `(x, y, z)`, where `z` measures depth — how near or far a point sits from the viewer. That single extra number is the whole leap from flat pictures to a world with space in it.

A 3D object is built from three kinds of thing:

- **Vertices** — the points in space, each an `(x, y, z)`.
- **Edges** — straight lines joining pairs of vertices.
- **Faces** — flat polygons (usually triangles) bounded by edges.

Together they form a **mesh** — the skeleton-and-skin of every 3D model, from a game character to a film monster.

</Step>

<Step title="A mesh you can spin" kind="interactive">

The simplest interesting mesh is a cube: **8 vertices, 12 edges, 6 faces**. Below, those eight points are stored as 3D coordinates and rotated a little every frame. Then comes the crucial trick — the computer **projects** each 3D point onto the flat 2D screen so it can actually be drawn.

<WireframeMesh />

A **wireframe** like this shows only the edges, so you can see straight through the model. It feels three-dimensional even though every line is computed from plain coordinates spun by a rotation formula and flattened onto your screen.

</Step>

<Step title="Projecting 3D onto a flat screen" kind="explain">

Your screen is two-dimensional, so every 3D point must be squashed down to a 2D `(x, y)` to be drawn. The most realistic way is **perspective projection**, which mimics how an eye or camera works: things farther away look smaller. A point at depth `z` is scaled by its distance using something like:

<Formula tex="x' = \dfrac{x \cdot f}{z + d}" block />

Here `f` is a focal length and `d` the camera distance. The bigger the `z` (the farther the point), the smaller `x'` becomes — so distant parts of the mesh shrink toward a **vanishing point**, exactly like railway tracks converging on the horizon. That shrinking is what your brain reads as depth.

</Step>

<Step title="Building models" kind="explain">

Real models are far denser than a cube — a film character can be **millions** of triangles. Artists sculpt them in 3D software, or they're captured by **3D scanners** that measure thousands of surface points off a real object. More triangles mean smoother curves and finer detail, at the cost of more numbers to store and process.

Whatever the source, the model is still just the same humble ingredients: a long list of vertices, the edges and faces connecting them, and the maths to rotate and project them onto your screen.

<Callout type="info" title="Why triangles?">
Faces are almost always **triangles** because three points always lie in a single flat plane — a triangle can never be bent or twisted. That makes triangles dead simple for hardware to process, which is why GPUs are built to draw billions of them.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- A 3D point adds a depth coordinate: `(x, y, z)`.
- A **mesh** is **vertices + edges + faces** (usually triangles); a cube has 8, 12 and 6.
- To draw it, each 3D point is **projected** onto the 2D screen — **perspective** projection shrinks distant points toward a vanishing point.
- Real models can be **millions of triangles**; more triangles mean more detail and more computation.

</Step>
