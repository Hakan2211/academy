<Lesson>

<Step title="A bad design quietly rots" kind="explain">

You can store the same data many ways, and the design matters enormously. A careless layout works fine on day one, then slowly corrupts itself as people update it. Good database design is mostly about one goal: **store each fact exactly once**.

When the same fact is duplicated across many rows, the copies drift out of sync. Fix the layout up front and a whole class of bugs simply cannot happen. The technique for getting there is called **normalisation**.

</Step>

<Step title="Redundancy and anomalies" kind="interactive">

Suppose every order row also stores the customer's name and city. A frequent customer's details then get copied into every order they place. Toggle "Before" and "After" to see the problem and the fix.

<NormalizationViz />

Duplication causes **anomalies**:

- **Update anomaly** — change a customer's city and you must edit every copy; miss one and the data contradicts itself.
- **Insertion anomaly** — you can't record a new customer until they place an order.
- **Deletion anomaly** — delete their last order and you accidentally erase the customer entirely.

</Step>

<Step title="Normalisation: split into related tables" kind="explain">

**Normalisation** is the process of splitting data into multiple related tables so that each piece of information lives in exactly one place. Customer details go in a Customers table (stored once); orders reference the customer by a **foreign key**.

Now updating a city touches a single row, and every order instantly sees the change because it only *links* to the customer rather than copying their data. The relationships you learned in the relational model are precisely the tool that lets you remove redundancy without losing any information.

</Step>

<Step title="Integrity, transactions, and ACID" kind="explain">

Good design also means setting rules the database will enforce — **integrity constraints**. A `NOT NULL` rule forbids blanks; a **foreign-key constraint** forbids an order that points to a customer who doesn't exist. The DBMS rejects any change that would break a rule, so bad data never gets in.

Some operations must happen all-or-nothing. Transferring money is two steps — subtract here, add there — that must both succeed or both fail. A **transaction** groups steps into one unit, and databases guarantee transactions are **ACID**: **A**tomic (all or nothing), **C**onsistent (rules stay satisfied), **I**solated (concurrent transactions don't interfere), and **D**urable (once committed, it survives a crash).

<Callout type="warn" title="Don't over-normalise blindly">
Splitting everything to the extreme means more joins and slower reads. Real design balances clean, redundancy-free storage against the cost of reassembling it. Most apps land at a sensible, mostly-normalised middle.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- Good design aims to store **each fact exactly once**; duplication causes **update, insertion, and deletion anomalies**.
- **Normalisation** splits data into related tables linked by **foreign keys** to remove redundancy.
- **Integrity constraints** (NOT NULL, foreign keys) let the DBMS reject invalid data automatically.
- A **transaction** groups steps into an all-or-nothing unit; databases guarantee them to be **ACID** (Atomic, Consistent, Isolated, Durable).
- Design is a **balance** — fully normalised storage versus the join cost of reassembling it.

</Step>
