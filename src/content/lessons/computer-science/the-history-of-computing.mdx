<Lesson>

<Step title="A 4,000-year head start" kind="explain">

Computers feel new, but the *idea* of automating calculation is ancient. For millennia people built tools to count and reckon faster than the human mind — beads, gears, levers — long before electricity.

The story of computing is really one long pursuit: **do more, faster, with less human effort.** Each breakthrough handed a little more thinking over to the machine.

</Step>

<Step title="The timeline" kind="interactive">

Walk through the milestones. Notice how each leap builds on the last — and how the pace keeps accelerating.

<HistoryTimeline />

The gap from the abacus to Babbage was thousands of years. The gap from the first microprocessor to AI that writes essays was about fifty. Computing is one of the fastest-moving stories in human history.

</Step>

<Step title="Two people who saw the future" kind="explain">

Two ideas tower over the rest:

- **Ada Lovelace (1840s)** realised Babbage's machine could manipulate *symbols*, not just numbers — that a computer could, in principle, do anything you could describe with a procedure. She wrote the first algorithm meant for a machine.
- **Alan Turing (1936)** defined, mathematically, what "computation" even *means* with an imaginary machine of breathtaking simplicity. He proved both the power and the *limits* of computers — before a single one had been built.

<Callout type="info" title="Hardware vs ideas">
The theory came first. Turing's and Lovelace's insights about *what computation is* are still true today. The hardware — tubes, transistors, chips — is just ever-better ways of carrying out those same ideas.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="The engine of progress" kind="explain">

Why did everything explode after the 1950s? One word: **miniaturisation.** The switch at the heart of a computer went from a room-sized relay, to a vacuum tube, to a transistor, to billions of transistors etched onto a fingernail-sized chip.

For decades the number of transistors on a chip *doubled* roughly every two years (often called **Moore's Law**). Each doubling made computers cheaper, faster and smaller — which is why the supercomputer of 1985 now fits in your pocket and costs less than dinner.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- Automating calculation is an **ancient goal**, from the abacus to Babbage's mechanical engine.
- **Ada Lovelace** saw computers could manipulate symbols; **Alan Turing** defined computation itself.
- The **theory came before the hardware** and still holds.
- **Miniaturisation** of the switch (relay → tube → transistor → chip) drove the explosive, accelerating pace of progress.

</Step>
