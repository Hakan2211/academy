<Step title="Turing's 1936 idea" kind="explain">

In **1936**, before any electronic computer existed, a 24-year-old named **Alan Turing** asked what it means to *compute* something. His answer was a machine so simple it sounds like a toy — yet it would define the limits of computation forever.

Picture a person with an endless strip of paper, reading and rewriting one symbol at a time, following a small rulebook, their attention on a single square. Strip the human away and you have a **Turing machine**: an idealised model of "following a procedure" with pencil and unlimited paper.

</Step>

<Step title="Tape, head, state, and rules" kind="explain">

A Turing machine has exactly four parts:

- An infinite **tape** of cells, each holding one symbol.
- A **head** that sits over one cell and can read or write it, then move left or right.
- A current **state**, from a finite set.
- A **transition table** of rules: given *(state, symbol read)*, it says what to **write**, which way to **move**, and which state to enter next.

That's the entire machine. The finite-state machine of the last lesson is just a Turing machine that may only move right and never writes — so the tape becomes unlimited, rewritable memory, and *that* is the leap in power.

</Step>

<Step title="Run one yourself" kind="interactive">

This machine **adds 1 to a binary number**. It walks right to the end of the number, then carries leftward: flip a `1` to `0` and keep carrying, or flip a `0` to `1` and stop. Step through it, or hit Run, and watch the tape, head, and state change by the rules alone.

<TuringMachine />

No cleverness lives in the machine — only blind rule-following. Yet by reading, writing, and moving, it performs real arithmetic. Chain enough such rules and you can compute *anything* a programming language can.

</Step>

<Step title="The Church–Turing thesis" kind="explain">

Here is the staggering claim. **Anything that can be computed at all can be computed by a Turing machine.** Different researchers invented totally different models — Church's lambda calculus, recursive functions, register machines — and every one turned out to have *exactly the same power* as Turing's. This convergence is the **Church–Turing thesis**: the Turing machine *is* the definition of computation.

<Callout type="info" title="The universal machine">
Turing went further. He described a **universal Turing machine**: one machine that reads a *description* of any other Turing machine from its tape and then simulates it. That is the deep idea behind every real computer — a single piece of hardware that runs any program you feed it. Your phone is a universal machine.
</Callout>

So your laptop, a supercomputer, and Python are all **Turing-complete** — equivalent in raw computational power to this little tape-and-head device. They differ in speed and convenience, never in what is *possible*.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Alan Turing (1936)** modelled computation as a **tape, head, state, and rule table** — unlimited rewritable memory.
- Stepping by simple rules, it performs real computation (here, adding 1 in binary).
- The **Church–Turing thesis**: anything computable by *any* method is computable by a Turing machine.
- A **universal Turing machine** simulates any other from a description — the idea behind every real computer.
- All powerful languages and machines are **Turing-complete**: equal in what is *possible*, differing only in speed.

</Step>
