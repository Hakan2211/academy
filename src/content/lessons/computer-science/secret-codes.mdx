<Step title="The ancient art of hiding meaning" kind="explain">

Long before computers, people needed to send messages past enemy hands. The answer was **cryptography** — from the Greek for "hidden writing." A message in plain readable form is **plaintext**; once scrambled it becomes **ciphertext**; the rule for scrambling and unscrambling is the **cipher**, and the secret setting it uses is the **key**.

Around two thousand years ago, Julius Caesar protected military orders with one of the simplest ciphers ever devised: shift every letter a fixed number of places along the alphabet. It is laughably weak today — but it is the perfect place to learn what a cipher is, and what makes a cipher *fail*.

</Step>

<Step title="The Caesar cipher" kind="interactive">

This is a **substitution cipher**: each letter is replaced by another according to a fixed shift. Wrap around the end of the alphabet so Z + 1 becomes A. The shift amount is the key — share it, and your friend can shift back to read the message.

<CaesarCipher />

It works, and it is reversible. But play with it and the fatal flaw appears: there are only **25** possible shifts, so an attacker simply tries all of them in an instant. Worse, the letter `E` is still the most common letter in the ciphertext — count letter frequencies and the shift gives itself away. A cipher is only as good as the difficulty of guessing its key.

</Step>

<Step title="One key to lock and unlock" kind="explain">

The Caesar cipher has a property that defines a whole family of modern encryption: the **same key** both encrypts and decrypts. Shift by 3 to lock, shift back by 3 to unlock. This is called **symmetric encryption**, and today's symmetric ciphers (like **AES**) are extraordinarily strong — not because the method is secret, but because the key is one of an astronomically large number of possibilities.

That is the real lesson of Caesar's weakness: secrecy must live entirely in the **key**, never in the method. Modern ciphers are public, studied by everyone, and still unbreakable — because guessing the key would take longer than the lifetime of the universe.

</Step>

<Step title="Symmetric encryption, with bits" kind="interactive">

Modern ciphers work on bits, not letters. Here is the simplest reversible bit operation — **XOR** — used as a toy symmetric cipher. XOR the message with the key to scramble it; XOR the scrambled bits with the *same* key, and the original returns.

<SymmetricCrypto />

Notice the elegance: lock and unlock are the *identical* operation. But notice the trap at the bottom too.

</Step>

<Step title="The key-distribution problem" kind="explain">

Symmetric encryption has a catch that nearly sank the whole field. Both parties must already **share the same secret key** — but how do two strangers agree on a key over the internet, when an eavesdropper is listening to the very channel they would use to exchange it?

You cannot just send the key; the attacker would read it. You cannot meet in person with everyone you will ever message. For most of history this **key-distribution problem** had no clean solution — secret keys had to be hand-delivered by trusted couriers. The breakthrough that solved it, without ever sending the key, is the subject of the next lesson.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Cryptography** turns readable **plaintext** into scrambled **ciphertext** using a **cipher** and a secret **key**.
- The **Caesar cipher** shifts letters by a fixed amount; it fails because it has only **25 keys** and falls to **frequency analysis**.
- **Symmetric encryption** uses **one key** to both lock and unlock (Caesar, XOR, and the very strong modern AES); secrecy must live in the **key, not the method**.
- The **key-distribution problem**: two strangers cannot safely exchange a shared key over a channel an eavesdropper is watching — solved next by public-key cryptography.

</Step>
