<Lesson>

<Step title="The structure of relationships" kind="explain">

A tree captures hierarchy, but it has rules: one root, no loops, every node has a single parent. The real world is messier. Your friends are friends with *each other*. Cities connect by many roads that crisscross and loop. Web pages link back and forth. To model **relationships between things**, with no restrictions, we need the most general structure of all: the **graph**.

A graph is just two ingredients:

- **nodes** (also called *vertices*) — the things,
- **edges** — connections between pairs of things.

That's it. Friends-and-friendships, cities-and-roads, pages-and-links — all the same shape.

</Step>

<Step title="Trees are special graphs" kind="explain">

Here's a tidy insight: a **tree is just a special kind of graph** — one that's connected and has no cycles, with a chosen root. Loosen those rules and you get a general graph, where edges can form **loops** and a node can connect to **many** others with no notion of "parent."

Graphs can also be **directed** (edges point one way, like web links or "follows" on social media) or **undirected** (edges go both ways, like friendships), and edges can carry **weights** (a road's distance, a flight's cost).

</Step>

<Step title="Exploring a graph" kind="interactive">

The big question with a graph is: starting somewhere, **how do I visit everything reachable?** That's **traversal**, and there are two famous ways. Step through both below, starting from node A.

<GraphViz />

- **BFS — Breadth-First Search** explores in rings: all the immediate neighbours first, then *their* neighbours, fanning outward by distance. It rides on a **queue** (FIFO) and naturally finds the **fewest-hop** path.
- **DFS — Depth-First Search** plunges down one path as far as it can before backing up. It rides on a **stack** (LIFO) — often just the call stack of recursion.

Watch the numbered visit order differ between the two, even on the same graph.

</Step>

<Step title="What graphs unlock" kind="explain">

Once your data is a graph, a whole toolbox of algorithms opens up:

- **Shortest path** — your map app finding the quickest route (BFS, or Dijkstra's algorithm with weights).
- **Recommendation & social features** — "people you may know," friends-of-friends.
- **The web itself** — search engines crawl the link graph and rank pages by it.
- **Networks** — routing data across the internet, modelling how a disease or rumour spreads.

<Callout type="info" title="One model, endless uses">
The same graph + traversal ideas describe maps, social networks, dependency charts in build systems, neurons, and molecules. Learning to *see a problem as a graph* is one of the most powerful moves in computer science — suddenly decades of ready-made algorithms apply.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- A **graph** is **nodes** plus **edges** — the most general way to model relationships, with loops and many-to-many links allowed.
- A **tree is a special graph**: connected, no cycles, rooted.
- Edges may be **directed/undirected** and **weighted**.
- **Traversal** visits reachable nodes: **BFS** fans out by distance (queue, shortest hops); **DFS** dives deep first (stack/recursion).
- Graphs model maps, social networks, the web and more — seeing a problem *as* a graph unlocks powerful algorithms.

</Step>
