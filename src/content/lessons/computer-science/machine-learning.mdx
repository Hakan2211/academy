<Step title="Teaching instead of programming" kind="explain">

Imagine writing a program to recognise a cat in a photo. You'd start listing rules: pointy ears, whiskers, fur… and immediately drown. What about a cat curled up? A black cat in shadow? A kitten? For every rule you write, reality offers a thousand exceptions. The hand-coded approach simply *cannot* keep up with the messy richness of the real world.

**Machine learning** flips the problem on its head. Instead of writing the rules, you collect thousands of labelled examples — "this is a cat, this is not" — and let an algorithm *adjust itself* until it gets them right. The rules are never written by a human; they're **discovered** by the machine from the data. This is the engine beneath almost all modern AI.

</Step>

<Step title="Finding the boundary" kind="interactive">

The simplest learning task is **classification**: sort examples into categories. Geometrically, the model is searching for a **boundary** that separates one class from another. Drag the line below to separate the two clouds of points, and watch the accuracy climb.

<MlClassifier />

Training a real model is exactly this, automated: it starts with a random boundary, measures how many examples it gets wrong, and nudges the boundary to reduce the error — over and over. There's no insight, no understanding of "blueness" — just relentless adjustment toward higher accuracy. Geometrically, learning *is* finding the right boundary.

</Step>

<Step title="Three flavours of learning" kind="explain">

Machine learning comes in three broad styles.

**Supervised learning** uses *labelled* data — examples paired with the right answer (the cat photos above). The model learns to map inputs to outputs. Most everyday AI — spam filters, medical image readers, price predictors — is supervised.

**Unsupervised learning** gets data with *no labels* and hunts for hidden structure on its own — clustering customers into groups, or compressing data to its essence. It discovers patterns nobody told it to look for.

**Reinforcement learning** has no fixed answers at all. An agent acts in an environment and receives **rewards** or penalties, learning by trial and error which behaviours pay off — how a program learns to play a game or a robot learns to walk.

</Step>

<Step title="The danger of memorising" kind="explain">

There's a trap. A model that's *too* flexible can ace its training examples by essentially **memorising** them — bending its boundary into knots to capture every quirk and stray point. It looks perfect on data it has already seen, then fails miserably on anything new. This is **overfitting**.

To catch it, we split our data: **train** the model on one portion, then judge it on a held-out **test** set it has never seen. True learning means **generalising** — performing well on the unseen. A model that scores 100% on training but 60% on the test set hasn't learned the concept; it has memorised the answer key.

<Callout type="warn" title="Garbage in, garbage out">
A model is only as good as its data. If your examples are biased, incomplete, or mislabelled, the model faithfully learns those flaws — a theme we'll return to when we weigh AI's risks.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Why now?" kind="explain">

The ideas behind machine learning are decades old. So why did it suddenly explode? Three forces converged: an **avalanche of data** (the internet, sensors, digitised everything), **cheap massive computation** (especially GPUs that crunch many numbers in parallel), and refined **algorithms**. Together they turned a quiet academic field into the most consequential technology of our era — and set the stage for the neural networks we explore next.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Machine learning** discovers rules *from data* instead of having them hand-coded — essential for messy, real-world tasks.
- **Classification** is geometrically a search for a **boundary** that separates classes; training nudges it to maximise accuracy.
- Three styles: **supervised** (labelled examples), **unsupervised** (find hidden structure), **reinforcement** (learn from rewards).
- **Overfitting** is memorising the training set; we hold out a **test set** to check the model **generalises**.
- ML exploded thanks to **data + compute + better algorithms** converging.

</Step>
