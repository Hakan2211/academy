<Lesson>

<Step title="Why squeeze data?" kind="explain">

Storage and bandwidth cost money and time. A film at full quality would be hundreds of gigabytes; an uncompressed photo, tens of megabytes. **Compression** makes data smaller so it's cheaper to store and faster to send — the reason streaming, video calls and the modern web are even possible.

The key insight: most real data is **predictable**. It has repetition and patterns. Compression finds that redundancy and describes the data more briefly, without (necessarily) losing anything.

</Step>

<Step title="Run-length encoding" kind="interactive">

The simplest method is **run-length encoding (RLE)**: replace a run of identical values with one copy plus a count. "■■■■■□□□" becomes "5 of ■, 3 of □". Edit the pattern and watch the compressed size respond.

<CompressionLab />

Notice the catch: RLE shrinks *smooth, repetitive* data dramatically, but on *noisy* data it can actually make things **bigger** (you store counts of 1 everywhere). No compression method wins on every input.

</Step>

<Step title="Smarter: encode common things shortly" kind="explain">

Better schemes exploit *frequency* rather than just runs. **Huffman coding** gives the most common symbols the **shortest** bit codes and rare symbols longer ones — like Morse code, where the frequent letter "E" is a single dot.

The everyday ZIP file combines this idea with a **dictionary** method (LZ): when a sequence repeats, store a short reference back to where it appeared before instead of the whole thing again. Together these power ZIP, PNG, and the gzip that compresses most web pages you load.

<Callout type="tip" title="Lossless = perfectly reversible">
RLE, Huffman and ZIP are all **lossless**: the decompressor rebuilds the original bit-for-bit. Essential for text, code and data, where a single changed character could break everything.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="The limits of compression" kind="explain">

Can you compress forever? No. **Truly random data cannot be compressed at all** — there's no pattern to exploit, and any scheme that shrinks some inputs must *enlarge* others (a neat counting argument). The amount of "real" information in data has a hard floor, a quantity called its **entropy**.

That's why a ZIP of a ZIP doesn't keep shrinking, and why a photo of TV static stays huge. Compression isn't magic — it's the art of removing redundancy, and once the redundancy is gone, you've hit the wall.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Compression** removes redundancy to make data smaller, cheaper to store and faster to send.
- **Run-length encoding** replaces repeated runs with a value + count — great for smooth data, bad for noisy data.
- **Huffman** (short codes for common symbols) and **dictionary/LZ** methods power ZIP, PNG and gzip — all **lossless**.
- **Random data can't be compressed**; the irreducible information content is its **entropy**.

</Step>
