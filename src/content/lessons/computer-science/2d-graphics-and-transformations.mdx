<Step title="Shapes are lists of coordinates" kind="explain">

A vector shape is stored as a handful of **points** — its corners — each a pair of numbers `(x, y)` saying where it sits on the canvas. A triangle is three points; a letter is a few dozen. Join the points with lines and you have the outline.

This is the key idea of computer graphics: **a picture is data, and drawing is arithmetic on that data.** Once a shape is just numbers, moving, spinning or resizing it isn't an art project — it's a calculation the computer can do millions of times a second.

</Step>

<Step title="Translate, rotate, scale" kind="interactive">

Three operations cover almost everything you do to a 2D shape:

- **Translate** — slide it, by *adding* an offset to every point.
- **Scale** — resize it, by *multiplying* every coordinate.
- **Rotate** — spin it, by mixing `x` and `y` together using sine and cosine.

Notice the original outline stays ghosted in place while the live shape transforms. Drag the sliders and watch a single rule applied to every corner reshape the whole letter at once.

</Step>

<Step title="The maths of rotation" kind="explain">

Translate and scale are obvious — add, multiply. Rotation is the clever one. To turn a point by angle θ around the origin, the new coordinates are:

<Formula tex="x' = x\cos\theta - y\sin\theta" block />

<Formula tex="y' = x\sin\theta + y\cos\theta" block />

Every transformation here is really a **matrix** — a little grid of numbers — and applying it is a **matrix multiplication**. The real magic is that you can *combine* several matrices into one by multiplying them together, so "scale, then rotate, then move" becomes a **single** operation applied to every point. Graphics hardware is built to do exactly this multiply, astonishingly fast.

</Step>

<Step title="Why vectors scale cleanly" kind="explain">

Because a vector shape is stored as coordinates and a transformation is just math on those numbers, you can scale it up a thousandfold and the edges stay perfectly sharp — the computer simply recomputes where each point lands and redraws crisp lines.

That's the opposite of a raster image, which has its pixels baked in and turns blocky when enlarged. It's why **fonts and logos are vectors**: the same letter "A" renders flawlessly on a tiny phone label and a giant billboard, from one set of coordinates plus a scale transform.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- A vector shape is a **list of coordinates**; transforming it is **arithmetic on those numbers**.
- **Translate** adds an offset, **scale** multiplies, and **rotate** mixes x and y with sin and cos.
- Each transform is a **matrix**; several can be **combined into one** matrix multiply applied to every point.
- Because shapes are stored as math, **vector graphics scale to any size without blurring** — which is why fonts and logos are vectors.

</Step>
