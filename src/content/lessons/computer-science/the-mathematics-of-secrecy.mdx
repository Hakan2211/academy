<Step title="Secrecy you can prove" kind="explain">

We have used a lovely picture — mixing paint — to explain public-key cryptography. But paint is just an analogy. The real thing rests on **mathematics**, and that is what makes it trustworthy. Modern encryption is not secure because the method is hidden; it is secure because breaking it would require solving a maths problem so hard that all the computers on Earth, running until the sun dies, could not finish.

This is the deepest idea in the whole subject: we build perfect-seeming secrecy on untrusted networks out of nothing but **numbers and the limits of computation**. Let us see how.

</Step>

<Step title="One-way functions: the asymmetry of effort" kind="explain">

Everything hinges on **one-way functions** — calculations that are easy to perform but extraordinarily hard to undo. We met the idea as un-mixable paint. In mathematics, the classic example is **multiplying primes**.

Multiplying two large prime numbers together is fast and easy, even by hand for small ones:

<Formula tex="61 \times 53 = 3233" block />

But run it backwards — given only **3233**, find which two primes were multiplied — and you must *search*. For tiny numbers that is quick. For numbers hundreds of digits long, **factoring** is believed to take longer than the age of the universe, even though multiplying those same numbers takes a fraction of a second. Easy forwards, infeasible backwards: the perfect lock.

</Step>

<Step title="Clock arithmetic" kind="explain">

Real cryptography adds a second ingredient that makes numbers "wrap around" and lose their tracks: **modular arithmetic**, or clock arithmetic. On a 12-hour clock, 5 hours after 9 o'clock is not 14 — it is 2. We wrote `9 + 5 = 2 (mod 12)`: take the remainder after dividing by the modulus.

Raising numbers to powers under a modulus is another one-way street. Computing the power is easy:

<Formula tex="c = m^e \bmod n" block />

But going the other way — recovering the exponent that produced a given result (the **discrete logarithm**) — has no known fast method. This wrap-around is exactly what hides the "private colours" in Diffie–Hellman: the public values are powers taken mod a huge prime, and un-doing them means solving a problem nobody can solve quickly.

</Step>

<Step title="RSA: a lock built from primes" kind="explain">

The famous **RSA** algorithm assembles these pieces into a public/private key pair. You pick two enormous secret primes and multiply them to get a public number `n`. Encrypting a message `m` is one modular exponentiation:

<Formula tex="c = m^e \bmod n" block />

and decrypting is another, using a private exponent `d` derived from those secret primes:

<Formula tex="m = c^d \bmod n" block />

Anyone can encrypt with the public `e` and `n`. But computing the private `d` requires knowing the two original primes — and recovering them from `n` means **factoring** a number hundreds of digits long. The public key is safe to publish precisely because the factoring problem stands between an attacker and the private key.

<Callout type="info" title="The same idea, two costumes">
RSA leans on the hardness of **factoring**; Diffie–Hellman leans on the **discrete logarithm**. Different problems, same shape: a one-way function whose forward direction is trivial and whose reverse direction is, as far as anyone knows, computationally hopeless.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Watching the asymmetry, one more time" kind="interactive">

Step back to the colour exchange — but now read it as mathematics. The public base is a shared number; each private colour is a secret exponent; each public mixture is that base raised to the secret power, mod a prime. The shared secret is the base raised to *both* secrets.

<PublicKeyExchange />

The eavesdropper has the base and both public mixtures, yet cannot find either secret exponent — that is the discrete logarithm problem, and it is hard. "Un-mixing paint" was never just a metaphor; it is what the maths actually refuses to let an attacker do.

</Step>

<Step title="Hashing rests on hardness too" kind="interactive">

The same philosophy powers hashing. A cryptographic hash is one-way not because of a secret, but because *finding an input that produces a chosen digest* would require an astronomically large search. And the avalanche effect ensures no clever shortcut links small input changes to small output changes.

<HashViz />

So the two great tools — encryption and hashing — are both monuments to the same insight: certain computations are easy one way and practically impossible the other. Security is the art of standing the entire internet's trust on that gap.

</Step>

<Step title="The quantum cloud on the horizon" kind="explain">

There is a catch on the horizon. Today's hardness assumptions hold for ordinary computers. But a sufficiently large **quantum computer** could run an algorithm (Shor's algorithm) that factors big numbers and cracks discrete logarithms *efficiently* — which would break RSA and Diffie–Hellman as we know them.

Such machines do not yet exist at the needed scale, but the threat is taken seriously enough that researchers are already standardising **post-quantum cryptography**: new one-way problems (based on mathematical lattices and other structures) that even quantum computers are not known to solve quickly. The deep pattern endures — find a hard problem, build trust on its hardness — only the problems change. Hashing, notably, is far less affected, and simply using longer keys keeps it safe.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- Modern encryption is secure not through secrecy of method but through **computational hardness**: breaking it would require solving an infeasible maths problem.
- **One-way functions** — easy forwards, infeasible backwards — are the core; multiplying primes is easy, **factoring** the product is not, and **modular arithmetic** hides numbers further (the **discrete logarithm** problem).
- **RSA** builds a key pair from two secret primes (security rests on factoring); **Diffie–Hellman** rests on discrete logs; **hashing** rests on the infeasibility of reversing a digest.
- A large **quantum computer** could break factoring- and discrete-log-based schemes (Shor's algorithm), motivating **post-quantum cryptography** — same deep pattern, harder problems.

</Step>
