<Lesson>

<Step title="Why bother sorting?" kind="explain">

**Sorting** — arranging items into order — is one of the most studied problems in all of computer science, and for good reason. Sorted data unlocks faster everything: binary search, finding duplicates, spotting the median, merging datasets. Spend a little effort ordering data once, and every later operation gets cheaper.

It's also the perfect arena to *compare algorithms*. There are dozens of sorting methods that all produce the same sorted result — but with wildly different amounts of work. We'll meet four classics.

</Step>

<Step title="The simple-but-slow trio" kind="explain">

Three sorting algorithms are easy to understand and easy to write:

- **Bubble sort** — repeatedly walk the list, swapping any two neighbours that are out of order, until no swaps are needed. The largest values "bubble" to the end.
- **Selection sort** — repeatedly find the smallest remaining item and place it next.
- **Insertion sort** — build a sorted section one item at a time, sliding each new item back into its correct spot (how many people sort a hand of cards).

All three are intuitive — but all do roughly **n²** work on a list of n items.

</Step>

<Step title="The clever one: merge sort" kind="explain">

**Merge sort** uses a smarter strategy called **divide and conquer**: split the list in half, sort each half (by splitting *them* in half, and so on), then **merge** the two sorted halves back together in one efficient pass.

Because halving the list only takes about log n levels, and each level does n work, merge sort runs in:

<Formula tex="O(n \log n)" block />

That sounds close to O(n²), but it isn't. For a list of a million items, n² is a *trillion* operations; n log n is about *twenty million* — fifty-thousand times less work.

</Step>

<Callout type="tip" title="Simple isn't always wrong">
For *tiny* lists, the simple algorithms can actually be faster — they have less overhead. The cleverness of merge sort only pays off as n grows. Choosing the right algorithm means knowing your data.
</Callout>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Sorting** orders data and makes many later operations (like binary search) much cheaper.
- **Bubble**, **selection** and **insertion** sort are simple but quadratic — about **O(n²)**.
- **Merge sort** uses **divide and conquer** to run in **O(n log n)** — far faster at scale.
- All four give the *same* sorted result; they differ only in how much **work** they do.
- The best choice depends on the data — simple sorts can win on tiny lists.

</Step>
