<Step title="A different question entirely" kind="explain">

The psychodynamic and cognitive-behavioural schools ask, in their own ways, *what's broken and how do we fix it?* The **humanistic** therapists asked something different: *what would help this person grow?*

Humanistic therapy starts from an optimistic view of human nature. People aren't bundles of conflicts or faulty thoughts to be corrected; they're fundamentally **growth-seeking**, with an innate drive toward becoming their fullest selves — **self-actualisation**. Distress, in this view, comes when that drive is **blocked** — when life and other people's conditions push someone away from their true self. The therapist's task is not to fix or analyse but to create the conditions in which natural growth can resume.

</Step>

<Step title="Rogers and person-centred therapy" kind="explain">

The most influential humanistic approach is **Carl Rogers'** **person-centred therapy** (also called client-centred). Rogers believed the client, not the therapist, is the expert on their own life — so the therapist leads from behind, providing not interpretations or techniques but a particular kind of *relationship*. Three conditions make it work:

- **Unconditional positive regard** — accepting and prizing the person completely, without judgement or conditions, just as they are.
- **Empathy** — striving to feel and reflect back the client's world from the inside.
- **Genuineness (congruence)** — being real and honest rather than hiding behind a professional mask.

A core skill is **active listening**: paraphrasing and reflecting what the client says, so they feel truly heard and can hear themselves more clearly. In this safe, accepting space, people loosen up, explore honestly, and move toward their own answers.

</Step>

<Step title="Gestalt and the here-and-now" kind="explain">

A livelier cousin is **Gestalt therapy**, developed by **Fritz Perls**. Where Rogers is gentle and reflective, Gestalt is active and confronting. Its watchword is the **here-and-now**: not endless analysis of the past, but full awareness of what you're feeling, sensing and avoiding *right now*.

Gestalt therapists use vivid experiments to make this immediate — most famously the **empty-chair technique**, where you speak directly to an imagined person (or a disowned part of yourself) seated in an empty chair, then switch chairs and answer. The aim is to reconnect split-off feelings into a whole ("gestalt") and take responsibility for one's own experience.

</Step>

This relationship-first emphasis turned out to matter far beyond Rogers' own school: his finding that warmth, empathy and genuineness drive change anticipated the modern discovery that the **therapeutic alliance** is one of the strongest ingredients in *any* therapy.

<Step title="Therapy beyond one-on-one" kind="explain">

Not all therapy happens with a single client. Some struggles are best treated where they actually live — in relationships and groups:

- **Group therapy** — one or two therapists work with several clients at once. Members realise they're **not alone** (universality), give and receive support, and practise new ways of relating in a safe social laboratory. It's also more affordable than individual work.
- **Couples therapy** — treats the *relationship* as the client, improving communication and breaking destructive cycles between partners.
- **Family therapy** — sees the family as a **system**: one person's symptoms often reflect patterns in the whole web of relationships, so the family is treated together.
- **Self-help and support groups** — peer-led groups (such as twelve-step or grief-support groups) where shared experience, not professional expertise, is the healing force.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Humanistic therapy** sees people as **growth-seeking**; distress comes from **blocked** self-actualisation, and the cure is conditions for growth rather than fixing symptoms.
- **Rogers' person-centred therapy** provides **unconditional positive regard, empathy and genuineness**, plus **active listening**, trusting the client as the expert on their own life.
- **Gestalt therapy** (Perls) is active and **here-and-now** focused, using experiments like the **empty-chair technique**.
- **Group, couples and family therapy** treat people in their relationships — family therapy sees symptoms as part of a **system** — while **self-help groups** heal through shared peer experience.

</Step>
