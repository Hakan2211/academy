<Step title="A camera that thinks" kind="explain">

Vision dominates the human brain — roughly a third of your cortex is devoted to it. And it all begins with an organ often compared to a camera: light enters through an opening, a lens focuses it, and an image lands on a light-sensitive surface at the back.

The comparison is useful but incomplete. A camera records an image. Your eye does something stranger and more interesting: it **takes the image apart** into millions of separate signals, throws away most of the raw data, and ships a heavily processed summary to the brain. By the time "seeing" happens, the picture has been rebuilt.

Let's follow the light in.

</Step>

<Step title="The path of light" kind="explain">

Light from the world travels through the eye in a fixed sequence:

- **Cornea** — the clear front dome. It does most of the focusing by bending incoming light.
- **Pupil** — the opening light passes through. The coloured **iris** muscle widens it in dim light and shrinks it in bright light.
- **Lens** — fine-tunes the focus by changing shape, a trick called **accommodation**: flatter for distant objects, rounder for near ones.
- **Retina** — the light-sensitive screen at the back, where the focused image finally lands — upside-down and reversed.

Because the cornea and lens bend the light, the image projected onto your retina is **inverted**. Your brain quietly flips it; you never notice.

</Step>

The retina is where the real action is. Everything before it just delivers a clean image. The retina turns that image into the language of the brain.

<Step title="Rods and cones: two ways to catch light" kind="explain">

The retina holds two kinds of photoreceptor, with a neat division of labour.

- **Rods** — about 120 million of them, spread across the periphery. Exquisitely sensitive, so they handle **dim light** and night vision. But they're colour-blind and give fuzzy detail.
- **Cones** — about 6 million, concentrated in the centre. They need **bright light**, but deliver sharp detail and **colour**.

The cones cluster densely in a tiny central pit called the **fovea** — your zone of sharpest vision. This is why you point your eyes *straight at* whatever you want to see clearly, and why, on a faint star at night, looking slightly *to the side* (onto the rod-rich periphery) can make it appear.

<Callout type="info" title="Dark adaptation">
Step into a dark cinema and you're briefly blind, then slowly start to see. That's your rods regenerating their light-catching pigment — a process that takes around 20–30 minutes to complete.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="The blind spot" kind="explain">

Here's a startling fact: there is a hole in each of your visual fields, and you've never noticed it.

Where the **optic nerve** gathers up all the retina's signals and punches through the back of the eye to reach the brain, there's a patch with **no rods and no cones**. Light landing there sends no signal at all. It's called the **optic disc**, or the **blind spot**.

You don't perceive a black hole in the world for one remarkable reason: your brain **fills it in**, seamlessly painting in whatever surrounds the gap. The same filling-in we'll meet again with illusions — perception is not a passive recording, it's an active construction, complete with educated guesses.

</Step>

<Step title="From eye to brain" kind="explain">

The signals leave each eye along the **optic nerve**. The two nerves meet at a crossover point (the *optic chiasm*), where information is sorted by which side of space it came from, not which eye — so the left half of what both eyes see goes to the right brain, and vice versa.

From there signals route through a relay station in the thalamus to the **visual cortex** at the very back of the brain, where the long work of perception begins: detecting edges, motion, shapes and faces. Seeing, it turns out, happens far more in the brain than in the eye.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- Light passes **cornea → pupil (iris) → lens → retina**; the cornea does most focusing, the lens fine-tunes it by **accommodation**, and the image lands **inverted**.
- The retina has two photoreceptors: **rods** (peripheral, dim-light, no colour) and **cones** (central, bright-light, sharp colour), with cones densest in the **fovea**.
- The **blind spot** is where the optic nerve exits — no receptors there — yet the brain **fills it in** so you never notice the gap.
- Signals travel the **optic nerve** to the **visual cortex** at the back of the brain, where perception really happens — seeing is mostly a brain process.

</Step>
