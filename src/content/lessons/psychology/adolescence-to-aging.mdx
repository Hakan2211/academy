<Step title="The second half of the story" kind="explain">

We've watched a single cell become a reasoning, moral, attached human being. But development doesn't stop at childhood — and the second half of life holds some of its most surprising findings. The teenage brain isn't a broken adult brain; emerging adulthood is a genuinely new life stage; and ageing, done well, brings *gains* alongside the losses.

This deep dive walks the rest of the arc: the stormy remodelling of adolescence, the strange new decade of the twenties, and what the science of ageing reveals about wisdom and a life well lived.

</Step>

<Step title="Puberty and the remodelled brain" kind="explain">

**Adolescence** opens with **puberty** — the surge of hormones that brings physical and sexual maturity. The body's changes are obvious. The brain's changes are not, and they're at least as important.

The adolescent brain is not finished; it's under intense renovation. Two processes dominate. **Synaptic pruning** trims away little-used neural connections, sharpening the wiring like an editor cutting a bloated manuscript. **Myelination** — the insulating of nerve fibres — speeds up signal transmission. Crucially, this remodelling doesn't finish everywhere at once. It proceeds from the back of the brain forward, and the *last* region to fully mature is the one right behind the forehead.

</Step>

<Step title="The maturation gap" kind="interactive">

Here is the key to the teenage years. Two brain systems mature on *different schedules*. The **limbic / reward system** — driving emotion, thrill and the pull of rewards — matures *early*, revving up in the teens. The **prefrontal cortex** — the seat of planning, impulse control and weighing consequences, the brain's *brakes* — matures *late*, not fully online until the mid-twenties.

For a few years, then, a teenager drives a car with a powerful accelerator and not-yet-finished brakes. Slide age across the lifespan and watch the gap:

The gap peaks in adolescence — which helps explain the classic pattern of teenage risk-taking, sensation-seeking and emotional intensity. It's not that teens *can't* assess risk; in cool, calm moments they often reason as well as adults. But in the *heat* of the moment — with peers watching, emotions high, a reward dangling — the eager accelerator overpowers the unfinished brakes.

<Callout type="info" title="A feature, not just a bug">
This developmental window may be partly *adaptive*. The drive to seek novelty, take risks and bond with peers is exactly what once pushed adolescents to leave the nest, explore, and form the new relationships of adulthood. The "reckless teenager" is, in part, doing the evolutionary job of growing up.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="When moral intuition fights moral logic" kind="interactive">

Remember the intuitionist idea from the last lesson — that gut feeling often beats reasoning to the verdict? Adolescence and adulthood are where you can feel it most sharply, in dilemmas designed to pit emotion against arithmetic.

Work through both versions of the classic trolley problem. The body count is *identical* in each — one life weighed against five — yet watch what your gut does:

Most people pull the lever but refuse to push the stranger, even though the maths is the same. Brain-imaging studies show why: the up-close, hands-on harm of *pushing* fires emotional circuits hard, while the impersonal *switch* engages cooler, calculating regions. Your moral verdict bends to the **framing**, not just the numbers — a vivid demonstration that feeling and reasoning are *both* at work, and don't always agree.

</Step>

<Step title="Emerging adulthood" kind="explain">

In Erikson's day you became an adult at around eighteen: job, marriage, children, settled. Today, across many societies, the late teens and twenties have stretched into something new. Psychologist Jeffrey Arnett named it **emerging adulthood** — a distinct life stage, roughly 18–25, that is neither adolescence nor full adulthood.

It's marked by **identity exploration** (in love and work), **instability** (frequent moves and changes), **self-focus**, a **feeling of being in-between**, and a wide-open sense of **possibility**. It maps onto Erikson's *intimacy versus isolation* but stretches the timeline: young people now build their sense of self and try out commitments for far longer before settling. Whether this is a luxury, a burden, or simply the new normal is one of developmental psychology's liveliest debates.

</Step>

<Step title="The two faces of cognitive ageing" kind="explain">

What happens to the mind as we grow old? The honest answer is: it depends *which* mind. Psychologists distinguish two kinds of intelligence, and they age in opposite directions.

**Fluid intelligence** — raw processing speed, working memory, on-the-spot reasoning with novel problems — tends to *peak in early adulthood and gradually decline*. **Crystallised intelligence** — accumulated knowledge, vocabulary, expertise, the wisdom of experience — tends to *hold steady or even grow* well into old age.

So the older adult is slower to learn an unfamiliar gadget (fluid) but commands a vast store of knowledge and judgement (crystallised). And recall the cross-sectional trap from the start of this world: some apparent "decline" in older groups is really a **cohort effect** — they grew up with less schooling — not pure ageing. The losses are real, but smaller and more selective than the gloomy stereotype suggests.

</Step>

<Step title="Successful ageing, wisdom and well-being" kind="explain">

Here's one of the most cheering findings in all of psychology: despite the losses, **emotional well-being often *improves* with age.** Older adults frequently report *more* happiness, *less* anxiety and *better* emotional regulation than the young.

One leading explanation is **socioemotional selectivity theory**: when people sense time is growing short, they shift priorities away from chasing new information and toward savouring meaningful relationships and positive experiences. They prune the trivial and invest in what matters. This dovetails with Erikson's final stage — **integrity versus despair** — and with the idea of **wisdom**: the integration of knowledge, experience and emotional balance into sound judgement about life's genuinely hard, uncertain questions.

**Successful ageing**, then, isn't merely the absence of decline. It's *selective optimisation with compensation* — focusing on what you do best, working to maintain it, and finding clever workarounds for what slips. Development, it turns out, is a project that runs all the way to the end — and can be done well right to the finish.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Puberty** brings physical maturity; the adolescent brain is heavily **remodelled** (synaptic pruning + myelination), maturing back-to-front with the prefrontal cortex last.
- The **maturation gap** — an early-maturing reward system versus late-maturing prefrontal control — peaks in adolescence and helps explain teen risk-taking, especially in emotional, peer-present moments.
- Moral dilemmas like the **trolley problem** show **framing** swaying our verdicts: gut emotion and cool reasoning are both at work and often disagree.
- **Emerging adulthood** (~18–25) is a distinct modern stage of exploration, instability and possibility.
- Cognitive ageing splits in two: **fluid** intelligence declines while **crystallised** intelligence holds or grows; some "decline" is a **cohort effect**.
- Well-being often *rises* with age (**socioemotional selectivity**); **successful ageing** means selective optimisation with compensation, and the integration of experience into **wisdom**.

</Step>
