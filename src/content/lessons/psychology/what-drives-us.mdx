<Step title="The question behind every action" kind="explain">

Why did you get out of bed today? Why does anyone cross a desert, or learn an instrument, or reach for a second slice of cake they don't even need?

**Motivation** is the engine of behaviour — the set of forces that *energise* an action and *direct* it toward a goal. Psychologists have spent a century asking what those forces actually are. The honest answer is that there isn't one engine but several, and the great theories of motivation are really arguments about *where the push comes from*.

Some say it comes from **inside** us — wired-in urges, or unpleasant needs we act to relieve. Others say it comes from **outside** us — goals and rewards that pull us toward them. Let's meet the four classic answers.

</Step>

<Step title="Four theories of the engine" kind="explain">

- **Instinct theory.** Early psychologists argued we are born with fixed, unlearned behaviour patterns — *instincts*. A bird builds a nest no one taught it; a baby roots for milk. The trouble: naming something an "instinct" doesn't explain it, and humans clearly learn far too much for instinct to be the whole story.
- **Drive-reduction theory.** A biological **need** (no water) creates a tense, unpleasant **drive** (thirst). We act to *reduce* the drive and return the body to balance — **homeostasis**. Once you drink, the drive falls and the behaviour stops.
- **Arousal theory.** We don't just reduce tension — sometimes we *seek* it. This theory says we act to keep stimulation at a comfortable, **optimal level**: hunting excitement when bored, calm when overwhelmed.
- **Incentive theory.** Behaviour is also **pulled** from outside by **incentives** — rewards and goals. You're full, yet the dessert still tempts you. No internal need is unmet; the pull is external.

</Step>

<Step title="Compare the four engines" kind="interactive">

Switch between the theories and watch where each one locates the engine of behaviour — a push from within, or a pull from the world.

<MotivationTheories />

Notice that none of them is simply *wrong*. Thirst really is a drive you reduce; dessert really is an incentive that pulls. Real motivation almost always blends inner pushes and outer pulls at the same time.

</Step>

<Step title="The sweet spot of arousal" kind="interactive">

Arousal theory makes a sharp, testable prediction: performance isn't best when arousal is lowest *or* highest, but somewhere in the **middle**. Too little and you're sluggish and bored; too much and you're anxious and scattered. This inverted-U is the **Yerkes-Dodson law**.

<YerkesDodson />

Slide the arousal up and down, and switch between a *simple* and a *hard* task. The peak shifts: easy, well-practised tasks tolerate high arousal (a sprinter wants to be fired up), while difficult, complex tasks need calmer focus (a surgeon does not). There is no single "right" amount of arousal — it depends on the task.

</Step>

<Step title="Why none of them wins alone" kind="explain">

Each theory captures a real slice of motivation and stumbles on the others:

- Drive-reduction explains why you eat when hungry, but **not** why you ride a roller-coaster, solve a puzzle for fun, or stay curious when no need is unmet.
- Arousal theory explains thrill-seeking and boredom-busting, but the *ideal* level differs by person and task.
- Incentive theory explains why we chase rewards we don't biologically need, but downplays genuine inner drive.

The modern view simply keeps all of them on the table. A single act — say, training for a marathon — can be pushed by arousal-seeking, pulled by the incentive of a medal, *and* shaped by needs. Motivation is rarely one thing.

<Callout type="tip" title="Push vs pull">
A quick test: ask whether the force is *behind* you (a need or drive pushing you forward) or *ahead* of you (a goal or reward pulling you toward it). Most real motives have a bit of both.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Motivation** energises and directs behaviour; the classic theories argue over *where the push comes from*.
- **Instinct** (inborn patterns), **drive-reduction** (relieve a need to restore homeostasis), **arousal** (seek an optimal level of stimulation), and **incentive** (pulled by external rewards) each capture part of the truth.
- The **Yerkes-Dodson law**: performance peaks at a *moderate* level of arousal, and the peak is higher for simple tasks than for hard ones.
- No single theory explains everything — real behaviour usually mixes inner **pushes** and outer **pulls**.

</Step>
