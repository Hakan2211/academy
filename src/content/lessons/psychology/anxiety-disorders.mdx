<Step title="When the alarm won't switch off" kind="explain">

Anxiety itself is not the enemy. It's one of evolution's best gifts — a built-in alarm that sharpens attention and readies the body when something matters. A jolt of nerves before an exam or a flash of fear at a screech of brakes is the system working *exactly as designed*.

An **anxiety disorder** is something different: the alarm fires **too often, too loudly, or for too long**, going off when there's no real danger and refusing to switch off. The fear becomes out of proportion to the situation and starts to interfere with the person's life. The dial, in effect, gets stuck on "high."

</Step>

<Step title="Meet the family" kind="interactive">

Anxiety disorders are the **most common** group of psychological disorders — and among the most treatable. They share that runaway-alarm quality but show up in different shapes. Explore the main ones below; for each, notice both what it looks like and, crucially, *how it differs from the everyday worry everyone feels*.

A gentle but important note: these describe people *experiencing* a condition, not "anxious types." The difference between everyday nerves and a disorder is rarely the *feeling* itself — it's the **intensity, the persistence, and how much it interferes** with a life.

</Step>

<Step title="A closer look at OCD" kind="explain">

**Obsessive-compulsive disorder** is worth pausing on, because it's so widely misunderstood. It is not "liking things tidy." It has two locked-together parts:

- **Obsessions** — intrusive, unwanted thoughts, images, or urges that spike anxiety (fear of contamination, of harm, of having made a terrible mistake). The person usually knows they're irrational, which makes them no less tormenting.
- **Compulsions** — repetitive acts (washing, checking, counting, arranging) performed to neutralise the obsession and relieve the anxiety.

The cruel twist: the compulsion *works* — but only for a moment. The relief is real and immediate, which is exactly why the behaviour gets repeated and entrenched. That pattern points to a loop worth understanding on its own.

</Step>

<Step title="The anxiety cycle" kind="interactive">

Why does anxiety so often *grow* instead of fading? The answer is a self-feeding loop. A **trigger** sparks **anxiety**; to make the fear stop, the person **avoids** the situation or performs a **safety behaviour**; this brings fast **relief** — which **reinforces** the fear, so next time it's even stronger.

<AnxietyCycle />

Here's the heartbreaking logic: avoidance *feels* like the cure but is actually the **fuel**. Because the person escapes, the brain never gets to learn that the situation was safe. This is why the gold-standard treatment, **exposure therapy**, does the counterintuitive thing — it gently, gradually faces the trigger *without* the safety behaviour, letting the alarm finally prove itself false.

</Step>

<Step title="Where it comes from" kind="explain">

Anxiety disorders, like most disorders, grow from several roots interacting (the biopsychosocial pattern again):

- **Biological** — a genetic tendency toward a more reactive fear circuit; an overactive amygdala; imbalances in calming neurotransmitters like GABA and serotonin.
- **Learned** — a fear can be *conditioned* (a frightening experience paired with a once-neutral thing) and then *maintained* by the avoidance loop you just explored.
- **Cognitive** — catastrophic thinking ("this will be a disaster," "that twinge means I'm dying") manufactures and magnifies threat.

No single root tells the whole story — and that's good news, because it means there are many points at which the cycle can be interrupted.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- Anxiety is a useful **alarm**; an **anxiety disorder** is that alarm firing too often, too loudly, or too long — out of proportion and interfering with life.
- The family includes **GAD, panic disorder, specific phobias, social anxiety, and OCD** — the most common and among the most treatable disorders.
- **OCD** pairs distressing **obsessions** with anxiety-relieving **compulsions**; the brief relief is what entrenches the rituals.
- The **anxiety cycle** — trigger → anxiety → avoidance → relief → reinforced fear — explains why anxiety *grows*; **exposure therapy** breaks it by facing the trigger without escaping.
- Causes are **biological, learned, and cognitive**, interacting — leaving many points where the cycle can be interrupted.

</Step>
