<Step title="The most charged question in psychology" kind="explain">

We've defined intelligence, theorised it, measured it, and mapped its bell curve. Now we reach the question that has caused more harm — and more careful, hard-won science — than almost any other in psychology: **where do differences in intelligence come from?**

This terrain has an ugly history. Early IQ testing was used to justify immigration quotas and forced sterilisation, dressing up prejudice as science. So we'll move carefully, hold the evidence to a high standard, and separate what the data actually show from what people have *claimed* they show. The honest answer turns out to be more interesting — and more humane — than either extreme.

</Step>

<Step title="What heritability really means" kind="explain">

Twin and adoption studies consistently find that intelligence is substantially **heritable** — estimates for adults often land around 50–70%. People hear that and conclude IQ is "mostly genetic and fixed." That conclusion is wrong, for a subtle reason worth getting exactly right.

**Heritability** is a statistic about *variation within a population*, not about an individual. It tells you how much of the *differences between people* in a particular group, in a particular environment, track genetic differences. It does **not** mean your own intelligence is "70% genes," and it says **nothing** about why two *different* groups might differ.

<Callout type="info" title="The seed analogy">
Plant genetically varied seeds in rich soil and the height differences among them are highly "heritable" — the soil is equal, so genes drive the variation. Plant the same seeds split between rich and poor soil, and the *between-plot* difference is entirely environmental. Same genes, same heritability within each plot — yet the gap between plots is all about the dirt. Heritability within groups tells you nothing about gaps between them.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="The Flynn effect: a knockout punch for 'fixed'" kind="interactive">

If intelligence were rigidly fixed by genes, average scores should hold steady over time. They don't. Across the 20th century, raw IQ-test performance **rose dramatically** — about three points every decade — in country after country. This is the **Flynn effect**, after James Flynn, who documented it.

<FlynnEffect />

Genes can't change meaningfully in two or three generations. So this enormous rise *must* be environmental — better nutrition, more and longer schooling, and a modern world that constantly trains abstract reasoning. The Flynn effect is decisive proof that environment powerfully shapes IQ scores, and a direct warning against reading any score as an "innate," unchangeable quantity.

</Step>

<Step title="The interaction, not the tug-of-war" kind="explain">

So is it nature *or* nurture? Neither — and the question itself is the mistake. As you learned early on this island, the honest frame is **always both, interacting**.

Genes and environment don't add up like separate ingredients; they *multiply* and feed back on each other:

- A child with a genetic knack for words seeks out books, which builds vocabulary, which earns praise, which fuels more reading. A tiny genetic head start gets *amplified* by the environment it creates around itself.
- Identical genes can yield very different outcomes in enriching versus deprived settings — exactly the Flynn effect, writ small.

Intelligence is the product of this lifelong dance, not a fixed dose handed out at birth.

</Step>

<Step title="Test bias and fairness" kind="explain">

If IQ scores can be pushed around by environment and situation, we have to ask whether the *tests themselves* are fair. Two distinct problems:

- **Cultural bias in content.** A test item that assumes familiarity with a particular vocabulary, object or custom measures *exposure to that culture* as much as reasoning. Test-makers work hard to strip these out (and "culture-reduced" items like matrices help), but no test floats entirely free of the world that made it.
- **Stereotype threat.** As you saw last lesson, simply reminding people of a negative stereotype before a test depresses their performance — no change in ability, just a change in the situation. Part of any measured gap between groups can come from the *testing context itself*.

These are reasons to treat group differences in scores with deep caution. A score gap is not evidence of an ability gap, and it is **never** evidence that any group is inherently inferior — that idea has been examined repeatedly and is not supported by the science; it reflects bias, opportunity and environment, not innate worth.

</Step>

<Step title="What tests miss" kind="explain">

Step back and remember how *narrow* a single IQ number is. Even a perfectly fair, unbiased test would still miss enormous swaths of what makes a mind effective:

- **Creativity** — generating genuinely new ideas, which most tests, demanding one right answer, can't capture.
- **Practical and emotional intelligence** — Sternberg's street smarts, the ability to read people and situations.
- **Wisdom, character and grit** — knowing what matters, and the persistence to pursue it. Terman's gifted "Termites" showed that motivation, not raw IQ, separated the high achievers.

<Callout type="tip" title="The mature view">
IQ tests measure something real and useful — a *g*-like reasoning ability that genuinely predicts certain outcomes. But they are a narrow, environment-sensitive, sometimes biased slice of a vast human capacity. Respect the measure; never mistake it for the whole person.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- IQ testing has a **harmful history**; the science demands extra care and a high evidentiary bar.
- **Heritability** describes variation *within a population in a given environment* — not an individual's "percent genes," not fixedness, and **not** anything about gaps *between* groups (the seed-and-soil analogy).
- The **Flynn effect** — roughly +3 IQ points per decade through the 20th century — proves environment (nutrition, schooling, abstraction) strongly shapes scores, since genes can't change that fast.
- Nature and nurture **interact and amplify** each other; "which one?" is the wrong question.
- **Test bias** (cultural content) and **stereotype threat** mean score gaps are not ability gaps and are **never** evidence of innate group inferiority.
- Even fair tests **miss** creativity, practical and emotional intelligence, wisdom and grit — respect the measure, but never confuse it with the whole person.

</Step>
