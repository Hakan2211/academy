<Step title="The question that needed an answer" kind="explain">

You've now met five great traditions, each confident it understands suffering and how to relieve it. But confidence isn't evidence. In 1952 the psychologist **Hans Eysenck** dropped a bombshell: reviewing the data, he claimed that people who got therapy improved no more than people who simply waited. If true, the entire enterprise was an expensive placebo.

The claim was wrong — but it was *productive*. It forced the field to stop assuming therapy worked and start **measuring** whether it did. The result is one of the better-tested claims in all of psychology. This deep-dive walks through how we know therapy helps, why the rival schools all seem to work, how to match a therapy to a problem, and where prevention fits in.

</Step>

<Step title="How you measure a therapy" kind="explain">

Answering "does it work?" honestly is hard, because people often improve anyway. To isolate therapy's effect you need controls:

- A **no-treatment** group (often a waitlist) shows how much improvement happens on its own — **spontaneous remission**.
- A **placebo / general-support** group controls for attention, hope and a kind listener — the non-specific extras.
- A **bona-fide therapy** group gets the real thing.

The strongest design is the **randomised controlled trial (RCT)**: randomly assign people to conditions so the groups start equivalent, then compare outcomes. But one trial is a single data point. To see the big picture we need a way to pool *hundreds* of them.

</Step>

<Step title="Meta-analysis and the effect size" kind="explain">

The tool that settled the debate is **meta-analysis** — statistically combining the results of many studies into one overall estimate. Instead of asking "was the difference significant?" in each study, it asks "**how big** is the effect, on average, across all of them?"

The measure is the **effect size** (Cohen's *d*): how far apart the treated and untreated groups are, in standard-deviation units.

<Formula tex="d = \dfrac{\bar{x}_{\text{treated}} - \bar{x}_{\text{control}}}{s_{\text{pooled}}}" block />

By convention, *d* ≈ 0.2 is small, 0.5 medium, 0.8 large. When **Smith and Glass** first meta-analysed psychotherapy in 1977, they found an effect size around **0.85** — large. The plain-English translation: **the average treated person ends up better off than about 80% of untreated people.** Eysenck was decisively refuted.

</Step>

<Step title="The dodo-bird verdict and common factors" kind="explain">

In *Alice in Wonderland*, the Dodo organises a chaotic race and, asked who won, declares: "**Everyone has won, and all must have prizes.**" Psychologists borrowed the line for one of the field's most replicated findings — the **dodo-bird verdict**: when properly delivered, the major therapies produce **roughly equivalent** outcomes overall.

If CBT, psychodynamic and person-centred therapy work about equally well despite their wildly different theories, then much of what heals can't be the specific theory. It must be what they **share** — the **common factors**:

- A strong **therapeutic alliance** (the single most consistent predictor of success).
- **Hope and positive expectancy** — believing change is possible.
- A believable **rationale** and a structured plan, giving the chaos of distress a shape.
- The chance to **face problems** rather than avoid them, with a caring, attentive listener.

This is why the alliance, first highlighted by Rogers, turns out to matter across *every* school.

</Step>

<Step title="But the dodo doesn't win every race" kind="explain">

The common-factors story is powerful, but taken too far it's wrong. The dodo-bird verdict holds *on average*, across mixed problems — and "on average" hides important exceptions. For some **specific disorders, specific methods clearly win**:

- **Phobias, panic and OCD** respond best to **exposure-based** behavioural and cognitive-behavioural methods.
- **Depression** has strong evidence for **CBT** and interpersonal therapy.
- **Bipolar disorder** and **schizophrenia** typically require **medication** as the foundation, with therapy supporting it.

So the mature view holds two truths at once: **common factors carry most therapies a long way**, *and* **matching the right specific method to the right problem adds real, sometimes decisive, value.** Good clinical practice — what's called **evidence-based practice** — combines both: use the alliance, and choose methods the evidence supports for *this* problem.

</Step>

<Step title="Better than cure: prevention" kind="explain">

Treating distress once it's entrenched is harder than heading it off. So a whole branch of the field works *upstream*, on **prevention**:

- **Primary prevention** stops problems before they start — reducing poverty and abuse, teaching coping and emotional skills in schools, building supportive communities.
- **Secondary prevention** catches problems early, when they're easiest to treat — screening, early-intervention services, brief support at the first sign of trouble.
- **Tertiary prevention** limits the damage and relapse of established conditions — rehabilitation, ongoing support, relapse-prevention plans.

The same logic that made medicine shift from treating disease to *preventing* it is reshaping mental health: the cheapest, kindest distress to treat is the kind that never fully develops.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Eysenck (1952)** claimed therapy didn't beat waiting; this spurred the field to **measure** outcomes rigorously.
- **Meta-analysis** pools many studies into one **effect size** (*d*). **Smith & Glass** found *d* ≈ 0.85 — the average treated person is better off than ~80% of the untreated. Therapy beats both no treatment and placebo.
- The **dodo-bird verdict**: well-delivered therapies work about equally on average, pointing to **common factors** — the **therapeutic alliance**, hope/expectancy, a believable rationale, and facing problems with a caring listener.
- But for **specific disorders**, specific methods still win (exposure for phobias/OCD, CBT for depression, medication-led care for bipolar and schizophrenia) — so **evidence-based practice** combines alliance *and* matched method.
- **Prevention** (primary, secondary, tertiary) works upstream — the kindest distress to treat is the kind that never fully develops.

</Step>
