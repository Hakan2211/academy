<Step title="Psychology studies how you study" kind="explain">

Here's a delightful twist: psychology has spent a century studying **learning and memory** — which means it can tell you, with evidence, how to learn its own material (and everything else) far more effectively than most students do.

The catch is that the methods that *feel* most productive are often the weakest, and the ones that work best feel harder. So before the content, let's spend a few minutes on the meta-skill that multiplies everything after it: **learning how to learn.**

</Step>

<Step title="The methods that actually work" kind="interactive">

Most students re-read and highlight. Both feel reassuring and both are nearly useless for long-term memory, because they're **passive** — your eyes move while your brain coasts. Compare the methods head to head:

<StudyScience />

The winners share one feature: they force **effortful retrieval**. Pulling an answer *out* of memory is what strengthens it — far more than putting information *in* again by re-reading.

</Step>

<Step title="Two ideas worth their weight in gold" kind="explain">

Two findings do most of the work:

**The testing effect (active recall).** Closing the book and trying to *retrieve* what you learned — even when you struggle — builds far stronger memories than reviewing. Every quiz on this island isn't just a check; it's a memory-building tool.

**Spaced practice.** Reviewing at *spreading intervals* (a day later, then three, then a week) beats cramming the same total time into one session. Forgetting a little and then recalling it is exactly the workout memory needs.

<Callout type="tip" title="This app is built on this">
The **Practice** mode in this app uses **spaced retrieval** — it brings back what you've learned right as you're about to forget it. That's not a gimmick; it's the single best-supported study technique in all of psychology.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Think, don't just absorb" kind="explain">

One more multiplier: **elaboration** — connecting new ideas to things you already know. Ask *why* and *how*, invent your own examples, explain a concept aloud as if teaching it. A fact tied into a web of meaning is far easier to find later than one floating alone.

And throughout, practise **critical thinking** — the habit psychology most wants to grow in you. Don't accept a claim because it's printed or confident. Ask: *What's the evidence? Could something else explain this? How would I test it?* That stance is the whole of science compressed into a daily reflex.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- Psychology can tell you how to learn — and the comfortable methods (**re-reading, highlighting**) are the weak ones.
- **Active recall** (the testing effect) and **spaced practice** are the most powerful study techniques known; both rely on **effortful retrieval**.
- **Elaboration** — linking new ideas to old and explaining them yourself — deepens memory further.
- Practise **critical thinking** everywhere: always ask for the evidence and the alternative explanation.

</Step>
