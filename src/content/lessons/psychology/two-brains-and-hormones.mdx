<Step title="A brain in two halves" kind="explain">

Look at the cortex from above and you'll see it's split down the middle into two **hemispheres**, left and right. They look like mirror images, but they don't do exactly the same work. This division of labour is called **lateralisation** — some functions lean more on one side than the other.

The most famous example: in the great majority of people, **language** lives mainly in the **left** hemisphere. The right hemisphere tends to specialise more in spatial tasks, faces, and the overall *gestalt* of a scene. But here's the catch that pop psychology always gets wrong — read on.

</Step>

<Step title="Wired across: each side controls the opposite body" kind="explain">

The two hemispheres aren't just side by side — they're **cross-wired** to the body and the world:

- The **left** hemisphere controls the **right** hand and receives sensation from the right side of the body.
- The **right** hemisphere controls the **left** hand and the left side.
- And, because the optic nerves cross, the **left visual field** projects to the **right** hemisphere, while the **right visual field** projects to the **left** hemisphere.

Normally none of this is noticeable, because a thick bridge of some 200 million nerve fibres — the **corpus callosum** — constantly shuttles information between the halves. The two hemispheres work as one seamless team. Until that bridge is cut.

</Step>

<Step title="The split-brain experiment" kind="interactive">

In rare cases, surgeons once cut the corpus callosum to stop severe epileptic seizures from spreading. The result — studied by Roger Sperry and Michael Gazzaniga — was extraordinary: a person with **two separate** information systems in one skull.

Try the classic test. Flash a word to one visual field and see whether the person can *name* it (left-hemisphere language) or only *point* to it (right hemisphere controlling the left hand). Toggle the bridge between intact and cut.

<HemisphereLab />

When the bridge is cut and a word hits the **left** visual field (→ right, mute hemisphere), the person says they saw *nothing* — yet their left hand can pick out the matching object. The right hemisphere knows; it just can't speak. The information never reached the talking side.

</Step>

<Step title="The myth of 'left-brained' and 'right-brained' people" kind="explain">

You've surely heard that logical people are "left-brained" and creative people "right-brained." It makes a tidy story. It's also **a myth.**

It's true that *specific functions* lateralise — language leans left, certain spatial skills lean right. But that is a far cry from whole *personalities* living in one hemisphere. In a healthy brain the corpus callosum keeps both sides in constant, lightning-fast collaboration; no one runs on half a brain. Brain-imaging studies find no evidence that individuals favour one hemisphere overall.

<Callout type="warn" title="Beware the neuro-myth">
"Left-brained vs right-brained personalities" is one of the most stubborn myths in popular psychology. The real finding — that certain *tasks* lateralise — got stretched into a claim about *people* that the evidence simply doesn't support. A good rule: be sceptical of any neat brain story that sounds like a personality horoscope.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="The other messaging system: hormones" kind="interactive">

Neurons aren't the body's only communication network. Running alongside the fast electrical nervous system is a slower, chemical one: the **endocrine system**, a set of **glands** that release **hormones** straight into the bloodstream to reach targets all over the body.

Explore the major glands below.

<Hormones />

The two systems make a beautiful contrast:

- **Nerves** are *electrical*, *fast* (milliseconds), *short-lived*, and *precisely targeted* — like a phone call to one person.
- **Hormones** are *chemical*, *slower* (seconds to hours), *longer-lasting*, and *broadcast* through the blood — like a message sent to everyone at once.

The **pituitary**, the "master gland," is controlled by the brain's **hypothalamus** — the very link you met in the limbic system. So the brain ultimately conducts both orchestras: the fast one and the slow one.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- The cortex has two **hemispheres** with some **lateralised** functions (language usually **left**); each hemisphere controls the **opposite** side of the body, and each visual field projects to the **opposite** hemisphere.
- The **corpus callosum** bridges the halves; **split-brain** patients (its severing) reveal two separate information systems — able to *point* to a left-field word but not *name* it.
- "Left-brained vs right-brained personalities" is a **myth**: tasks lateralise, but whole people don't run on half a brain.
- The **endocrine system** of glands and **hormones** is a second, slower communication network — chemical, broadcast, long-lasting — versus the fast, targeted, electrical nervous system.
- The brain's **hypothalamus** commands the **pituitary** master gland, so the brain conducts both systems.

</Step>
