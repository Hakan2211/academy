<Step title="The gap that makes a mind" kind="explain">

When the action potential reaches the end of the axon, it stops at a cliff edge. Between one neuron's terminal and the next cell lies a microscopic gap — the **synaptic cleft** — and the electrical impulse cannot leap it. To cross, the signal changes form: from **electrical** to **chemical**.

This hand-off happens at the **synapse**, and it is arguably the most important junction in all of biology. A single neuron may form **thousands** of synapses, and it's at these connections — strengthening, weakening, forming, pruning — that learning and memory physically live. The gap is not a flaw; it's where the brain becomes flexible.

</Step>

The normal cycle, step by step:

1. The impulse arrives at the **terminal** and triggers tiny sacs called **vesicles** to move to the membrane.
2. The vesicles release their cargo — molecules of a **neurotransmitter** — into the cleft.
3. The neurotransmitter drifts across and binds **receptors** on the receiving neuron, like a key fitting a lock — passing the message on.
4. Almost immediately, the messenger is cleared: pumped back into the sending neuron for reuse (**reuptake**) or broken down. The signal is brief and clean, ready for the next one.

</Step>

<Step title="Excite or inhibit" kind="explain">

A neurotransmitter doesn't simply "turn the next neuron on". Depending on the receptor it fits, a signal can be **excitatory** (nudging the receiving neuron *toward* firing) or **inhibitory** (nudging it *away* from firing).

This matters enormously. The receiving neuron is constantly tallying a vote from thousands of synapses at once — excitatory signals pushing *up*, inhibitory signals pushing *down*. Only if the net total crosses threshold does it fire its own all-or-none spike. The brain isn't just a network of "on" switches; it's a vast, continuous balance of *go* and *stop*.

</Step>

<Step title="Meet the messengers" kind="explain">

Different neurotransmitters carry different kinds of message. A handful you'll meet again and again — try selecting each in the demo above:

- **Dopamine** — reward, motivation, and movement. Too little is linked to Parkinson's tremor; its reward pathway is central to learning and to addiction.
- **Serotonin** — mood, sleep, and appetite. Many antidepressants work by keeping it in the cleft longer.
- **GABA** — the brain's main **brake**: the chief *inhibitory* messenger that calms neural activity. Anti-anxiety drugs and alcohol both boost its effect.
- **Acetylcholine** — muscle movement, attention, and memory. Its loss in the brain is a hallmark of Alzheimer's disease.

<Callout type="info" title="Why this is where drugs act">
Almost every drug that changes mood, thought, or behaviour — from caffeine to antidepressants to heroin — works at the synapse. Some mimic a neurotransmitter, some block its receptors, some jam its reuptake so it lingers. That's the whole point of the "drug modes" in the demo, and you'll return to them when you study consciousness and therapy. The synapse is the brain's most drug-able lever.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- Neurons communicate across a tiny gap, the **synaptic cleft**, by converting the **electrical** impulse into a **chemical** message.
- The cycle: impulse → **vesicles** release a **neurotransmitter** → it crosses and binds **receptors** → it's cleared by **reuptake** or breakdown.
- Signals can be **excitatory** or **inhibitory**; a neuron fires only if the net vote from thousands of synapses crosses threshold.
- Key messengers: **dopamine** (reward/movement), **serotonin** (mood/sleep), **GABA** (the inhibitory brake), **acetylcholine** (muscles/memory).
- The synapse is where **learning** is stored and where nearly every **psychoactive drug** acts.

</Step>
