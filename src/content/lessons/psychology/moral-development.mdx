<Step title="Where does morality come from?" kind="explain">

A two-year-old grabs a toy without a flicker of guilt. A ten-year-old refuses to cheat "because it's against the rules." An adult risks their job to expose an injustice they believe is simply *wrong*. Somewhere along the way, our sense of right and wrong doesn't just grow stronger — it changes *kind*.

**Lawrence Kohlberg** built the most famous theory of how moral reasoning develops. His radical move, borrowed from Piaget, was to study not *what* people decide but *why* — the reasoning behind the verdict. And to get at that reasoning, he told people a story.

</Step>

<Step title="The Heinz dilemma" kind="explain">

Kohlberg presented people with moral dilemmas, most famously this one:

A woman is dying of cancer. A druggist has discovered the one drug that might save her but charges ten times what it costs to make — far more than her husband, Heinz, can afford. Heinz begs, borrows and pleads, but the druggist refuses to lower the price or wait for payment. In desperation, Heinz considers breaking in to steal the drug. *Should he?*

Here's the twist: Kohlberg didn't much care whether you said *yes* or *no*. Two people can give opposite verdicts for reasoning at the *same* moral level — and the same verdict for *wildly different* reasons. It's the justification that reveals the structure of moral thought.

</Step>

<Step title="The three levels" kind="explain">

Kohlberg sorted moral reasoning into three broad **levels**, each with two stages:

- **Preconventional** — morality is about *consequences to me*. Right means avoiding punishment and getting rewards. ("Don't steal — you'll go to jail.") Typical of young children.
- **Conventional** — morality means *upholding social rules and others' approval*. Right means being a good person in others' eyes and maintaining law and order. ("Steal it — a good husband would" or "Don't — it's illegal and society needs laws.") Typical of most adolescents and adults.
- **Postconventional** — morality rests on *abstract principles* — justice, human rights, the value of life — that can outrank a specific law. ("Steal it — a human life is worth more than any rule.") Reached by some adults, not all.

Notice the *trajectory*: from the self, outward to society, and finally up to universal principles.

</Step>

<Step title="A different voice: Gilligan's critique" kind="explain">

Kohlberg's theory drew a powerful objection from **Carol Gilligan**, once his own student. She noticed his original research was based almost entirely on *boys and men* — and warned that his scale might be measuring one kind of morality while missing another.

Kohlberg's framework prizes an **ethic of justice**: abstract rights, rules and logic. Gilligan argued that many people — she emphasised women, though the pattern isn't strictly gendered — tend to reason from an **ethic of care**: relationships, responsibilities, and the concrete needs of real people. Asked about Heinz, a "care" reasoner might focus less on rights versus laws and more on *talking to the druggist, preserving the relationships, finding another way*. Such answers can score *lower* on Kohlberg's scale — not because they're less moral, but because the scale wasn't built to value them. Care and justice, most now agree, are *two complementary voices* in moral life.

</Step>

<Step title="The intuition challenge" kind="explain">

A newer wave of research adds a further twist: maybe moral reasoning isn't even in the driver's seat. **Jonathan Haidt** and others argue we often reach a verdict by fast, gut **intuition** first, then invent the reasoning *afterward* to justify what we already felt — the "emotional dog and its rational tail."

The strongest evidence comes from dilemmas where our intuitions clash with our logic — and you'll meet exactly that kind of dilemma in the next lesson. For now, hold both ideas at once: Kohlberg captured how our *reasoning* matures, while the intuitionist view reminds us that feeling often gets there first.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Kohlberg** studied moral *reasoning* — the *why* behind a verdict, not the verdict itself — using dilemmas like the **Heinz** story.
- Three levels: **preconventional** (consequences to me) → **conventional** (rules and approval) → **postconventional** (abstract principles like justice and life).
- The same verdict can reflect different levels, and opposite verdicts the same level — maturity is about the **reasoning**.
- **Gilligan** critiqued the theory's justice bias and added an **ethic of care** (relationships and needs) as a complementary moral voice.
- Newer **intuitionist** views suggest gut feeling often comes first, with reasoning following to justify it.

</Step>
