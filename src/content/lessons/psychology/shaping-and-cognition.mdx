<Step title="How do you reward a behaviour that never happens?" kind="explain">

Operant conditioning reinforces behaviours *after* they occur. But what if you want a dog to roll over, or a pigeon to peck a tiny disc — behaviours the animal would essentially **never** do by chance? You can't reinforce what never appears.

Skinner's solution was **shaping**: reinforcing **successive approximations** — steps that get gradually closer to the target behaviour. You reward whatever is *nearest* the goal right now, then raise the bar, then raise it again, sculpting the behaviour into existence one small step at a time.

</Step>

<Step title="Shaping in action" kind="explain">

To shape a rat into pressing a far-off lever, you might reinforce, in turn:

- merely *turning* toward the lever,
- then taking a step toward it,
- then approaching and touching it,
- and finally pressing it.

Each new requirement is just a little harder than the last, so the rat can always succeed and earn the reward. Animal trainers, speech therapists, and teachers all use shaping. It's also how you'd learn a complex skill: nail the rough version first, then refine.

</Step>

<Step title="Shape a behaviour yourself" kind="interactive">

A rat wanders near the left wall; the lever is at the far right. Each round it offers a few attempts. Reinforce the one **closest to the goal**, and watch the whole behaviour creep rightward. Keep raising the bar until it presses the lever.

Notice you never *told* the rat the plan — you simply reinforced the best approximation each time, and a complex new behaviour emerged. That's shaping by successive approximation.

</Step>

<Step title="Is learning always about behaviour?" kind="explain">

The behaviourists insisted learning was *only* observable behaviour-change driven by reinforcement. But a wave of findings showed the **mind** does real work in between — ushering in **cognitive learning**.

The opening shot came from **Edward Tolman**. He let rats explore a maze with **no reward at all**, then later introduced food at the goal. These rats suddenly ran the maze as well as rats that had been reinforced all along. They had been learning the layout the whole time — they just hadn't *shown* it.

</Step>

<Step title="Latent learning and cognitive maps" kind="explain">

Tolman called this **latent learning** — learning that occurs without reinforcement and stays *hidden* until there's a reason to use it. It proves a crucial point: learning and *performance* are different things. You can know something without displaying it, and reinforcement may affect whether you *perform* a behaviour more than whether you *learned* it.

He argued the rats had built a **cognitive map** — an internal mental representation of the maze's spatial layout, not just a chain of rewarded turns. The behaviourist story had no room for such an inner model; the evidence demanded one.

</Step>

<Step title="Insight: the sudden click" kind="explain">

More cracks appeared. **Wolfgang Köhler** studied chimpanzees with bananas hung out of reach. Rather than fumbling through trial-and-error, a chimp named Sultan would sit, *appear to ponder*, then suddenly stack boxes or join two sticks to reach the fruit — solving the problem all at once.

This **insight** — a sudden grasp of a solution after a period of no apparent progress — is exactly the "aha!" moment you feel when a puzzle abruptly clicks. It implies the animal was *mentally* reorganising the problem, not just being shaped by external rewards.

</Step>

<Step title="Learned helplessness" kind="explain">

A final, darker discovery joined cognition to motivation. **Martin Seligman** gave dogs inescapable shocks. Later, in a box where they *could* easily escape by stepping over a low barrier, many simply lay down and took the shock — they had *learned* they were helpless.

The key was **cognitive**: the animals had formed the *expectation* that nothing they did mattered, and that belief generalised to a new, escapable situation. **Learned helplessness** became an influential model of human depression — when people come to believe their actions can't change their outcomes, they stop trying, even when escape is possible.

<Callout type="info" title="From cage to clinic">
Learned helplessness reframed depression as partly *learned expectation*, not just brain chemistry — and pointed toward therapies that rebuild a sense of control. You'll meet these ideas again in the disorders and therapy worlds.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Shaping** builds new behaviours by reinforcing **successive approximations**, raising the bar step by step toward a target that would never occur by chance.
- **Cognitive learning** showed the mind does real work: **Tolman's latent learning** (learning without reinforcement, hidden until needed) and **cognitive maps** distinguished *learning* from *performance*.
- **Köhler's insight** — sudden "aha!" solutions — revealed mental reorganisation, not just trial-and-error.
- **Seligman's learned helplessness** — learning the *expectation* that one's actions don't matter — became a model of depression, joining cognition to motivation.

</Step>
