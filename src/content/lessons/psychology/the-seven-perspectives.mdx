<Step title="One mind, many lenses" kind="explain">

Modern psychology no longer fights over a single "correct" school. Instead it offers a toolkit of **perspectives** — complementary points of view, each shining its light from a different angle. None is the whole truth; together they give depth.

Think of seven photographers around the same statue. One shoots the marble's chemistry, another the play of light, another the sculptor's intentions, another the crowd's reaction. Each photo is real and partial. Psychology's perspectives work the same way on human behaviour.

</Step>

<Step title="The seven perspectives" kind="explain">

- **Biological** — behaviour grows from the brain, genes, hormones and neurotransmitters. *The hardware.*
- **Evolutionary** — our tendencies exist because they once helped ancestors survive and reproduce. *The deep history.*
- **Psychodynamic** — unconscious drives and early experiences steer us in ways we don't see. *The hidden current.*
- **Behaviourist** — we are shaped by rewards and punishments; behaviour is **learned**. *The training.*
- **Cognitive** — what matters is how we **process information**: perceive, remember, interpret, decide. *The software.*
- **Humanistic** — people are growth-seeking and free; what counts is subjective experience and reaching one's potential. *The person.*
- **Sociocultural** — thought and action are shaped by other people, culture and context. *The crowd.*

</Step>

<Step title="See them work together" kind="interactive">

Watching the lenses applied to a single behaviour is the fastest way to feel the difference. Pick a behaviour and read all seven explanations side by side — notice how each is plausible, and how none alone is complete.

<PsychPerspectives />

The richest understanding usually comes from **combining** lenses. Aggression, for instance, is best explained by biology *and* learning *and* culture interacting — not by any one of them.

</Step>

<Step title="Nature, nurture, and the truce" kind="explain">

Behind the perspectives lies psychology's oldest debate: **nature versus nurture**. Are we shaped by our biology (nature) or our experience and environment (nurture)?

The modern answer is decisive: **both, always, intertwined.** Genes set possibilities; environments determine which possibilities are realised. The same gene can lead to different outcomes in different settings, and experience itself can switch genes on and off. The question was never "which one" but "**how do they work together**" — a theme you'll meet again and again on this island.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- Psychology uses **seven complementary perspectives** rather than one true school: **biological, evolutionary, psychodynamic, behaviourist, cognitive, humanistic, sociocultural**.
- Each is real but **partial**; combining lenses gives the fullest picture.
- The classic **nature–nurture** debate is resolved as **both, interacting** — genes and environment continually shape each other.

</Step>
