<Step title="The idea that shook the world" kind="explain">

Trait theory *describes* personality. Sigmund **Freud** wanted to *explain* it — and his answer was explosive: most of what drives you, you cannot see. Beneath the small, lit stage of conscious thought, he argued, lies a vast dark theatre of **unconscious** wishes, fears and conflicts that secretly pull the strings.

Working as a physician in 1890s Vienna with patients whose suffering had no physical cause, Freud became convinced that buried psychological material was the culprit. From those cases he built **psychoanalysis** — the first full theory of personality, and one of the most influential (and most criticised) ideas in all of psychology. We meet it here as history that still shapes how everyone talks about the mind.

</Step>

<Step title="The iceberg of the mind" kind="interactive">

Freud's favourite image was the **iceberg**. The tip above the water is the **conscious** mind. Just below is the **preconscious** — things you could easily call up. And the enormous submerged mass is the **unconscious**, hidden from you yet, he claimed, running the show.

Across these levels he placed three forces — the **id, ego and superego**. Click each part to explore it:

<FreudIceberg />

- The **id** (fully unconscious) is the demanding infant: *I want it, now* — pleasure and survival drives.
- The **superego** is the internalised parent and society: rules, ideals, guilt.
- The **ego** is the harried negotiator in the middle, trying to satisfy the id realistically without enraging the superego.

</Step>

<Step title="A mind at war with itself" kind="explain">

In Freud's picture, personality *is* the running compromise the ego strikes between these three. A person dominated by the id is impulsive; one ruled by a punishing superego is rigid and guilt-ridden; a strong ego keeps a workable balance.

When the conflict gets too hot — when an unacceptable id impulse threatens to surface — the ego feels **anxiety**. And to manage that anxiety, it deploys a toolkit of unconscious tricks.

</Step>

<Step title="Defence mechanisms" kind="interactive">

These tricks are the **defence mechanisms**: ways the ego distorts reality just enough to keep anxiety at bay, all without your awareness. Click through the gallery — each comes with a concrete example:

<DefenseMechanisms />

A little of this is normal and even healthy (Freud rated **sublimation** — turning a raw impulse into something productive — the most mature). The trouble comes when defences become a person's whole way of dealing with the world.

</Step>

<Step title="The psychosexual stages" kind="interactive">

Where does all this structure come from? Freud's boldest and most disputed claim: personality is largely set in early childhood, as the child passes through five **psychosexual stages**. At each, pleasure-seeking energy focuses on a different body zone, and an unresolved conflict can leave a lasting **fixation** that colours the adult character.

<PsychosexualStages />

Almost no modern psychologist accepts these stages as literal science — there's little evidence for them, and the theory is unfalsifiable in places. But the underlying intuition — that **early experience shapes lifelong personality** — has proven durably important.

<Callout type="warn" title="Read this as history">
Freud's specific claims (penis envy, the Oedipus complex, oral fixations) are not supported by modern evidence and are presented here as the historically pivotal theory they are — not as established fact.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- Freud's **psychodynamic** theory roots personality in the **unconscious** — the submerged bulk of the mind's iceberg.
- Three structures negotiate constantly: the **id** (impulse), the **superego** (conscience) and the **ego** (the realistic referee). Personality is their running compromise.
- The ego fends off **anxiety** with **defence mechanisms** — repression, denial, projection, displacement, rationalisation, sublimation, regression — all unconscious.
- Freud claimed personality forms across five **psychosexual stages**, where unresolved conflict leaves a **fixation**.
- The specifics are largely rejected by modern science, but the core insights — **unconscious influence** and the **importance of early experience** — endure.

</Step>
