<Step title="The science of mind and behaviour" kind="explain">

Ask ten people what psychology is and you'll hear about dream meanings, lie detection, or reading body language at a party. The real definition is at once broader and more precise: **psychology is the scientific study of behaviour and mental processes.**

Unpack that and two words do the heavy lifting. **Behaviour** is everything an organism *does* that we can observe — a smile, a button-press, a racing heart, a child sharing a toy. **Mental processes** are the private events behind that behaviour — perceiving, remembering, feeling, deciding. And **scientific** is the word that separates psychology from a horoscope: its claims are built from evidence that anyone can check, not from intuition or authority.

</Step>

<Step title="Why 'scientific' changes everything" kind="explain">

Common sense is full of confident, contradictory advice. *Birds of a feather flock together* — but also *opposites attract*. *Out of sight, out of mind* — yet *absence makes the heart grow fonder*. Both can't be reliably true, and our gut can't tell us which is.

Psychology's answer is to stop guessing and **test**. Gather data, run experiments, measure carefully, and let reality settle the argument. This is why psychology lives among the sciences rather than among the self-help books — and why a surprising amount of "everybody knows" turns out, under scrutiny, to be wrong.

<Callout type="tip" title="Hindsight is sneaky">
Once you hear a finding, it can feel obvious ("of course!"). But people often judge *opposite* findings as equally obvious. Predicting a result **before** you know it is the real test — and far harder than it looks.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="The four goals" kind="interactive">

Whatever the topic — phobias, prejudice, memory, love — psychologists pursue the same four goals, in order. They want to **describe** what happens, **explain** why, **predict** when it will happen again, and, where helpful, **control** (change) it. Step through one ordinary behaviour to see the chain in action.

<GoalsOfPsychology />

Notice how each goal builds on the one before. You can't predict what you can't explain, and you can't explain what you haven't carefully described first.

</Step>

<Step title="Basic and applied" kind="explain">

Those goals split psychology into two great streams. **Basic research** chases understanding for its own sake — *how* does memory work, *why* do we sleep? **Applied research** takes that understanding and solves real problems — designing a safer cockpit, a better therapy, a less biased hiring test.

They feed each other constantly. A basic discovery about how fear is learned becomes an applied treatment for phobias; a stubborn real-world problem sends scientists back to the lab with a sharper question.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Psychology** is the **scientific** study of **behaviour** (observable actions) and **mental processes** (private inner events).
- Being scientific — testing claims against evidence — is what separates it from common sense, which is often confident but contradictory.
- Its **four goals** run in sequence: **describe → explain → predict → control**.
- **Basic research** seeks understanding; **applied research** solves problems; the two continually feed each other.

</Step>
