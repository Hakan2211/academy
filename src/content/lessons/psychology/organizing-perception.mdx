<Step title="From a flood of signals to a coherent scene" kind="explain">

So far we've followed raw energy into the senses and watched it become neural signals. But that's only sensation. What arrives at the brain is a chaotic flood — millions of receptor firings, a flat upside-down retinal image, overlapping sounds. Out of that chaos you experience something utterly different: a stable, three-dimensional world of *objects* you can name and reach for.

The work of turning the flood into a scene is **perception**, and it follows a sequence of clever moves:

- **Select** what to attend to.
- **Group** the pieces into wholes.
- **Judge depth** to build three dimensions.
- **Hold objects constant** as they move and change.

Let's walk through each.

</Step>

<Step title="Selective attention: the spotlight" kind="explain">

You cannot process everything, so perception begins with a choice: **selective attention**, the spotlight of the mind. At a noisy party you can lock onto one conversation and let the rest blur into babble — the famous **cocktail party effect**. Yet if someone across the room says *your name*, you snap to it instantly: the unattended channel was being monitored all along.

The flip side is how *much* you miss. In studies of **inattentional blindness**, people counting basketball passes fail to notice a person in a gorilla suit stroll right through the scene. Attention doesn't just sharpen what you see — it largely *determines* what you see at all. The world you experience is the slice your spotlight happened to land on.

</Step>

<Step title="Gestalt grouping: the whole is more" kind="explain">

Once attention is on a region, the mind organises its parts into wholes — automatically, before you can stop it. The **Gestalt** psychologists ("Gestalt" is German for *form* or *whole*) catalogued the rules. Their motto: *the whole is other than the sum of its parts.*

A few of the principles:

- **Proximity** — things near each other group together.
- **Similarity** — things that look alike group together.
- **Closure** — we mentally complete familiar shapes from fragments.
- **Continuity** — we prefer smooth, continuous paths over abrupt breaks.
- **Figure-ground** — we split any scene into a *figure* (the object) and its *background*.

</Step>

<Step title="Watch grouping happen" kind="interactive">

Toggle the Gestalt principles on a field of simple shapes and feel the grouping snap into place. The dots never move under "similarity" — only their colour changes — yet your perception of rows-versus-columns flips instantly.

<GestaltGrouping />

You didn't *decide* to see groups; the system did it for you. Perception is built on these automatic organising habits.

</Step>

<Step title="Depth cues: building the third dimension" kind="explain">

Your retina is a flat, two-dimensional screen — yet you perceive a world with depth. The brain reconstructs that third dimension from **depth cues**, in two families:

- **Monocular cues** work with just one eye, and are largely *pictorial* (artists use them): **relative size** (smaller image = farther), **linear perspective** (parallel lines converge), **interposition** (a blocking object is closer), and **texture gradient** (texture grows finer with distance).
- **Binocular cues** need both eyes: **retinal disparity** (each eye gets a slightly different view; the brain compares them) and **convergence** (the eyes turn inward for near objects). These give precise depth up close — and are what 3-D films exploit.

</Step>

<Step title="Toggle the depth cues" kind="interactive">

Turn the monocular cues on and off in a single scene and watch depth appear or collapse into flatness. Each cue is a hint; together they conjure a convincing 3-D world out of a flat image.

<DepthCues />

Strip them all away and the scene flattens to coloured shapes — proof that depth is something your brain *adds*, not something it merely receives.

</Step>

<Step title="Perceptual constancy and motion" kind="explain">

Two final feats make the perceived world stable rather than a flickering jumble.

**Perceptual constancy** — objects keep a steady identity even as the retinal image changes wildly. A friend walking away doesn't seem to *shrink*, though their retinal image does (**size constancy**); a door stays "rectangular" even as it swings and projects a slanted trapezoid (**shape constancy**). Your brain corrects for distance and angle automatically.

**Motion perception** — you detect movement partly from images sweeping across the retina, and partly by tracking objects with your eyes. The system is so eager to find motion that it *invents* it: a row of lights flashing in sequence looks like one moving light (the **phi phenomenon**) — the very trick behind every film and animation you've ever watched.

</Step>

<Step title="See constancy in action" kind="interactive">

Compare the raw retinal image with what you actually perceive. As an object recedes or a door swings, the image distorts — yet the object stays the same size and shape in your mind.

<PerceptualConstancy />

Constancy is so automatic it's invisible. But without it, every step you took would seem to make the world balloon and shrink — perception would be unusable.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Perception** organises the sensory flood into a coherent scene through selection, grouping, depth and constancy.
- **Selective attention** is a spotlight — the **cocktail party effect** shows its focus, **inattentional blindness** shows its cost.
- **Gestalt grouping** (proximity, similarity, closure, continuity, figure-ground) binds parts into wholes automatically.
- **Depth cues** rebuild 3-D from a flat retina: **monocular** (relative size, perspective, interposition, texture) and **binocular** (retinal disparity, convergence).
- **Perceptual constancy** keeps objects stable in size and shape despite a changing image; **motion perception** even invents movement (the **phi phenomenon** behind film).

</Step>
