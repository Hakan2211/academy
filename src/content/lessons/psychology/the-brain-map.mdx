<Step title="One organ, built in layers" kind="explain">

The human brain weighs about 1.4 kilograms and looks, frankly, like a wrinkled walnut. Yet inside that grey mass sits everything you are. One useful way to read it is as a structure built in **evolutionary layers** — older, survival-focused machinery at the core, newer thinking machinery wrapped around the outside.

We'll tour it from the bottom up: the **brainstem** that keeps you alive, the **cerebellum** that smooths your movements, the **limbic system** that feels, and the great **cortex** that thinks. Each layer matters; none works alone.

</Step>

A quick map of what you just explored:

- **Brainstem (incl. the medulla)** — the oldest part, sitting where the spinal cord meets the brain. It runs the automatic processes you never think about: heartbeat, breathing, blood pressure.
- **Cerebellum** — the "little brain" at the back. It coordinates muscles for **balance** and smooth, precise, well-timed movement (and helps with some learning).
- **Hypothalamus** — small but mighty: it runs **homeostasis** (temperature, hunger, thirst) and links the brain to the hormone system.
- **Cerebrum** — the large, wrinkled top: the seat of thinking, memory, language, the senses, and conscious movement.

</Step>

<Step title="The limbic system: where feeling lives" kind="explain">

Nestled between the brainstem and the cortex is the **limbic system** — a cluster of structures handling emotion, memory, and motivation. Three are worth knowing by name:

- **Amygdala** — the brain's alarm bell, especially for **fear** and threat. It can trigger a fight-or-flight response *before* your conscious mind has caught up.
- **Hippocampus** — essential for forming **new long-term memories**. Damage it and you can lose the ability to lay down new memories at all, even while older ones remain.
- **Hypothalamus** — the master regulator of drives (hunger, thirst, body temperature) and the gateway to the hormone system.

This is the emotional heart of the brain — fast, ancient, and powerful, often pulling the strings beneath conscious thought.

</Step>

<Step title="The cortex and its four lobes" kind="explain">

The wrinkled outer sheet of the cerebrum is the **cerebral cortex** — the crowning achievement of the human brain, where our most sophisticated thinking happens. Those wrinkles are no accident: folding crams far more surface area (and so more neurons) into the skull.

The cortex divides into **four lobes**, each with a signature role:

- **Frontal lobe** (front) — planning, judgement, decision-making, voluntary movement, and much of your personality. The very front, the *prefrontal cortex*, is the executive office of the brain.
- **Parietal lobe** (top/back) — touch, temperature, and your sense of the body in space.
- **Temporal lobe** (sides, by the ears) — hearing and language comprehension.
- **Occipital lobe** (very back) — vision; almost nothing else.

<Callout type="info" title="The frontal lobe and who you are">
The frontal lobe matures last — not fully until the mid-twenties — which helps explain teenage impulsivity. Its importance was revealed by Phineas Gage, a 19th-century railway worker who survived an iron rod blasting through his frontal lobe but emerged, by all accounts, a changed person: impulsive and erratic where he had been steady. The lesson stuck: this region houses judgement, restraint, and much of personality.
</Callout>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- The brain is organised in **layers**, from ancient survival machinery at the core to newer thinking machinery on the outside.
- **Brainstem** (incl. medulla): automatic life support (heartbeat, breathing). **Cerebellum**: balance and smooth movement.
- The **limbic system** is the emotional core: **amygdala** (fear/threat), **hippocampus** (new memories), **hypothalamus** (drives + hormone link).
- The **cerebral cortex** has four lobes: **frontal** (planning/judgement/movement/personality), **parietal** (touch/body sense), **temporal** (hearing/language), **occipital** (vision).
- The **frontal lobe** houses executive control and matures last — into the mid-twenties.
