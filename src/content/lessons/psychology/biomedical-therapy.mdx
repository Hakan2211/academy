<Step title="Treating the brain directly" kind="explain">

So far every approach has worked through *experience* — talking, learning, relating. **Biomedical therapy** takes a different route: it acts on the **body and brain directly**, on the assumption that some psychological distress reflects underlying biology — neurotransmitter imbalances or disrupted brain circuits.

The most common form is **psychopharmacology**: medication. Since the 1950s, psychiatric drugs have transformed lives, made the worst symptoms manageable, and helped empty the old asylums. To see how they work, we need to return to the **synapse** — the tiny gap where one neuron signals the next with chemical messengers.

<Callout type="info" title="Medication is a tool, not a verdict">
Psychiatric medication helps many people, often alongside therapy rather than instead of it. Decisions about any medication belong to a qualified prescriber and the individual — this lesson explains the *mechanisms*, not what anyone should take.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="A refresher on the synapse" kind="explain">

When a signal reaches the end of a neuron, it releases a **neurotransmitter** into the **synaptic cleft**. The messenger drifts across, **binds receptors** on the next neuron, and briefly changes that neuron's activity. Then it's cleared away — much of it pumped back into the sending neuron for reuse, a process called **reuptake**.

Almost every psychiatric drug works by **nudging this single scene**: changing how much messenger is in the cleft, how long it lingers, or whether it can bind at all. Keep that picture in mind — in the next step you'll manipulate it directly.

</Step>

<Step title="See how a drug changes the synapse" kind="interactive">

Pick a neurotransmitter, then switch the **mode** to watch how different drug classes act on the very same synapse:

- **Reuptake blocker** — the recycling pump is jammed, so the messenger lingers in the cleft and keeps signalling. This is how **SSRIs (antidepressants)** work: they block the **reuptake of serotonin**, leaving more of it available. Switch to *serotonin* and *reuptake blocker* to see it.
- **Agonist** — the drug mimics the neurotransmitter and binds receptors itself, *boosting* the signal.
- **Antagonist** — the drug plugs receptors so the real messenger can't bind, *blocking* the signal. This is how most **antipsychotics** work: they act as **dopamine antagonists**, dialling down an overactive dopamine signal. Switch to *dopamine* and *antagonist* to see it.

<SynapseViz />

Three small tweaks to one synapse — block reuptake, mimic, or block the receptor — underlie a huge fraction of psychiatric medicine.

</Step>

<Step title="The major drug classes" kind="explain">

Mapping those mechanisms onto the medicine cabinet:

- **Antidepressants** — many (the **SSRIs**) are **serotonin reuptake blockers**, leaving more serotonin in the cleft; they typically take weeks to work fully, which hints the story is more than "just chemistry".
- **Antipsychotics** — chiefly **dopamine antagonists** that block dopamine receptors, easing symptoms like hallucinations and delusions.
- **Anti-anxiety drugs (anxiolytics)** — many enhance **GABA**, the brain's main inhibitory ("brake") messenger, producing fast calming; some carry dependence risk and are used carefully.
- **Mood stabilisers** — such as **lithium**, used to even out the highs and lows of bipolar disorder.

A theme runs through all of them: drugs manage **symptoms** powerfully but don't, by themselves, teach new skills or rework patterns — which is why they're often combined with therapy.

</Step>

<Step title="When the brain needs more: stimulation and surgery" kind="explain">

For severe cases that don't respond to medication, biomedical therapy has more direct tools:

- **Electroconvulsive therapy (ECT)** — a brief, controlled electrical current induces a short seizure under anaesthesia. Despite its grim reputation, modern ECT is safe and can be **strikingly effective for severe, treatment-resistant depression**.
- **Repetitive transcranial magnetic stimulation (rTMS)** — magnetic pulses applied to the scalp gently stimulate specific brain regions. It's **non-invasive**, needs no anaesthesia, and offers another option for stubborn depression.
- **Psychosurgery** — operating directly on brain tissue. The crude old **lobotomy** rightly horrified people; today's rare, precise procedures are a last resort for the most severe, otherwise-untreatable cases.

</Step>

<Step title="Biology meets learning" kind="explain">

It's tempting to see biomedical therapy as the *opposite* of the talking and learning therapies — but the brain doesn't draw that line. Recall the behaviour therapies from earlier: exposure and counterconditioning literally **re-wire** fear circuits through learning. Talking therapy *changes the brain* too.

So the modern view is integrative. The **biopsychosocial model** says biology, psychology and environment all matter at once — and for many conditions the best outcomes come from **medication and therapy together**: the drug quiets the worst symptoms enough that the person can do the learning and relating that produces lasting change.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Biomedical therapy** acts on the brain directly, mainly through **medication**, which works by nudging the **synapse**.
- Drug mechanisms: **SSRIs** = **serotonin reuptake blockers**; **antipsychotics** = **dopamine antagonists**; **anxiolytics** often boost **GABA** (the brake); **mood stabilisers** (e.g. lithium) even out bipolar swings.
- For severe, treatment-resistant cases: **ECT** (effective for severe depression), **rTMS** (non-invasive magnetic stimulation), and rare, precise **psychosurgery** (the old lobotomy is rightly abandoned).
- The brain blurs the line between "biological" and "learned" — therapy changes the brain too — so the best results often come from **medication and therapy combined**.

</Step>
