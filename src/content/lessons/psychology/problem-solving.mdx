<Step title="What a problem really is" kind="explain">

A **problem** is just a gap: you're *here*, you want to be *there*, and the path isn't obvious. Getting a stuck jar open, planning a trip, proving a theorem — all share that shape. A goal, a starting point, and obstacles in between.

Psychologists study how minds cross that gap. The headline finding is that we have two very different gears: a slow, careful, guaranteed gear, and a fast, cheap, usually-good-enough gear. Knowing which one you're in — and when each fails — is most of the skill.

</Step>

<Step title="Algorithms vs heuristics" kind="explain">

There are two broad strategies for crossing the gap:

- An **algorithm** is a step-by-step procedure that's *guaranteed* to find the answer if you follow it completely. To find a word in the dictionary, you could check every page in order. It always works — but it can be painfully slow.
- A **heuristic** is a mental shortcut, a rule of thumb that's *usually* fast and good enough but offers no guarantee. To find that word, you flip straight to roughly the right region by its first letter. Fast — but it can misfire.

Most real thinking runs on heuristics, because life rarely gives us time to be exhaustive. One especially useful heuristic is **means–end analysis**: at each step, ask *what move most reduces the gap between where I am and the goal?* — then make it.

</Step>

<Step title="Solve one, then get stuck on one" kind="interactive">

Time to feel both sides. First, **solve** a puzzle using strategy. The Tower of Hanoi: move the whole stack to the right peg, never placing a larger disk on a smaller one. Use means–end analysis — every move should shrink the distance to a finished tower.

Then switch tabs to a famous problem that gets almost everyone **stuck** — and notice *why*.

<ProblemSolving />

The Hanoi puzzle rewards strategy: flailing randomly takes forever; thinking in terms of "what brings me closer?" gets you there in seven clean moves. The candle problem is different — it's blocked not by difficulty but by a hidden assumption you didn't know you were making.

</Step>

<Step title="Functional fixedness" kind="explain">

The candle problem traps us with **functional fixedness**: the tendency to see an object only in terms of its *usual* function. You see a box of tacks as a *container for tacks* — so it never occurs to you that the box itself could be a shelf.

The fix arrives the instant you let the box be something new. Empty it, tack the *box* to the wall, stand the candle on it. People who are handed the tacks *outside* the box (so the box reads as "just a box") solve it far more often — the framing did all the work.

Functional fixedness is everywhere: the coin you don't think to use as a screwdriver, the chair you don't think to stand on. Creativity is largely the act of breaking these invisible assumptions.

</Step>

<Step title="Mental set and the flash of insight" kind="explain">

A close cousin is **mental set** — the tendency to keep using a strategy that worked before, even when a simpler one is now available. If three jugs solved your last five water-measuring puzzles by a fixed formula, you'll keep using that long formula even when the next puzzle could be solved by one obvious pour. The rut that once helped now blinds you.

Sometimes, though, the answer arrives all at once — the **"aha!" moment** of **insight**. The pieces suddenly reorganise and the solution is simply *there*, often after you've stepped away from the problem. That sudden restructuring is exactly what breaking functional fixedness or a mental set *feels* like from the inside.

<Callout type="info" title="Why a walk helps">
Insight problems are notorious for cracking during a shower or a walk — when you stop forcing the old approach, the unhelpful mental set loosens and a fresh structure can surface.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- A **problem** is the gap between where you are and a goal, with obstacles between.
- **Algorithms** guarantee a solution but are slow; **heuristics** are fast rules of thumb that usually work but can fail.
- **Means–end analysis** is a key heuristic: at each step, make the move that most reduces the gap to the goal (as in Tower of Hanoi).
- **Functional fixedness** (seeing an object only in its usual role) and **mental set** (over-using a once-successful strategy) are invisible assumptions that block solutions.
- **Insight** is the sudden "aha!" restructuring — often arriving when you stop forcing the old approach.

</Step>
