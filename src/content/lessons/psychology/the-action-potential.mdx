<Step title="A battery, charged and waiting" kind="explain">

How does a neuron actually carry a signal down its axon? Not as a current flowing through a wire, but as a travelling **wave of electrical change** called the **action potential**. To understand it, start with the neuron at rest.

Even when it's doing nothing, a neuron is a tiny charged battery. The inside of the axon is slightly **negative** relative to the outside — about **−70 millivolts**. This **resting potential** is maintained by pumps in the membrane that push positive sodium (Na⁺) ions out and pull potassium (K⁺) ions in, keeping the cell *polarised* and primed, like a drawn bow waiting to release.

</Step>

<Step title="The spike, traced" kind="interactive">

When a strong enough signal arrives, the neuron fires. Watch the membrane voltage spike from rest, shoot up, and crash back — and read each phase as the marker passes through it.

<ActionPotential />

The phases, in order:

- **Resting (−70 mV)** — polarised and ready.
- **Depolarisation** — sodium channels fly open, Na⁺ floods *in*, and the voltage rockets up past **+40 mV**.
- **Repolarisation** — potassium channels open, K⁺ rushes *out*, and the voltage plunges back down.
- **Refractory period** — a brief overshoot and recovery during which the neuron *cannot* fire again, no matter how strong the next stimulus.

</Step>

<Step title="Threshold and the all-or-none law" kind="explain">

A neuron does not fire a little for a weak signal and a lot for a strong one. It obeys the **all-or-none law**: either the incoming signal pushes the membrane past a critical **threshold** (around −55 mV) and a *full* spike fires, or it falls short and *nothing* happens. There's no half-spike.

So how does the brain encode intensity — the difference between a tap and a slap? Not by bigger spikes, but by **how often** neurons fire and **how many** fire at once. A bright light or loud sound means a higher *firing rate* across *more* neurons — like Morse code, where meaning lives in the pattern of identical pulses, not their size.

</Step>

<Step title="Refractory period and speed" kind="explain">

Two more features make the system work:

- **The refractory period** forces a brief pause after each spike. This caps the maximum firing rate — and, importantly, it ensures the impulse travels in **one direction only**, since the patch just behind the spike is momentarily unable to re-fire. The wave can't run backwards.
- **Myelin sets the speed.** Recall the fatty insulation around many axons. The action potential doesn't crawl down a myelinated axon inch by inch — it **leaps** between the tiny gaps in the sheath, a process called *saltatory* (jumping) conduction. A myelinated axon can carry signals over **100 metres per second**; a bare one is far slower.

<Callout type="info" title="The same spike, every time">
Because firing is all-or-none, every action potential a given neuron sends is essentially identical in size. The richness of your experience — the difference between a whisper and a shout — is written entirely in *timing* and *numbers*, never in the height of any single spike.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="From electrical to chemical" kind="explain">

The action potential races to the end of the axon — and then hits a wall. Between one neuron and the next lies a microscopic gap, and electricity can't jump it. So at the terminal, the *electrical* signal converts into a *chemical* one, releasing messenger molecules that drift across the gap to the next cell.

That hand-off — from electrical impulse to chemical message — happens at the **synapse**, which is exactly where the next lesson begins.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- A neuron rests at about **−70 mV** (polarised), maintained by ion pumps — a charged battery, primed to fire.
- The **action potential**: **depolarisation** (Na⁺ in, voltage spikes to ~+40 mV) → **repolarisation** (K⁺ out, voltage falls) → a **refractory period** of recovery.
- Firing is **all-or-none**: a stimulus either crosses **threshold** (~−55 mV) and fires a full, fixed-size spike, or nothing happens.
- Intensity is coded by **firing rate** and the **number** of neurons firing — never by spike size.
- The **refractory period** enforces one-way travel; **myelin** lets the impulse *leap* between gaps for huge gains in speed.
- At the axon's end, the electrical impulse becomes a **chemical** message at the **synapse** — the next lesson.

</Step>
