<Step title="Reinforce every time, or only sometimes?" kind="explain">

So far we've reinforced *every* correct response. But in the real world, payoffs come on a schedule — sometimes after a set amount of work, sometimes after a set wait, sometimes unpredictably. A **schedule of reinforcement** is simply the *rule* deciding when a behaviour gets reinforced, and it shapes behaviour with surprising precision.

Reinforcing every single response is **continuous reinforcement** — great for *teaching* a brand-new behaviour fast. Reinforcing only *some* responses is **partial (intermittent) reinforcement** — and that, it turns out, makes behaviour far more persistent.

</Step>

<Step title="Two questions make four schedules" kind="explain">

Skinner found the partial schedules sort neatly along two axes:

1. Is reinforcement based on the **number of responses** (a *ratio*) or on the **passage of time** (an *interval*)?
2. Is the requirement **fixed** (always the same) or **variable** (changes unpredictably)?

Cross them and you get the famous four:

- **Fixed Ratio (FR)** — reward after a set number of responses (every 10th lever press; a coffee card stamped on the 10th cup).
- **Variable Ratio (VR)** — reward after an *unpredictable* number of responses (a slot machine).
- **Fixed Interval (FI)** — reward for the first response after a set time (a weekly paycheck; checking the oven as the timer nears).
- **Variable Interval (VI)** — reward for the first response after an *unpredictable* time (checking for a reply that could come any time).

</Step>

<Step title="Each schedule has a signature shape" kind="explain">

Skinner read behaviour off a **cumulative record**: a line that steps up with every response, so a *steep* line means rapid responding and a *flat* line means a pause. Each schedule carves a characteristic shape:

- **Ratio schedules drive high rates** — the faster you respond, the sooner the reward. **Variable ratio is the highest and steadiest of all**, because the next response *might* be the winning one.
- **Fixed schedules cause a post-reinforcement pause.** Right after a payoff you can relax for a bit (you *know* the next reward is a while off), then ramp back up.
- **Fixed interval produces a "scallop"** — barely any responding right after a reward, accelerating into a flurry as the deadline approaches (think of cramming as the exam nears).

</Step>

<Step title="Read the cumulative records" kind="interactive">

Toggle between the four schedules and study the trace. Look for the staircase pauses of **FR**, the relentless straight climb of **VR**, the lazy **FI** scallop, and the plodding even line of **VI**. The little ticks mark each delivered reinforcer.

The shapes aren't arbitrary — each falls straight out of the rule. When effort pays (ratio), you work fast; when only timing pays (interval), you slow down and watch the clock.

</Step>

<Step title="Why intermittent reinforcement is so sticky" kind="explain">

Here's the counterintuitive payoff. If you want a behaviour to *survive* once the rewards stop, reinforce it **partially**, not continuously. This is the **partial-reinforcement extinction effect**: behaviours learned on intermittent schedules are far more **resistant to extinction**.

The reason is about expectations. Under continuous reinforcement, the very first missed reward signals "the rules changed — stop." Under intermittent reinforcement, the learner is *used to* going unrewarded for stretches, so a run of nothing feels normal. They keep going long after the payoffs have dried up.

</Step>

<Step title="Why slot machines hook us" kind="explain">

Now you can explain the most engineered behaviour trap ever built. A slot machine pays on a **variable-ratio** schedule: a win could come on the very next pull, or the fiftieth — you can never know.

That combination is maximally compelling: VR produces the *highest, most persistent* response rate, *and* the variability makes it extraordinarily resistant to extinction. Every losing pull is just "not yet," never "stop." The same mechanism powers the pull-to-refresh feed, the loot box, and the buzz of *maybe* there's a new notification. Understanding the schedule is the first step to seeing the hook for what it is.

<Callout type="warn" title="Designed to persist">
Apps and games deliberately use variable-ratio rewards (random likes, surprise drops) precisely because that schedule resists extinction. Noticing the schedule helps you reclaim the choice.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- A **schedule of reinforcement** is the rule for *when* a response is reinforced; **continuous** reinforcement teaches fast, **partial** reinforcement builds persistence.
- The four partial schedules cross **ratio vs. interval** with **fixed vs. variable**: **FR, VR, FI, VI**.
- **Ratio schedules → high rates** (VR highest and steadiest); **fixed schedules → post-reinforcement pauses**; **FI → the scallop** toward a deadline.
- The **partial-reinforcement extinction effect**: intermittently reinforced behaviour resists extinction far better than continuously reinforced behaviour.
- **Variable-ratio** is why slot machines, loot boxes and feeds hook us — high, persistent responding plus strong resistance to extinction.

</Step>
