<Step title="More than a bad day" kind="explain">

Everyone has low days and high days. **Mood disorders** are not those — they are lasting, intense disturbances of emotional state that take over a person's life for weeks, months, or longer.

The two big ones sit at opposite shapes. **Major depressive disorder** is a persistent, crushing *low*. **Bipolar disorder** is a *swing* — between depressive lows and abnormally elevated highs called mania. Both go far beyond ordinary sadness or excitement, and both are real, common, and treatable.

</Step>

<Step title="Major depression" kind="explain">

**Major depressive disorder** is far more than feeling sad. Its hallmark is often **anhedonia** — the draining-away of pleasure and interest in things that once mattered. To meet the diagnosis, a cluster of symptoms must persist most of the day, nearly every day, for at least two weeks:

- a persistent low or empty mood, and loss of interest or pleasure;
- changes in sleep and appetite (too much or too little);
- fatigue and slowed thinking or movement;
- feelings of worthlessness or excessive guilt;
- trouble concentrating; and
- thoughts of death or suicide.

It's worth saying plainly: depression is not weakness, laziness, or a choice, and you cannot simply "snap out of it" any more than you can will away a fever. It involves real changes in the brain and body.

</Step>

<Step title="Beck's cognitive triad" kind="interactive">

*Why* does depression dig in and sustain itself? Aaron **Beck** offered an answer that reshaped treatment. He noticed that depressed thinking falls into three automatic, negative patterns — the **cognitive triad** — about the **self**, the **world**, and the **future**.

These thoughts feel like simple truths, but they act like a filter that lets in confirming evidence and screens out everything else — a self-confirming spiral. Beck's great insight was practical: if distorted *thinking* sustains the low mood, then learning to *test and reshape* those thoughts can lift it. That's the engine of **cognitive behavioural therapy (CBT)**, one of the most effective treatments we have.

</Step>

<Step title="Bipolar disorder" kind="interactive">

**Bipolar disorder** adds the other pole: **mania** — periods of abnormally elevated or irritable mood, racing thoughts, little need for sleep, grandiosity, rapid speech, and impulsive, sometimes risky behaviour. Mania can feel euphoric and productive at first, which is part of what makes it dangerous: judgement collapses while energy soars.

The contrast is the whole point. We *all* have ups and downs that stay within a normal range. In bipolar disorder the swings are larger, last far longer (often weeks), and disrupt the person's ability to function — a difference of kind, not just degree.

</Step>

<Step title="Biological factors" kind="explain">

Mood disorders have deep **biological** roots, which is why the cognitive picture is only half the story:

- **Neurotransmitters** — imbalances in serotonin, norepinephrine, and dopamine are linked to depression, and most antidepressants act on these systems.
- **Genetics** — both depression and (especially) bipolar disorder run strongly in families; bipolar disorder has one of the highest heritabilities of any psychological disorder.
- **Brain and body** — altered activity in mood-regulating circuits, and disruptions to the stress system and sleep, are common.

The honest summary is the biopsychosocial one: biology sets a vulnerability, and life stress, thinking patterns, and circumstances shape whether and how a disorder unfolds.

</Step>

<Step title="Suicide, and how to help" kind="explain">

Depression and bipolar disorder both raise the risk of **suicide**, so this deserves clear, caring attention.

<Callout type="warn" title="If this is urgent">
If you or someone you know is in crisis or thinking about suicide, please reach out right now — contact a local emergency number, a suicide-prevention helpline, or a trusted doctor or mental-health professional. You don't have to handle it alone, and help is available.
</Callout>

A few evidence-based truths that cut against common myths:

- **Asking directly does *not* plant the idea.** Calmly asking "are you thinking about suicide?" doesn't increase risk — it lowers it, by opening a door and showing you care.
- **Take it seriously, every time.** Talk of suicide, giving away possessions, or saying goodbye are warning signs, not bids for attention to be brushed off.
- **Listen, don't fix.** Stay with the person, listen without judgement, and *connect them to professional help* — you're a bridge, not the whole solution.

The most powerful thing an ordinary person can offer is presence, a willingness to ask, and help getting the person to someone trained.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Mood disorders** are lasting, intense disturbances of emotional state — far beyond ordinary ups and downs.
- **Major depression** centres on persistent low mood and **anhedonia** (loss of pleasure) plus changes in sleep, appetite, energy, and thinking; it is not weakness and can't be willed away.
- Beck's **cognitive triad** — negative views of **self, world, future** — is a self-confirming spiral that sustains depression and is the target of **CBT**.
- **Bipolar disorder** swings between **mania** and depression; the swings are larger, longer, and more disruptive than everyday mood variation.
- **Biological factors** (neurotransmitters, strong genetics, brain and stress-system changes) interact with thinking and circumstance.
- **Suicide** risk is real: ask directly (it helps, not harms), take every sign seriously, listen, and connect the person to professional help.

</Step>
