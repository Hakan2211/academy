<Step title="One memory, three stores" kind="explain">

In the 1960s, Richard Atkinson and Richard Shiffrin proposed a map of memory so clean and useful that it still anchors the field. In their **modal model**, information flows through **three stores**, each with a different capacity and a different lifespan:

- A **sensory register** that briefly holds raw incoming sensation.
- A **short-term store** that holds a tiny amount for a few seconds.
- A **long-term store** that holds vast amounts, potentially for life.

The drama is in the *flow between* them. Almost everything that enters is lost within seconds. Only the slivers you **attend to** and **rehearse** make the journey into lasting memory. Memory, in this picture, is a series of bottlenecks — and understanding them tells you exactly where information leaks away.

</Step>

<Step title="The sensory register: a flash that fades" kind="explain">

The senses dump far more into the brain than you could ever use, so the first store catches it all for an instant and then lets nearly all of it vanish.

- **Iconic memory** — a high-detail *visual* snapshot that lasts roughly a quarter of a second. (When a sparkler traces a line in the dark, you are seeing iconic memory.)
- **Echoic memory** — an *auditory* trace that lingers a few seconds, which is why you can answer "what did you say?" *after* you've already heard it.

This store has huge capacity but a brutally short life. Its only job is to hold the world steady just long enough for **attention** to grab the few things worth keeping.

</Step>

<Step title="Short-term memory: small and brief" kind="explain">

Whatever attention selects passes into **short-term memory** — the contents of your mind *right now*. It has two famous limits:

- **Capacity** is tiny: about **seven items, give or take two** (George Miller's "magical number seven"). That's why a phone number is hard and a licence plate slips away.
- **Duration** is short: without rehearsal, information fades in roughly **15–20 seconds**. Look up a number, get distracted for half a minute, and it's gone.

The way to beat the time limit is **rehearsal** — repeating the information to refresh it. The way to beat the capacity limit is **chunking** — but that's a trick we'll unpack next lesson.

</Step>

<Step title="Long-term memory: vast and durable" kind="explain">

The third store is a different beast entirely. **Long-term memory** appears to have **no known capacity limit** and can hold information for a **lifetime**. Your childhood home, the alphabet, a song from years ago — all wait here, often for decades.

But "long-term" does not mean "permanent and perfect". Retrieving from it can fail, and the trace can be distorted on the way out (you'll see just how much later in this world). The crucial point for now: the only way into this rich, lasting store is *through* the narrow short-term bottleneck — which is why how you handle those first few seconds decides what you keep.

<Callout type="info" title="A model, not a place">
The three "stores" aren't three rooms in your skull. They're a model of memory's behaviour — different capacities and durations. Later refinements (like working memory, coming next) revise the details, but the three-store flow remains a brilliantly useful starting map.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- The **Atkinson–Shiffrin (modal) model** routes information through three stores: **sensory register → short-term → long-term**.
- The **sensory register** holds raw sensation briefly and with huge capacity: **iconic** (visual, ~0.25s) and **echoic** (auditory, a few seconds). **Attention** selects the keepers.
- **Short-term memory** holds about **7 ± 2 items** for only **~15–20 seconds**; **rehearsal** refreshes it, **encoding** moves it onward.
- **Long-term memory** has effectively **unlimited capacity** and a **lifelong** duration — but it's reached only through the short-term bottleneck.

</Step>
