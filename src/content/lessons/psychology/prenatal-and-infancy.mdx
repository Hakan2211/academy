<Step title="The most dramatic chapter" kind="explain">

Nothing else in your life will ever change as fast as your first nine months. You go from a single cell to a fully formed human being with a beating heart, working organs and a brain wiring itself at a quarter of a million neurons a minute. The whole construction project happens before you take a single breath.

Because so much is being built so quickly, this is also a uniquely **vulnerable** time. To understand it, we divide the prenatal period into three stages — and the order matters, because what's being built changes which dangers matter most.

</Step>

<Step title="The three prenatal stages" kind="interactive">

The prenatal period runs in three acts: the **germinal** stage (the first two weeks, when the fertilised egg divides and implants), the **embryonic** stage (weeks 2–8, when the heart, brain and every major organ first take shape), and the **fetal** stage (week 9 to birth, when organs mature and grow).

Step through them below — and watch the **teratogen-sensitivity** bar, which shows how the danger window shifts.

<PrenatalTimeline />

A **teratogen** is any agent — alcohol, certain drugs, some viruses, radiation — that can harm prenatal development. The crucial insight is *timing*: the embryonic stage is most dangerous precisely because that's when organs are being constructed. The same exposure can be catastrophic at week 5 and far less harmful at week 30.

</Step>

<Step title="Teratogens and the embryonic window" kind="explain">

Why does timing matter so much? Imagine a teratogen as a wrench thrown into a factory. During the **embryonic** stage the factory is building each organ from scratch — a wrench in the works can derail the whole structure. Heavy alcohol exposure here, for example, can cause **fetal alcohol syndrome**: facial differences, stunted growth and lasting cognitive problems.

By the **fetal** stage the organs already exist, so damage is less likely to be structural — but the brain keeps developing right up to birth (and beyond), so it stays vulnerable throughout. The lesson for development generally: the *same* input can have wildly different effects depending on *when* it lands. Critical and sensitive periods, in action.

</Step>

<Step title="The surprisingly capable newborn" kind="explain">

For centuries newborns were dismissed as passive blobs. We now know they arrive remarkably equipped, starting with a toolkit of automatic **reflexes**:

- **Rooting** — stroke a newborn's cheek and they turn toward it, mouth open, hunting for the nipple.
- **Sucking** and **swallowing** — feeding, hardwired from the first day.
- **Grasping** — a touch to the palm and the tiny fingers clamp down with surprising strength.
- **Moro (startle)** — a sudden loss of support and the arms fling out, then clutch inward.

These reflexes are survival kits, present at birth and fading as voluntary control takes over. Newborns also see (best at about 20–30 cm — roughly the distance to a feeding caregiver's face), prefer face-like patterns, recognise their mother's voice and smell within days, and turn toward familiar sounds.

</Step>

<Step title="Maturation: the unfolding plan" kind="explain">

Much of early development follows **maturation** — the orderly, biologically driven sequence of growth that unfolds largely on its own schedule, relatively independent of experience.

Babies the world over roll, then sit, then crawl, then stand, then walk, in nearly the same order and around the same ages, regardless of culture or how much you "train" them. You can't rush a child into walking before their nervous system is ready, and you rarely need to push a healthy one who is. Experience shapes the *details and timing*, but the *basic sequence* is written into the biology — nature and nurture, once again, working as a team.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- The prenatal period has three stages: **germinal** (0–2 wks, dividing and implanting), **embryonic** (2–8 wks, organs form), and **fetal** (9 wks–birth, organs mature and grow).
- A **teratogen** is a harmful agent; the **embryonic** stage is most vulnerable because organs are being built then — *timing* decides the damage.
- Newborns arrive with survival **reflexes** (rooting, sucking, grasping, Moro) and real abilities: near-distance vision, a preference for faces, and recognition of a caregiver's voice and smell.
- **Maturation** is the orderly, biologically driven growth sequence (roll → sit → crawl → walk) that experience shapes only at the margins.

</Step>
