<Step title="More than the couch" kind="explain">

Say "psychologist" and most people picture a therapist nodding beside a couch. That's one real job — but it's a sliver of a sprawling field. Psychologists design aircraft cockpits, advise juries, coach athletes, build the algorithms that recommend your videos, study how toddlers learn words, and test whether a new teaching method actually works.

What unites them isn't a setting; it's a **way of asking questions about mind and behaviour, and answering with evidence.**

</Step>

Most of these map onto the perspectives you just met: a biological psychologist leans on the biological lens, a social psychologist on the sociocultural one. The lenses are the *ideas*; the fields are the *careers*.

</Step>

<Step title="Three jobs people confuse" kind="explain">

Three similar-sounding roles trip everyone up:

- A **clinical psychologist** holds a doctorate in psychology, specialises in assessing and treating mental disorders, and (mostly) treats with **talk therapy**. In most places they cannot prescribe drugs.
- A **psychiatrist** is a **medical doctor** (MD) who specialises in mental health and therefore **can prescribe medication** and other medical treatments.
- A **psychoanalyst** is specifically trained in the Freudian tradition — a particular *kind* of therapy, not a separate degree.

<Callout type="tip" title="Quick test">
"Can they prescribe pills?" usually means psychiatrist (a physician). "Doctorate, talk-based treatment, focused on disorders" usually means clinical psychologist. Many people see both — sometimes on the same team.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Researchers and practitioners" kind="explain">

Cutting across every field is one more split. Some psychologists are mainly **researchers**, generating new knowledge in universities and labs. Others are **practitioners**, applying that knowledge directly — in clinics, schools, companies, courts and hospitals.

The healthiest version of the field is a loop: practitioners surface the problems that matter, researchers investigate them, and findings flow back to improve practice. A psychologist is, at heart, a **scientist-practitioner** — someone who treats their own work as a hypothesis to be tested.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- Psychology is far wider than therapy — it spans clinical, cognitive, developmental, social, biological, organisational, educational and forensic work, among others.
- **Fields** are careers; the **perspectives** are the underlying ideas they draw on.
- **Clinical psychologist** (doctorate, talk therapy) ≠ **psychiatrist** (MD, can prescribe) ≠ **psychoanalyst** (Freudian-trained).
- Across all of it runs the **researcher ↔ practitioner** loop — the **scientist-practitioner** ideal.

</Step>
