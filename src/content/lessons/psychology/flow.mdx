<Step title="The moments time disappears" kind="explain">

Think of the last time you were so absorbed in something — a sport, a game, music, code, a conversation — that you looked up and an hour had vanished. You weren't checking whether you were happy. You weren't checking anything. You were simply *in* it.

The psychologist **Mihaly Csikszentmihalyi** (pronounced *cheek-sent-me-high*) spent decades studying these moments across artists, athletes, surgeons and chess players. He named the state **flow**: complete, energised absorption in an activity. Flow is the **E** in PERMA — *engagement* — and people who report more of it report richer, more satisfying lives. Curiously, flow doesn't *feel* like pleasure while it's happening; the joy arrives afterward, in the glow of having been fully alive.

</Step>

<Step title="The ingredients of flow" kind="explain">

Flow isn't random luck. Csikszentmihalyi found it tends to appear when several conditions line up:

- **Clear goals** — you know what you're trying to do at each moment.
- **Immediate feedback** — you can tell at once whether it's working.
- **A balance of challenge and skill** — the single most important ingredient (next step).
- **Deep concentration** on a limited field, with distractions gone.
- A merging of **action and awareness** — the self-conscious inner critic falls silent.

Notice what's missing: comfort. Flow lives at the *edge* of your ability, not in the easy middle. It's the opposite of slumping on a couch — it's effortful, and that's exactly why it's so rewarding.

</Step>

<Step title="The challenge–skill channel" kind="interactive">

Here is the heart of the theory. Plot how **challenging** a task is against how much **skill** you bring. Two things can go wrong, and one thing can go right:

- Challenge far above your skill → **anxiety**. You feel overwhelmed.
- Skill far above the challenge → **boredom**. It's too easy to hold you.
- Challenge and skill **matched, and both high** → the **flow channel**.

Drag both sliders and find the diagonal band where flow lives.

<FlowChannel />

The practical genius of this map: when you fall out of flow, you have two levers. **Bored?** Raise the challenge — add a constraint, a deadline, a harder target. **Anxious?** Build the skill, or shrink the challenge into a smaller piece. Flow is something you can *engineer*.

</Step>

<Step title="Flow, arousal, and the sweet spot" kind="interactive">

The flow channel echoes a law you may have met in the stress world: the **Yerkes–Dodson** curve. Performance — and engagement — rises with arousal up to a point, then collapses when arousal becomes overwhelm. Too little and you're flat; too much and you're frazzled; in between is the productive sweet spot.

<YerkesDodson />

Flow is essentially that sweet spot experienced from the inside: aroused enough to be fully switched on, not so aroused that anxiety floods in. Designing your day to spend more time in this band — meaningful challenges matched to growing skill — is one of the most reliable routes to engagement.

</Step>

<Step title="Savouring: flow's gentle cousin" kind="explain">

Not every good moment is high-octane flow. Positive psychology also prizes **savouring** — deliberately slowing down to fully notice and stretch out a pleasant experience: the first sip of coffee, a sunset, a friend's laugh.

Savouring matters because of the treadmill from the last lesson: we adapt to the good things and stop noticing them. Savouring is the antidote — it counters adaptation by bringing attention *back* to what's already good. Where flow gives you the joy of total absorption, savouring gives you the joy of full appreciation. Both are skills of attention, and both are trainable.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Flow** (Csikszentmihalyi) is complete, energised absorption — the **E**ngagement pillar of PERMA; its joy is felt mostly *afterward*.
- It needs **clear goals, immediate feedback, deep focus**, and above all a **balance of challenge and skill**.
- The **challenge–skill channel**: too much challenge → **anxiety**, too little → **boredom**, matched-and-high → **flow** — and you can engineer it by adjusting challenge or skill.
- It mirrors the **Yerkes–Dodson** sweet spot of arousal. **Savouring** is its quieter cousin: slowing down to fully appreciate good moments, countering hedonic adaptation.

</Step>
