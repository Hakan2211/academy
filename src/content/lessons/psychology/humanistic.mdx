<Step title="A hopeful rebellion" kind="explain">

By the mid-twentieth century, two gloomy pictures of human nature dominated. Freud's psychodynamics said we're driven by dark unconscious instincts. Behaviourism said we're nothing but conditioned responses to rewards and punishments. To a new generation of thinkers, both felt cold, deterministic and demeaning.

So the **humanistic** psychologists launched a "third force". Their starting belief was radically optimistic: people are basically **good**, fundamentally **free**, and driven above all by an innate push to **grow** and fulfil their potential. The task of personality theory wasn't to diagnose what's broken — it was to understand the conditions that let a person flourish.

</Step>

<Step title="Rogers: the self and its conditions" kind="explain">

Carl **Rogers** built his theory around the **self-concept** — your organised picture of who you are. He drew a crucial gap between two selves:

- the **real self** — who you actually are right now, and
- the **ideal self** — who you wish to be.

When these two roughly match, Rogers called it **congruence**, and it brings contentment and healthy growth. When they gap wide apart — **incongruence** — the result is anxiety, defensiveness and low self-worth. You feel like a fraud, or chronically not-enough.

</Step>

<Step title="The magic ingredient: unconditional positive regard" kind="explain">

What keeps the two selves congruent? Rogers' famous answer is the way others treat us — especially in childhood.

**Conditional positive regard** is love with strings: *I'll accept you if you get the grades, win the game, stay quiet.* The child learns to bury the disapproved-of parts of themselves, distorting the self-concept to keep the love. **Unconditional positive regard** is acceptance without strings: *I value you, full stop — even when I dislike what you did.* It frees a person to be honest about who they really are, and so to grow.

<Callout type="tip" title="Acceptance is not approval">
Unconditional positive regard doesn't mean approving of every behaviour. A parent can firmly stop a child hitting while still communicating "you are loved and valued." It separates the *person* (always accepted) from the *act* (which can be corrected).
</Callout>

This idea became the engine of Rogers' **person-centred therapy**, where the therapist offers exactly this warm, non-judgemental acceptance — and it reshaped counselling worldwide.

</Step>

<Step title="Maslow: climbing toward your potential" kind="explain">

Abraham **Maslow** gave the third force its most famous image: a **hierarchy of needs**, often drawn as a pyramid. We must satisfy lower, more urgent needs before higher ones pull at us:

1. **Physiological** — food, water, sleep.
2. **Safety** — security, shelter, stability.
3. **Love and belonging** — friendship, intimacy, acceptance.
4. **Esteem** — respect, achievement, recognition.
5. **Self-actualisation** — becoming everything you are capable of becoming.

That summit, **self-actualisation**, is the realisation of one's full, unique potential — the same growth drive Rogers describes. Maslow studied people he saw as self-actualised (Lincoln, Einstein) and found common traits: creativity, deep relationships, acceptance of self and others, and occasional **peak experiences** of profound joy and meaning.

</Step>

<Step title="The verdict" kind="explain">

The humanistic view transformed therapy, education and how we talk about self-worth — and its emphasis on growth and the self anticipated today's **positive psychology**.

Its weakness is scientific. Concepts like "self-actualisation" are warm but **vague and hard to measure or test**, and the rosy assumption that people are innately good can look naive against the historical record. Like the psychodynamic theories, humanism is rich in insight and thin in hard evidence — a portrait of the *experiencing person* that the trait and social-cognitive approaches complement rather than replace.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Humanistic** psychology — the "third force" — sees people as basically good, free and driven to **grow**.
- **Rogers** centred the **self-concept**, distinguishing the **real** from the **ideal** self; their match is **congruence**, their gap **incongruence**.
- Growth is fostered by **unconditional positive regard** — acceptance without strings — versus the self-distorting **conditional** kind.
- **Maslow's hierarchy of needs** runs from physiological up to **self-actualisation**: becoming your full potential.
- The approach reshaped therapy and seeded **positive psychology**, but its key concepts are **vague and hard to test scientifically**.

</Step>
