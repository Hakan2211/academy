<Step title="What is an attitude?" kind="explain">

An **attitude** is a learned evaluation — a favourable or unfavourable feeling — toward a person, object or idea. You have thousands of them: about coffee, politicians, your hometown, broccoli. They steer how you notice, judge and react to the world.

The obvious assumption is that attitudes drive behaviour: if I like recycling, I recycle. But the link is looser and stranger than it looks — and once you understand it, you understand both how persuasion works *and* how it sometimes runs backwards, with behaviour reshaping attitude.

</Step>

<Step title="The attitude–behaviour gap" kind="explain">

For decades, psychologists were embarrassed to find that attitudes often predict behaviour **poorly**. People who claim to value the environment still fly often; people who say they're honest still cheat a little. Why the gap?

Attitudes guide behaviour best when:

- Other influences are **weak** (no strong situational pressure pushing the other way),
- The attitude is **specific** to the action ("I intend to recycle *this bottle, now*" beats a vague "I care about the planet"),
- The attitude is **strong, stable and easily recalled** — formed from direct experience, not just hearsay.

So the situation strikes again: a strong attitude can be overridden by a strong situation, and a vague attitude barely moves behaviour at all.

</Step>

<Step title="Two roads into your mind" kind="explain">

When someone tries to change your attitude — an ad, an argument, a campaign — the persuasion travels by one of two routes (the **elaboration-likelihood model**):

- The **central route** works through the *substance*: evidence, logic, strong arguments. It demands that you think. Attitudes formed this way are **durable and resistant** to later attack.
- The **peripheral route** works through *cues* unrelated to merit: an attractive spokesperson, a celebrity, an expert's say-so, sheer repetition, a catchy jingle. It bypasses thinking. Attitudes formed this way are **shallow and fleeting**.

Which route lands depends on you: when you're motivated and able to think hard, the central route wins; when you're distracted, tired or indifferent, the peripheral cues do the work.

</Step>

Notice that the *peripheral* messages feel persuasive precisely because they ask nothing of you. That's their danger — and why advertisers love them. Central-route persuasion costs more effort but earns attitudes that actually last.

</Step>

<Step title="When action changes attitude: dissonance" kind="explain">

Here's persuasion's secret reverse gear. Leon **Festinger's** theory of **cognitive dissonance** says we feel an uncomfortable tension whenever our actions and attitudes clash — and we're driven to reduce it. Usually it's easier to bend the *attitude* to fit the *behaviour* than to undo what we've already done.

His famous study: people did a crushingly dull task, then were paid either **$1 or $20** to tell the next person it was fun. Counter-intuitively, those paid just $1 later reported actually *enjoying* the task more. With $20, you have a ready excuse for lying ("I did it for the money") — no dissonance. With $1, there's no excuse, so the only way to soothe the clash is to decide the task wasn't so bad after all.

<CognitiveDissonance />

This is why getting someone to *act* — sign the petition, try the product, do the small favour — can shift their attitude more reliably than any argument. We don't just act on our beliefs; we come to believe in our acts.

</Step>

<Step title="Compliance tricks" kind="explain">

Persuaders exploit predictable quirks to win **compliance** — a yes to a request:

- **Foot-in-the-door** — agree to a tiny request, and you're far more likely to agree to a big one later. Having acted, you adjust your self-image ("I'm the kind of person who helps") — dissonance at work again.
- **Door-in-the-face** — start with an outrageous request you expect refused, then the real (smaller) one feels like a reasonable concession worth meeting.
- **Lowball** — get a commitment at a great price, then reveal hidden costs; many stick with the now-worse deal because they've already committed.
- **Reciprocity** — a free sample or small gift creates a felt obligation to give back.

Each works by enlisting *your own* tendencies — consistency, fairness, self-image — against you.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- An **attitude** is a learned evaluation; it predicts behaviour best when situational pressures are weak and the attitude is **specific, strong and accessible**.
- Persuasion travels two routes: **central** (arguments, logic → durable attitudes) and **peripheral** (cues like attractiveness and repetition → fleeting attitudes).
- **Cognitive dissonance**: action–attitude clashes create tension we relieve by bending the attitude — the $1/$20 study shows *less* reward can produce *more* attitude change.
- **Compliance tricks** — foot-in-the-door, door-in-the-face, lowball, reciprocity — exploit our drive for consistency, fairness and a stable self-image.

</Step>
