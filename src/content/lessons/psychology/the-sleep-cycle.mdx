<Step title="Sleep is not a blank" kind="explain">

For most of history people assumed sleep was simply the brain switching off — a nightly nothing. Then, in the 1950s, researchers wired sleepers to an **EEG** (a recorder of brain-wave activity) and discovered the opposite. The sleeping brain is busy, and it moves through a precise, repeating choreography of distinct **stages** every night.

Sleep splits into two great families. **NREM** (non-rapid-eye-movement) sleep, which itself has three stages of deepening rest, and **REM** sleep, the strange phase of vivid dreams. You don't simply sink and stay down — you cycle up and down through these stages all night long.

</Step>

<Step title="Descending through NREM" kind="explain">

When you first drift off, you slide down through three NREM stages, each deeper than the last:

- **N1 — the doorway.** A brief, light stage of drifting between wake and sleep. Brain waves slow, you're easily woken, and you may feel a sudden falling sensation (a *hypnic jerk*). Lasts only minutes.
- **N2 — light sleep.** The stage you spend the most time in. The brain produces bursts called *sleep spindles* and large *K-complexes*; your heart rate and temperature drop further.
- **N3 — deep, slow-wave sleep.** The deepest stage, marked by tall, slow *delta* waves. You're very hard to wake, and if roused you'll be groggy and confused. This is the most physically restorative sleep.

</Step>

<Step title="The REM surprise" kind="explain">

After descending into N3, you climb back up — and then enter something stranger than any NREM stage: **REM sleep**.

In REM your eyes dart rapidly beneath closed lids, and your brain waves look almost identical to *being awake*. This is when the most vivid, story-like **dreams** play. Yet your major muscles are effectively **paralysed** — a clever safety feature that stops you physically acting out the dream. Because the brain is so active while the body is switched off, REM is often called **paradoxical sleep**.

<Callout type="info" title="Why the paralysis matters">
The temporary REM paralysis is protective. When it fails, people can thrash, kick, or leap out of bed acting out their dreams — a condition called REM sleep behaviour disorder. The system is normally so reliable that we never notice it working.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="A whole night at a glance" kind="interactive">

Put it all together and a night of sleep forms a repeating wave: down into NREM, up into REM, and around again, each loop lasting about **90 minutes**. Plot the stages over the hours and you get a **hypnogram**.

Look for the two big patterns the hypnogram reveals: **deep N3 sleep dominates the early cycles** (your body's restoration happens first), while **REM periods grow longer toward morning** — which is why your most elaborate dreams, and the ones you're most likely to remember, come just before you wake.

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- Sleep is an active, structured process of distinct **stages**, revealed by the **EEG**.
- **NREM** deepens through **N1** (light doorway), **N2** (light sleep, most of the night, spindles & K-complexes), and **N3** (deep slow-wave, most restorative, hard to wake).
- **REM** sleep brings an awake-like brain, darting eyes, vivid dreams, and muscle **paralysis** — hence *paradoxical sleep*.
- A night cycles through these stages roughly every **90 minutes**; the **hypnogram** shows **N3 dominating early** and **REM lengthening toward morning**.

</Step>
