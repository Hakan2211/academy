<Step title="The problem is the behaviour" kind="explain">

The behaviour therapists made a radical break with Freud. Forget the hidden depths, they said — there's nothing buried that needs excavating. A phobia *is* a learned fear. A compulsion *is* a learned habit. If the trouble was **learned**, through the same conditioning that shapes all behaviour, then it can be **unlearned** — directly, efficiently, without years on a couch.

This is the heart of **behaviour therapy**: apply the laws of classical and operant conditioning (which you met earlier on this island) to change unwanted behaviour itself. Don't ask *why* the fear formed in childhood; ask *how to change the response now*.

</Step>

<Step title="Counterconditioning: pairing a new response" kind="explain">

The signature trick is **counterconditioning** — taking a stimulus that triggers an unwanted response and re-pairing it with a *new, incompatible* response, so the new one wins.

The most famous example is **systematic desensitization**, developed by **Joseph Wolpe**. Its logic is beautifully simple: **you cannot be deeply relaxed and intensely afraid at the same time** (relaxation and fear are physically incompatible — a principle called *reciprocal inhibition*). So if you can stay relaxed while facing the feared thing, the fear has nowhere to live.

The recipe has three parts: learn deep **relaxation**, build a **fear hierarchy** (a ranked ladder of feared scenes from mild to terrifying), then work *up* the ladder while staying calm — moving on only once each rung feels comfortable.

</Step>

<Step title="Climb the fear ladder" kind="interactive">

Here's the method in action for a spider phobia. Each rung is a more frightening scene. Keep the **relaxation meter** high: stay on a rung, breathing and relaxing, until its anxiety rating drops below the comfort line — *then* climb. Watch the dread drain out of each scene with repeated, relaxed exposure.

<SystematicDesensitization />

Notice what's happening: you never flood the person with their worst fear at once. You inch up a hierarchy, letting calm replace dread one small step at a time. That gentleness is exactly why it's so effective.

</Step>

<Step title="Exposure and flooding" kind="explain">

Systematic desensitization is gentle and graded. **Exposure therapy** more broadly rests on a deeper engine: **extinction**. If you face the feared thing and *don't* escape — and the dreaded catastrophe never arrives — your fear naturally rises, peaks, and then falls on its own. Do it repeatedly and each encounter is less frightening than the last.

At the steep end sits **flooding**: confronting the most feared situation fully and at once (safely, with support), riding the wave of anxiety until it subsides because, again, nothing terrible happens. It's intense but can be remarkably fast.

</Step>

<Step title="Watch fear extinguish" kind="interactive">

This is the curve at the core of all exposure work. Within a single session, anxiety spikes then falls as you stay put (**habituation**). Across sessions, drag the slider and watch two things change: each session's **peak gets lower**, and the fall comes **faster** — the fear is **extinguishing**.

<ExposureExtinction />

The brain is quietly re-learning: *this thing is actually safe.* That re-learning, repeated, is what frees people from phobias, panic and obsessive fears.

</Step>

<Step title="Operant tools: token economies and aversion" kind="explain">

Where classical conditioning gives us exposure and desensitization, **operant** conditioning supplies tools built on reward and punishment:

- **Token economies** — in settings like hospital wards or classrooms, desired behaviours earn **tokens** that can be exchanged for real privileges. By systematically reinforcing good behaviour, you shape it upward. (You met shaping and reinforcement earlier; this is them applied.)
- **Aversion therapy** — pairing an unwanted behaviour (say, problem drinking) with an unpleasant stimulus, so the behaviour itself starts to feel off-putting. It's controversial, its effects often fade once the aversive pairing stops, and it raises real ethical questions — so it's used sparingly and carefully.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Behaviour therapy** treats the unwanted behaviour itself as a **learned** response that can be **unlearned** — no hidden conflict required.
- **Counterconditioning** re-pairs a trigger with an incompatible response; **systematic desensitization** (Wolpe) uses relaxation + a **fear hierarchy** because fear and calm can't coexist.
- **Exposure** works through **extinction**: stay with the fear without escaping and it peaks then falls (**habituation**); repeated, peaks drop and fall faster. **Flooding** does this all at once.
- Operant tools include **token economies** (reinforcing desired behaviour) and **aversion therapy** (pairing an unwanted act with something unpleasant — controversial and limited).

</Step>
