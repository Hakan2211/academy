<Lesson>

<Step title="Memory as a workbench, not a box" kind="explain">

The three-store model pictures short-term memory as a passive holding box — information just sits there fading. But that can't be the whole story, because you don't merely *hold* numbers in mind; you *do things* with them. You add the digits, reverse the letters, picture the route while reciting directions. You think.

So Alan Baddeley reimagined the short-term store as **working memory** — not a box but a small, busy **workbench** where information is held *and manipulated* in the present moment. It is where understanding a sentence, doing mental arithmetic, and following these instructions all actually happen. Working memory is, in a real sense, the seat of conscious thought.

</Step>

<Step title="Feel the limit" kind="interactive">

Before the parts, feel the *size* of this workbench for yourself. Watch a string of digits flash, then tap them back in order. Succeed and the next string grows; fail and it shrinks — until you find your ceiling.

<MemorySpan />

Most people top out around **seven**, the limit George Miller famously dubbed "the magical number seven, plus or minus two". Notice that the failure is sudden: one digit past your span and the whole thing collapses. That hard edge is the finite capacity of working memory, laid bare.

</Step>

<Step title="Beating the limit: chunking" kind="interactive">

If the workbench is so small, how do you ever hold a phone number, an address, a chess position? The secret is that working memory counts **chunks**, not raw symbols — and *you* decide what a chunk is.

<ChunkingDemo />

Thirteen meaningless letters overwhelm you; the same letters grouped into four familiar acronyms slip in effortlessly. **Chunking** repackages many items into a few meaningful units, and meaning is the glue. This is also why expertise multiplies memory: a chess master doesn't see 32 pieces but a handful of recognisable patterns, and a musician hears phrases, not notes.

</Step>

<Step title="Baddeley's model: a boss and two specialists" kind="explain">

Baddeley's great insight was that working memory isn't one resource but a small **team** of components:

- The **central executive** — the boss. It directs *attention*, decides what to focus on, and hands work to the specialists below. It does no storage itself; it coordinates.
- The **phonological loop** — the *inner voice*. It holds verbal and auditory material — words, numbers, sounds — by silently rehearsing them. (This is the voice you use to keep a phone number alive.)
- The **visuospatial sketchpad** — the *inner eye*. It holds and manipulates visual and spatial information — shapes, layouts, mental maps, rotating an image in your mind.
- The **episodic buffer** — added later, it binds the streams together and links them to long-term memory, so a remembered scene combines sights, sounds, and meaning into one coherent episode.

</Step>

<Step title="Load the subsystems" kind="interactive">

Here is the elegant prediction this model makes: because the loop and the sketchpad are *separate*, two tasks that use *different* subsystems barely interfere — but two that use the *same* one collide. Load a verbal task, then a visual one, then both at once, and watch which parts light up.

<WorkingMemory />

This is why you can listen to a podcast (loop) while navigating an unfamiliar street (sketchpad) — but try to do mental arithmetic *while* repeating a phone number and the whole thing jams: both are fighting over the single phonological loop.

</Step>

<Step title="Why working memory matters" kind="explain">

Working memory capacity predicts a startling range of real-world abilities — reading comprehension, reasoning, even measures of fluid intelligence. When you "lose the thread" of a complicated argument, or can't follow a recipe and a conversation at once, you are hitting the limits of this workbench.

The practical lessons fall straight out of the model:

- **Chunk** to fit more on the bench — group, abbreviate, find the pattern.
- **Offload** what you can — write it down, so the workbench is free for *thinking* rather than *holding*.
- **Don't double-load one subsystem** — studying complex material is far harder with lyrics playing (the words fight your phonological loop) than with wordless music.

<Callout type="tip" title="Free the bench">
Working memory is your most precious cognitive bottleneck. Anything you can store on paper or chunk into meaning is one fewer item competing for those few precious slots — leaving room to actually reason.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Working memory** replaces the passive short-term "box": it both **holds and manipulates** information — the seat of conscious thought.
- Its capacity is small (**~7 ± 2 items**) and the limit is felt as a hard edge; **chunking** beats it by repackaging many items into a few meaningful units.
- **Baddeley's model** has a **central executive** (directs attention) commanding a **phonological loop** (inner voice, verbal) and a **visuospatial sketchpad** (inner eye, visual), bound by an **episodic buffer**.
- Tasks using **different** subsystems barely interfere; tasks loading the **same** subsystem collide — which is why you can look and listen at once but not run two verbal streams.
- Practically: **chunk**, **offload** to free the bench, and avoid double-loading one subsystem.

</Step>
