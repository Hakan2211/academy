<Step title="Development doesn't stop at adulthood" kind="explain">

Piaget mapped the growing mind, but his story essentially ends in adolescence — as if, once you can reason abstractly, development is *done*. **Erik Erikson** disagreed profoundly. He saw psychological growth as a project of the *entire* lifespan, with the richest chapters still to come in adulthood and old age.

Erikson began in Freud's circle but broke from it in a crucial way. Where Freud focused on inner drives, Erikson stressed the **psychosocial**: how the individual meets the demands of *society* at each age. His theory of eight stages is one of the most enduring maps of a human life.

</Step>

<Step title="A crisis at every stage" kind="explain">

Erikson's engine is the **crisis**. At each of his eight stages, a person faces a central tension — a tug-of-war between two pulls, like *trust versus mistrust* or *identity versus role confusion*.

"Crisis" here doesn't mean catastrophe; it means a **turning point**, a developmental task that society presents at that age. Resolve it well and you gain a lasting **virtue** — a psychological strength like hope, competence or wisdom — and you're better equipped for the next stage. Resolve it poorly and you carry that unfinished tension forward. The stages build on one another, but Erikson believed earlier crises can always be revisited and re-resolved.

</Step>

<Step title="The teenage crisis: identity" kind="explain">

The stage Erikson is most famous for sits right at the hinge of the lifespan: adolescence, and the crisis of **identity versus role confusion**.

The teenager's central question is *"Who am I?"* They try on roles, values, friend groups, politics, styles and beliefs — sometimes dizzyingly fast — searching for a coherent, integrated sense of self. Resolve it and you emerge with a stable **identity** and the virtue of *fidelity*: the capacity to commit to people and ideals. Fail to, and you're left in **role confusion** — drifting, unsure who you are or what you stand for. (Erikson actually coined the phrase "identity crisis.")

</Step>

<Step title="The adult arc" kind="explain">

What makes Erikson's map special is that it keeps going long after the teen years:

- **Intimacy vs isolation** (young adulthood) — can you form deep, committed bonds, or do you end up lonely? This stage *builds on* identity: it's hard to merge your life with another's before you know who you are.
- **Generativity vs stagnation** (middle adulthood) — do you contribute to the next generation, through children, work, mentoring and creativity — or stall in self-absorption?
- **Integrity vs despair** (late adulthood) — looking back, can you feel your life had meaning and coherence (integrity, yielding *wisdom*), or are you overcome by regret (despair)?

That final stage reframes old age not as mere decline but as a genuine developmental task — the work of making peace with the life you've lived.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Erikson** extended development across the *whole* lifespan and emphasised the **psychosocial** — meeting society's demands at each age.
- Each of his **eight stages** poses a **crisis** (a turning point); resolving it well grants a lasting **virtue** and prepares you for the next stage.
- Adolescence centres on **identity vs role confusion** — the search for "Who am I?" — yielding the virtue of *fidelity*.
- The adult stages — **intimacy vs isolation**, **generativity vs stagnation**, **integrity vs despair** — show that growth continues all the way to the end of life.

</Step>
