<Step title="A survival kit run too long" kind="explain">

The stress response is brilliant in a sprint. The trouble is what happens when there's no finish line. The body's emergency settings — high cortisol, mobilised sugar, suppressed "non-essential" systems — are meant to last *minutes*. Run them for *months*, and the very system built to protect you starts to grind you down.

This is the heart of **health psychology's** most important finding: it isn't the dramatic acute scares that do the most harm, but the **chronic, low-grade stress** that never lets up. Selye's curve shows exactly why.

</Step>

<Step title="Riding Selye's curve to exhaustion" kind="interactive">

Recall Selye's three stages of the **General Adaptation Syndrome**: alarm, resistance, exhaustion. The curve below tracks the body's *resistance* to stress over time. Slide through a relentless, unresolving stressor and watch what happens.

<GASCurve />

The shape tells the story. In **alarm**, resistance dips with the shock, then surges. In **resistance**, the body braces at a high plateau — outwardly coping, but quietly draining its reserves to hold the line. And in **exhaustion**, those reserves are spent: resistance crashes *below normal*. The body is now more vulnerable to illness than if the stress had never come at all. Chronic stress is the long, slow walk into that exhaustion phase.

</Step>

<Step title="Stress and the immune system" kind="explain">

One of the clearest ways chronic stress harms us is by **suppressing the immune system**. Cortisol, useful in short bursts, dampens immune activity when it stays high for too long — leaving you more open to infection and slower to heal.

The evidence is striking. Stressed people catch colds more easily when exposed to a virus. Wounds heal more slowly during stressful periods. Students show weaker immune responses during exam weeks. The field that studies these links even has a name — **psychoneuroimmunology** — the science of how mind, nervous system and immune system talk to one another.

<Callout type="info" title="Mind and body, one system">
The old idea that "psychological" and "physical" health are separate doesn't survive contact with the evidence. A thought appraised as threatening sets off real hormonal cascades with real effects on real tissue. Mind and body aren't two things influencing each other — they're one system.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Psychosomatic, not 'imaginary'" kind="explain">

When stress contributes to a physical illness, we call the illness **psychosomatic** (from *psyche*, mind, and *soma*, body). This word is badly misunderstood. It does **not** mean the symptoms are imaginary or "all in your head" — it means a genuine physical problem in which psychological stress plays a real causal role.

Stress is now recognised as a contributing factor in tension headaches, high blood pressure, some digestive disorders, and the worsening of conditions from asthma to skin flare-ups. The pain and the damage are entirely real; stress is simply one of the things feeding them.

</Step>

<Step title="Type A and the heart" kind="explain">

The most studied stress–illness link is with **heart disease**. Cardiologists Friedman and Rosenman noticed that many heart-attack patients shared a style: hurried, competitive, impatient, easily irritated. They called it the **Type A** personality, in contrast to the more relaxed **Type B**.

Later research refined the picture. It turned out that *time-urgency* and ambition weren't the dangerous part — the truly toxic ingredient was chronic **hostility and anger**. People who simmer with frequent anger keep their cardiovascular system in a near-constant state of stress arousal, and that wear and tear raises heart-disease risk over the years. The lesson is hopeful, though: it's a *modifiable* pattern, not a fixed fate.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- Stress harms health when the emergency response runs **chronically**; Selye's curve ends in an **exhaustion** phase where resistance falls *below normal*.
- Prolonged **cortisol suppresses the immune system** — slower healing, more infections (the field of **psychoneuroimmunology**).
- **Psychosomatic** illness is *real* physical illness in which stress plays a causal role — not imaginary symptoms.
- The strongest stress–illness link is with **heart disease**; within the **Type A** pattern, chronic **hostility/anger** is the toxic — and modifiable — ingredient.

</Step>
