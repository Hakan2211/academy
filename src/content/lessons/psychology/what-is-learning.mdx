<Step title="A change written by experience" kind="explain">

You weren't born knowing how to flinch at a dentist's drill, crave a particular song, or check your phone the instant it buzzes. You **learned** those things. To a psychologist, **learning is a relatively permanent change in behaviour or knowledge that comes from experience.**

Pull that definition apart and three words do the work:

- **Relatively permanent** — a fleeting change (you duck once because a ball flew at you) isn't learning; a lasting tendency is.
- **Change** — learning shows up as something you now *do* or *know* differently.
- **From experience** — the change is caused by what happened to you, not by simply growing up.

</Step>

<Step title="Not everything that changes is learned" kind="explain">

Behaviour changes for reasons that have nothing to do with learning, and it's worth ruling them out:

- **Maturation.** A toddler walks not because of practice alone but because their nervous system and muscles ripened on a biological timetable. Birds fly, kittens' eyes open — growth, not experience.
- **Reflexes.** Jerk your hand off a hot stove, blink at a puff of air, salivate at food in your mouth. These are **innate, wired-in** responses you were born with.
- **Temporary states.** Fatigue, drugs, or injury change behaviour, but once they pass, so does the change. Nothing was learned.

<Callout type="tip" title="The test">
Ask: *did experience cause a lasting change?* If a behaviour would have appeared anyway with age, or vanishes when you rest, it isn't learning.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="The simplest learning of all" kind="explain">

Before any bells or rewards, organisms show the two most basic forms of learning — both about a single repeated stimulus:

- **Habituation** — responding *less* to a stimulus that repeats harmlessly. A new clock ticks loudly the first night; within a week you don't hear it at all. Your brain learned "this is safe, ignore it."
- **Sensitization** — responding *more* after an intense or threatening stimulus. After a near-miss car crash, you jump at every horn. The system learned "stay on alert."

Even a sea slug does both. They are learning stripped to its essentials: experience tuning how strongly you react.

</Step>

<Step title="The behaviourist programme" kind="explain">

In the early 1900s a bold movement called **behaviourism**, led by **John B. Watson** and later **B. F. Skinner**, reshaped psychology. Their manifesto: stop guessing about invisible thoughts and feelings, and study only what you can **observe and measure** — behaviour, and the conditions that change it.

Their core bet was that nearly all behaviour is **learned** through associations between events. Watson boasted he could take any healthy infant and train them into any profession, regardless of talent or ancestry. That overstated the case — biology and cognition matter too — but the behaviourists discovered the precise *rules* of association that the rest of this world is built on.

</Step>

<Step title="A taste of associative learning" kind="interactive">

The behaviourists' great discovery was **associative learning**: linking one event to another. Here is the most famous example in all of psychology — a dog learning to connect a bell with food. Ring the bell, present food, and especially **pair them** several times, then ring the bell alone.

Watch the bell — at first meaningless — gradually gain the power to make the dog salivate. That transfer, built purely from paired experience, is learning made visible. The next lesson takes it apart piece by piece.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Learning** is a *relatively permanent change in behaviour or knowledge that results from experience.*
- It must be distinguished from **maturation** (growth), **reflexes** (innate responses) and **temporary states** (fatigue, drugs).
- The simplest forms are **habituation** (responding less to a harmless repeated stimulus) and **sensitization** (responding more after a threatening one).
- **Behaviourism** (Watson, Skinner) studied only observable behaviour and uncovered the rules of **associative learning** — the engine behind the rest of this world.

</Step>
