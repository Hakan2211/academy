<Step title="The belief that shapes the outcome" kind="explain">

Two students hit the same wall — a failed exam. One thinks, *"I'm just not smart enough."* The other thinks, *"I haven't figured this out yet."* They felt the same setback. But the belief each carries about *why* will quietly steer everything they do next — whether they study harder or give up, ask for help or hide.

This world's big theme is that **how you interpret events** matters as much as the events themselves. Two of psychology's most powerful interpretive habits are **mindset** (your beliefs about ability) and **optimism** (your beliefs about why good and bad things happen). Both turn out to be **learnable** — which means a happier, more resilient way of seeing is something you can practise.

</Step>

<Step title="Fixed vs growth mindset" kind="explain">

Psychologist **Carol Dweck** discovered that people carry one of two implicit theories about their own abilities:

- A **fixed mindset** treats intelligence and talent as carved in stone — a fixed quantity you either have or you don't.
- A **growth mindset** treats them as **trainable** — qualities that grow with effort, strategy, and help from others.

This sounds abstract until you watch what each does to behaviour. To the fixed mindset, **effort is threatening** (if you were really talented, it would be easy) and **failure is a verdict** (it reveals your limit). To the growth mindset, **effort is how ability grows** and **failure is information**. The same brain, the same task — wildly different responses.

</Step>

<Step title="Same setback, two readings" kind="interactive">

Watch the two mindsets diverge on real situations. Pick a scenario, choose the interpretation that sounds like your inner voice, and see where each one leads.

<GrowthMindset />

Dweck's most striking finding is how *changeable* this is. Students simply *taught* that the brain grows stronger with use — that struggle builds new connections — began choosing harder problems and earning better grades. The belief itself was the lever. Crucially, the praise that builds a growth mindset targets **effort and strategy** ("you found a clever approach") rather than fixed traits ("you're so smart") — because praising the trait quietly teaches that the trait is what matters, and is on the line every time.

</Step>

<Step title="Learned optimism vs learned helplessness" kind="explain">

Mindset's close cousin is **optimism** — and here the story starts somewhere dark. Seligman's early work found that animals and people exposed to bad events they *couldn't control* often stopped trying even when escape later became possible. He called it **learned helplessness**: the belief *"nothing I do matters"* generalising into passive giving-up.

But not everyone succumbed — and the difference was their **explanatory style**: the habitual way they explained bad events. Seligman found three dimensions:

- **Permanent vs temporary** — "this always happens" vs "this was a one-off."
- **Pervasive vs specific** — "this ruins everything" vs "this one thing went wrong."
- **Personal vs not** — "it's all my fault" vs "several things caused this."

The **pessimist** explains setbacks as permanent, pervasive and personal — and slides toward helplessness. The **optimist** explains them as temporary, specific, and not wholly personal — and stays in the game. The good news in Seligman's title *Learned Optimism*: this style can be **retrained**.

</Step>

<Step title="Your explanatory style" kind="interactive">

Explore the four ways we can explain an outcome — inside me or out in the world (internal/external), and lasting or changeable (stable/unstable). Switch between a failure and a success and watch how each combination feels.

<AttributionStyle />

The optimistic pattern: explain good events as **internal and stable** ("I'm capable, and I prepare"), and bad events as **external and unstable** ("a rough patch, this once"). The pessimistic pattern flips it — and corrodes motivation. Reframing your inner explanations toward the optimistic pattern is, quite literally, the practice of learned optimism.

</Step>

<Step title="Self-compassion: the gentle engine" kind="explain">

There's a trap lurking here. People sometimes hear "growth mindset" and turn it into a whip — *"I should be improving faster, what's wrong with me?"* The research points to a kinder and more effective engine: **self-compassion**, studied by Kristin Neff.

Self-compassion means treating yourself in a hard moment as you'd treat a good friend — with **kindness** instead of harsh judgement, recognising that struggle is part of our **common humanity**, and holding your pain with balanced **awareness** rather than drowning in it. Counter-intuitively, it doesn't make people lazy or complacent. People higher in self-compassion actually take *more* responsibility for mistakes, bounce back faster, and persist longer — because they're not wasting energy attacking themselves. Growth needs both an accurate mirror *and* a safe place to stand while you look in it.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Dweck**: a **fixed mindset** sees ability as carved in stone (so effort threatens and failure is a verdict); a **growth mindset** sees it as trainable (so effort builds and failure informs).
- Mindset is **changeable**, and the praise that grows it targets **effort and strategy**, not fixed traits.
- **Learned helplessness** is the belief that nothing you do matters; the antidote is an optimistic **explanatory style** — explaining setbacks as **temporary, specific, and not wholly personal**.
- **Self-compassion** (kindness, common humanity, balanced awareness) is the gentle engine of growth — it speeds recovery and persistence rather than breeding complacency.

</Step>
