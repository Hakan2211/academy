<Step title="The question under all the others" kind="explain">

We've toured anxiety, mood, trauma, psychosis, personality, and developmental conditions. Now we step back and ask the deepest question of the whole world: **where do psychological disorders come from?**

It's tempting to want one answer. *It's a chemical imbalance. It's a bad childhood. It's genetics. It's stress.* Each of those is real — and each, alone, is wrong. The honest, evidence-based answer is an **interaction**, and this lesson builds the two frameworks that capture it: the **diathesis-stress model** and its broader cousin, the **biopsychosocial model**. Then we'll turn to something that causes harm beyond any disorder itself: **stigma**.

</Step>

<Step title="Diathesis-stress: vulnerability meets life" kind="explain">

Here is the single most useful idea for understanding *why this person, why now*. The **diathesis-stress model** says a disorder emerges from the combination of two things:

- a **diathesis** — an underlying *vulnerability* (genetic predisposition, temperament, early experience, brain differences) that a person carries, often silently; and
- **stress** — the demands and adversities of life (loss, trauma, conflict, hardship) that press on them.

Neither alone is usually enough. A vulnerability can lie **dormant** for a whole life if the stress never comes; ordinary stress is **harmless** to someone with little vulnerability. A disorder appears only when their *combination* crosses a **threshold**:

Try three experiments. First, set vulnerability high and stress to zero — notice no onset (vulnerability can stay dormant). Second, set stress high and vulnerability to zero — again, no onset. Third, raise *both* moderately — and watch them cross the line *together*, neither one extreme.

This is the model's great explanatory payoff: it tells us **why the same event devastates one person and barely touches another**. The event isn't the whole story — the vulnerability each person brings to it sets how much stress it takes to tip over.

</Step>

<Step title="The biopsychosocial model" kind="explain">

Diathesis-stress tells us *that* vulnerability and stress combine. The **biopsychosocial model** — which you first met back in the perspectives world — fills in *what* those ingredients actually are, across three interacting levels:

- **Bio** — genes, brain structure and chemistry, prenatal factors, evolved tendencies. (Most "diatheses" live here.)
- **Psycho** — thinking styles, beliefs, learning history, coping skills, the cognitive triad, self-esteem.
- **Social** — family, relationships, culture, poverty, discrimination, trauma, social support.

The word that matters is **interacting**. A genetic risk (bio) might never surface in a loving, low-stress environment (social) but might in a harsh one — and how the person *interprets* their experiences (psycho) tips the balance either way. No level is "the real cause"; behaviour and disorder **emerge** from all three together. This is why the best care is also multi-level: medication *and* therapy *and* social support, matched to the person.

</Step>

<Step title="Stigma: the second illness" kind="explain">

Now to a harm that no diagnosis creates but society does. **Stigma** — the cluster of negative stereotypes, prejudice, and discrimination aimed at people with mental illness — is sometimes called a *second illness*, because it can hurt as much as the condition itself.

It does its damage in layers:

- **Public stigma** — society's stereotypes ("dangerous," "weak," "faking").
- **Self-stigma** — when a person turns those stereotypes inward, eroding their own self-worth and hope.
- **Structural stigma** — when the prejudice is baked into institutions: under-funded services, discrimination in work and housing.

The cruellest consequence is concrete: stigma stops people from **seeking help**. Fear of being judged keeps people silent and untreated — turning a treatable condition into a prolonged crisis. Reducing stigma isn't mere politeness; it is a public-health intervention that saves lives.

</Step>

Three of these are worth holding onto. **Violence:** people with mental illness are far more likely to be *victims* than perpetrators. **Weakness:** disorders arise from biology and circumstance interacting, not weak character — willpower no more cures depression than it cures diabetes. **Treatability:** most disorders respond well to treatment, and many people recover fully, *especially* with early help.

</Step>

<Step title="How to be part of the cure" kind="explain">

Understanding disorder isn't only academic — it changes how you act. A few evidence-based ways anyone can reduce stigma and help:

- **Watch your words.** "A person experiencing schizophrenia," not "a schizophrenic." Drop "crazy" and "psycho" as casual insults. Language shapes attitudes.
- **Talk openly.** Contact and honest conversation are among the most effective ways to dissolve prejudice — for others *and* for the speaker.
- **Treat it as health, not character.** Respond to mental illness as you would to any other health condition: with curiosity and support, not judgement.
- **Know it's treatable, and point toward help.** Encourage anyone who is struggling to reach out to a doctor, mental-health professional, or helpline — and remind them that recovery is the norm, not the exception.

<Callout type="info" title="A closing reminder">
This world has been **educational, not diagnostic**. Recognising a pattern here is not the same as having a diagnosis — that requires a trained professional. If you or someone you know is struggling, please reach out to a doctor, a mental-health professional, or a local helpline. Help exists, and seeking it is a sign of strength.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- Disorders come from an **interaction**, never a single cause.
- The **diathesis-stress model**: a disorder appears only when underlying **vulnerability** plus **life stress** crosses a **threshold** — explaining why the same event affects people so differently (vulnerability can stay dormant; ordinary stress is harmless without it).
- The **biopsychosocial model** names the ingredients — **bio × psycho × social**, interacting — which is why the best care is multi-level (medication *and* therapy *and* support).
- **Stigma** is a "second illness": **public, self, and structural**: its worst effect is stopping people from seeking treatable help.
- Stigma runs on **myths** (violence, weakness, untreatability) that evidence dismantles — and anyone can help by watching their **language**, talking openly, treating it as health, and pointing toward professional help.

</Step>
