<Step title="What happens when the food stops?" kind="explain">

You've conditioned a dog to salivate at a bell. Now ring the bell again and again with **no food following**. At first the dog keeps drooling — but the response weakens, and soon the bell means nothing again. This fading is called **extinction**: the conditioned response dies out when the CS no longer predicts the UCS.

Extinction isn't *unlearning*, though — the old association is suppressed, not erased. Here's the proof: let the dog rest a while, then ring the bell once more. The salivation often briefly **returns**. This comeback is called **spontaneous recovery**, and it tells us the original learning was still there underneath all along.

</Step>

<Step title="Generalization: close enough counts" kind="explain">

A conditioned response doesn't fire only for the exact original signal. Condition a dog to a 1000 Hz tone, then test a 1100 Hz tone — it'll salivate too, just a bit less. This spread to *similar* stimuli is **generalization**, and it's everywhere: a child bitten by a German shepherd may fear all dogs, even small friendly ones.

The pattern is lawful. Plot response strength against how similar the new stimulus is to the CS and you get a smooth peak centred on the original — the **generalization gradient**. The more alike, the stronger the response; the more different, the weaker.

</Step>

<Step title="Discrimination: learning the difference" kind="explain">

Generalization's mirror image is **discrimination** — learning to respond to the CS but **not** to similar stimuli. How? By experience teaching the difference: the 1000 Hz tone is followed by food, but the 1100 Hz tone never is. Over time the dog stops responding to the impostor.

Discrimination *sharpens* the generalization gradient into a narrow spike around the true CS. It's how you learn that *your* phone's ringtone matters but the identical one in a TV ad doesn't.

</Step>

<Step title="See the gradient" kind="interactive">

A subject was conditioned to a **1000 Hz** tone. Slide a test tone up and down in pitch and watch the conditioned response rise and fall with similarity. Then flip on **discrimination training** and watch the broad hill collapse into a sharp peak.

The width of that curve *is* the difference between generalizing and discriminating: a wide hill spreads the response to anything similar; a narrow spike reserves it for the real thing.

</Step>

<Step title="Little Albert: fear, conditioned" kind="explain">

In 1920, John Watson and Rosalind Rayner showed that human emotions can be classically conditioned. They presented an 11-month-old, "**Little Albert**," with a white rat (neutral — he liked it). Each time he reached for it, they struck a steel bar behind his head with a hammer. The loud clang (UCS) made him cry in fear (UCR).

After several pairings, the rat alone (now a CS) made Albert cry and crawl away (CR). And his fear **generalized** — to a rabbit, a dog, a fur coat, even a Santa Claus mask. The study is also a notorious **ethics** lesson: Albert left the study still afraid and was never deconditioned, something no ethics board would permit today.

</Step>

<Step title="Taste aversion: when biology bends the rules" kind="explain">

Classical conditioning has limits set by biology. **John Garcia** found that rats made sick *hours* after eating a novel food developed a powerful aversion to its **taste** — in a single trial, across a long delay. This shatters two "rules": conditioning usually needs many pairings and near-instant timing.

The explanation is **biological preparedness**: evolution wired animals to learn certain associations easily because they mattered for survival. Linking *taste* to *sickness* (not a sound or a light to sickness) helps an animal avoid poison. We are not blank slates onto which any association can be stamped — we come pre-tuned to learn what kept our ancestors alive.

<Callout type="info" title="You've felt this">
Ever get food poisoning and find that one dish revolting for years afterward, even knowing it wasn't the food's fault? That one-trial, long-delay learning is conditioned taste aversion in action.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Extinction** — the CR fades when the CS no longer predicts the UCS; it suppresses rather than erases, as shown by **spontaneous recovery** (the CR briefly returns after a rest).
- **Generalization** — the CR spreads to *similar* stimuli, following a smooth **generalization gradient** peaked on the original CS.
- **Discrimination** — learning to respond to the CS but not to similar stimuli, which *sharpens* the gradient.
- **Little Albert** showed fears can be conditioned (and generalize) in humans — and remains a cautionary ethics tale.
- **Taste aversion** (Garcia) reveals **biological preparedness**: some associations (taste–sickness) are learned in one trial across long delays because evolution favoured them.

</Step>
