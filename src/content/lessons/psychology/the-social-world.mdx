<Lesson>

<Step title="The question that runs through everything" kind="explain">

Here is a fact that should unsettle you a little: **the situation you're in shapes what you do far more than you'd ever guess.** Not your character. Not your values. The room, the crowd, the role you've been handed.

Social psychology is the science of how we think about, influence, and relate to one another. And its single most repeated lesson — proven again and again, in study after study you'll meet this week — is the **power of the situation**. Good people do cruel things in the wrong setting. Ordinary people perform extraordinary kindness in the right one. The person matters, but the situation matters more than we want to believe.

</Step>

<Step title="We don't see reality — we construct it" kind="explain">

You might assume we all react to the same world. We don't. We each react to the world *as we interpret it.* Two fans watch the same foul and see two different games. Two people read the same text — "Fine." — and one feels reassured, the other crushed.

This is **constructing social reality**: we don't passively record events, we actively build meaning, filtered through our beliefs, moods and expectations. The objective stimulus might be identical; the experience is not. Much of social life is two people responding not to *what happened* but to *what they each decided it meant.*

<Callout type="info" title="Why this matters">
If behaviour follows from interpretation, then changing how someone sees a situation can change what they do — without changing the situation at all. That insight powers everything from therapy to conflict resolution.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Person versus situation" kind="explain">

When someone does something — cuts a queue, snaps at a waiter, volunteers to help — we instinctively explain it with their **disposition**: that's just who they are. Rude. Kind. Lazy.

But psychologists draw a sharp line between two kinds of cause:

- **Dispositional** — internal: personality, attitudes, character.
- **Situational** — external: the setting, the pressure, the role, the moment.

The great recurring finding of this field is that we *systematically overweight the first and underweight the second.* We see a person and forget the stage they're standing on. Hold that thought — the very next lesson puts a name to this error and lets you feel it yourself.

</Step>

<Step title="Feel the construction" kind="interactive">

Let's make "person versus situation" concrete. Below, someone cuts to the front of a long queue. Decide why — then flip the perspective so that *you* are the one who did it, and watch how your explanation changes for the identical act.

<AttributionError />

The act never changed. Only your seat did. That gap between how we explain *others* and how we explain *ourselves* is the engine of half this island's surprises.

</Step>

<Step title="Why a whole field for this?" kind="explain">

It's tempting to think social behaviour is just "common sense." But common sense is a master of **hindsight**: once you hear a result, it feels obvious — and the opposite result would have felt obvious too. ("Birds of a feather flock together"… "opposites attract.") Both can't be right, and only careful studies can tell us which holds, when.

That's the job of social psychology: to test the things everyone "already knows" about people — and to keep discovering that the situation was pulling strings we never noticed.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Social psychology** studies how we think about, influence and relate to one another.
- Its central lesson is the **power of the situation** — context shapes behaviour more than we intuitively credit.
- We don't passively perceive events; we **construct social reality**, responding to our *interpretation* rather than raw fact.
- Behaviour has **dispositional** (internal) and **situational** (external) causes, and we reliably overweight the dispositional.
- "Common sense" about people is a hindsight trap — which is why we test it rather than trust it.

</Step>
