<Step title="The students who broke away" kind="explain">

Freud gathered a circle of brilliant followers — and then watched many of them leave. The **neo-Freudians** kept his foundational insight (that an **unconscious** and **early experience** shape personality) but rejected what they saw as his obsession with sex and aggression. They asked: isn't there more to us than buried instincts?

Each carved out a different "more". Together they pushed psychodynamic thinking toward the social, the purposeful and the future-oriented — and seeded ideas that still echo through pop psychology today.

</Step>

<Step title="Jung: the deeper unconscious" kind="explain">

Carl **Jung**, once Freud's chosen heir, split over the nature of the unconscious itself. Yes, Jung agreed, you have a *personal* unconscious of forgotten and repressed material. But beneath it, he argued, lies a **collective unconscious** — a layer inherited by *all* humans, stocked with **archetypes**: universal images and themes (the Hero, the Shadow, the Mother, the Wise Old One) that surface in the myths and dreams of every culture.

Jung also gave us the now-everyday pair **introversion** and **extraversion** — energy turned inward versus outward — a distinction that flowed straight into the Big Five you met earlier.

<Callout type="info" title="From Jung to the office quiz">
The popular Myers-Briggs (MBTI) personality test is built on Jung's ideas. It's beloved in workplaces — but psychologists treat it with caution: its "types" don't hold up well to scientific scrutiny, unlike the dimensional Big Five.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Adler: the drive to overcome" kind="explain">

Alfred **Adler** rejected the unconscious-sexual engine entirely. The master motive, he argued, is the universal childhood experience of being small, weak and dependent — a feeling of **inferiority**. Healthy personality is the lifelong **striving for superiority**: not domination, but the push toward growth, mastery and competence.

When that striving curdles, you get an **inferiority complex** (paralysing self-doubt) or its overcompensating cousin, a **superiority complex** (bluster masking insecurity). Adler also stressed **birth order** and social belonging — putting the *social* firmly into a once-private theory. His phrases — "inferiority complex", "compensation" — are now part of everyday speech.

</Step>

<Step title="Horney: challenging the bias" kind="explain">

Karen **Horney** (pronounced *HORN-eye*) took on Freud directly. Where he saw women as driven by "penis envy", she countered that women's frustrations came not from anatomy but from a **culture** that denied them power — and tartly suggested men might suffer "womb envy" of women's ability to bear children.

Her lasting contribution was the idea of **basic anxiety**: the insecurity a child feels in a threatening world. To cope, people adopt broad strategies — moving **toward** people (seeking approval), **against** people (seeking control), or **away** from people (seeking independence). Lean too hard on any one and personality becomes lopsided.

</Step>

<Step title="Weighing the whole tradition" kind="explain">

Step back and judge the psychodynamic project — Freud and his heirs together. What's the verdict?

**On the credit side**, it put the **unconscious**, **internal conflict**, **defence mechanisms** and the **power of childhood** permanently on the map. The very idea that we don't fully know our own minds is now common sense, and it shaped a century of therapy.

**On the debit side**, the theories are hard to **test or falsify** — almost any behaviour can be explained after the fact, which is a scientific weakness, not a strength. They rested heavily on a handful of **case studies** rather than controlled research, and several core claims have simply not held up. Modern psychodynamic clinicians keep the useful insights and quietly drop the rest.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- The **neo-Freudians** kept Freud's unconscious and early-experience emphasis but rejected his focus on sexual instinct.
- **Jung** added the **collective unconscious** and **archetypes**, and gave us **introversion/extraversion**.
- **Adler** centred personality on overcoming **inferiority** through a healthy **striving for superiority**.
- **Horney** challenged Freud's gender bias and described **basic anxiety**, coped with by moving toward, against or away from others.
- **Verdict on the tradition:** lasting credit for the unconscious, conflict, defences and childhood — but the theories are **hard to falsify** and rest on **case studies**, so modern clinicians keep the insights and drop the unsupported specifics.

</Step>
