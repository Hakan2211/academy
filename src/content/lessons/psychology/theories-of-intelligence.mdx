<Step title="One number, or many?" kind="explain">

Last lesson left us with a tension: the *measurements* keep pointing to a single general ability, **_g_**, yet everyday life is full of people who are brilliant in one arena and hopeless in another. The great theories of intelligence are really different ways of resolving that tension.

How did Spearman even find *g*? With a tool called **factor analysis** — a statistical method that hunts through a tangle of test scores for hidden common threads. Feed it dozens of mental tests and it reports back: "most of the variation here can be explained by one big factor." That factor is *g*. The theories that follow either embrace it, split it, or insist it misses the point.

</Step>

<Step title="Gardner: eight kinds of smart" kind="explain">

Howard Gardner looked at *g* and said: that's not wrong, it's too small. Why should we call a Nobel physicist "intelligent" but a world-class violinist merely "talented"? In 1983 he proposed **multiple intelligences** — a set of relatively *independent* abilities, each with its own kind of genius.

His evidence came partly from brain damage: a stroke can wipe out language while leaving musical or spatial skill untouched, suggesting these run on separate systems. Standard IQ tests, he argued, mostly measure just the first two.

<MultipleIntelligences />

The idea reshaped education — "teach to every child's strengths." But it's controversial: critics note these "intelligences" are hard to measure, and some look more like *talents* or *personality* than intelligence in Spearman's sense.

</Step>

<Step title="Sternberg: smarts that work in the world" kind="explain">

Robert Sternberg agreed *g* was too narrow but went a different way. His **triarchic theory** keeps the list short — just three — and grounds it in what actually helps you succeed:

- **Analytical** intelligence — the "book smarts" classic IQ tests measure: analyse, compare, evaluate.
- **Creative** intelligence — inventing, imagining, dealing with novelty.
- **Practical** intelligence — the "street smarts" to adapt to your real environment and *get things done*.

His sharpest point: school rewards analytical intelligence almost exclusively, yet life rewards all three. The straight-A student who can't navigate an office, and the "average" student who builds a thriving business, are both completely real — and standard tests see only half the picture.

</Step>

<Step title="The newest contender: emotional intelligence" kind="explain">

A fourth idea broke out of the lab and into everyday language: **emotional intelligence (EQ)** — the ability to *perceive, understand, manage and use emotions*, in yourself and others. Popularised in the 1990s, it overlaps with Gardner's interpersonal and intrapersonal intelligences.

The appeal is obvious: reading a room, staying calm under pressure, motivating a team — these clearly matter, and *g* barely touches them. The caution: when EQ is measured by self-report questionnaires it looks a lot like the personality trait of agreeableness, and its boldest claims ("EQ matters more than IQ") outrun the evidence. Real, useful, but easy to oversell.

<Callout type="tip" title="The honest summary">
No theory has won. *g* is the best-measured and most predictive single factor we have. Gardner, Sternberg and EQ are powerful correctives, reminding us how much a single IQ number leaves out — even if they're harder to pin down with hard data.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Factor analysis** is the statistical tool that first revealed **_g_** by finding a common thread across many test scores.
- **Spearman**: one general intelligence runs everything (best-measured, most predictive).
- **Gardner's multiple intelligences**: eight relatively independent abilities (linguistic, logical-mathematical, spatial, musical, bodily-kinesthetic, interpersonal, intrapersonal, naturalistic) — broad and intuitive, but hard to test.
- **Sternberg's triarchic theory**: three practical kinds — **analytical, creative, practical** — and school rewards mainly the first.
- **Emotional intelligence**: perceiving and managing emotions — real and useful, but often oversold and hard to measure cleanly.
- No single theory has won; each is a useful lens on the same hard question.

</Step>
