<Step title="The great evolutionary puzzle" kind="explain">

Sleep is, on its face, a terrible idea. For roughly a third of your life you lie helpless, blind to predators, eating nothing, achieving nothing. Any trait this costly that survived hundreds of millions of years of evolution must be doing something essential. So *why* do we sleep?

There is no single answer — sleep almost certainly serves several jobs at once. But two theories carry most of the weight: sleep **restores** the body and brain, and sleep **consolidates** memory. They aren't rivals; they're two of the things sleep is *for*.

</Step>

<Step title="Restoration and memory" kind="explain">

The **restoration theory** says sleep is the body's overnight repair shift. Deep N3 sleep is when growth hormone is released, tissues are repaired, the immune system is strengthened, and — newly discovered — the brain literally flushes out metabolic waste that builds up while you're awake. You feel rotten after a bad night because the maintenance crew never clocked in.

The **memory-consolidation theory** says sleep is when learning becomes permanent. During sleep the brain *replays* the day's experiences, strengthening the connections worth keeping and weakening the rest. The evidence is strong: people who sleep after studying remember far more than those kept awake, and both slow-wave and REM sleep play distinct roles in locking in skills and facts.

<Callout type="warn" title="The cost of skimping">
Chronic sleep loss isn't just grogginess. It impairs attention, memory, mood and judgement, weakens immunity, and is linked to weight gain and accidents. "I'll sleep when I'm dead" gets the causality backwards — too little sleep brings that day closer.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Why we dream — three theories" kind="interactive">

Dreams feel meaningful, so humans have always asked what they're *for*. Science offers competing answers, and comparing them side by side is the clearest way to see the debate.

<DreamTheories />

The classic clash is between **Freud** and **activation-synthesis**. Freud insisted dreams are *disguised wishes* — the remembered story hiding a censored desire. Activation-synthesis turned this on its head: dreams are the cortex spinning a story out of essentially *random* signals fired by the sleeping brainstem, more side-effect than secret message. A third view, **information-processing**, ties dreaming to the memory consolidation we just met. None is proven, and the truth may blend them.

</Step>

<Step title="When sleep goes wrong" kind="interactive">

Because sleep is so finely staged, there are many ways for it to break — and sleep **disorders** are far more common than people admit. Explore the gallery and notice which stage of the cycle each one disrupts.

<SleepDisorders />

A revealing pattern: **night terrors** and **sleepwalking** both erupt out of deep **N3** sleep, not from dreaming — which is why the person remembers nothing and is so hard to wake. **Narcolepsy**, by contrast, is REM gone rogue, intruding into waking life. Knowing the stage tells you the story.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- Sleep is too costly to be an accident; its main jobs are **restoration** (repair, growth, immune support, waste clearance — largely in deep N3) and **memory consolidation** (replaying and strengthening the day's learning).
- Chronic sleep loss harms attention, memory, mood, immunity and safety.
- Dream theories compete: **Freudian wish-fulfilment** (disguised desires), **activation-synthesis** (story from random signals), and **information-processing** (memory consolidation) — none proven.
- **Sleep disorders** map onto the cycle: **night terrors** and **sleepwalking** arise from deep **N3**; **narcolepsy** is **REM** intruding on waking; **insomnia** and **apnea** wreck sleep continuity.

</Step>
