<Step title="The first talking cure" kind="explain">

Picture a quiet study in 1900s Vienna, a couch, and a patient talking — saying whatever drifts into mind — while the doctor listens from out of sight. This is **psychoanalysis**, the original psychotherapy, invented by **Sigmund Freud**. Every therapy that followed is, in some sense, a reply to it.

Freud's bold claim: psychological symptoms aren't random. They're the surface ripples of **unconscious conflict** — wishes, fears and memories, often from childhood, that were pushed out of awareness because they were too threatening to face. Buried, they don't vanish; they leak out as anxiety, depression, or self-defeating patterns. The cure, then, is to make the unconscious conscious — to drag the hidden conflict into the light where it loses its power.

</Step>

<Step title="The techniques: getting at the hidden" kind="explain">

How do you reach material the mind is actively hiding? Psychoanalysis developed a toolkit:

- **Free association** — say *everything* that comes to mind, however trivial, embarrassing or absurd, without censoring. The detours, slips and sudden silences are clues to what's being defended against.
- **Dream analysis** — Freud called dreams "the royal road to the unconscious." The remembered dream (the *manifest* content) is read as a disguised expression of deeper wishes (the *latent* content).
- **Interpreting resistance** — when a patient suddenly goes blank, changes the subject or "forgets" an appointment, the therapist treats that **resistance** as a signpost: you're getting close to something painful.

The work is slow because the mind doesn't surrender its secrets easily.

</Step>

<Step title="Transference: the past in the room" kind="explain">

The most distinctive idea of all is **transference**. Over time, the patient starts to relate to the therapist as though they were a key figure from the past — a parent, a rival, a lost love — replaying old feelings and patterns *in the room*.

Far from being a distraction, this is gold. The therapist watches the relationship itself become a live re-enactment of the patient's deepest patterns — and by interpreting it ("you seem to fear I'll abandon you, the way you feared your father would"), helps the patient see and finally rework those patterns. The therapist's own emotional reactions to the patient are called **countertransference**, and they too must be examined.

</Step>

<Step title="From the couch to brief modern therapy" kind="explain">

Classic psychoanalysis took years — several sessions a week, lying on a couch. Few people today can manage that, and the field has changed accordingly.

Modern **psychodynamic therapy** keeps Freud's core insights — the unconscious, early relationships, the value of insight, the meaning of transference — but is usually **briefer, face-to-face, and focused** on a specific theme rather than excavating the entire psyche. Approaches like *short-term psychodynamic therapy* run for months, not years, and a solid body of evidence shows they genuinely help with depression, anxiety and relationship difficulties. The couch became a chair; the central idea — that understanding hidden patterns can free us from them — survived.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Psychoanalysis** (Freud) was the first psychotherapy; it sees symptoms as surface signs of **unconscious conflict**, often from childhood, and aims to make the unconscious conscious.
- Core techniques: **free association**, **dream analysis**, and interpreting **resistance**.
- **Transference** — relating to the therapist as a figure from the past — is a prized window onto the client's deepest patterns; the therapist's own reactions are **countertransference**.
- Modern **psychodynamic therapy** keeps the core ideas but is **briefer and more focused**, with evidence it helps depression, anxiety and relationship problems.

</Step>
