<Step title="What does 'hungry' even mean?" kind="explain">

It feels obvious: you're hungry when your stomach is empty, full when it's full. For a long time that's exactly what scientists thought — hunger lived in the belly.

Then came a startling finding. People (and animals) whose stomachs had been removed *still felt hunger*. The stomach matters, but it isn't the control centre. Hunger turns out to be orchestrated by the **brain**, reading a stream of chemical signals from all over the body. Eating is one of the most heavily regulated behaviours we have — because for our ancestors, getting it wrong meant starving or wasting precious energy.

</Step>

<Step title="The signals: stomach, hormones, and the hypothalamus" kind="explain">

Three sources of information feed the system:

- **The stomach** sends stretch signals: a full, distended stomach says "stop." But it's a crude, short-term cue.
- **Hormones** carry the real message. An empty stomach releases **ghrelin**, the "I'm hungry" hormone that ramps up appetite. Your fat cells release **leptin** in proportion to how much fat you're storing — a long-term "I have enough reserves" signal that dampens hunger.
- **The hypothalamus**, deep in the brain, is the control centre. It reads ghrelin, leptin, blood sugar and more, then turns hunger up or down. One region tends to switch eating *on*; another tends to switch it *off*.

The result is a **negative feedback loop**, the same logic that keeps a thermostat steady: signals of low energy crank up hunger; signals of plenty quiet it down.

</Step>

<Step title="Defending a set point" kind="interactive">

**Set-point theory** says your body has a weight it "wants" to defend — a setpoint the feedback loop fights to maintain. Push weight below it and the body raises hunger and slows metabolism to claw the weight back; push above it and hunger falls. Try it:

Hit "crash diet" and watch the loop pull weight back up — ghrelin surges, leptin drops, metabolism slows. This is a big part of why diets so often **rebound**: you're not just fighting willpower, you're fighting a biological control system defending its setpoint. (Turn the feedback off to see what weight would do without it.)

<Callout type="info" title="The set point can drift">
The setpoint isn't fixed for life. Genetics, long-term diet, and environment can shift it — which is why the body that defends 70 kg today might settle at a different value after years of changed habits. It resists *quick* change far more than slow change.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Hunger is psychological, too" kind="explain">

If hunger were purely about energy, you'd stop the instant your needs were met. But you've felt the pull of dessert when already full, or noticed your appetite vanish when anxious. Eating is shaped by far more than biology:

- **The sight and smell of food** can trigger hunger on its own — a bakery window, a sizzling pan.
- **Variety** keeps us eating: offered one food we tire of it (sensory-specific satiety), but a buffet of options keeps appetite alive.
- **Learning and emotion** matter: we eat to soothe stress, to celebrate, or simply because the clock says "lunchtime."

</Step>

<Step title="The culture on your plate" kind="explain">

Zoom out and **culture** turns out to script much of what, when and how much we eat. What counts as delicious in one place — insects, blue cheese, fermented shark — is revolting in another. Portion norms, mealtime rituals, what's "breakfast food," even how thin or heavy is considered attractive: all vary enormously across societies and eras.

This is the **biopsychosocial** model in miniature. Biology sets the hunger machinery and defends a setpoint (bio); learning, emotion and the sight of food modulate it (psycho); and culture shapes the menu, the portions and the meanings (social). To explain why a person eats *this*, *now*, *this much*, you need all three levels at once.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- Hunger is governed by the **brain**, not the stomach alone: the **hypothalamus** reads chemical signals and turns eating up or down.
- **Ghrelin** (empty stomach) ramps up hunger; **leptin** (from fat cells) signals plenty and dampens it — a **negative feedback loop**.
- **Set-point theory**: the body defends a target weight, raising hunger and slowing metabolism when weight drops — a key reason diets rebound.
- Eating is also **psychological and cultural** — driven by sight, smell, variety, emotion, learning and the deep scripts of culture. Hunger is **biopsychosocial**.

</Step>
