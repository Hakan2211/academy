<Lesson>

<Step title="Knowledge is not the only thing that matters" kind="explain">

Psychology studies *people*, and people can be harmed in the name of finding things out. The history of research includes studies that left lasting damage — and modern ethics rules were written, often in direct response, to make sure they can't happen again.

The core question is never just "what could we learn?" but "**at what cost, to whom, and did they agree to pay it?**" Every study now passes through this filter before a single participant is recruited.

</Step>

<Step title="The core protections" kind="explain">

A handful of principles cover most of the ground:

- **Informed consent** — before taking part, people must understand what the study involves and freely agree, knowing they can quit at any time without penalty.
- **Protection from harm** — risks must be minimised, and the study's benefits must outweigh whatever risks remain. Serious or lasting harm is off the table.
- **Confidentiality** — data is kept private and, wherever possible, anonymous, so taking part can't come back to hurt someone.
- **The right to withdraw** — participants can stop and pull their data out, at any point, no reason required.

</Step>

<Step title="When deception is unavoidable" kind="explain">

Some studies only work if participants *don't* know the real purpose — tell people you're testing conformity and they'll behave unnaturally. Limited **deception** is therefore allowed, but only under strict conditions: it must be genuinely necessary, it must not involve serious risk, and it must be followed by a thorough **debriefing**.

Debriefing means that, immediately afterward, you reveal the truth, explain why the deception was needed, answer questions, and make sure the participant leaves no worse than they arrived. Deception without debriefing is simply a violation.

</Step>

<Step title="The gatekeeper" kind="explain">

No researcher gets to be the sole judge of their own study's ethics — the temptation to wave through your own exciting idea is too strong. So proposals go before an independent **review board** (in the US, an **IRB** — Institutional Review Board) that weighs risks against benefits and can demand changes or refuse approval outright.

**Animal research** faces its own parallel rules: animals can't consent, so the burden is on researchers to justify their use, minimise the number, and prevent unnecessary suffering, overseen by a dedicated committee.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- Research ethics weigh what we can learn against the cost to participants — **informed consent**, **protection from harm**, **confidentiality**, and the **right to withdraw** are the core protections.
- **Deception** is allowed only when necessary and low-risk, and always requires a thorough **debriefing** afterward.
- An independent **review board (IRB)** must approve studies in advance; **animal research** carries its own duty to justify, minimise, and prevent suffering.
- These rules are responses to real studies that caused real harm — they exist to keep knowledge from being bought at too high a price.

</Step>
