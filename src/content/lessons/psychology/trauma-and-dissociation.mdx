<Step title="When an event won't stay in the past" kind="explain">

Some experiences are so overwhelming — combat, assault, a disaster, abuse — that the mind struggles to file them away as *over*. The danger has passed, but the alarm keeps ringing as if it hasn't. This is the territory of **trauma- and stress-related disorders**.

What unites them is a clear external trigger: a frightening, threatening event. Most people who live through trauma recover with time and support; for some, the response gets stuck, and the past keeps breaking into the present. The most studied of these is **PTSD**.

</Step>

<Step title="PTSD" kind="explain">

**Post-traumatic stress disorder** can follow exposure to a traumatic event, and its symptoms tend to cluster in four groups:

- **Intrusion** — unwanted memories, nightmares, and **flashbacks** so vivid the person feels the event is happening again, *now*.
- **Avoidance** — steering clear of any reminder — places, people, conversations, even thoughts — that might reawaken it.
- **Negative shifts** in mood and thinking — persistent fear, guilt, shame, numbness, or a bleak view of oneself and the world.
- **Hyperarousal** — being constantly on edge: jumpy, irritable, unable to sleep, scanning for threat.

The avoidance, you'll recognise, is the same trap from the anxiety cycle — it brings relief but keeps the fear from resolving. Effective treatments (notably trauma-focused therapies) work, in part, by helping the person safely process the memory instead of fleeing it.

</Step>

<Step title="Dissociation: the mind's circuit-breaker" kind="explain">

Faced with the unbearable, the mind has an extreme defence: it can **dissociate** — disconnecting normally linked threads of memory, identity, perception, and awareness. In the moment of overwhelming trauma this can be protective, a kind of psychological circuit-breaker. As a *disorder*, the disconnection persists and disrupts life.

The **dissociative disorders** include:

- **Dissociative amnesia** — an inability to recall important personal information, usually around a traumatic event, far beyond ordinary forgetting.
- **Depersonalisation/derealisation** — a persistent sense of being detached from one's own body or thoughts, or that the world is unreal or dreamlike.
- **Dissociative identity disorder (DID)** — the presence of two or more distinct identity states, with gaps in memory between them. Strongly linked to severe, repeated early childhood trauma, DID is rare, frequently sensationalised by films, and often misunderstood — and it is *not* the same as schizophrenia.

</Step>

<Step title="Somatic symptom disorders" kind="explain">

There's a third way distress can surface: through the **body**. In **somatic symptom disorders**, a person experiences real, distressing physical symptoms — pain, fatigue, weakness — along with disproportionate thoughts, feelings, and worry about them.

The key point is one of respect: the symptoms are **genuinely felt**, not faked or "all in the head." The mind and body are deeply linked, and psychological distress can express itself physically. Dismissing such a person as a malingerer is both unkind and clinically wrong — the suffering is real and the condition is treatable.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Trauma-related disorders** follow an overwhelming external event; most people recover, but for some the alarm stays stuck on.
- **PTSD** clusters into **intrusion** (flashbacks, nightmares), **avoidance**, **negative mood/thinking**, and **hyperarousal**; avoidance keeps the fear from resolving.
- **Dissociation** disconnects memory, identity, perception, and awareness; dissociative disorders include **amnesia**, **depersonalisation/derealisation**, and **DID** (linked to severe early trauma — and *not* schizophrenia).
- **Somatic symptom disorders** express distress through *genuinely felt* physical symptoms — never "faked."
- On the diagnostic map these sit as related but distinct families, and in real life they often travel together.

</Step>
