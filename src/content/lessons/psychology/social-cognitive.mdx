<Step title="Personality as a thinking, learning thing" kind="explain">

The trait, psychodynamic and humanistic approaches all locate personality *inside* the person — as dimensions, drives or a self. The **social-cognitive** approach pushes back: personality, it argues, emerges from the constant interplay between how you **think** and the **situations** you meet. It grew out of behaviourism but added the missing ingredient — the mind. We don't just react to rewards; we *interpret*, *expect*, *believe* and *plan*.

This makes the approach the most rigorously scientific of the four families: its concepts are defined precisely and tested in controlled studies. Three thinkers built it.

</Step>

<Step title="Bandura: it's a two-way street" kind="interactive">

Albert **Bandura** noticed that earlier theories drew a one-way arrow: environment shapes person. He replaced it with a triangle he called **reciprocal determinism** — your **Person** (beliefs), your **Behaviour**, and your **Environment** all influence one another, continuously. <ReciprocalDeterminism />

You are not a billiard ball pushed around by your surroundings; you also choose, shape and create those surroundings, which then loop back to shape you.

</Step>

<Step title="Self-efficacy: the belief that moves you" kind="explain">

At the heart of Bandura's triangle sits his most influential idea: **self-efficacy** — your belief in your own ability to succeed at a specific task. It is not the same as self-esteem (how much you *like* yourself); it's a focused *"can I do this?"*

Self-efficacy is a quiet powerhouse. People high in it attempt harder goals, persist through setbacks, and recover faster from failure — which produces real successes, which raises efficacy further. Low self-efficacy does the reverse: you avoid challenges, give up early, and never gather the evidence that might prove you wrong. Bandura showed it can be deliberately built — through small successes, watching others like you succeed, and encouragement.

</Step>

<Step title="Rotter: where you locate control" kind="interactive">

Julian **Rotter** added another belief that shapes personality: your **locus of control** — where you locate the causes of what happens to you.

- An **internal** locus: *outcomes flow from my own effort and choices.*
- An **external** locus: *outcomes are down to luck, fate or powerful others.*

Internals tend to take initiative and cope actively; pushed to the extreme, an external locus can collapse into **learned helplessness**. But context matters too — sometimes things genuinely *are* outside your control.

</Step>

<Step title="Mischel and the person-situation debate" kind="explain">

Then Walter **Mischel** lobbed a grenade. Reviewing the data in 1968, he reported that trait scores often correlate only modestly (around r = 0.30) with behaviour in any *specific* situation. People, he argued, are far less consistent across situations than trait theory assumes — the same person is bold with friends and timid with strangers. This kicked off the decades-long **person-situation debate**.

The resolution was a richer view, not a winner. Traits *do* predict behaviour — but **aggregated** across many situations, not in any single one (recall the batting-average idea). And Mischel's own answer was elegant: people have stable **"if…then" signatures** — *if* with friends, *then* outgoing; *if* with authority, *then* reserved. That pattern of variation is *itself* a consistent part of personality. Person **and** situation, interacting — exactly Bandura's triangle.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- The **social-cognitive** approach roots personality in the interplay of **thought** and **situation**, adding the mind back into behaviourism — the most testable of the four families.
- Bandura's **reciprocal determinism**: person, behaviour and environment all shape one another in a loop.
- **Self-efficacy** — belief in one's ability at a task — drives effort, persistence and resilience, and can be built.
- Rotter's **locus of control** (internal vs external) shapes initiative and coping; an extreme external locus risks **learned helplessness**.
- Mischel's **person-situation debate** showed people vary across situations; the resolution is stable **"if…then" signatures** — person *and* situation, interacting.

</Step>
