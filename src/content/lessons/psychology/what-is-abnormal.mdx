<Step title="The hardest word in the chapter" kind="explain">

We're about to study psychological disorders — and the very first task is the trickiest. Before we can talk about a *disorder*, we have to ask a slippery question: what makes a thought, feeling, or behaviour **"abnormal"** in the first place?

It's harder than it sounds. *Unusual* won't do — great artists and Olympic athletes are unusual, and they're not disordered. *Disliked by others* won't do either — that just describes anyone who breaks a social rule. Psychologists have learned to be very careful here, because the label "abnormal" carries real weight in people's lives.

<Callout type="info" title="Before we begin">
This world is **educational, not diagnostic**. Nothing here is a tool for diagnosing yourself or anyone else — that takes a trained professional and a real conversation. If you or someone you know is struggling, please reach out to a doctor, a mental-health professional, or a local helpline. Throughout, we describe people *experiencing* a condition — never reduce a person to a label.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="The four D's" kind="explain">

Rather than one rule, clinicians weigh several signals together. A common shorthand is the **four D's**:

- **Deviance** — does the behaviour fall well outside what's typical for the person's culture and situation? (Note: *deviant* depends entirely on context — what's normal at a concert isn't normal in a library.)
- **Distress** — does it cause the person real, lasting suffering? Their *own* experience matters enormously.
- **Dysfunction** — does it get in the way of everyday life: work, relationships, looking after oneself?
- **Danger** — does it create genuine risk of harm to the person or to others? (This one is rarer than the stereotype suggests.)

No single D decides anything. Being unusual isn't a disorder; feeling distressed once isn't a disorder. It's the **pattern across several dimensions** — and how much it interferes with a life — that a clinician weighs.

</Step>

Here's the crucial idea the four D's hide: each one is a **continuum**, not a switch. A behaviour doesn't suddenly flip from "fine" to "disordered" — it sits at some level on each dimension, and the overall picture shifts gradually.

Notice that maxing out *deviance* alone (just being unusual) never lands you in clinical territory. It takes a combination — especially distress and dysfunction — and even then, a thoughtful clinician treats this as a starting point, not a verdict.

</Step>

<Step title="The objectivity problem" kind="explain">

So why not just write down firm rules and be done with it? Because "abnormal" is partly a **judgement**, and judgements can go wrong — sometimes badly.

History is full of warnings. Behaviours once labelled "disorders" — left-handedness, certain religious experiences, and, shamefully, homosexuality (only removed from the official manual in 1973) — were really just **violations of the norms of the time**. The label said more about the society doing the labelling than about the people being labelled.

This is the **objectivity problem**: judgements of abnormality are shaped by culture, era, and power. Good clinical science fights back with careful definitions, evidence, and a constant question — *is this truly causing harm and distress, or is it merely different?*

</Step>

<Step title="Models of disorder" kind="explain">

How we *explain* a disorder shapes how we respond to it. A few broad models:

- The **medical (biological) model** treats disorders much like physical illnesses — rooted in the brain, genes, and biochemistry — and so leans toward biological treatment.
- The **psychological models** (cognitive, behavioural, psychodynamic) locate the trouble in learning, thinking, or unresolved conflict.
- The **sociocultural model** points to a person's environment — poverty, discrimination, family strain, trauma.

The modern view doesn't pick one. It folds them into the **biopsychosocial model** you've met before on this island: biology, psychology, and social context all interact to produce — and to relieve — distress. We'll return to this in force in the final lesson.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- Defining **"abnormal"** is genuinely hard: *unusual* or *disapproved-of* won't do.
- Clinicians weigh the **four D's** — **deviance, distress, dysfunction, danger** — together; no single one is decisive, and *deviance alone* never is.
- Each D is a **continuum**, so "disorder" is a judgement across several dimensions, not an on/off switch.
- The **objectivity problem**: judgements of abnormality are shaped by culture and era (e.g. behaviours once wrongly labelled disorders), so careful, evidence-based definitions matter.
- **Models of disorder** — medical, psychological, sociocultural — combine in the **biopsychosocial model**: biology × psychology × social context, interacting.

</Step>
