<Step title="A discovery by accident" kind="explain">

Around 1900 the Russian physiologist **Ivan Pavlov** was studying digestion in dogs — measuring how much they salivated when fed. Then he noticed something odd: the dogs began drooling *before* the food arrived, at the mere sound of the assistant's footsteps or the clink of the bowl.

Most scientists would have called that a nuisance. Pavlov realised he had stumbled onto a whole new kind of learning — how a previously meaningless signal can come to trigger a reflex. He spent the rest of his career mapping its rules, and won a Nobel Prize along the way. We call it **classical conditioning**.

</Step>

<Step title="The four key terms" kind="explain">

Classical conditioning links a **neutral signal** to an **automatic reflex**. Four terms name the moving parts:

- **Unconditioned stimulus (UCS)** — something that triggers a reflex with no learning needed. *Food in the mouth.*
- **Unconditioned response (UCR)** — the automatic, unlearned reaction. *Salivating at the food.*
- **Conditioned stimulus (CS)** — a once-neutral signal that, after pairing, comes to trigger the reflex. *The bell.*
- **Conditioned response (CR)** — the learned reaction to the CS. *Salivating at the bell.*

The trick to keeping them straight: **un**conditioned = **un**learned (you're born with it); conditioned = learned through experience.

</Step>

<Step title="From neutral to conditioned" kind="explain">

Before training, the bell is a **neutral stimulus (NS)** — ring it and the dog just pricks up its ears. Food (UCS) reliably makes the dog salivate (UCR).

Now comes the engine of the whole process. Repeatedly present the bell **just before** the food:

<Formula tex="\text{Bell (NS)} + \text{Food (UCS)} \;\rightarrow\; \text{Salivation (UCR)}" block />

After enough pairings, the bell alone does the job. The neutral stimulus has graduated into a **conditioned stimulus**, and the drooling it now causes is a **conditioned response**:

<Formula tex="\text{Bell (CS)} \;\rightarrow\; \text{Salivation (CR)}" block />

The dog learned that the bell *predicts* food — and its body prepares accordingly.

</Step>

<Step title="Acquisition" kind="explain">

The phase where the CS–UCS link is built up is called **acquisition**. A few facts about it:

- It usually takes **several pairings**, and the conditioned response **strengthens gradually** — fast at first, then levelling off.
- **Timing matters.** The CS should come *just before* the UCS (about half a second is ideal). If the bell comes *after* the food, little or no learning occurs — the signal has to *predict* the event.
- It's about **predictiveness**, not mere closeness in time. A signal that reliably forecasts the UCS conditions strongly; a signal that's only sometimes followed by it conditions weakly.

</Step>

<Step title="Run the experiment yourself" kind="interactive">

Be Pavlov. Ring the bell alone first (notice: no drool). Present the food (full drool — that's the unconditioned reflex). Now **pair bell + food** several times and watch the bell's power climb on the acquisition curve. Then ring the bell alone again.

When the curve rises and the bell alone makes the dog salivate, you've conditioned a response from scratch. Flip to the **extinction** phase to see what happens when the bell stops predicting food — covered in the next lesson.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Classical conditioning** (Pavlov) links a neutral signal to an automatic reflex.
- The four terms: **UCS** (triggers a reflex unlearned), **UCR** (the unlearned reaction), **CS** (a once-neutral signal that now triggers the reflex), **CR** (the learned reaction). *Un*conditioned = *un*learned.
- A **neutral stimulus becomes a CS** by being paired, repeatedly and just *before*, with the UCS: NS + UCS → UCR, until CS → CR.
- **Acquisition** is gradual and depends on the CS *predicting* the UCS — timing and reliability matter more than mere co-occurrence.

</Step>
