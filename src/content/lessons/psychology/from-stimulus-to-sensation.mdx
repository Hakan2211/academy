<Step title="The world gets in through narrow doors" kind="explain">

Right now, an entire world is pressing against you — light, sound, pressure, temperature, chemistry in the air and on your tongue. None of it reaches your mind directly. Everything you will ever know about reality has to squeeze through a handful of **sense organs** and be translated into the only language your brain speaks: **nerve impulses**.

That translation is the subject of this whole world. We split it into two jobs:

- **Sensation** — the sense organs detecting raw physical energy and converting it to neural signals.
- **Perception** — the brain organising and interpreting those signals into meaningful experience.

This lesson is about the first job: how a thing *out there* becomes a signal *in here*.

</Step>

<Step title="Distal and proximal: two versions of the stimulus" kind="explain">

A subtle but crucial distinction starts us off. Picture a tree across a field.

- The **distal stimulus** is the actual object in the world — the tree itself, at a distance from you.
- The **proximal stimulus** is the pattern of energy that physically reaches your sense organ — here, the image the tree casts on your retina.

You never sense the distal stimulus directly. You only ever get the proximal one — a small, often distorted, two-dimensional, upside-down imprint — and from *that* your brain reconstructs the tree. The same is true of a bell: the distal stimulus is the bell; the proximal stimulus is the pressure wave wobbling your eardrum.

This gap between the object and its imprint is exactly why perception is such hard, clever work — and why it can be fooled.

</Step>

<Step title="Transduction: turning energy into signals" kind="explain">

How does light, or sound, or a smell become something neurons can carry? Through **transduction** — the conversion of one form of energy into the electrochemical signals of the nervous system.

Every sense has specialised **receptor cells** tuned to one kind of energy:

- Light → photoreceptors (rods and cones) in the retina.
- Sound vibration → hair cells in the cochlea.
- Airborne chemicals → olfactory receptors in the nose.
- Dissolved chemicals → taste receptors on the tongue.
- Pressure, temperature, pain → receptors in the skin.

Once transduced, every sense uses the *same* currency: trains of nerve impulses racing to the brain. Remarkably, what makes vision feel different from hearing isn't the signal itself — it's **which brain area** the signal arrives at.

</Step>

<Step title="Trace the journey" kind="interactive">

Walk one stimulus all the way through: from the object in the world, to the energy that lands on your sense organ, to receptors firing, to a finished experience. Step through it for vision, hearing and smell — notice the four stages are identical every time.

<TransductionFlow />

The headline: by the time you *experience* anything, it has already been transformed several times. You are never in direct contact with the world — only with your brain's reconstruction of it.

</Step>

<Step title="More than five senses" kind="explain">

You were probably taught there are five senses: sight, hearing, smell, taste, touch. The truth is richer.

"Touch" alone is really several senses braided together — separate receptors for pressure, warmth, cold and pain. And two more get left off the classic list entirely:

- **Vestibular sense** — your inner ear's feel for balance and head position.
- **Kinesthesis** — your sense of where your body parts are, even with your eyes shut.

<Callout type="tip" title="Try it">
Close your eyes and touch your nose with your fingertip. You found it instantly — without sight or touch guiding the way in. That's kinesthesis, a sense you use constantly and almost never notice.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Sensation** detects raw physical energy and converts it to neural signals; **perception** organises and interprets those signals.
- The **distal stimulus** is the object in the world; the **proximal stimulus** is the energy pattern that actually reaches your sense organ. You only ever sense the proximal.
- **Transduction** is the conversion of physical energy into nerve impulses by specialised **receptor cells**, one type per kind of energy.
- All senses then share the same neural currency — what differs is which brain region receives the signal.
- We have **more than five senses**, including the **vestibular** sense (balance) and **kinesthesis** (body position).

</Step>
