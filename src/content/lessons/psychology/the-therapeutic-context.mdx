<Step title="A room, two people, and a problem" kind="explain">

Imagine sitting across from someone whose whole job is to understand you — not to judge, not to fix you with a wave of a hand, but to help you make sense of what hurts and find a way through it. That, stripped to its essentials, is **psychotherapy**: a planned, collaborative relationship in which psychological methods are used to relieve distress and help a person live better.

Therapy isn't advice from a clever friend, and it isn't mysticism. It's a set of techniques, refined over a century, that genuinely change how people feel, think and act. Over the next seven lessons you'll meet every major approach — and finish by asking the honest question: *does any of it actually work?*

<Callout type="info" title="Seeking help is a strength">
Therapy works for a wide range of struggles, and reaching out for it is a sign of courage and good judgement, not weakness. This lesson is educational — it explains how therapy is done, not personal medical advice. If you or someone you know is struggling, a qualified professional or local helpline is the right place to turn.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="What therapy is trying to do" kind="explain">

Whatever the school, most therapies share a handful of goals:

- **Relieve distress** — ease the painful symptoms, the anxiety, the low mood, the intrusive thoughts.
- **Build insight** — help you understand the patterns driving your difficulties.
- **Change behaviour** — replace what isn't working (avoidance, lashing out, self-criticism) with something healthier.
- **Strengthen the self** — improve relationships, coping skills and a sense of agency, so you're more resilient when life pushes again.

Notice that "cure" isn't quite the right word. Therapy aims less at perfection than at **functioning, growth and freedom** — being less ruled by the problem.

</Step>

<Step title="Who provides it" kind="explain">

"Therapist" is an umbrella. The people under it differ in training and in what they can do:

- **Clinical & counselling psychologists** — doctoral-level training in psychological assessment and the talking therapies.
- **Psychiatrists** — medical doctors who specialise in mental health; they can prescribe medication and often combine it with therapy.
- **Counsellors, clinical social workers and psychotherapists** — trained to provide talking therapy and support, often for specific settings or problems.

The key distinction: only **medically qualified** professionals (chiefly psychiatrists) prescribe drugs. Everyone else works mainly through the relationship and through psychological technique — which, as you'll see, is powerful in its own right.

</Step>

<Step title="The thing that does the healing" kind="explain">

Across every school, decades of research keep pointing at one ingredient as the strongest predictor of whether therapy helps: the **therapeutic alliance** — the bond of trust, warmth and shared purpose between client and therapist.

It has three parts: an emotional **bond**, agreement on the **goals** of therapy, and agreement on the **tasks** (what you'll actually do together). When the alliance is strong, almost any sensible method tends to work better. When it's weak, even the "right" technique tends to fall flat. Hold onto this — it returns in the final lesson as one of the great findings of the whole field.

</Step>

<Step title="From asylums to community care" kind="explain">

How we treat psychological suffering has changed dramatically — and not always for the better, until recently.

- For centuries, distress was blamed on demons or sin; "treatment" meant confinement, even cruelty.
- In the late 1700s, reformers like **Philippe Pinel** literally unchained patients and argued for **moral treatment** — humane care, dignity, routine.
- The 1900s brought the talking cure (Freud), then behavioural and cognitive methods, and from the 1950s, **psychiatric medications**.
- Those drugs helped fuel **deinstitutionalisation**: emptying the vast asylums in favour of **community-based care** — outpatient clinics, community teams, and today, online and brief therapies.

The arc bends toward treating people with more dignity, in their own communities, with methods that have been tested rather than merely assumed.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Psychotherapy** is a planned, collaborative relationship using psychological methods to relieve distress and help people live better; seeking it is a strength, not a weakness.
- Common goals: **relieve distress, build insight, change behaviour, strengthen the self** — aiming at functioning and growth more than "cure".
- Providers differ: **psychologists, counsellors and social workers** work through technique and relationship; only medically qualified professionals (mainly **psychiatrists**) prescribe medication.
- The **therapeutic alliance** (bond + agreed goals + agreed tasks) is one of the strongest predictors of success across all schools.
- The field moved from **asylums and confinement** through **moral treatment** to today's **community-based care** and tested therapies.
