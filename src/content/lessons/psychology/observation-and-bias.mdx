<Lesson>

<Step title="The simplest tool: just watch" kind="explain">

Before you can explain behaviour, you have to *describe* it accurately. The most direct way is **naturalistic observation** — watching people or animals in their normal setting without interfering. A researcher counting how often strangers help someone who drops their bags, or logging how toddlers share toys at a daycare, is doing exactly this.

Its great strength is realism: you see behaviour as it actually happens, not as people imagine they'd behave. Its weakness is that you can only describe and find patterns — watching alone can never show *cause*.

</Step>

<Step title="The case study: depth over breadth" kind="explain">

Sometimes one person is the whole study. A **case study** is an intensive, in-depth look at a single individual — often someone rare or remarkable. A patient who can no longer form new memories after brain surgery, or a child raised in extreme isolation, can reveal more about how the mind works than a thousand ordinary cases.

The trade-off is the mirror image of observation's: enormous depth, but **you cannot generalise** from one person to everyone. A case study generates rich hypotheses; it rarely settles them.

</Step>

<Step title="The watcher changes the watched" kind="explain">

Observation sounds objective, but the observer is a person with expectations — and those expectations leak into the data.

- **Observer bias** — you unconsciously notice and record what fits what you already expect, and overlook what doesn't.
- **The expectancy effect** — your expectations actually *change the behaviour you're measuring*, often through tiny, unintended cues.

<Callout type="warn" title="Clever Hans">
A famous horse, Clever Hans, seemed to do arithmetic by tapping his hoof. He was right only when his questioner knew the answer — and stopped at the right number because they unconsciously tensed or leaned. Hans wasn't counting; he was reading the human's unintended cues. Ever since, "the Clever Hans effect" names exactly this trap.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="How careful observation feeds the loop" kind="interactive">

Observation is the first turn of a larger wheel: what you carefully describe becomes the question you hypothesise about, predict from, and test. Watch the cycle and notice that it *starts* with observing — and keeps coming back to it.

<ScientificMethod />

The fixes for bias live in this loop too: define ahead of time exactly what counts (a clear operational definition), and keep observers **blind** to what's expected so their hopes can't tilt the data.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Naturalistic observation** captures real behaviour in its natural setting but can only describe, never prove cause.
- A **case study** trades breadth for depth: rich insight from one rare individual, but you can't generalise it.
- **Observer bias** tilts what you record toward what you expect; the **expectancy effect** can change the behaviour itself — the **Clever Hans** lesson.
- Guard against both with pre-set operational definitions and **blind** observers.

</Step>
