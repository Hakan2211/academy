<Step title="The one method that shows cause" kind="explain">

Observation and case studies can tell you that two things go *together*. Only one method can tell you that one thing **causes** another: the **experiment**. Its trick is simple but powerful — instead of passively watching, you actively **change one thing** and see what happens.

That deliberately changed thing is the **independent variable** (IV) — the suspected cause. The thing you measure in response is the **dependent variable** (DV) — the effect, which *depends* on the IV. Caffeine versus no caffeine is the IV; reaction time is the DV.

</Step>

<Step title="Two groups, treated the same — except one thing" kind="explain">

To see the effect of your IV, you compare groups:

- The **experimental group** gets the treatment (the real caffeine).
- The **control group** gets everything *identical* except the treatment (a decaf that looks and tastes the same).

If the only difference between the groups is the IV, then any difference in the DV at the end must be **caused by the IV**. The control group is your "what would have happened anyway" baseline. Without it, you'd have nothing to compare against.

</Step>

<Step title="Random assignment: the secret ingredient" kind="explain">

Here's the catch: people differ wildly before your study even begins — in sleep, motivation, talent, mood. If those differences pile up unevenly into your two groups, you can't tell whether the IV or the people produced the result.

The fix is **random assignment**: every participant has an equal chance of landing in either group (a coin flip). Randomising doesn't make the groups identical, but it makes them **equivalent on average** — the differences scatter evenly. It is the single feature that turns a comparison into a true experiment.

</Step>

<Step title="Confounds: the lurking spoiler" kind="explain">

A **confound** is any variable *other than the IV* that differs between your groups and could explain the result. Test caffeine in the morning and decaf at night, and "time of day" is now confounded with your treatment — you'll never untangle which one moved the DV.

Two more guards against confounds, especially for expectations:

- **Placebo** — give the control group a fake treatment that's indistinguishable from the real one, so *believing* you got the drug can't masquerade as the drug's effect.
- **Double-blind** — neither the participants *nor* the researchers measuring them know who got the real treatment, shutting down both placebo effects and experimenter expectancy at once.

</Step>

<Step title="Design one yourself" kind="interactive">

Build a small experiment: pick what you'll change, what you'll measure, and how you'll assign people to groups. Some assignment choices quietly smuggle in a confound — watch for the warning, and see what fixes it.

<ExperimentDesigner />

Try the sloppy options on purpose. Letting people choose their own group, or sorting them by a trait, breaks the one rule that gives experiments their power: groups equivalent *before* the manipulation.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- An **experiment** establishes cause by deliberately changing the **independent variable** and measuring the **dependent variable**.
- The **control group** is the no-treatment baseline; compare it with the **experimental group** to isolate the IV's effect.
- **Random assignment** makes groups equivalent on average — the feature that licenses causal claims.
- A **confound** is any other difference between groups that could explain the result; **placebos** and **double-blind** procedures shut out expectation effects.

</Step>
