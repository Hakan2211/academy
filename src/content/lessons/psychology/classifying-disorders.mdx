<Step title="Why classify at all?" kind="explain">

Once you accept that some patterns of thought and behaviour cause real suffering, a practical problem appears: how do clinicians, researchers, and patients all talk about the *same thing*?

If my "depression" and your "depression" mean different things, we can't compare treatments, pool research, or even reliably hand a patient from one doctor to the next. So psychology built a shared **classification system** — a common map and a common vocabulary. The goal isn't to put people in boxes; it's to make sure everyone is describing the same territory.

</Step>

<Step title="The DSM" kind="explain">

The dominant map in much of the world is the **Diagnostic and Statistical Manual of Mental Disorders**, the **DSM**, published by the American Psychiatric Association (the current edition is the DSM-5). A parallel system, the **ICD**, is maintained by the World Health Organization and used widely worldwide.

The DSM does a few key things:

- It groups conditions into **families** — anxiety disorders, mood disorders, trauma-related disorders, and so on.
- For each, it lists **criteria**: specific symptoms, how many must be present, and for how long.
- It is **descriptive**, not theoretical — it says *what a condition looks like*, deliberately staying neutral about what causes it.

This shared standard is why a diagnosis means roughly the same thing across clinics and countries.

</Step>

<Step title="The value of a diagnosis" kind="explain">

A good diagnosis is genuinely useful — sometimes life-changing:

- **Communication.** It lets professionals share information precisely and quickly.
- **Treatment.** It connects a person to the therapies and medications with the best evidence for *their* pattern.
- **Research.** Consistent categories let scientists study causes and test treatments across thousands of people.
- **Relief.** For many people, simply having a *name* for what they're experiencing — and learning they are not alone — is its own kind of help.

A diagnosis, used well, is a doorway to the right support.

</Step>

<Step title="The risks of labelling" kind="explain">

But a label is powerful, and power cuts both ways. Diagnoses carry real risks:

- **Stigma.** A label can attract prejudice and discrimination (we'll devote the final lesson to fighting this).
- **The self-fulfilling label.** Once labelled, a person may be seen — and may see themselves — only through that lens, their every action reinterpreted to fit.
- **Losing the person.** "A schizophrenic" reduces a whole human being to a category. "A person experiencing schizophrenia" keeps the person first. The words matter.
- **False precision.** The map is not the territory. Categories have fuzzy edges and overlap; real distress doesn't always fit neatly in a box.

The aim is to use diagnosis as a tool *for* the person, never as a cage around them.

</Step>

<Step title="The Rosenhan study" kind="explain">

In 1973, psychologist David **Rosenhan** put the risks to a dramatic test. Eight perfectly healthy people ("pseudopatients") went to psychiatric hospitals and each reported a single symptom: hearing a voice say "empty," "hollow," "thud." Otherwise they behaved completely normally.

All eight were admitted — most diagnosed with schizophrenia. Once inside, they acted entirely sane, yet the *label stuck*: staff reinterpreted ordinary behaviour through it (taking notes became "writing behaviour"; pacing from boredom became "anxiety"). They stayed an average of **19 days**, and all were eventually released as having schizophrenia *"in remission"* — never as healthy. Tellingly, it was often the *other patients*, not the staff, who suspected the pseudopatients were faking.

Rosenhan titled his report *"On Being Sane in Insane Places."* His point was sharp: once a label is applied, it can colour everything — a vivid warning about the power of diagnosis. (The study had real flaws and has been criticised since, but its core caution still echoes through psychiatry.)

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- Classification gives clinicians, researchers, and patients a **shared map and vocabulary** so everyone describes the same thing.
- The **DSM** (and the WHO's **ICD**) groups disorders into families and lists symptom **criteria**; it is **descriptive**, staying neutral on causes.
- Diagnosis has real **value** — communication, matched treatment, research, and the relief of a name — but real **risks**: stigma, self-fulfilling labels, reducing a person to a category, and false precision.
- **Rosenhan's** study showed how *sticky* a label can be: once diagnosed, the pseudopatients' normal behaviour was read through the label — a lasting caution about its power.

</Step>
