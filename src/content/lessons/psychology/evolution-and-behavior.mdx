<Step title="Why does the mind work this way?" kind="explain">

Your brain comes pre-loaded with strange, powerful defaults. You find sweet and fatty food delicious. A sudden snake-like shape makes you flinch before you can think. A baby's face melts you. You blush when others judge you. Nobody taught you these — and they are surprisingly **universal** across cultures.

The deepest "why" behind such tendencies is the same engine that shaped your eyes and hands: **evolution by natural selection**. The mind, like the body, was built by survival and reproduction across millions of years. To understand behaviour, it helps to ask not just *how* it works now, but *why such machinery exists at all.*

</Step>

<Step title="The logic of natural selection" kind="explain">

Charles Darwin's idea is almost embarrassingly simple, yet it explains the whole living world. It needs just three ingredients:

- **Variation** — individuals in a population differ (in beak shape, in boldness, in how readily they fear heights).
- **Heredity** — some of that variation is passed to offspring.
- **Selection** — the variants that help an organism survive and reproduce in its environment leave more descendants.

Run that loop for generation after generation and the helpful variants spread. Nobody designs anything; the environment simply does the "choosing". A trait that boosts reproductive success is said to be **adaptive**, and over time the population fills with it.

</Step>

Notice what selection is *not*: it isn't an individual striving or "trying" to adapt. It's just differential survival of variants that already exist. The beetle didn't choose to be dark; the dark ones simply got eaten less.

</Step>

<Step title="From bodies to behaviour" kind="explain">

Here's the leap that founds **evolutionary psychology**: if natural selection shaped hearts and hands, it also shaped *behaviour* — because behaviour, too, affects survival and reproduction.

An ancestor with a strong **fear of snakes** avoided venom and lived to reproduce. One who craved **sugar and fat** survived famine better, when those foods were rare and precious. One who felt **disgust** at rot avoided disease. One who formed tight **social bonds** got protection and help raising children. Those tendencies were selected for — which is why we still carry them, even when (like our sweet tooth in a world of cheap sugar) they now misfire.

<Callout type="info" title="Adaptations can be 'out of date'">
Our minds were tuned to the ancestral world, not the modern one. A craving for sugar was wise on the savannah and unwise beside a vending machine. A jolt of social fear helped in a tribe where exile meant death, but feels excessive before a harmless presentation. The mismatch between our evolved mind and our modern world explains a lot of our struggles.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Proximate vs ultimate causes" kind="explain">

Evolutionary explanations answer a *different question* from biological ones — and the two never compete.

- A **proximate** cause is the immediate mechanism: *right now, this hormone surged, that brain circuit fired.*
- An **ultimate** cause is the evolutionary reason the mechanism exists at all: *because, long ago, individuals with it out-reproduced those without.*

Ask "why do new parents feel a fierce urge to protect their infant?" Proximate answer: oxytocin and dedicated brain circuitry. Ultimate answer: ancestors who felt that urge raised more surviving children. Both are fully true; they're answers to *how* versus *why it's there.*

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Natural selection** needs only **variation**, **heredity**, and differential **selection**; repeated over generations it fills a population with **adaptive** traits — no designer required.
- **Evolutionary psychology** extends this to the mind: tendencies like fearing snakes, craving sugar, feeling disgust, and bonding socially were selected because they aided survival and reproduction.
- Evolved tendencies can be **out of date** — adaptive on the savannah, sometimes maladaptive in the modern world.
- Evolution gives **ultimate** ("why it exists") causes that complement, never compete with, the **proximate** ("how it works now") causes of biology.

</Step>
