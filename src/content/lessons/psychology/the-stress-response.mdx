<Step title="The body's emergency broadcast" kind="explain">

Picture a near-miss: a car swerves toward you and, before you've even *thought*, your heart is hammering and your whole body is primed to move. That instant transformation is the **stress response** — one of the most ancient and reliable systems you own.

Walter Cannon named its core the **fight-or-flight** response: faced with a threat, the body mobilises every resource to either *fight* the danger or *flee* from it. Energy floods to the muscles, the senses sharpen, and anything not needed *right now* — like digesting your lunch — is put on hold. It's not a malfunction. It's a 200-million-year-old survival kit firing exactly as designed.

</Step>

<Step title="The fast track: sympathetic arousal" kind="explain">

The first wave is **electric-fast**, running through the **sympathetic** branch of the autonomic nervous system. Within a second of perceiving threat, it fires the **adrenal medulla** (the inner core of the glands sitting atop your kidneys), which floods the blood with **adrenaline** (epinephrine).

The result is the familiar surge: pounding heart, quick breath, dilated pupils, a jolt of energy. This is sometimes called the **SAM** pathway (sympathetic–adreno–medullary), and its hallmark is *speed*. It gets you ready to act in the time it takes to gasp.

</Step>

<Step title="The slow track: the HPA axis" kind="interactive">

Behind the fast surge runs a slower, more sustained system: the **HPA axis**. It's a chemical relay race down through the body:

1. The **Hypothalamus** detects threat and releases CRH.
2. CRH tells the **Pituitary** gland to release ACTH into the blood.
3. ACTH reaches the **Adrenal cortex** (the outer shell of those same glands), which releases **cortisol** — the body's main stress hormone.

Cortisol keeps blood sugar high and energy mobilised for the longer haul — minutes to hours, not seconds. Trigger the cascade below and watch both tracks light up: the fast fight-or-flight dials and the slow HPA relay.

So the stress response is really *two* systems: a sprinter (adrenaline, instant) and a marathon-runner (cortisol, sustained). Together they carry you through a crisis — and, if the crisis never ends, they're also what start to wear you down.

</Step>

<Step title="Where the hormones come from" kind="interactive">

Both stress chemicals come from your **endocrine system** — the network of glands that broadcast hormones through the bloodstream. The star of the stress show is the small **adrenal** gland perched on each kidney, with its adrenaline-pumping core and its cortisol-pumping shell. Explore the major glands to place it in context.

Notice the chain of command: the brain's **hypothalamus** drives the **pituitary** master gland, which in turn commands the **adrenals**. Your stress response is the brain conducting the body's chemical orchestra.

</Step>

<Step title="Selye's three stages" kind="explain">

Hans Selye discovered something striking: no matter *what* the stressor — heat, cold, injury, fear — the body mounted the **same** general defence. He called it the **General Adaptation Syndrome (GAS)** and split it into three stages:

- **Alarm** — the initial shock and the fight-or-flight mobilisation you just met.
- **Resistance** — the body settles into a costly, sustained state of high defence, coping but burning reserves.
- **Exhaustion** — if the stressor *still* won't quit, the reserves run dry and the body becomes vulnerable to illness.

That last stage is the bridge to the next lesson: it's exactly *how* a survival system, run too long, starts to harm the very body it evolved to protect.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- The **stress response** is an ancient survival system: Cannon's **fight-or-flight** mobilises the body to confront or flee a threat.
- The **fast track** (SAM) is sympathetic arousal firing the **adrenal medulla** to release **adrenaline** — instant surge.
- The **slow track** is the **HPA axis**: **hypothalamus → pituitary → adrenal cortex → cortisol**, a sustained chemical relay.
- Selye's **General Adaptation Syndrome** runs **alarm → resistance → exhaustion**: the same defence to any stressor, which becomes harmful if it never switches off.
