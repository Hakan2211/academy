<Step title="Three faces of the same problem" kind="explain">

It helps to keep three closely related ideas distinct:

- A **stereotype** is a *belief* — a generalised picture of a group ("group X is like that").
- **Prejudice** is an *attitude* — usually a negative feeling toward a group and its members.
- **Discrimination** is a *behaviour* — treating people unequally because of their group.

You can hold a stereotype without acting on it, or feel prejudice you'd be ashamed to admit. But the three feed one another, and together they form one of social psychology's most important — and most hopeful — areas of study. Hopeful, because the same science that explains prejudice also shows how to reduce it.

</Step>

<Step title="Why prejudice forms" kind="explain">

Prejudice isn't a single thing with a single cause. It grows from several roots at once:

- **Social roots** — unequal status breeds prejudice that *justifies* the inequality ("they deserve their lot"). We also absorb the attitudes of our group and culture.
- **Emotional roots** — frustration seeks a target; a **scapegoat** (often a weaker out-group) gets blamed for problems it didn't cause. Fear and threat sharpen "us versus them."
- **Cognitive roots** — prejudice is partly a *by-product of how thinking works*. We categorise the world to simplify it, and categories of people are no exception.

That last root is the deep one — because it means some of the machinery of prejudice runs automatically, in every normal brain.

</Step>

<Step title="Us and them" kind="explain">

The moment we sort people into groups, two biases switch on:

- **In-group bias** — we favour "us": more trust, more warmth, more benefit of the doubt for our own group, even when the groups were assigned moments ago at random.
- **Out-group homogeneity** — "they" all seem alike to us ("you've seen one, you've seen them all"), while we see rich individual variety within our own group.

These appear astonishingly easily. In "minimal group" studies, people split by nothing more than a coin flip or a preference for one painter still favour their own side. The tendency to draw an *in-group / out-group* line seems built into social cognition itself.

</Step>

<Step title="Bias below awareness" kind="explain">

Much prejudice today is not the open hostility of the past but something quieter: **implicit bias** — automatic associations we hold without endorsing them, often contradicting our sincere conscious beliefs. A person genuinely committed to fairness can still carry faster, more positive associations with one group than another, absorbed from a lifetime in a biased culture.

Psychologists probe this with speeded sorting tasks like the **Implicit Association Test**, which times how quickly you pair categories with "good" and "bad." Try a miniature version — then read the caveat carefully.

The honest summary: implicit associations are *real* and worth understanding, but a single noisy reaction-time gap is a weak, unreliable readout of any individual person. Treat it as a window onto how cultural learning shapes automatic thought — not as a verdict on someone's heart.

</Step>

<Step title="Reducing prejudice: the contact hypothesis" kind="explain">

Here is the field's most practical payoff. The **contact hypothesis** holds that bringing groups into contact reduces prejudice — but only under the right conditions. Just mixing people can backfire. Contact works when it is:

- **Equal status** — neither group positioned as superior in the situation,
- **Cooperative** toward a **shared goal** — working *together*, not competing,
- **Supported** by authorities, laws or customs, and
- **Personal enough** to build real acquaintance, puncturing the "they're all alike" illusion.

The classic demonstration: Muzafer Sherif's **Robbers Cave** study turned two warring groups of boys into friends — not by talking, but by forcing them to cooperate on **superordinate goals** (problems neither group could solve alone). Shared tasks dissolve the line that mere proximity only redraws.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- Distinguish **stereotype** (belief), **prejudice** (attitude) and **discrimination** (behaviour) — linked but not identical.
- Prejudice has **social** (status, learning), **emotional** (scapegoating, threat) and **cognitive** (categorisation) roots.
- Sorting people into groups triggers **in-group bias** and **out-group homogeneity**, even with trivially assigned groups.
- **Implicit bias** is automatic association below awareness; the IAT measures it, but single results are noisy and shouldn't label individuals.
- The **contact hypothesis** reduces prejudice under conditions of equal status, cooperation toward **superordinate goals**, support and genuine acquaintance — as in **Robbers Cave**.

</Step>
