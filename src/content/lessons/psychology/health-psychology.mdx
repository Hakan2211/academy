<Step title="More than the absence of disease" kind="explain">

For most of medical history, health meant one thing: the **absence of disease**. Find the germ, fix the broken part, and you're "healthy." This is the **biomedical model**, and for infections and injuries it works brilliantly.

But it leaves out almost everything that drives modern illness. Why does one person recover quickly and another slowly? Why do stress, loneliness and poverty predict who gets sick? Why do so many of today's biggest killers — heart disease, diabetes, many cancers — trace back to *behaviour*: what we eat, whether we move, smoke, sleep, or manage stress? The biomedical model has no good answer. **Health psychology** was born to supply one.

</Step>

<Step title="The biopsychosocial model" kind="interactive">

Health psychology's foundational idea is the **biopsychosocial model**: your health emerges from three interacting domains, not one.

- **Biological** — genes, hormones, immune function, the body's hardware.
- **Psychological** — beliefs, appraisals, coping, personality, habits.
- **Social** — relationships, support, work, culture, access to care.

The model is usually drawn as three overlapping rings, because the action is in the *overlap*. Click each ring to explore what it contributes — then notice the centre, where real health lives.

<BiopsychosocialModel />

The power of the model is the **interaction**. A genetic vulnerability (bio) might stay silent in a supported, low-stress life (social) with optimistic coping (psycho) — and erupt without them. You can't predict health from any single ring. And you can buffer a weakness in one ring by strengthening another.

</Step>

<Step title="Health behaviours: the daily votes" kind="explain">

If behaviour drives so much modern illness, then **health behaviours** — the small, repeated things we do — are where health is mostly won or lost. Not eaten, smoked, moved, slept, or coped: each is a tiny vote, cast daily, for or against your future health.

The frustrating puzzle of health psychology is that *knowing* rarely changes *doing*. Almost everyone knows smoking harms them; knowledge alone barely budges the behaviour. What works better is changing the **environment and the cues** — making the healthy choice the easy, automatic, default choice — and building habits so the behaviour no longer depends on willpower in the moment. (You may recognise this from how we beat procrastination back in the goals-of-psychology lesson.)

</Step>

<Step title="When the stress is the job: burnout" kind="explain">

One chronic stressor deserves its own name because it's so common: **job burnout**. Coined by Christina Maslach, burnout is the syndrome that builds when work stress grinds on with too little recovery or reward. It has three classic signs:

- **Emotional exhaustion** — feeling drained, depleted, running on empty.
- **Cynicism (depersonalisation)** — growing detached, negative, or numb toward the work and the people in it.
- **Reduced efficacy** — a sinking sense that nothing you do makes a difference.

Crucially, burnout is **not** simply a personal failing or weakness. The biopsychosocial lens shows it's a *mismatch* between a person and their work conditions — workload, control, fairness, reward, community. That reframing matters: it points the fix not only at the individual but at the **environment** that produced the strain.

<Callout type="tip" title="The recovery ingredient">
The antidote to chronic work stress isn't only working less — it's *recovering* more. Genuine psychological detachment from work (truly switching off), restful sleep, and activities that replenish you let the stress systems reset. Without recovery, even moderate demands accumulate into burnout.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- The **biomedical model** (health = absence of disease) misses behaviour, mind and context; **health psychology** addresses them.
- The **biopsychosocial model** sees health as the **interaction** of **biological, psychological and social** factors — the action is in the overlap.
- **Health behaviours** are daily votes for future health; changing **environments, cues and habits** beats relying on knowledge or willpower.
- **Job burnout** — emotional **exhaustion**, **cynicism**, reduced **efficacy** — is a person–work *mismatch*, not a personal weakness, and **recovery** (not just less work) is the antidote.

</Step>
