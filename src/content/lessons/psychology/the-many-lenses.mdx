<Step title="A question worth all seven lenses" kind="explain">

Let's put the whole toolkit to work on one hard, real question: **Why are people aggressive?**

It's the perfect test case. Aggression is everywhere — playground shoves, road rage, war — and everyone has a pet theory. *It's just human nature. It's bad parenting. It's violent video games. It's testosterone.* Each of those is actually a different **perspective** in disguise, and each captures something real. The mistake is thinking any one of them is the whole answer.

By the end of this lesson you'll see why the best science treats these not as rivals but as **levels of a single explanation**.

</Step>

<Step title="Levels of analysis" kind="explain">

The key idea that dissolves the "which theory is right?" argument is **levels of analysis**. A single act of aggression can be explained at many zoom levels at once, like asking "why is the kettle boiling?":

- *Because the water molecules have enough kinetic energy* (a low-level, mechanistic answer).
- *Because I switched it on to make tea* (a high-level, purpose answer).

Both are completely true; they're just different levels. Likewise, a punch can be explained by **neurons firing** (biological), by a **lifetime of rewarded aggression** (behaviourist), by a **hostile interpretation** of a glance (cognitive), and by **cultural norms** about defending honour (sociocultural) — all simultaneously.

</Step>

<Step title="From proximate to ultimate" kind="explain">

Two of the lenses deserve special pairing. The **biological** perspective gives a *proximate* cause — the immediate machinery: this hormone, that brain region, right now. The **evolutionary** perspective gives an *ultimate* cause — why that machinery exists at all: because, in the ancestral past, individuals who could aggress to defend resources, mates and offspring left more descendants.

Neither replaces the other. "Testosterone surged" and "the capacity for aggression was naturally selected" are answers to *different questions* — *how* versus *why it's there in the first place.*

<Callout type="info" title="Reductionism's limit">
You could insist everything "really" reduces to physics. But explaining a war purely in terms of neurons would be useless — like explaining a novel by listing ink molecules. Higher levels (culture, meaning, learning) carry real explanatory power that lower levels can't replace.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="The biopsychosocial synthesis" kind="explain">

Modern psychology folds the lenses into one integrated framework, often called the **biopsychosocial model**:

- **Bio** — genes, hormones, brain circuits, evolved tendencies.
- **Psycho** — learning history, beliefs, interpretations, emotions, personality.
- **Social** — family, peers, culture, situation, norms.

Real behaviour emerges from these three **interacting**, not adding up independently. A genetic tendency toward impulsivity (bio) might never become violent in a calm, supportive environment (social) but might in a harsh one — and how the person *interprets* events (psycho) tips the balance either way. You'll meet this exact model again when we study stress, health and psychological disorders.

</Step>

<Step title="Why this protects you from bad arguments" kind="explain">

This integrated view is a practical thinking tool, not just theory. It inoculates you against two common errors:

**The single-cause trap.** "Video games cause violence" or "it's all genetics" — any one-factor headline about human behaviour is almost certainly oversimplified. Behaviour is overdetermined: many causes at many levels.

**The nature-*or*-nurture trap.** Asking whether aggression is "born or made" is like asking whether a rectangle's area is due to its length *or* its width. The honest answer is always **both, interacting** — and the interesting question is *how*.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- The "which perspective is right?" debate dissolves once you see them as **levels of analysis** of one behaviour, all true at once.
- **Biological** gives *proximate* (immediate mechanism) causes; **evolutionary** gives *ultimate* (why-it-exists) causes — different questions, not rivals.
- The **biopsychosocial model** integrates the lenses: **bio × psycho × social**, interacting, produce behaviour.
- This guards against the **single-cause** and **nature-*or*-nurture** traps — human behaviour is **overdetermined** and always an interaction.
- You'll reuse this integrated framework across stress, disorders and therapy later on the island.

</Step>
