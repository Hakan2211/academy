<Step title="Chemistry that rewrites experience" kind="explain">

A few milligrams of a molecule can transform consciousness — slowing the world to a crawl, speeding it to a blur, painting the air with colour, or blanketing everything in warm relief. **Psychoactive drugs** are substances that cross into the brain and alter perception, mood, thinking or behaviour. They are the most dramatic everyday demonstration that your mind is, at bottom, chemistry.

To understand any of them, you need one idea from biology: the **synapse**, the tiny gap where one neuron signals the next using chemical messengers called **neurotransmitters**. Nearly every drug works by hijacking this hand-off. Get the synapse, and the whole zoo of drug effects clicks into place.

</Step>

<Step title="How drugs hijack the synapse" kind="interactive">

At a normal synapse, the sending neuron releases neurotransmitter molecules; they drift across the gap, briefly bind receptors on the receiving neuron, and are then pumped back for reuse (**reuptake**). Drugs intervene at exactly these steps. Switch the **drug mode** below and watch what changes.

<SynapseViz />

The four modes map onto whole classes of drugs:

- **Normal** — the baseline hand-off, with reuptake clearing the gap.
- **Reuptake blocker** — the pump is jammed, so the messenger lingers and keeps signalling. **Cocaine** does this to dopamine; **SSRI antidepressants** do it to serotonin.
- **Agonist** — the drug *mimics* a neurotransmitter and binds the receptor itself, boosting or faking the signal. Nicotine, opioids and many others act this way.
- **Antagonist** — the drug *plugs* receptors so the real neurotransmitter can't bind, blocking the signal — the basis of many antipsychotics and antidotes.

One synapse, four levers — and most psychoactive effects come from pulling one of them.

</Step>

<Step title="The four families" kind="explain">

Group the drugs by what they do to the nervous system and the landscape simplifies into four families:

- **Depressants** *slow* the central nervous system — alcohol, sedatives, and benzodiazepines. They boost the brain's main brake, **GABA**, producing relaxation, slurred coordination, and at high doses dangerously suppressed breathing.
- **Stimulants** *speed it up* — caffeine, nicotine, cocaine, amphetamines. They raise dopamine and noradrenaline, bringing energy, alertness and euphoria, followed by a crash.
- **Hallucinogens** *distort perception* — LSD, psilocybin, and (with its own profile) cannabis. They scramble normal sensory processing, often by acting on serotonin systems, producing altered perception and sometimes profound shifts in thought.
- **Opioids** *kill pain and flood reward* — heroin, morphine, prescription painkillers. They mimic the body's own endorphins (agonists at opioid receptors), producing intense relief and euphoria — and steep, fast-building dependence.

</Step>

<Step title="The reward pathway — the common thread" kind="interactive">

Different families pull different levers, yet addictive drugs share one final target: the brain's **dopamine reward pathway**. Dopamine surges from the **VTA** to the **nucleus accumbens** and on to the **prefrontal cortex** — the circuit that normally tags food, sex and achievement as "do that again." Addictive drugs flood this circuit far beyond anything natural, screaming "this matters most of all."

Drag the **uses** slider and watch the catch. The brain fights back against the flood by **down-regulating** — making fewer receptors and less dopamine. So the same dose produces a smaller and smaller response. We can even sketch the shape of that decline:

That fading curve is **tolerance** — and it sets a cruel trap, which the next step unpacks.

</Step>

<Step title="Tolerance, dependence, addiction" kind="explain">

These three words are often muddled, but they name three distinct things:

- **Tolerance** — the body adapts, so you need *more* drug to get the same effect (the fading curve you just saw). It pushes the dose ever upward.
- **Dependence** — the body has adapted so thoroughly that removing the drug causes **withdrawal**: the opposite of the drug's effects (a depressant's withdrawal brings anxiety and tremor; a stimulant's brings crushing fatigue and low mood). The person now needs the drug just to feel *normal*.
- **Addiction** — compulsive seeking and use *despite harm*, with loss of control. The reward circuit has been hijacked so the drug feels more important than food, relationships or safety.

The escalation has a grim engine. Tolerance demands higher doses; the body becomes dependent; withdrawal punishes any attempt to stop; and the hijacked reward pathway makes the drug feel essential. Notice that the **prefrontal cortex** — the very region for planning and self-control — is part of the damaged circuit, which is why "just use willpower" so badly underestimates addiction.

<Callout type="warn" title="Why willpower isn't enough">
Addiction physically remodels the brain's motivation and control systems. Treating it as a simple moral failure ignores the biology — which is why effective treatment combines medical, psychological and social support, not shame.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Psychoactive drugs** alter consciousness by hijacking the **synapse**, acting as **reuptake blockers** (e.g. cocaine on dopamine, SSRIs on serotonin), **agonists** (mimicking a neurotransmitter, e.g. opioids), or **antagonists** (blocking receptors).
- The four families: **depressants** (slow the CNS via GABA — alcohol, sedatives), **stimulants** (speed it up via dopamine/noradrenaline — caffeine, cocaine), **hallucinogens** (distort perception — LSD, psilocybin), and **opioids** (pain relief + reward — heroin, morphine).
- Addictive drugs converge on the **dopamine reward pathway** (**VTA → nucleus accumbens → prefrontal cortex**), flooding it far beyond natural rewards.
- The brain **down-regulates**, producing **tolerance** (need more for the same effect), which feeds **dependence** (withdrawal without the drug) and **addiction** (compulsive use despite harm) — a biological trap, not mere weak will.

</Step>
