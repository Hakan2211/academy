<Step title="A different kind of learning" kind="explain">

Classical conditioning explains how we learn to *anticipate* events — the bell predicts food. But it can't explain how a dog learns to *roll over* for a treat, or how you learned to study for good grades. Those behaviours aren't reflexes triggered by a signal; they're **voluntary actions you do because of what happened last time you did them.**

That's **operant conditioning**: learning in which the **consequences** of a behaviour make it more or less likely to happen again. Classical conditioning is about associating two *stimuli*; operant conditioning is about associating a *behaviour* with its *outcome*.

</Step>

<Step title="Thorndike's law of effect" kind="explain">

The groundwork came from **Edward Thorndike**, who put cats in "puzzle boxes" they could escape by pressing a lever. At first a cat clawed and squirmed randomly; eventually it hit the lever by accident and got out (to reach food). Put back in, it escaped a little faster each time, until it pressed the lever immediately.

Thorndike summed this up as the **law of effect**: behaviours followed by **satisfying** consequences are *strengthened* (more likely to recur), and behaviours followed by **unpleasant** consequences are *weakened*. Consequences select behaviours, much as the environment selects traits in evolution.

</Step>

<Step title="Skinner and the operant box" kind="explain">

**B. F. Skinner** turned the law of effect into a precise science. He built the **operant chamber** — the "**Skinner box**" — a controlled cage with a lever (or key) and a mechanism to deliver food or a mild shock, plus a recorder tracking every response.

Skinner's key terms:

- An **operant** is a behaviour that "operates" on the environment to produce a consequence (pressing the lever).
- A **reinforcer** is any consequence that **increases** the behaviour it follows.
- A **punisher** is any consequence that **decreases** the behaviour it follows.

Crucially, these are defined *by their effect*: if a consequence makes a behaviour more frequent, it's a reinforcer — full stop.

</Step>

<Step title="The 2×2 that organises everything" kind="explain">

Here's the part students most often mangle, so go slow. Two independent questions define four outcomes:

1. Are we **adding** or **removing** a stimulus? That's what **positive** / **negative** mean here — *add* or *remove*, **not** good or bad.
2. Does the behaviour go **up** or **down**? *Up* = **reinforcement**; *down* = **punishment**.

Combine them and you get four cells: **positive reinforcement** (add something pleasant), **negative reinforcement** (remove something aversive), **positive punishment** (add something aversive), and **negative punishment** (remove something pleasant). The two reinforcements *strengthen*; the two punishments *weaken*.

</Step>

<Step title="Work the 2×2" kind="interactive">

Click through each cell. Pay special attention to **negative reinforcement** — the one almost everyone confuses with punishment. Removing something unpleasant (silencing that seatbelt beep) *rewards* the behaviour, so it goes *up*.

Burn in the mantra: **positive = add, negative = remove; reinforcement = more behaviour, punishment = less.** Every operant consequence is one combination of those two ideas.

</Step>

<Step title="Try it in the box" kind="interactive">

Now run a Skinner box. Choose what follows each lever press and press it several times. Watch the response rate climb under reinforcement and fall under punishment.

<OperantBox />

Notice that both *giving a treat* and *stopping a shock* drive the rate up — both are reinforcement — while *giving a shock* and *taking the treat* drive it down. The direction of the rate tells you, exactly as Skinner insisted, whether a consequence was reinforcing or punishing.

</Step>

<Step title="A note on punishment" kind="explain">

Punishment can suppress behaviour, but psychologists generally favour reinforcement, for good reasons:

- Punishment teaches what *not* to do, not what *to* do instead.
- It often produces fear, anger, or avoidance of the punisher rather than genuine learning.
- Its effects are frequently temporary — the behaviour returns when the punisher is absent.

A more effective recipe: **reinforce the desired alternative** while withholding reinforcement from the unwanted behaviour.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Operant conditioning** = learning driven by the **consequences** of voluntary behaviour (vs. classical conditioning's stimulus–stimulus links).
- **Thorndike's law of effect**: satisfying consequences strengthen a behaviour; unpleasant ones weaken it.
- **Skinner** formalised it with the **operant chamber**; a **reinforcer** increases behaviour and a **punisher** decreases it, *defined by their effect*.
- The **2×2**: positive = *add*, negative = *remove*; reinforcement = *more*, punishment = *less* → four consequences.
- **Reinforcement is usually preferable to punishment**, which suppresses rather than teaches and often backfires.

</Step>
