<Step title="The mind's two systems" kind="explain">

Every day you make thousands of judgments under uncertainty — *Is this safe? How likely is that? Which choice is better?* — and almost all of them happen in a flash, without conscious calculation.

A useful picture, made famous by Daniel Kahneman, is **two systems**:

- **System 1** is fast, automatic, effortless and emotional. It jumps to conclusions. ("That noise is a threat!")
- **System 2** is slow, deliberate, effortful and logical. It checks the maths. ("Actually, let's compute the odds.")

System 1 runs the show, because it's cheap and usually right. To work so fast, it relies on **heuristics** — mental shortcuts. The shortcuts are brilliant most of the time, which is exactly why their predictable failures are so surprising. Let's catch two of them red-handed.

</Step>

<Step title="Two heuristics, two traps" kind="interactive">

Below are two quick challenges. Answer each with your gut *before* revealing the explanation — the point is to catch your own System 1 in the act.

<HeuristicsLab />

If you ranked "bank teller *and* feminist" above "bank teller", you fell for the **representativeness** heuristic. If you thought sharks out-kill vending machines, that's the **availability** heuristic. Don't feel bad — these trip up the overwhelming majority of people, including statisticians. Now let's name exactly what happened.

</Step>

<Step title="Representativeness" kind="explain">

The **representativeness heuristic** judges probability by *resemblance*: how much does this case look like the typical example? Linda *sounds like* a feminist, so "feminist bank teller" feels more probable than plain "bank teller" — even though, logically, a subset can never be larger than the whole (the **conjunction fallacy**).

The same shortcut produces **base-rate neglect**. Told that "Steve is shy, withdrawn and detail-loving", people guess he's a librarian rather than a farmer — ignoring that there are *vastly* more farmers than librarians. The vivid stereotype (representativeness) drowns out the cold background frequency (the base rate). We forget to ask: *how common is each option in the first place?*

<Callout type="info" title="Base rates matter most when they're extreme">
If a disease affects 1 in 10,000 people, even a "99% accurate" test will flag far more healthy people than sick ones — because the base rate is tiny. Ignoring base rates here can be genuinely dangerous.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Availability" kind="explain">

The **availability heuristic** judges how *common* or *likely* something is by how *easily examples come to mind*. Things that are vivid, recent, emotional or heavily reported feel more frequent than they are.

That's why people fear shark attacks and plane crashes (rare, but unforgettable) more than car crashes and heart disease (common, but mundane). After seeing a news story about a lottery winner, the jackpot suddenly feels winnable. Whatever floods the memory feels probable — regardless of the real statistics.

Availability and representativeness are System 1's workhorses. They're not stupidity; they're *efficient approximations* that usually serve us well. The skill is knowing the specific situations — conjunctions, base rates, vivid rare events — where the approximation breaks, and deliberately calling in System 2.

</Step>

<Step title="Why this matters" kind="explain">

These aren't parlour tricks. The same shortcuts steer doctors estimating diagnoses, jurors weighing evidence, investors judging risk, and you deciding whether your neighbourhood is "dangerous". Whole industries — insurance, advertising, politics — are built on knowing how your gut estimates probability.

You can't switch the heuristics off; they're built in. But you *can* learn to recognise the warning signs — "this case fits a stereotype perfectly", "this example is unusually vivid" — and pause to ask the slow questions: *What are the actual base rates? Is a vivid memory inflating this? Can a part really be bigger than the whole?* That pause is System 2, and it's the most valuable thinking habit this world can give you.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- Judgment under uncertainty runs mostly on **System 1** (fast, automatic) using **heuristics**, with **System 2** (slow, logical) as backup.
- The **representativeness heuristic** judges probability by resemblance, producing the **conjunction fallacy** and **base-rate neglect** (ignoring how common each option really is).
- The **availability heuristic** judges frequency by how easily examples spring to mind, so vivid or recent events feel more likely than they are.
- These shortcuts are efficient and usually right — the skill is spotting the situations where they predictably fail and switching on **System 2**.

</Step>
