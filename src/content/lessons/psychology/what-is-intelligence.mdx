<Step title="The hardest word to define" kind="explain">

You know it when you see it. The friend who fixes anything, the classmate who grasps a new idea in seconds, the grandmother who always knows the right thing to say — we call them all **intelligent**. But press psychologists for a definition and you'll start an argument that has run for over a century.

A workable starting point: **intelligence is the capacity to learn from experience, reason well, and adapt to new situations.** Notice what that *doesn't* say. It isn't a pile of facts you've memorised, and it isn't a single talent. It's something more general — a kind of mental horsepower you can point at almost any problem.

The deep question is whether that horsepower is really *one* thing, or many.

</Step>

<Step title="Spearman's big discovery: g" kind="explain">

In 1904 Charles Spearman noticed something strange. Children who did well on one mental test — say, vocabulary — *also* tended to do well on completely unrelated ones, like spotting patterns or judging weights. Good performances clustered together. Statisticians call this the **positive manifold**.

Spearman reasoned that a single underlying ability must be feeding all of them. He named it **general intelligence**, or **_g_**. On this view every test taps two things: *g* (the shared mental power) plus some skill specific to that task.

<Callout type="info" title="Why g matters">
*g* isn't just a tidy idea. It's one of the most reliable findings in psychology, and it predicts real outcomes — school grades, job performance, even health — better than almost any other single measure. Whatever it is, it's measuring *something* real.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Two flavours of intelligence" kind="explain">

Later psychologists split that mental horsepower into two kinds that behave very differently over a lifetime:

- **Fluid intelligence** — your raw, on-the-spot reasoning power: solving a novel puzzle, spotting a pattern you've never seen, thinking fast. It needs no prior knowledge. It *peaks in early adulthood and slowly declines.*
- **Crystallised intelligence** — the accumulated store of knowledge, vocabulary and skills you've built up. It's what experience buys you. It *keeps growing well into old age.*

This is why a sharp 20-year-old might solve a brand-new brain-teaser faster, while a 60-year-old expert runs circles around them in their own field. Different intelligences, ageing in opposite directions.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Intelligence** is best defined as the capacity to **learn from experience, reason, and adapt** — not a store of facts or a single talent.
- Spearman found a **positive manifold** (people good at one mental task tend to be good at others) and proposed a single **general intelligence, _g_**, underlying them all.
- *g* is remarkably real: it predicts school, job and life outcomes better than almost any other measure.
- Mental power comes in two ageing-opposite kinds: **fluid** (raw reasoning, peaks young) and **crystallised** (accumulated knowledge, keeps growing).
- The central tension — **one intelligence or many?** — sets up the rival theories you'll meet next.

</Step>
