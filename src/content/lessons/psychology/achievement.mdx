<Step title="The drive to do well" kind="explain">

Some motivations aren't about the body at all. The pull to master a challenge, to meet a high standard, to *succeed* — psychologists call this the **need for achievement** (often written *n-Ach*). It varies a lot from person to person, and it predicts real outcomes: people high in achievement motivation tend to persist longer and seek out demanding goals.

But here's the subtle part. People high in n-Ach don't chase the *hardest* possible tasks, and they don't pick trivially easy ones either. They gravitate toward **moderately challenging** goals — difficult enough to be meaningful, achievable enough that effort actually matters. A task that's certain to succeed teaches nothing; one that's certain to fail can't be earned. The sweet spot is where skill and effort make the difference.

</Step>

<Step title="The story we tell about why" kind="explain">

What truly shapes whether we keep trying isn't just the outcome — it's the **story we tell about why** the outcome happened. This is **attribution**: the explanation we attach to a success or failure.

Imagine you fail an exam. You could explain it as *"I'm just not smart"* or *"I didn't study enough this time"* or *"that test was unfair"* or *"I had an off day."* The event is identical. But these explanations point you in wildly different directions — toward giving up, or toward trying again. Attribution theory says it's the *explanation*, not the *event*, that drives what you do next.

</Step>

<Step title="Two dimensions of explanation" kind="explain">

Bernard Weiner's attribution theory sorts our explanations along two key dimensions:

- **Locus** — is the cause **internal** (something about me: ability, effort) or **external** (something about the situation: luck, task difficulty, other people)?
- **Stability** — is the cause **stable** (lasting, unlikely to change: my fixed ability, a consistently hard subject) or **unstable** (temporary, changeable: how hard I tried this time, a one-off distraction)?

Cross these two and you get a **2×2** of four basic kinds of explanation. The combination you reach for — especially the *stability* part — has a powerful effect on motivation. Attributing failure to something *stable* ("I'm just bad at this") breeds helplessness; attributing it to something *unstable* ("I didn't prepare") keeps hope alive.

</Step>

<Step title="Optimistic and pessimistic explanatory styles" kind="explain">

Do this consistently and it becomes your **explanatory style** — a habitual way of explaining events.

- An **optimistic** style credits good events to internal, stable causes ("I'm good at this, and that won't change") and blames bad events on external, unstable, *specific* causes ("that one test was unfair"). It protects motivation and bounces back from setbacks.
- A **pessimistic** style does the opposite: bad events feel internal, stable and global ("I'm a failure at everything"), while good events feel like flukes. Over time this style is linked to giving up easily — and, in the extreme, to **learned helplessness** and depression.

<Callout type="warn" title="Watch the word 'always'">
The danger sign is *stable, global* language about failure: "I **always** mess this up," "I'm just **not a** maths person." These turn a single event into a permanent verdict. Reframing toward the unstable and specific — "I didn't prepare *this time*" — is one of the most practical motivation tools there is. You'll meet this again in Positive Psychology.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- The **need for achievement** drives us toward **moderately challenging** goals — where skill and effort genuinely decide the outcome.
- **Attribution** is the explanation we give for success or failure; it shapes motivation more than the outcome itself.
- Weiner's two dimensions: **locus** (internal vs external) and **stability** (stable vs unstable), forming a **2×2** of explanations.
- A persistent pattern becomes your **explanatory style**: an **optimistic** style (good = internal/stable, bad = external/unstable/specific) protects motivation; a **pessimistic** one breeds helplessness.

</Step>
