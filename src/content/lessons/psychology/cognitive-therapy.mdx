<Step title="It's not the event — it's how you read it" kind="explain">

Two people get the same curt email from their boss. One shrugs: *busy day, no big deal.* The other spirals: *I'm about to be fired, I'm a failure.* Same event, opposite emotions. The difference lives entirely in the **interpretation**.

That's the founding insight of **cognitive therapy**: our emotions and behaviours flow not from events themselves but from the **thoughts and beliefs** we hold about them. Distorted, irrational thinking produces distorted, painful feelings. Fix the thinking, and the feeling follows. The ancient Stoic Epictetus said it first — "people are disturbed not by things, but by the views they take of them" — but two clinicians turned it into a method.

</Step>

<Step title="Ellis and the ABC model" kind="explain">

**Albert Ellis** built **Rational Emotive Behaviour Therapy (REBT)** around a simple, powerful sequence — the **ABC model**:

- **A — Activating event.** Something happens.
- **B — Belief.** You hold a belief *about* what happened.
- **C — Consequence.** That belief produces an emotional and behavioural consequence.

The naive view assumes A causes C directly. Ellis insisted that **B sits in the middle** and does the real work. The villain is the **irrational belief** — rigid demands ("I *must* be approved of by everyone"), catastrophising ("this is unbearable"), and all-or-nothing thinking. REBT's job is to vigorously **dispute** those beliefs and replace them with rational ones.

</Step>

<Step title="Flip the belief, change the feeling" kind="interactive">

See the ABC engine for yourself. The activating event stays *fixed*. Toggle the belief in the middle between an irrational version and a disputed, rational one — and watch the emotional consequence transform.

The event never moved. Only B changed — and C changed with it. That's the whole game: you can't always control what happens to you, but you can learn to dispute the toxic beliefs you bolt onto it.

</Step>

<Step title="Beck and cognitive therapy" kind="explain">

Around the same time, psychiatrist **Aaron Beck** arrived at a similar place from treating depression. He noticed depressed clients were flooded with **automatic negative thoughts** — quick, believable, distorted appraisals that popped up unbidden: *I'm worthless, nothing will work out, it's all my fault.*

Beck mapped the **cognitive distortions** behind them — overgeneralisation, catastrophising, all-or-nothing thinking, mental filtering (noticing only the bad) — and at the deepest layer, harsh **core beliefs** ("I am unlovable"). His **cognitive therapy** is gentler than Ellis's confrontation: it treats thoughts as **hypotheses to be tested**, guiding clients to examine the evidence like a fair-minded scientist. This testing-and-rebuilding is called **cognitive restructuring**.

</Step>

<Step title="Run a thought record" kind="interactive">

The everyday tool of cognitive therapy is the **thought record**. Catch a hot automatic thought, then weigh the evidence for and against it — fairly, not as a prosecutor — and build a more **balanced thought**. Pick a thought, tick the evidence you find convincing, then write the reframe and watch the mood rating ease.

<CBTThoughtRecord />

Crucially, the *facts* didn't change — only the reading of them did. That's cognitive restructuring in miniature: not forced positivity, but a fairer, more accurate appraisal.

</Step>

<Step title="CBT: the great integration" kind="explain">

If behaviour therapy says *change what you do* and cognitive therapy says *change how you think*, **Cognitive Behavioural Therapy (CBT)** says: **do both**. It's the marriage of the two traditions, and today the most widely practised and most heavily researched approach in the world.

A typical CBT course is **structured, present-focused and time-limited** (often around 12–20 sessions). It pairs cognitive work — spotting distortions, restructuring thoughts — with behavioural work, especially **behavioural experiments** (going out and testing a feared prediction in reality) and exposure. You also get **homework**: thought records, activity scheduling, practising new skills between sessions. The result is a practical, skills-based therapy with strong evidence across depression, anxiety, OCD and much more.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Cognitive therapy** holds that distorted **thoughts and beliefs**, not events themselves, drive painful emotions — so changing the thinking changes the feeling.
- **Ellis's REBT** uses the **ABC model** (Activating event → Belief → Consequence) and vigorously **disputes irrational beliefs**.
- **Beck's cognitive therapy** targets **automatic negative thoughts** and **cognitive distortions**, treating thoughts as hypotheses to test — the work of **cognitive restructuring** (e.g. a thought record).
- **CBT** integrates cognitive and behavioural methods (thought records *plus* behavioural experiments and homework). It's structured, time-limited, and the most-researched, most-used therapy today.

</Step>
