<Step title="From a pile of numbers to a story" kind="explain">

Run a study and you end up holding a heap of raw numbers — hundreds of scores that mean nothing at a glance. **Descriptive statistics** are the tools that compress that heap into a few honest summaries: a typical value, and a sense of how spread out the data are. They *describe* your sample; they don't yet draw conclusions beyond it (that's the next lesson).

Two questions do most of the work: **where is the center?** and **how spread out is it?**

</Step>

<Step title="Measures of center" kind="explain">

Three numbers answer "what's typical," and they don't always agree:

- **Mean** — the arithmetic average: add everything, divide by how many. Uses every value, but gets dragged around by extremes.
- **Median** — the middle value when the data are lined up in order. Half fall below, half above. It shrugs off outliers.
- **Mode** — the most frequent value. The only one that works for categories (the most common eye color), and the only one that can name several "peaks."

When a few extreme values pull the mean far from the median, that gap is itself a signal — the data are **skewed**.

</Step>

<Step title="Measures of spread" kind="explain">

A center alone can mislead. Two classes can both average 70%, yet one is all 68–72 while the other is half 40s and half 90s. **Spread** captures that difference:

- **Range** — simply the highest value minus the lowest. Quick, but hostage to a single outlier.
- **Variance** — the average *squared* distance of each value from the mean.
- **Standard deviation (SD)** — the square root of the variance, which pulls the answer back into the original units. It's the workhorse: the *typical* distance a value sits from the mean. Small SD = tightly clustered; large SD = widely scattered.

</Step>

<Step title="Shape: the normal curve and skew" kind="explain">

Plot enough naturally varying measurements — heights, reaction times, test scores — and they tend to pile into a symmetric, bell-shaped **normal distribution**: most values clump near the middle, tapering off evenly on both sides. In a perfect bell, the mean, median, and mode all sit together at the peak.

When the data are **skewed**, a tail stretches out to one side, dragging the mean toward the tail and away from the median. The bell below is the symmetric, no-skew baseline — the shape against which we judge whether real data lean.

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Descriptive statistics** summarise a sample's center and spread without yet generalising beyond it.
- **Center:** the **mean** (average, sensitive to outliers), the **median** (middle, outlier-resistant), and the **mode** (most frequent).
- **Spread:** the **range**, the **variance**, and especially the **standard deviation** — the typical distance from the mean, in the original units.
- A symmetric **normal distribution** puts mean, median, and mode together; **skew** stretches a tail and separates the mean from the median.

</Step>
