<Step title="Bending to the group" kind="explain">

**Conformity** is adjusting your behaviour or thinking to match a group standard. It's everywhere and mostly invisible: you clap when others clap, lower your voice in a library, dress roughly like your peers. Most of it is harmless social glue.

But how far does it go? Would you deny the evidence of your own eyes just because everyone around you did? In the early 1950s, Solomon **Asch** built an experiment to find out — and the answer rattled psychology.

</Step>

<Step title="Asch's lines" kind="explain">

The task could not be simpler. You see a reference line and three comparison lines; you say aloud which one matches. The right answer is obvious — people working alone get it right 99% of the time.

The trick: you're in a room with several other "participants" who are secretly **confederates** working for the experimenter. On certain rounds, every one of them confidently announces the *same wrong answer* before it's your turn. Now your own eyes say one thing and the unanimous group says another. What do you do?

</Step>

<Step title="Your turn at the table" kind="interactive">

Sit in the participant's chair. The match is plain — but the whole group just said otherwise.

<AschConformity />

Asch found that about **a third** of responses went along with the obviously wrong majority, and roughly **75%** of people conformed on at least one trial. People knew the answer and gave the wrong one anyway — some to avoid standing out, some genuinely doubting their own perception.

</Step>

<Step title="Two reasons we cave" kind="explain">

Why conform when you can *see* the truth? Two distinct pressures, and it helps to keep them apart:

- **Normative influence** — conforming to be *liked and accepted*, to avoid the discomfort of being the odd one out. You may not even believe the group; you just don't want to be the lone dissenter.
- **Informational influence** — conforming because you assume the group *knows something you don't*. When you're unsure, other people are useful evidence — so you defer to the crowd's apparent wisdom.

Asch's line task is mostly *normative* (the answer is obvious, so you cave to fit in). When a situation is genuinely ambiguous, *informational* influence dominates.

</Step>

<Step title="What turns the pressure up — and off" kind="explain">

Asch and others mapped the dials that control conformity:

- **Group size** — pressure climbs sharply from one to about three or four people, then largely plateaus. You don't need a mob; a small unanimous group is nearly as powerful as a large one.
- **Unanimity** — this is the big one. A *single* ally who breaks ranks — even one who gives a *different* wrong answer — shatters the effect, dropping conformity dramatically. It's the *unanimity* that crushes you, not the numbers. (Toggle the ally in the demo above and feel it.)
- **Commitment and culture** — writing your answer privately, or committing to it first, frees you; collectivist cultures tend to show somewhat higher conformity than individualist ones.

</Step>

<Step title="When agreement goes toxic: groupthink" kind="explain">

Conformity has a dangerous big sibling. **Groupthink** is what happens when a tight, like-minded group prizes harmony over honest analysis. Members self-censor doubts, the group feels invulnerable, dissent is quietly punished, and the illusion of unanimity hardens into a bad decision nobody actually examined.

It has been blamed for political and corporate disasters alike. The fix is built straight from Asch's findings: deliberately invite dissent, assign a **devil's advocate**, gather views privately before discussing, and welcome outside critics. Manufacture the one dissenting ally on purpose — because unanimity, real or fake, is what does the damage.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Conformity** is adjusting behaviour or belief toward a group standard.
- **Asch's line experiment**: ~1/3 of responses, and ~75% of people at least once, matched a unanimous wrong majority against the evidence of their own eyes.
- **Normative influence** = conform to be accepted; **informational influence** = conform because you assume others know better.
- Conformity rises with **group size** up to ~3–4 then plateaus; **unanimity** is decisive — a single ally breaks the spell.
- **Groupthink** is conformity inside decision-making groups; the cure is engineered dissent — devil's advocates, private opinions, outside critics.

</Step>
