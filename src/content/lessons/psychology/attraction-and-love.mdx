<Step title="The science of liking" kind="explain">

Why do we like the people we like? It feels like fate or chemistry — but psychology has found that attraction follows surprisingly mundane, reliable rules. Most of who you end up close to is predicted not by destiny, but by who happens to be *near* you, *like* you, and *who likes you back.*

Three ingredients do most of the early work: **proximity**, **similarity** and **reciprocity** — with **physical attractiveness** quietly tilting first impressions.

</Step>

<Step title="Proximity: nearness breeds liking" kind="explain">

The single best predictor of who becomes your friend or partner is **proximity** — sheer physical (and now digital) closeness. We befriend the people next door, the desk neighbour, the regular at the same café.

The engine is the **mere-exposure effect**: repeated exposure to something — a face, a tune, a word — makes us like it more, simply because familiarity feels safe. Proximity multiplies exposure, exposure breeds comfort, and comfort warms into liking. Romance, it turns out, often begins with logistics.

</Step>

<Step title="Similarity and reciprocity" kind="explain">

Two more rules:

- **Similarity** — despite the proverb that "opposites attract," the evidence overwhelmingly favours *birds of a feather*. We're drawn to people who share our attitudes, values, interests and background. Similarity is validating (they confirm our view of the world) and friction-free, and it predicts lasting relationships far better than complementary differences do.
- **Reciprocity** — we tend to like those who like *us*. Learning that someone is fond of us makes us warm to them in turn. Liking is contagious; expressed affection invites affection back.

</Step>

<Step title="The pull of a pretty face" kind="explain">

We'd like to think looks don't matter much. They do — especially at first. **Physical attractiveness** strongly shapes initial attraction, and it triggers the **halo effect**: we assume good-looking people are also kinder, smarter and more competent ("what is beautiful is good"). It isn't true, but the bias is powerful and automatic.

The good news: as we get to know someone, *who they are* increasingly outweighs how they look — and people we love come to *look* more attractive to us. Attractiveness opens the door; the rest of the list keeps it open.

</Step>

<Step title="What is love? Sternberg's triangle" kind="interactive">

Liking and loving aren't the same, and not all love is alike. Robert **Sternberg's triangular theory of love** breaks love into three ingredients:

- **Intimacy** — closeness, warmth, connection, sharing.
- **Passion** — romance, physical attraction, intense longing.
- **Commitment** — the decision to stay and to maintain the bond.

Mix them in different proportions and you get different *kinds* of love. Slide the three dials and watch the love change names.

<LoveTriangle />

Intimacy with passion but no commitment is **romantic love**; intimacy and commitment without the spark is **companionate love** (the deep, steady bond of long marriages and old friendships); all three together is **consummate love** — complete, and famously hard to sustain. Passion tends to *cool* over time while intimacy and commitment can *deepen*, which is why many lasting relationships mature from romantic into companionate love.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- Attraction follows reliable rules: **proximity** (via the **mere-exposure effect**), **similarity**, and **reciprocity** do most of the early work.
- **Physical attractiveness** drives first impressions and triggers the **halo effect** ("beautiful = good"), but matters less as we truly know someone.
- **Sternberg's triangular theory** builds love from **intimacy**, **passion** and **commitment**; their proportions name the kind of love.
- **Romantic** (intimacy + passion), **companionate** (intimacy + commitment) and **consummate** (all three) are key types; passion tends to cool while intimacy and commitment can deepen.

</Step>
