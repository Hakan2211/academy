<Step title="Two directions of inference" kind="explain">

**Reasoning** is drawing conclusions from evidence. It runs in two directions.

**Deductive** reasoning goes from general rules to a specific, *certain* conclusion. *All mammals breathe air. A whale is a mammal. Therefore a whale breathes air.* If the premises are true and the logic valid, the conclusion **must** be true. It's airtight — but it never tells you anything that wasn't already buried in the premises.

**Inductive** reasoning goes the other way — from specific observations to a general, *probable* conclusion. *Every swan I've seen is white, so all swans are white.* Induction is how science and daily life actually work; it lets us learn from experience. But it's never certain: one black swan in Australia, and the rule collapses.

Deduction guarantees; induction generalises. We need both — and we make characteristic mistakes with each.

</Step>

<Step title="Syllogisms: the logic checkup" kind="explain">

A **syllogism** is a tidy deductive argument with two premises and a conclusion:

*All A are B. All B are C. Therefore all A are C.*

Logical validity is purely about *form*, not content. "All blickets are dax. All dax are wug. Therefore all blickets are wug" is perfectly valid even though the words are nonsense. Conversely, an argument can reach a *true* conclusion through *invalid* logic — and that's exactly where humans get fooled. Our minds, it turns out, don't evaluate the form. They peek at the content.

</Step>

<Step title="Belief bias" kind="explain">

Consider this argument: *No addictive things are inexpensive. Some cigarettes are inexpensive. Therefore some addictive things are not cigarettes.* The conclusion sounds reasonable, so most people stamp it "valid". In fact the logic is flawed.

This is **belief bias**: we judge an argument's logic by whether we *agree with the conclusion*, not by whether it actually follows. A believable conclusion makes a bad argument look valid; an unbelievable conclusion makes a perfectly valid argument look broken. Belief hijacks logic. The lesson: a conclusion you like can be reached by terrible reasoning — and feeling that it's "obviously right" is not the same as it being *valid*.

</Step>

<Step title="The 2-4-6 game: confirmation bias" kind="interactive">

Now the deepest trap of all. There's a rule behind the sequence 2, 4, 6. Your job: discover it by proposing your own triples. I'll only tell you whether each one *fits*.

Play before reading on — really try to crack the rule.

<ConfirmationBias />

Here's the twist most people fall into: you form a hunch ("even numbers, going up by two"), then test *only triples that already match it* — 8-10-12, 20-22-24 — and every "fits" feels like proof. But you've learned almost nothing, because you never tried to make your rule **fail**. The single most informative move is to test a triple you *expect* to be rejected. The true rule is far broader than the obvious guess, and you can only find it by attacking your own idea.

</Step>

<Step title="Confirmation bias, everywhere" kind="explain">

**Confirmation bias** is the tendency to seek, notice and remember evidence that *supports* what we already believe, while neglecting or explaining away evidence that contradicts it. The 2-4-6 task strips it bare, but it runs through everything:

- We read news that agrees with us and dismiss the rest.
- We remember the times our hunch was right and forget the misses.
- We ask questions designed to get a "yes" ("You're an extravert, aren't you?").

The antidote is genuinely uncomfortable: deliberately go looking for the evidence that would prove you *wrong*. Good scientists, good detectives and good thinkers all share this habit — they try hardest to break their own favourite ideas. It's the opposite of what comes naturally.

<Callout type="warn" title="The bias you can't feel">
Confirmation bias is invisible from the inside. You never experience yourself as "ignoring counter-evidence" — you experience yourself as "being reasonable". That's exactly why it's so persistent.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Deductive** reasoning goes general → specific and is *certain* if valid; **inductive** reasoning goes specific → general and is only *probable* (one black swan breaks it).
- **Syllogisms** show that logical validity is about *form*, not content — but humans judge by content.
- **Belief bias**: we rate an argument "valid" when we *agree with its conclusion*, regardless of whether the logic holds.
- The **2-4-6 task** exposes **confirmation bias** — testing only cases that confirm a hunch while never trying to disconfirm it.
- The hard but powerful antidote is to actively seek evidence that would prove you **wrong**.

</Step>
