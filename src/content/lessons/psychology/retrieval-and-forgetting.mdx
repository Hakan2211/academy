<Step title="The problem isn't storage — it's finding it" kind="explain">

Here is one of the most freeing ideas in the study of memory: **most forgetting is a failure of retrieval, not of storage.** The memory is still in there. You just can't find the path to it right now.

The proof is everywhere. A name on the tip of your tongue that pops up an hour later was never gone — only momentarily unreachable. You can't *recall* a classmate's name, yet you'd instantly *recognise* it in a list. The information survived; what failed was the search. So the real art of remembering is less about cramming things *in* and more about building good ways to *get them back out*.

</Step>

<Step title="Retrieval cues: the hooks that find a memory" kind="explain">

Memories are found by **retrieval cues** — associated bits of information that act as hooks pulling the target back into mind. The richer the web of cues you encode *with* a memory, the more routes you have to reach it later.

Two striking consequences:

- **Context-dependent memory** — you recall better in the *place* where you learned. Divers who studied a word list underwater remembered it best back underwater; the surroundings themselves became cues. (This is the real reason walking into a room can make you forget why you came — you left the cues behind in the other room.)
- **State-dependent memory** — you recall better in the same internal *state* (mood, even level of caffeine) you were in when you learned. Sad moods bring back sad memories, which is part of why low moods can spiral.

The takeaway for studying: vary where and how you practise, so the memory isn't chained to one set of cues.

</Step>

<Step title="The forgetting curve" kind="interactive">

In the 1880s Hermann Ebbinghaus memorised endless nonsense syllables and measured his own forgetting over time. He found a sharp, lawful pattern: memory drops **fast at first, then levels off** — an exponential decay now called the **forgetting curve**. Most of what you lose, you lose within the first day.

That sounds grim, until you see what *review* does. Drag time forward, then hit **Review** and watch the curve.

Each spaced review does two things: it snaps retention back to full **and flattens the next decline** — you forget more slowly each time. That saw-tooth is the engine behind **spaced practice**: a few well-timed reviews keep you near the top for a fraction of the effort that cramming wastes.

</Step>

<Step title="Why we forget: decay vs interference" kind="explain">

Why does the curve fall at all? Two main culprits, and they're different:

- **Decay** — memory traces may simply *fade* with disuse over time, like a path growing over when no one walks it. Time itself is the enemy.
- **Interference** — other memories *get in the way*, and this often matters more than decay. In **proactive** interference, old learning blocks new (last year's locker combination intrudes on this year's). In **retroactive** interference, new learning blocks old (your new phone number makes the old one hard to recall).

Interference explains why similar memories crowd each other out, and why a good night's sleep — a stretch with little new input to interfere — protects what you learned that day.

</Step>

<Step title="Tip-of-the-tongue: forgetting in slow motion" kind="explain">

The **tip-of-the-tongue** state is retrieval failure caught in the act. You *know* you know the word — you might recall its first letter, its rhythm, how many syllables — yet the word itself won't come. It is vivid proof that **the memory is stored and partially accessible**; only the final retrieval link is jammed.

Often the very effort of straining makes it worse (you keep activating the *wrong* neighbour), and the word surfaces the moment you stop trying. The phenomenon shows memory isn't all-or-nothing: a trace can be *there*, *findable in pieces*, and still maddeningly out of reach.

<Callout type="tip" title="Study by retrieving, not reviewing">
Because forgetting is mostly a retrieval problem, the cure is retrieval practice. Close the book and try to *recall* — struggling to pull the answer out strengthens the path far more than re-reading it ever could. Space those attempts out, and the forgetting curve works in your favour.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- Most forgetting is a **retrieval failure**, not lost storage — shown by tip-of-the-tongue states and by recognising what you can't recall.
- Memories are found via **retrieval cues**; matching the encoding context boosts recall (**context-dependent** memory) as does matching the internal state (**state-dependent** memory).
- Ebbinghaus's **forgetting curve** falls fast then levels off; each **spaced review** restores retention *and* flattens the next decline — the basis of spaced practice.
- Forgetting comes from **decay** (fading with time) and, often more powerfully, **interference** — **proactive** (old blocks new) and **retroactive** (new blocks old).
- The **tip-of-the-tongue** state proves memory is graded, not all-or-nothing: the trace is there and partly accessible, but the final link is jammed.

</Step>
