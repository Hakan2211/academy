<Step title="More than a feeling" kind="explain">

We talk about emotion as if it were a single thing — *a feeling*. But pause on a moment of fear and you'll notice it has several parts working at once. Your heart pounds. Your face tightens. And you *know*, in some wordless way, that you are afraid.

Psychologists define an **emotion** as a full-body response to a meaningful event, made of **three components** woven together. Pull them apart and you can see the machinery:

- **Physiological arousal** — what the body does.
- **Expressive behaviour** — what others can see.
- **Subjective experience** — what it feels like from the inside.

A complete emotion is all three at once. Tease them apart and you get the central puzzles of the whole field.

</Step>

<Step title="The three components" kind="explain">

- **Physiology — the body's surge.** Emotion is physical. The sympathetic nervous system fires: heart rate climbs, breathing quickens, palms sweat, pupils widen, stress hormones flood in. This is your body *preparing to act* — to fight, flee or embrace.
- **Expression — the outward signal.** Emotion leaks out through the face, voice, posture and gesture. A clenched jaw, a trembling lip, a grin. These signals communicate our state to others, often faster than words and sometimes against our will.
- **Subjective experience — the felt quality.** And then there's the *what-it's-like*: the conscious, private feeling of joy or dread or tenderness. This includes our **appraisal** — the meaning we read into the situation, which colours the whole experience.

The deep questions of emotion are all about how these three relate. Does the body cause the feeling, or the feeling the body? You'll tackle exactly that in the next lesson.

</Step>

<Step title="Are some emotions basic?" kind="explain">

Out of the thousands of feeling-words in human languages, are some emotions **fundamental** — the primary colours from which the rest are mixed?

Many researchers think so. The strongest candidates are a small set of **basic emotions** that appear early in life, show up across all cultures, and have distinctive facial expressions: typically **happiness, sadness, anger, fear, surprise** and **disgust**. The argument for their realness is that they seem to be biologically built in rather than learned — a claim we'll test directly when we study facial expressions.

Everything else, on this view, is a **blend** or a culturally-shaped shading of these basics — much as orange is built from red and yellow.

</Step>

Two ideas are worth carrying away. First, emotions come in **opposites** — joy and sadness, trust and disgust — which is why you can't easily feel both at full strength at once. Second, the feelings we actually name are mostly **blends**: joy + trust gives love; fear + surprise gives awe. A handful of primaries, endlessly combined.

</Step>

<Step title="What emotions are for" kind="explain">

Why have emotions at all? They aren't noise in an otherwise rational mind — they're **functional**, shaped by evolution to help us survive and connect.

- **Emotions prepare the body for action.** Fear readies you to flee; anger readies you to confront. The physiological surge is the body getting ready *fast*, before slow deliberation can finish.
- **Emotions communicate.** A face of fear warns the group of danger; a smile signals safety and invites approach. Expression is social glue.
- **Emotions guide decisions.** Far from opposing reason, emotions tag options with value — *this feels wrong, that feels right* — letting us choose quickly. People who lose this emotional tagging through brain injury become startlingly *bad* at everyday decisions, even with reasoning intact.

Emotion, in short, is not the enemy of good thinking. It's part of how good thinking works.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- An **emotion** has **three components**: **physiological arousal** (the body), **expressive behaviour** (the outward signal) and **subjective experience** (the felt quality, including appraisal).
- Many researchers propose a small set of **basic emotions** — happiness, sadness, anger, fear, surprise, disgust — that are universal and biologically built in.
- On **Plutchik's wheel**, basics come in **opposing pairs** and combine into **blends** (joy + trust = love); most named feelings are mixtures.
- Emotions are **functional**: they ready the body for action, communicate to others and guide decisions — partners to reason, not its enemy.

</Step>
