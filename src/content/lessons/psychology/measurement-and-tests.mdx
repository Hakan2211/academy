<Lesson>

<Step title="You can only study what you can measure" kind="explain">

Psychology studies things you can't hold in your hand — anxiety, intelligence, motivation, prejudice. To study them at all, you have to turn them into numbers. But a number is only as trustworthy as the instrument that produced it. A study built on a shaky measure is shaky no matter how clever its design.

Two questions decide whether a measure is any good, and they are *not* the same question: is it **reliable**, and is it **valid**?

</Step>

<Step title="Reliability vs validity" kind="explain">

- **Reliability** = **consistency**. Measure the same thing twice and you get the same answer. A reliable bathroom scale shows 70 kg each time you step on it this morning.
- **Validity** = **accuracy**. The measure actually captures what it claims to. A *valid* scale shows your *true* weight.

The crucial, counter-intuitive point: a measure can be perfectly reliable yet completely invalid. A scale stuck 5 kg high reads 75 every single time — rock-solid reliable, and reliably **wrong**. Reliability is necessary but not sufficient; validity is the harder, more important target.

</Step>

<Step title="The dartboard" kind="interactive">

The clearest way to feel the difference is to aim at a target. The bullseye is the trait you mean to measure. *Clustered* darts are reliable (consistent); darts *centred on the bullseye* are valid (on target). Flip between the four combinations.

Spend a moment on "reliable, not valid" — the tight cluster sitting off-target. It's the most dangerous case, because the consistency *fools you* into trusting a measure that's systematically wrong.

</Step>

<Step title="Standardisation: a level playing field" kind="explain">

For scores to mean anything, the test must be given and scored the same way for everyone — this is **standardisation**. Same instructions, same time limit, same scoring rules. It also means having a **norm group**: a large, representative sample whose scores tell you what "typical" looks like, so your 80% is interpretable as high or low.

Without standardisation, you can't compare two people's scores at all — you'd be measuring with a ruler that stretches differently for each person.

</Step>

<Step title="The trouble with self-report" kind="explain">

A huge amount of psychology relies on simply **asking people** — surveys, questionnaires, interviews. It's cheap, fast, and the only way to reach private experience. But it leaks error in predictable ways:

- **Social desirability** — people answer to look good, under-reporting the embarrassing and over-reporting the admirable.
- **Faulty self-knowledge** — we're often wrong about our own minds, so even an honest answer can be mistaken.
- **Wording and order effects** — small changes to a question's phrasing can swing the answers.

<Callout type="tip" title="Triangulate">
Because every measure has blind spots, strong studies don't rely on one. Pair self-report with behaviour or physiology; where independent measures agree, you can trust the result more.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Reliability** is consistency (same answer on retest); **validity** is accuracy (measuring what you intend) — and they're independent.
- A measure can be **reliable but not valid** (consistently wrong) — the most deceptive case; validity is the harder goal.
- **Standardisation** (uniform administration plus a norm group) is what makes scores comparable across people.
- **Self-report** is powerful but distorted by social desirability, poor self-knowledge, and wording effects — so good research triangulates with other measures.

</Step>
