<Step title="A very old question, a very young science" kind="explain">

People have wondered about the mind for as long as there have been people. The questions are ancient — *What is a thought? Why do we dream? Are we born a certain way or made?* — and for most of history they belonged to **philosophy**, answered by argument from the armchair.

Psychology as a *science* is barely 150 years old. Its birthday is usually given as **1879**, when Wilhelm Wundt opened the first laboratory dedicated to studying the mind with experiments instead of opinions. That single move — measuring the mind — is what turned timeless musings into a science.

</Step>

<Step title="The first schools" kind="explain">

Early psychologists disagreed about what, exactly, to study.

**Structuralism**, growing from Wundt's lab, tried to break consciousness into its basic elements — like a chemistry of the mind — using trained **introspection** (carefully reporting one's own sensations). It was a bold start, but introspection proved unreliable: two trained observers often disagreed about their own inner states.

**Functionalism**, led by the American **William James**, asked a different question: not *what* the mind is made of, but *what it is for*. Why do we have memory, emotion, habit? James argued mental life evolved because it **helps us survive and adapt** — a hint of the evolutionary thinking still central today.

</Step>

<Step title="The pioneers who were nearly erased" kind="explain">

The standard story is crowded with men, but women shaped psychology from the very start — often while being denied the credit.

**Mary Whiton Calkins** completed every requirement for a Harvard PhD and outperformed her male classmates; Harvard refused to grant a woman the degree. She went on to invent an important memory technique and became the **first woman president of the American Psychological Association** in 1905. **Margaret Floy Washburn** became the **first woman to actually earn a psychology PhD** (1894) and was a leader in studying animal behaviour.

<Callout type="info" title="Why this matters">
Science is done by people, and who gets to do it — and be remembered — shapes what questions even get asked. Recovering these pioneers isn't just fairness; it's a fuller, more accurate history.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- Questions about the mind are ancient and belonged to **philosophy**; psychology became a **science** in **1879** when **Wundt** opened the first lab.
- **Structuralism** (Wundt) sought the mind's **elements** via introspection; **functionalism** (**James**) asked what the mind is **for**.
- The field grew as a chain of movements — psychodynamic, behaviourist, cognitive, humanistic, positive — each answering the last.
- Women like **Calkins** and **Washburn** were founders from the beginning, frequently despite being denied their rightful credit.

</Step>
