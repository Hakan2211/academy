<Step title="The mind as a detective" kind="explain">

Every day you act as an amateur detective, asking *why* — why did she ignore my message, why did he get the promotion, why am I so tired? **Attribution theory** is the study of how we answer that question: how we assign causes to behaviour, our own and other people's.

We sort causes into two bins:

- **Internal (dispositional)** — it's the *person*: their personality, ability, effort, mood.
- **External (situational)** — it's the *circumstances*: the setting, luck, pressure, the task.

The catch is that we're not neutral judges. We lean — hard, and in predictable directions.

</Step>

<Step title="The fundamental attribution error" kind="explain">

When explaining *other people*, we overestimate disposition and underestimate situation. The driver who cuts you off is "a jerk" — not someone rushing to a hospital. The classmate who fumbles a presentation is "not very bright" — not someone whose laptop just crashed.

This pull is so strong and so common it earned a grand name: the **fundamental attribution error**. We see the person vividly, centre-stage, and the situation fades into invisible background. So the person gets the blame — or the credit.

<Callout type="tip" title="A quick antidote">
Before judging someone's character from one act, ask: *"What situation could make a reasonable person do this?"* You'll almost always find one — and you'll be right more often than your first instinct.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="The actor–observer flip" kind="interactive">

Here's the twist that proves it's a *bias* and not just truth. When the actor is someone else, we blame the person. When the actor is *us*, we suddenly notice the situation. You weren't lazy — you were exhausted and overloaded.

Run the queue scenario again, but pay attention to the flip between "someone else does it" and "you do it."

<AttributionError />

That's the **actor–observer effect**. Part of it is simple viewpoint: when you act, you literally see the *situation* in front of you; when you watch someone act, you see *them*. The camera is pointed the other way.

</Step>

<Step title="The self-serving bias" kind="explain">

Our attributions aren't just lopsided — they're flattering. The **self-serving bias** is our tendency to take credit for successes (internal: *I'm skilled*) but blame failures on circumstances (external: *the exam was unfair, the ref was biased*).

It protects our self-esteem, and in moderation it's healthy — it keeps us trying. But pushed too far it blinds us: every setback is someone else's fault, so there's nothing to learn and nobody improves. Notice it especially in groups, where "we won because we're great; we lost because of bad luck" is the anthem of nearly every team.

</Step>

<Step title="When beliefs make themselves true" kind="explain">

Attributions don't just describe the world — sometimes they *create* it. A **self-fulfilling prophecy** is a belief that brings about its own fulfilment.

Suspect a new colleague is cold and unfriendly, and you'll act guarded around them — short replies, no warmth. Sensing your coolness, they pull back too. Now they *are* distant, and you feel vindicated: "I knew it." But your expectation **caused** the very behaviour it predicted. Teachers' expectations can lift or sink students' results this way; our hunches about people quietly shape how those people end up treating us.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Attribution** is how we assign causes — **internal/dispositional** versus **external/situational** — to behaviour.
- The **fundamental attribution error**: judging others, we overweight disposition and underweight the situation.
- The **actor–observer effect**: we explain our *own* behaviour situationally but others' dispositionally — the same act, judged from two seats.
- The **self-serving bias**: we credit our successes internally and blame failures externally, protecting self-esteem.
- A **self-fulfilling prophecy** is a belief that causes its own confirmation — our expectations of people shape how they behave toward us.

</Step>
