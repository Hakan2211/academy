<Step title="The thing you can't doubt" kind="explain">

Right now, something it is *like* to be you is happening. You see these words, perhaps hear a hum in the room, feel the chair against your back, notice a thought drifting in. That private, first-person stream of experience is **consciousness** — your awareness of yourself and your surroundings.

It is at once the most obvious fact in your life and the hardest thing in all of science to explain. You can doubt almost anything, but you cannot doubt that you are *having an experience*. Yet how a few pounds of electrified jelly produces that inner movie — the so-called **hard problem** — remains genuinely open. This world is about what we *do* know: how awareness rises and falls, switches off in sleep, and bends under hypnosis and drugs.

</Step>

<Step title="Awareness comes in levels" kind="interactive">

The first thing to unlearn is that consciousness is a simple on/off switch. It is more like a **dimmer** sliding through levels of arousal — from a coma with no awareness at all, up through deep sleep and drowsiness, to ordinary alert waking, and on to the razor-sharp absorption of intense focus.

Slide along the spectrum and click each level to feel the difference.

<ConsciousnessLevels />

Each level is a different *amount* of awareness and responsiveness — how much of the world you register, and how readily you can act on it. You travel up and down this dial every single day.

</Step>

<Step title="What consciousness is for" kind="explain">

If awareness is so costly to produce, what does it buy us? Psychologists point to a few key **functions**:

- **Restricting information.** The world floods you with far more than you could ever use. Consciousness acts as a filter, letting only a manageable trickle into the spotlight.
- **Combining information.** It binds sights, sounds, memories and goals into a single, unified scene — one coherent "now" rather than a jumble of separate signals.
- **Planning and flexible control.** Awareness lets you step back from automatic habits, weigh options, rehearse the future, and choose a deliberate response instead of a reflex.

In short, consciousness is what lets a creature stop merely *reacting* and start *deciding*.

</Step>

<Step title="Attention versus awareness" kind="explain">

Two words get tangled here, and pulling them apart is one of the most useful moves in this whole world.

**Awareness** is the broad field of everything you are currently experiencing — the whole lit stage. **Attention** is the narrow spotlight you can aim within that field, sharpening one part while the rest dims.

You can be *aware* of background music while your *attention* is on this sentence. Shift the spotlight and the music leaps into focus. This is why you can miss something in plain sight: if attention is aimed elsewhere, an event can fall inside your visual field yet never reach the spotlight — a gap dramatic enough that people fail to notice a person in a gorilla suit walking through a scene they are watching.

<Callout type="tip" title="The cocktail-party effect">
At a noisy party you can tune your attention to one conversation and tune out the rest — yet if someone across the room says *your name*, it snaps your spotlight around. Unattended information isn't fully gone; it's monitored at a low level, just outside the bright centre of awareness.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Consciousness** is your first-person awareness of yourself and your surroundings — obvious to live, famously hard to explain (the **hard problem**).
- It is not on/off but a **spectrum of levels** of arousal, from coma through sleep and drowsiness to alert and intense focus.
- Its **functions** include filtering the flood of information, **binding** it into one unified scene, and enabling **planning and flexible control**.
- **Awareness** is the whole lit field of experience; **attention** is the movable spotlight within it — which is why we can miss things in plain sight.
