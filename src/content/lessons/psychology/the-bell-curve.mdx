<Step title="The shape of a population" kind="explain">

Measure the IQ of a whole population and a beautiful, predictable shape emerges: the **normal distribution**, or **bell curve**. Most people cluster near the middle; the further from average you go — in either direction — the rarer people become.

This isn't a coincidence. When a trait is shaped by *many* small influences — countless genes plus countless environmental nudges — the result almost always piles up into this bell shape. Height does it, blood pressure does it, and so does IQ.

By design, IQ is centred on a **mean of 100** with a **standard deviation of 15**. Those two numbers let you locate *anyone* on the curve.

</Step>

<Step title="Reading the curve" kind="interactive">

The standard deviation is the curve's natural ruler. About **68%** of people fall within one (IQ 85–115), about **95%** within two (70–130), and over **99%** within three (55–145).

Drag the marker to convert any IQ into its **z-score** (how many standard deviations from the mean) and its **percentile** (the share of people scoring below it):

<NormalCurve mean={100} sd={15} unit="" label="IQ" />

Try a few landmarks: an IQ of **130** sits two SDs up, around the **98th percentile** — rarer than 1 in 40. An IQ of **70** sits two SDs down, near the **2nd percentile**. These two marks — **70** and **130** — traditionally flag the two tails psychologists study most.

</Step>

<Step title="The high tail: giftedness" kind="explain">

Roughly the top 2% — IQ around 130 and above — are often labelled **gifted**. A famous long-term study by Lewis Terman followed over a thousand high-IQ children (nicknamed the "Termites") for decades.

The findings were nuanced. The gifted children grew up healthy, successful and well-adjusted on average — puncturing the myth that brilliance means social awkwardness. But high IQ alone didn't guarantee eminence: motivation, opportunity and persistence mattered enormously. A high score *opens doors*; it doesn't walk through them for you.

</Step>

<Step title="The low tail: intellectual disability" kind="explain">

At the other extreme, an IQ below about 70 is *one* criterion for **intellectual disability** — but, crucially, never the only one. A diagnosis also requires significant limitations in **adaptive functioning**: the everyday practical and social skills of communicating, self-care, and living independently.

<Callout type="info" title="A score is not a person">
This two-part rule exists for a reason. Many people with low test scores live full, capable, independent lives. A number on a test can never, by itself, define someone's abilities or worth — adaptive functioning in the *real world* matters just as much.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="When the situation moves the score" kind="interactive">

Here's the twist that should make you cautious about treating any IQ score as a fixed, pure measure of ability: **the testing situation itself can shift the number.**

**Stereotype threat** is the best-documented example. When people are reminded — even subtly — of a negative stereotype about their group right before a test, the extra anxiety and self-monitoring consume mental resources, and their scores drop. Same ability, lower number. Remove the cue and performance bounces back:

This matters enormously. It means part of any group difference in scores can come from the *context* of testing, not from underlying ability — a theme we'll confront head-on in the final lesson.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- IQ follows a **normal (bell) curve** with mean **100** and SD **15**; about **68% / 95% / 99%** fall within 1 / 2 / 3 standard deviations.
- A **z-score** says how many SDs from the mean a score is; a **percentile** says what fraction scores below it (IQ 130 ≈ 98th percentile; IQ 70 ≈ 2nd).
- The **high tail (~130+)** is labelled **gifted** — but motivation and opportunity, not IQ alone, drive eminence.
- The **low tail (~below 70)** is *one* criterion for **intellectual disability**, which also requires deficits in **adaptive functioning** — a score is not a person.
- **Stereotype threat** shows the testing *situation* can depress scores without any change in ability — so IQ is never a perfectly pure measure.

</Step>
