<Step title="A disorder of reality itself" kind="explain">

Most of the disorders we've met disturb mood or anxiety. **Schizophrenia** reaches deeper: it disrupts the very machinery of thought and perception, loosening a person's grip on **shared reality**. It affects roughly 1 in 100 people worldwide, usually emerging in late adolescence or early adulthood.

Let's clear away the biggest myth at the outset. Schizophrenia is **not** "split personality" or "multiple personalities" — that's a different and much rarer condition (dissociative identity disorder). The word means "split mind," but it refers to a *fragmentation* of thought, emotion, and perception, not a person housing several selves.

</Step>

<Step title="Positive and negative symptoms" kind="interactive">

Clinicians sort schizophrenia's symptoms into two families — and the names are a famous source of confusion. **Positive** and **negative** here mean **added** versus **taken away**, *not* good versus bad.

- **Positive symptoms** are experiences *added* to normal functioning: hallucinations, delusions, disorganised thought and speech.
- **Negative symptoms** are normal functions *reduced or lost*: flat emotional expression, loss of drive (avolition), social withdrawal.

<Step title="The course of the illness" kind="explain">

Schizophrenia typically unfolds in phases rather than arriving all at once:

- A **prodromal** phase — subtle early changes: social withdrawal, odd ideas, slipping function — that can precede the first episode by months.
- An **active** phase — when positive symptoms (hallucinations, delusions) are prominent, often the point of diagnosis.
- A **residual** phase — when positive symptoms ease but negative symptoms and some impairment may linger.

The course varies enormously between people. With treatment — typically antipsychotic medication combined with psychological and social support — many people manage their symptoms well, and some recover substantially. Early intervention improves outcomes.

</Step>

<Step title="The dopamine hypothesis — and beyond" kind="explain">

The longest-standing biological account is the **dopamine hypothesis**: that the positive symptoms of schizophrenia involve **overactivity of dopamine** systems in certain brain pathways. The evidence is suggestive:

- drugs that *block* dopamine receptors (antipsychotics) reduce positive symptoms; and
- drugs that *boost* dopamine (like amphetamines) can produce psychotic-like symptoms.

But it's incomplete. Dopamine-blocking drugs help positive symptoms far more than negative ones, and other neurotransmitters (notably **glutamate**) are clearly involved. The hypothesis is best seen as one important piece, not the whole answer.

Beyond dopamine, the causes are genuinely multi-layered:

- **Genetic** — schizophrenia is highly heritable; risk rises sharply with closeness of an affected relative, though no single "schizophrenia gene" exists.
- **Neurodevelopmental** — subtle differences in brain structure and prenatal factors (maternal illness, birth complications) raise risk.
- **Environmental** — cannabis use in adolescence, urban upbringing, and severe stress are associated with higher risk in vulnerable people.

This is the **diathesis-stress** pattern in action: a biological vulnerability that environmental stress can tip into illness — the very model we'll build in the final lesson.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Schizophrenia** disrupts thought and perception, loosening a person's grip on shared reality — and is **not** "split personality."
- **Positive** symptoms are *added* (hallucinations, delusions, disorganised thought); **negative** symptoms are *lost* (flat affect, avolition, withdrawal) — added vs taken away, not good vs bad.
- It typically moves through **prodromal → active → residual** phases; with treatment and early help, many people do well.
- The **dopamine hypothesis** (dopamine overactivity behind positive symptoms) is supported but incomplete — glutamate and other factors matter too.
- Causes are **genetic, neurodevelopmental, and environmental**, interacting — a clear case of the **diathesis-stress** pattern.

</Step>
