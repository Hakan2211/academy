<Step title="Built-in blind spots" kind="explain">

The heuristics that make us fast also leave us with **cognitive biases** — systematic, predictable errors in thinking. The key word is *systematic*: these aren't random slips. Everyone tips the same direction, in the same situations, again and again. That predictability is unsettling — but it also means biases can be studied, anticipated, and sometimes defused.

In this lesson we'll meet five of the most famous, starting with one that proves your choices can be flipped by nothing more than *wording*.

</Step>

<Step title="Framing and loss aversion" kind="interactive">

Here's the same life-or-death decision, described two ways. Make a choice in each frame — then see whether you flipped.

<FramingDemo />

If your choice changed between the two frames, congratulations: you're human. The outcomes were *mathematically identical* — "200 saved" out of 600 is the same world as "400 die". Only the wording changed, yet preferences reliably reverse. This is the **framing effect**.

What drives it is **loss aversion**: losses hurt about twice as much as equivalent gains feel good. So a "gain" frame ("saved") makes us cautious — we lock in the sure win. A "loss" frame ("die") makes us reckless — we gamble to avoid a *certain* loss. The fear of losing, not the value of the outcome, steers the wheel.

</Step>

<Step title="Loss aversion in the wild" kind="explain">

Once you see loss aversion, you see it everywhere:

- **Free trials.** Once something feels like *yours*, cancelling feels like a *loss* — so you keep paying.
- **The sunk-cost fallacy.** You sit through a bad film because you "already paid"; you finish a doomed project because you've "put so much in". Walking away feels like accepting a loss, even though the money or time is already gone and irrecoverable.
- **Markets.** Investors hold losing stocks far too long, unwilling to "realise" the loss, while selling winners too soon.

The rational move ignores what's already spent and asks only *what's best from here*. Loss aversion makes that move feel almost physically painful.

</Step>

<Step title="Overconfidence and hindsight" kind="explain">

Two biases conspire to make us feel smarter than we are.

**Overconfidence** is the tendency to be more certain than accurate. Asked for a range we're "90% sure" contains the right answer, the truth falls outside it far more than 10% of the time. Most drivers rate themselves "above average". The trouble isn't being wrong — it's not knowing *how* wrong we might be.

**Hindsight bias** — the "I-knew-it-all-along" effect — is its partner. Once you know the outcome, it feels like it was *obvious* and *predictable* all along. After the game, the result was "inevitable"; after the crash, the warning signs were "clear". Hindsight quietly rewrites your memory of what you actually expected, which inflates your confidence in *future* predictions. Together they form a loop: we overestimate what we knew, so we overestimate what we know.

<Callout type="tip" title="A cure for hindsight">
Write your predictions *down* before the outcome. A prediction you can check is far harder for hindsight to rewrite — and it's a humbling, useful habit.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="The gambler's fallacy" kind="explain">

Flip a fair coin and it lands heads five times in a row. What's the chance the next flip is tails? Your gut screams "high — surely it's *due*!" Your gut is wrong: it's still exactly **50%**. The coin has no memory.

This is the **gambler's fallacy** — believing that past *independent* events change the odds of the next one, that a run of one outcome makes the opposite "due". The roulette wheel that's hit red ten times is no more likely to hit black on the next spin. Each spin is independent; the wheel doesn't owe you anything.

(Its mirror image, the **hot-hand** belief, is the opposite mistake — assuming a streak will *continue*.) Both spring from the same root: our deep, mistaken intuition that randomness should look *even* in the short run. It doesn't. Real chance is lumpy, streaky and surprising — and that mismatch funds every casino on Earth.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Cognitive biases** are *systematic*, predictable errors — everyone tilts the same way in the same situations.
- The **framing effect**: identical options described as gains vs losses flip our choices, driven by **loss aversion** (losses hurt about twice as much as equal gains feel good) — which also fuels the **sunk-cost fallacy**.
- **Overconfidence** (more certain than accurate) and **hindsight bias** ("I knew it all along") inflate our sense of how much we know.
- The **gambler's fallacy** wrongly assumes independent events are "due" to reverse; chance has no memory and is lumpier than intuition expects.

</Step>
