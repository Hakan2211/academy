<Lesson>

<Step title="Why psychology needs a method" kind="explain">

Everyone has theories about people. *Opposites attract. Venting your anger calms you down. We only use 10% of our brains.* They sound reasonable — and every one of them turns out to be wrong.

That is the whole problem. Human behaviour is exactly the area where our intuitions feel most certain and are most often mistaken. So psychology does not settle questions by who argues best or what *seems* obvious. It settles them with **evidence, gathered systematically**. The method is what separates psychology from a collection of confident opinions.

</Step>

<Step title="From hunch to hypothesis" kind="explain">

Science moves through a chain, each link sharpening the one before:

- **Theory** — a broad, organised explanation. *Sleep helps the brain consolidate what it learned that day.*
- **Hypothesis** — a specific, testable prediction the theory implies. *Students who sleep after studying will recall more the next morning than students who stay awake.*
- **Operational definition** — exactly how you will *measure* each fuzzy idea, so anyone could repeat your study. *"Recall" = number of words correctly remembered from a 40-word list; "sleep" = 8 hours in a lab bed monitored by EEG.*
- **Test** — collect the data and see whether the prediction holds.

The magic is in the operational definition: it drags a vague word like "recall" down to something concrete and countable.

</Step>

<Step title="Why it has to be falsifiable" kind="explain">

A good hypothesis sticks its neck out — it has to be able to **fail**. "Sleep improves memory" is scientific because a result could prove it wrong. "Invisible energies shape your mood, but they cannot be detected" is not, because no result could ever count against it.

<Callout type="tip" title="The honest test">
Before running any study, ask: *what result would convince me I'm wrong?* If you can't answer, you don't yet have a scientific hypothesis — you have a belief.
</Callout>

A theory survives not by being clever but by surviving every fair attempt to knock it down.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- Intuition is unreliable for behaviour, so psychology relies on **systematically gathered evidence**, not opinion.
- The chain runs **theory → hypothesis → operational definition → test**, each step more specific than the last.
- An **operational definition** turns a fuzzy concept into something concrete and countable, so others can repeat the study.
- A scientific claim must be **falsifiable** — capable of being shown wrong; that is exactly what makes it testable.

</Step>
