<Step title="Memory is a storyteller, not a camera" kind="explain">

We feel as though remembering is *replaying* — pressing play on a recording of what happened. It is one of the most convincing illusions the mind produces, and it is wrong.

Memory is **reconstructive**. When you recall an event, you don't retrieve a stored video; you *rebuild* the experience from fragments — a few real details, your general knowledge of how such things go, your expectations, your current mood — and stitch them into a seamless, confident scene. Most of the time the reconstruction is good enough. But the very same process that lets memory be flexible and efficient also lets it be **systematically wrong** — and wrong in ways that feel exactly like being right.

</Step>

<Step title="Schemas: helpful, and quietly distorting" kind="explain">

Why does the mind fill gaps so confidently? Because it leans on **schemas** — organised packets of expectation about how the world usually works. A schema for "restaurant" includes menus, ordering, paying; a schema for "office" includes desks and computers.

Schemas make perception and memory fast and efficient — but they edit your memories toward the *typical*. In a classic study, people who waited in an office later "remembered" seeing **books** that were never there, because books fit the office schema. You don't just forget details; you **add** plausible ones that were never present, and **smooth away** the odd details that don't fit. Memory drifts, over time, toward what *should* have been.

</Step>

<Step title="The misinformation effect" kind="explain">

The most consequential discovery in this field is Elizabeth Loftus's **misinformation effect**: information encountered *after* an event can rewrite the memory of the event itself.

In her famous experiment, people watched a video of a car crash, then were asked how fast the cars were going when they **"smashed"** — or, for others, when they **"hit"**. The single changed word reshaped the memory: the "smashed" group reported higher speeds, and a week later were more likely to "remember" **broken glass** that was never in the film. A mere suggestion, slipped in afterward, became part of what they sincerely recalled.

This is why a **leading question** ("How fast was the *speeding* car going?") is so dangerous: it doesn't just bias the answer, it can edit the underlying memory.

</Step>

<Step title="Why eyewitnesses can be confidently wrong" kind="explain">

Put these pieces together and you reach a sobering conclusion about **eyewitness testimony** — long treated by courts as gold-standard evidence. It is far less reliable than it feels:

- **Suggestion rewrites it.** Leading questions, biased line-ups, and even overheard accounts from other witnesses can plant details (the misinformation effect).
- **Confidence is not accuracy.** A witness can be *certain* and *wrong* — and jurors find confident testimony deeply persuasive, exactly when they should be cautious.
- **Whole events can be implanted.** Researchers have led people to vividly "remember" being lost in a mall as a child, or other events that never happened, simply through repeated suggestion.

DNA evidence later cleared hundreds of wrongly convicted people — and **mistaken eyewitness identification** was the leading cause of those wrongful convictions. The reconstructive mind, doing its ordinary job, can send the wrong person to prison.

<Callout type="warn" title="Confidence is not proof">
A memory's *vividness* and the *confidence* you feel in it say almost nothing about its accuracy. False memories can be as detailed and certain as true ones. Treat your own most confident recollections — and other people's — with humility.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- Memory is **reconstructive**, not a recording: recall *rebuilds* an event from fragments, expectations, and current state — confidently, even when wrong.
- The **DRM illusion** shows the mind storing the **gist** and falsely "remembering" a strongly-implied word that was never presented.
- **Schemas** speed memory but distort it toward the *typical* — adding plausible details and smoothing away the atypical.
- Loftus's **misinformation effect**: post-event suggestion (e.g. a leading question's wording) can **rewrite** the original memory.
- Consequently **eyewitness testimony is unreliable**: suggestion plants details, **confidence does not equal accuracy**, and even whole events can be implanted — a leading cause of wrongful convictions.

</Step>
