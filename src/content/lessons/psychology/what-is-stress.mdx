<Step title="Stress is a relationship, not a thing" kind="explain">

We talk about stress as if it lived inside us — "I'm so stressed." But psychologists found something more useful: **stress is a relationship** between you and a demand, when that demand feels like it's pushing against what you can handle.

Two pieces make it up. The **stressor** is the event or demand out there — an exam, a traffic jam, a new baby, a deadline. **Stress** is your *response* to it — the racing heart, the churning thoughts, the bracing. The very same stressor can feel like nothing to one person and overwhelming to another. So what makes the difference? Not the event itself, but how you *size it up*.

</Step>

<Step title="Appraisal: the secret step" kind="explain">

Richard Lazarus showed that between a stressor and your stress response sits a hidden step: **appraisal**, your mind's split-second sizing-up of the situation. It happens in two passes.

- **Primary appraisal** asks: *"Is this a threat to me?"* Is this dangerous, harmful, challenging — or irrelevant? A spider is terrifying to one person and a non-event to another, and that's all primary appraisal.
- **Secondary appraisal** asks: *"Can I handle it?"* What are my resources, my options, my support?

Here's the key formula of stress: you feel stressed when the **threat looks bigger than your resources**. Shrink the threat (primary) *or* grow your resources (secondary) and the same event stops feeling like stress and starts feeling like a manageable challenge.

</Step>

<Step title="Acute vs chronic stress" kind="explain">

Not all stress is equal. The crucial split is *how long it lasts*.

- **Acute stress** is short and sharp: a near-miss in traffic, a job interview, a sudden scare. Your body surges, you cope, and then — crucially — you **recover**. This kind of stress is normal, often useful, and your body is beautifully built for it.
- **Chronic stress** is the slow grind: an unhappy job, money worries, a strained relationship, caregiving that never lets up. The threat never resolves, so the body's emergency systems never fully switch off. **This** is the kind that wears the body down — the kind health psychology worries about most.

A helpful way to think about it: acute stress is a sprint your body is designed for; chronic stress is being asked to sprint all day, every day.

</Step>

<Step title="Measuring life's load" kind="interactive">

In the 1960s, Holmes and Rahe noticed that people often fell ill after periods of major life change — and not only after *bad* events. Getting married, having a baby, even a holiday all demand **readjustment**, and readjustment is taxing.

They built the **Social Readjustment Rating Scale**: a list of life events, each weighted in "life-change units," that you tally up over the past year. Higher totals predicted higher rates of illness. Try a version below — and notice that it's the **accumulation**, including the happy events, that adds up.

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Stress** is a *relationship*: your response when a **stressor's** demands feel bigger than your ability to cope — not a property of the event itself.
- **Appraisal** is the hidden step: **primary** ("is this a threat?") and **secondary** ("can I handle it?"). Stress = threat appraised as larger than resources.
- **Acute** stress is short, sharp, and recoverable; **chronic** stress never switches off and is the kind that wears the body down.
- The **Holmes–Rahe** scale tallies life-change units; even *positive* events demand readjustment, and it's the **accumulation** that predicts risk — though it can't see your appraisal or coping.

</Step>
