<Lesson>

<Step title="From going along to being ordered" kind="explain">

Conformity is yielding to *peers*. **Obedience** is something starker: following the direct orders of an *authority*. We're taught obedience as a virtue — children obey parents, soldiers obey officers, citizens obey the law — and most of the time it keeps society running.

But after the Second World War, one question haunted psychology. Ordinary people had carried out monstrous orders. Were they uniquely evil monsters — or could *almost anyone* be led to harm an innocent person, simply because someone in charge said to? In 1961, Stanley **Milgram** decided to test it. He expected the answer to be reassuring. It wasn't.

</Step>

<Step title="The shock generator" kind="explain">

You arrive at a Yale lab to help study "learning and memory." You're made the **teacher**; another volunteer (secretly an actor) is the **learner**, strapped to electrodes in the next room. Each time he answers wrong, you flip a switch delivering a shock — and every switch is 15 volts higher than the last, running from a mild 15V up to a final switch marked **450V — XXX**.

The learner (on tape) starts to complain, then cries out, then bangs on the wall, then falls ominously silent. You want to stop. But beside you, a calm man in a grey lab coat says, simply, that you must continue.

</Step>

<Step title="How far would you go?" kind="interactive">

Turn the dial. At each voltage, see what fraction of Milgram's real participants were *still obeying* — and hear the experimenter's scripted prods.

<MilgramObedience />

Before the study, experts predicted almost no one would reach the top — maybe one in a thousand sadists. The actual result: **65% of participants delivered the full 450 volts.** Not because they were cruel — many were visibly distressed, sweating and trembling and begging to stop — but because a quiet authority kept telling them to go on, and they couldn't bring themselves to defy him.

</Step>

<Step title="What drives obedience" kind="explain">

Milgram ran dozens of variations to find the *situational* levers. Obedience went **up** when:

- The **authority felt legitimate and close** — present in the room, prestigious (Yale), in uniform/lab coat.
- **Responsibility shifted upward** — "I'm responsible, not you" let people enter what Milgram called an **agentic state**, feeling like a mere instrument carrying out another's will.
- The **victim was distant** — out of sight, in another room.
- There was **no model of defiance** — no one else refusing first.

Obedience dropped sharply when the authority left the room, when the learner was right beside the teacher, when the orders came by phone, or when a peer rebelled first. Once again: the *situation*, not the person, did most of the work.

</Step>

<Step title="Losing yourself in the role: deindividuation" kind="explain">

A close cousin helps explain why people do, in groups and uniforms, what they'd never do alone. **Deindividuation** is the loss of self-awareness and personal responsibility that can come from anonymity and immersion in a group — a crowd, a mask, a uniform, a darkened room.

Stripped of individual identity and accountability, people become more impulsive and more willing to follow the group or the situation's pull — whether that's a cheering crowd, a riot, or anonymous cruelty online. Like the agentic state, it loosens the personal brakes that normally hold harmful behaviour in check.

</Step>

<Step title="The price of the knowledge" kind="explain">

Milgram's findings are profound — and the way he got them is now a cautionary tale. Participants were **deceived** (the shocks were fake, the learner an actor) and many endured genuine, intense distress believing they were torturing a stranger. The study helped force a hard rethink of what researchers may do to the people they study.

The classic studies of social power each anchor a different rule. Match these scenarios to the ethical principle most at stake — and notice that today's safeguards were written *because of* studies like this one.

<EthicsScenarios />

Modern rules now require **informed consent**, the **right to withdraw**, **protection from harm**, and thorough **debriefing** when deception is used. Milgram did debrief his participants — but a study causing that much distress would face intense scrutiny from a review board today, and likely would not run in its original form.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Obedience** is following an authority's direct orders; Milgram tested how far ordinary people would go.
- In the **shock experiment**, **65%** delivered the maximum 450V to a (faked) screaming stranger — not from cruelty but under a calm authority's prods.
- Obedience rises with **legitimate, close authority**, **shifted responsibility** (the **agentic state**), and a **distant victim**; it falls with remote authority, a near victim, or a defiant peer.
- **Deindividuation** — anonymity and group immersion — loosens personal responsibility, enabling behaviour people wouldn't show alone.
- The study's **deception and distress** helped drive modern research-ethics rules: consent, the right to withdraw, protection from harm, and debriefing.

</Step>
