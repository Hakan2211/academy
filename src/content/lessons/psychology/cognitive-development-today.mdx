<Step title="A brilliant theory, gently corrected" kind="explain">

Piaget changed everything — but no theory that big survives a century untouched. Later research showed he got the *sequence* mostly right while being too pessimistic about *timing*: with cleverer experiments, infants and young children turn out to know far more, far earlier, than his tasks revealed.

His theory also had a blind spot. By watching children solve puzzles largely *alone*, Piaget cast development as a solo journey of discovery. A Russian psychologist working at the very same time saw something he missed entirely: development is **social** to its core.

</Step>

<Step title="Vygotsky and the social mind" kind="explain">

**Lev Vygotsky** argued that children learn to think by interacting with more knowledgeable people — parents, teachers, older kids. Thinking, he said, starts *between* people and only later moves *inside* the individual. Language isn't just a product of thought; it's the very tool that builds it. (His ideas reached the West late — he died in 1934 at just 37 — but now rival Piaget's in influence.)

Where Piaget asked "what can a child do alone?", Vygotsky asked "what can a child do *with help*?" — and found that the second question predicts learning far better than the first.

</Step>

<Step title="The zone of proximal development" kind="explain">

Vygotsky's signature idea is the **zone of proximal development (ZPD)**: the gap between what a learner can do *alone* and what they can do *with guidance* from someone more skilled.

Picture three zones. Inside the first, tasks are already mastered — boring, no growth. Far outside lies the impossible — frustrating, no growth either. Between them sits the ZPD: tasks just out of solo reach but achievable *with* help. **That** is where learning happens. A child who can't yet tie their shoes alone but can with a parent guiding their hands is learning right inside their ZPD.

</Step>

<Step title="Scaffolding" kind="explain">

How does a helper move a child through the ZPD? Through **scaffolding** — temporary support that's gradually withdrawn as the learner gains competence, exactly like the scaffold around a building that comes down once the structure stands on its own.

A good teacher scaffolds constantly: starting with a worked example, then a hint, then just a nudge, then nothing. The skill is in *fading* the help at the right pace — enough to keep the child in the productive zone, never so much that they stop doing the thinking themselves. It's a different picture of development from Piaget's lone explorer: here, growth is something we do *together*.

</Step>

<Step title="Theory of mind" kind="explain">

One of the most important things a young child discovers is that other people have *minds of their own* — separate beliefs, desires and knowledge that can differ from reality and from the child's own. Psychologists call this a **theory of mind**.

The classic test is the **false-belief task**. A child watches Sally hide a marble in a basket, then leave. While she's gone, Anne moves it to a box. Where will Sally look when she returns? Children under about four say "the box" — they know where it *really* is, so they assume Sally does too. Around age four, the leap happens: children answer "the basket," realising Sally still holds a *false belief*. This is the dawn of understanding deception, surprise, empathy — the entire social world.

</Step>

<Step title="The information-processing view" kind="interactive">

A third modern approach drops stages altogether. The **information-processing** view treats the mind like a computer and asks how its components — attention, memory, processing speed — improve *gradually* with age.

From this angle, a ten-year-old outperforms a four-year-old not because they've crossed into a new Piagetian stage, but because their working memory holds more, their attention filters distractions better, and they process information faster. </Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- Later research kept Piaget's **sequence** but showed children are capable **earlier** than his tasks suggested.
- **Vygotsky** reframed development as **social**: thinking begins between people, with language as the key tool.
- The **zone of proximal development** is the gap between solo ability and assisted ability — where learning happens — bridged by **scaffolding** (temporary, fading support).
- **Theory of mind** (around age 4, shown by the false-belief task) is the realisation that others hold separate, sometimes false, beliefs.
- The **information-processing** view drops stages and explains growth through gradual gains in attention, memory and processing speed.

</Step>
