<Step title="The cell that thinks" kind="explain">

Every thought you have ever had, every feeling, every move — all of it is the work of cells called **neurons**. You have roughly **86 billion** of them, knitted into networks of staggering complexity. They don't think individually any more than a single transistor "computes"; mind emerges from the conversation between billions of them.

A neuron has one job: to **receive signals, decide whether to fire, and pass a signal on.** To see how, let's look at its parts.

</Step>

<Step title="Anatomy of a neuron" kind="interactive">

<Neuron />

The neuron is built for one-way flow, from the receiving end to the sending end:

- **Dendrites** — the bushy branches that *receive* incoming signals from thousands of other neurons.
- **Cell body (soma)** — keeps the cell alive and **sums up** all the incoming signals.
- **Axon** — the long output cable that carries the impulse *away* from the cell body — sometimes over a metre, from your spine to your toe.
- **Myelin sheath** — a fatty insulating wrap around the axon that makes the signal travel dramatically faster.
- **Terminals (synapses)** — the branched endings that *pass the signal on* to the next cell across a tiny gap.

</Step>

<Step title="Three jobs, three kinds of neuron" kind="explain">

Neurons specialise by where they sit in the chain of information:

- **Sensory (afferent) neurons** carry messages *inward* — from your eyes, skin, and ears toward the brain and spinal cord. *"Here's what's happening out there."*
- **Motor (efferent) neurons** carry commands *outward* — from the central nervous system to your muscles and glands. *"Do this."*
- **Interneurons** sit in between, inside the brain and spinal cord, connecting the others and doing the bulk of the processing. They vastly outnumber the rest.

A reflex — yanking your hand off a hot stove before you even feel pain — is sensory neuron → interneuron → motor neuron, a loop fast enough to act before your conscious brain catches up.

</Step>

<Step title="The forgotten half: glia" kind="explain">

Neurons get the glory, but they're not alone. The brain is packed with **glial cells** ("glue" cells), at least as numerous as neurons. Far from mere packing material, glia are essential support staff:

- They **build the myelin** that insulates axons and speeds signals.
- They **feed** neurons and mop up waste and excess chemicals.
- They **guide** new connections during development and help prune unused ones.
- They form part of the immune defence of the brain.

<Callout type="info" title="Why myelin matters">
Myelin isn't continuous — it comes in segments with tiny gaps between them. The impulse leaps from gap to gap rather than crawling along every inch, which makes a myelinated axon transmit many times faster than a bare one. When myelin breaks down (as in multiple sclerosis), signals slow or fail — a vivid reminder of how much this insulation does.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- The brain runs on ~86 billion **neurons**, cells specialised to **receive, integrate, and transmit** signals.
- A neuron's parts in flow order: **dendrites** (receive) → **cell body/soma** (integrate) → **axon** (transmit, insulated by **myelin**) → **terminals/synapses** (pass on).
- Three functional types: **sensory** (inward), **motor** (outward), and **interneurons** (processing in between, the most numerous).
- **Glial cells** are the essential support crew — building myelin, feeding neurons, guiding connections, and defending the brain.

</Step>
