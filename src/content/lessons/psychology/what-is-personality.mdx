<Step title="The thing that makes you, you" kind="explain">

Think of someone you know well — really well. Now picture them at a party, in a crisis, alone on a rainy afternoon. Different situations, but you can almost predict how they'll act. That stable, recognisable signature is their **personality**.

Psychologists define personality as a person's characteristic, **enduring pattern of thinking, feeling and behaving**. Two ideas are doing the heavy lifting in that definition:

- **Consistency** — you are roughly the same person across time and across situations. The friend who's warm today was warm last year and will likely be warm next week.
- **Distinctiveness** — personality is what sets you *apart*. It explains why two people, handed the exact same situation, respond in completely different ways.

</Step>

<Step title="Stable, but not frozen" kind="explain">

"Consistent" doesn't mean rigid. You behave differently at a funeral than at a festival — situations matter enormously. The claim is subtler: relative to *other people*, your ranking tends to hold. The most cheerful person in the room at the party is often the most cheerful in the meeting too.

Personality also drifts slowly across a lifetime. Most people grow a little more conscientious and a little calmer as they age — a trend so common it's been nicknamed the **maturity principle**. So personality is best pictured as a deep, slow-moving current, not a locked door.

<Callout type="tip" title="Personality vs mood vs character">
A **mood** is a passing weather pattern ("I'm grumpy this morning"). **Personality** is the climate — the long-run average. **Character** adds a moral flavour (honest, kind), which personality describes more neutrally.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Two ways to study a person" kind="explain">

How should we even go about studying personality? Two great strategies split the field:

- The **nomothetic** approach hunts for *universal dimensions* — a small set of dials (like height and weight for the body) on which *everyone* can be placed and compared. It's the path of the trait theorists you'll meet next.
- The **idiographic** approach zooms all the way in on the *unique individual* — their personal history, their one-of-a-kind combination of qualities — refusing to flatten them into a score. It's the path of case studies and the humanistic thinkers.

Neither is "right". The nomothetic lens lets us do science across millions of people; the idiographic lens captures the texture a number always loses. Good psychology uses both.

</Step>

<Step title="Four big answers" kind="interactive">

Over a century, four broad families of theory have tried to explain where personality comes from. You'll spend a lesson or two with each on this world:

- **Trait** theories — describe personality as a profile of measurable dimensions.
- **Psychodynamic** theories — trace it to unconscious drives and early childhood.
- **Humanistic** theories — root it in our drive to grow and become our true selves.
- **Social-cognitive** theories — see it as the product of thoughts, beliefs and the situations we meet.

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Personality** is a person's enduring, characteristic pattern of thinking, feeling and behaving.
- Its two hallmarks are **consistency** (stable across time and situation) and **distinctiveness** (it sets people apart).
- Consistency is *relative*, not rigid — and personality drifts slowly across life (the **maturity principle**).
- The **nomothetic** approach compares everyone on universal dimensions; the **idiographic** approach studies the unique individual in depth.
- Four theory families — **trait, psychodynamic, humanistic, social-cognitive** — each offer a different answer for where personality comes from.

</Step>
