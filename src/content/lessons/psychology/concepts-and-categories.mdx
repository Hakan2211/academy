<Step title="Why your mind groups things" kind="explain">

Look around you. You don't see a chaos of unique objects — you see *chairs*, *cups*, *people*. Each is one of a kind, yet your mind instantly slots it into a familiar group. That slotting is the most basic act of thought.

A **concept** is a mental grouping of things that share something in common: *dog*, *justice*, *blue*, *birthday*. Concepts are the mind's filing system. Without them you'd have to figure out every new object from scratch — *what is this four-legged thing and can it hurt me?* With the concept "dog", you already know.

By the end of this lesson you'll see that these categories aren't the tidy, rule-based boxes they seem — they have fuzzy edges and warm centres.

</Step>

<Step title="The textbook idea: categories as rules" kind="explain">

The old, common-sense view says a category is defined by a **set of rules** — necessary and sufficient features. Something is a *triangle* if and only if it's a closed figure with three straight sides. Check the boxes, and you're in.

This works beautifully for triangles and odd numbers. The trouble is that almost nothing in everyday life behaves so neatly. Try writing the rules for "game". Must it have a winner? (Not catch.) Must it be fun? (Not for the loser.) Be played with others? (Not solitaire.) Every rule you propose has exceptions, yet you recognise a game instantly.

Real concepts, it turns out, aren't held together by rules at all.

</Step>

<Step title="The prototype effect" kind="interactive">

Instead of rules, the mind seems to store a **prototype** — the *best example* of a category — and judge new things by how much they resemble it. Some members are "better" examples than others: a robin is a *birdier* bird than a penguin, even though both are 100% birds.

Notice the pattern: the closer something sits to the prototype, the faster and more confidently you judge it. Penguins and ostriches are real birds, but your snap judgement hesitates. That speed gap is the **typicality effect**, and it's powerful evidence that categories are organised around prototypes, not checklists.

<Callout type="tip" title="A quick test you can run on anyone">
Ask a friend to "name a bird". Almost no one says *penguin* or *emu* — they say robin, sparrow, eagle. The first thing that pops to mind is the prototype.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Hierarchies and the comfortable middle" kind="explain">

Concepts nest inside one another in **hierarchies**, from broad to specific:

- **Superordinate** (broad): *animal*
- **Basic** (the comfortable middle): *dog*
- **Subordinate** (specific): *border collie*

The middle — the **basic level** — is special. It's the level you naturally reach for. You point at a pet and say "dog", not "animal" (too vague) and not "border collie" (more detail than needed). Children learn basic-level words first, we name pictures at this level fastest, and it carries the most useful information for the least effort. The mind gravitates to the level that's *just right*.

</Step>

<Step title="Schemas: concepts in action" kind="explain">

Bundle a cluster of concepts together with expectations about how they fit, and you get a **schema** — a mental framework for a whole situation. You have a "restaurant" schema: you expect a host, a menu, a server, a bill. You've never been to *this* restaurant, yet you know exactly how to behave.

Schemas make life efficient: they let you predict and fill in gaps automatically. But they also bias you. If a schema says "waiters are men", you may mis-remember a female server, or be briefly confused. The very structures that let you think fast also quietly shape — and sometimes distort — what you notice and recall. Hold that thought: the rest of this world is largely about the price we pay for thinking fast.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- A **concept** is a mental grouping that lets you treat new things as instances of something familiar — the mind's filing system.
- Categories aren't really held together by **rules**; everyday concepts (like "game") resist definition yet are recognised instantly.
- The mind stores a **prototype** (best example) and judges by resemblance — so typical members are rated "better" and judged **faster** (the **typicality / prototype effect**).
- Concepts nest in **hierarchies**, and the **basic level** (dog, not animal or border collie) is the comfortable default.
- **Schemas** package concepts into situation frameworks — efficient, but a source of bias and distorted memory.

</Step>
