<Step title="A clock you were born with" kind="explain">

Long before alarm clocks, living things kept time. Plants open and close their leaves on schedule; you get sleepy at night and alert by mid-morning even in a windowless room. That is your **circadian rhythm** — an internal cycle of roughly 24 hours (*circa* = about, *dies* = day) that governs sleepiness, body temperature, hormones and alertness.

The remarkable part: this rhythm runs on its own. Volunteers sealed away from all time cues — no clocks, no sunlight — keep cycling on a near-24-hour beat for weeks. The clock is *built in*. Daylight doesn't create the rhythm; it merely **resets** it each day to stay locked to the real world.

</Step>

<Step title="The master clock in your head" kind="explain">

Where does the ticking happen? In a tiny cluster of neurons in the hypothalamus called the **suprachiasmatic nucleus (SCN)** — the brain's master clock. It sits right above where the optic nerves cross, and that location is no accident: it gets a direct line from the eyes.

Special light-sensing cells in the retina report "it's bright" or "it's dark" straight to the SCN. Using that signal, the SCN keeps its near-24-hour cycle tuned to the actual day, then broadcasts the time to the rest of the body — telling organs, hormones and even individual cells what hour it thinks it is.

</Step>

<Step title="Reading the dial" kind="interactive">

The SCN's instructions show up as smooth daily curves. Three of the most important ride around this 24-hour dial: **core body temperature** (lowest in the small hours, peaking in the early evening), **melatonin** — the "darkness hormone" the brain releases at night to invite sleep — and **alertness**, which tracks temperature.

In daylight, the SCN suppresses melatonin; as darkness falls, melatonin rises and you feel drowsy. Watch how the three curves line up with an ordinary day, then drag the **schedule shift** to fly across time zones or work a night shift.

<CircadianClock />

When you shift your schedule abruptly, your external day jumps but the SCN catches up only slowly — about a day per time zone.

</Step>

<Step title="When the clock and the world disagree" kind="explain">

That slow catch-up is exactly why **jet lag** feels awful. Fly from London to New York and your body insists it's bedtime when the city is at lunch; melatonin floods in at the wrong hour and temperature bottoms out mid-afternoon. The mismatch eases only as the SCN re-syncs, roughly one time zone per day — which is why eastward flights (where you must sleep "early") tend to hit hardest.

**Shift work** is jet lag that never resolves. A night nurse is asked to be alert when her SCN is screaming "sleep" and to sleep when it's broadcasting "wake." Chronic misalignment isn't just unpleasant — it's linked to poorer sleep, mood and long-term health.

<Callout type="info" title="Resetting the clock">
The strongest reset signal is bright light at the right time — morning light nudges the clock earlier, evening light pushes it later. This is why bright screens late at night delay your rhythm, and why morning sunlight is one of the best cures for jet lag.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- A **circadian rhythm** is an internal ~24-hour cycle controlling sleepiness, body temperature, hormones and alertness; it runs even with no time cues.
- The **suprachiasmatic nucleus (SCN)** in the hypothalamus is the master clock, reset daily by light reaching it directly from the eyes.
- **Melatonin**, released in darkness, signals "night" and promotes sleep; bright light suppresses it.
- **Jet lag** and **shift work** are mismatches between the slowly-resetting internal clock and an abruptly-changed schedule — the clock re-syncs about one time zone per day.

</Step>
