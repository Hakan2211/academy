<Step title="Coping is something you do" kind="explain">

If appraisal decides *whether* something stresses you, **coping** is what you *do* about it once it has. Lazarus defined coping as the effort to manage demands that feel like they're stretching your resources — and crucially, it's a *skill*, not a fixed trait. You can get better at it.

Psychologists sort the countless things people do into two broad families. The difference between them is simple but powerful: are you trying to change the **problem**, or change how you **feel** about it?

</Step>

<Step title="Problem-focused vs emotion-focused" kind="explain">

- **Problem-focused coping** goes after the **stressor itself**. You make a plan, gather information, ask for help, fix the bug, have the difficult conversation. The goal is to *remove or reduce the source* of the stress.
- **Emotion-focused coping** goes after **your emotional response**. You reframe the situation, seek comfort, breathe, accept what can't be changed, let yourself grieve. The goal is to *manage the distress* the stressor causes.

Beginners often assume problem-focused coping is "mature" and emotion-focused coping is "weak." That's wrong. **Neither is better** — what matters is the *fit* between strategy and situation.

</Step>

<Step title="The rule of controllability" kind="explain">

Here's the rule that ties it together: **match your coping to how much control you have.**

- When a stressor is **controllable** — a deadline, a fixable conflict, a skill you can build — **problem-focused** coping shines. Acting on the problem actually solves it.
- When a stressor is **uncontrollable** — a terminal diagnosis, a bereavement, a past you can't undo — pouring energy into "fixing" it only adds frustration. Here **emotion-focused** coping, especially **acceptance**, is the healthier path. You can't change the event, so you tend to the feelings.

The skilled coper reads the situation first, then picks the tool. Trying to *fix* the unfixable, or merely *soothing* the fixable, are the two classic mismatches.

</Step>

<Step title="Two powerful buffers" kind="explain">

Beyond individual strategies, two factors reliably soften almost any stressor.

**Social support.** Simply having people you can turn to — to talk, to help, to reassure — buffers the impact of stress on both mind and body. Supported people recover faster from illness, live longer, and rate the same stressors as less threatening. We are built to cope *together*.

**Perceived control.** Notice the word *perceived*. The mere *belief* that you have some say over what happens to you dramatically reduces stress — even when the actual control is small. In classic studies, people given a (rarely-used) "stop" button for an unpleasant noise found it far less stressful than those without one. Reappraising a situation to find the slice of control you *do* have is one of the most effective things you can do.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Coping** is the effort to manage stretching demands — a learnable skill, not a fixed trait.
- **Problem-focused** coping targets the **stressor**; **emotion-focused** coping targets your **emotional response**. Neither is inherently better.
- Match the tool to **controllability**: problem-focused fits **controllable** stressors; emotion-focused (especially **acceptance**) fits **uncontrollable** ones.
- **Social support** and **perceived control** are powerful buffers — and control need only be *perceived* to help.

</Step>
