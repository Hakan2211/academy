<Step title="The boy who watched children think" kind="explain">

In the 1920s, a young Swiss scientist named **Jean Piaget** took a job scoring children's intelligence tests. He found the *wrong* answers far more fascinating than the right ones — because children of the same age tended to get them wrong in the *same way*. That pattern hinted at something profound: kids don't just know less than adults, they **think differently**.

Piaget spent the next half-century watching children (his own three among them) and arrived at the most influential theory of cognitive development ever written. Its core claim: the mind grows through a fixed sequence of **stages**, each a new way of understanding the world.

</Step>

<Step title="Schemas: the mind's filing system" kind="explain">

Piaget said we organise knowledge into **schemas** — mental frameworks or "files" for categories of things. A toddler has a schema for *dog*: four legs, furry, barks. Schemas grow through two complementary processes:

- **Assimilation** — fitting new information into an *existing* schema. The toddler sees a cat and says "doggie!" — cramming it into the dog file.
- **Accommodation** — *changing* the schema when it no longer fits. Told "no, that's a cat — it meows and doesn't fetch," the child splits the file in two.

Development, for Piaget, is this endless cycle: the world throws up surprises, the child's schemas don't quite fit, and the mind reorganises to restore balance. That drive to resolve the mismatch is the engine of cognitive growth.

</Step>

<Step title="The four stages" kind="interactive">

Out of all that watching, Piaget mapped **four stages**, each with a signature new ability — and a signature limitation that vanishes at the next stage. Click through them:

The order is the same for every child and every culture; what varies is the *timing*. And the magic is in the *limitations* — the things a child reliably *can't* yet do reveal how their thinking is structured. Two of those limitations are so striking they're worth seeing for yourself.

<Step title="Object permanence" kind="interactive">

In the **sensorimotor** stage, a young infant seems to believe that things which disappear from view simply cease to exist. Hide a toy under a cloth and a four-month-old won't reach for it — not because they can't, but because, for them, it's *gone*.

Around eight to twelve months this changes. The infant develops **object permanence**: the understanding that objects continue to exist when out of sight. Try it:

<ObjectPermanence />

This is why peekaboo is pure magic to a young baby (you really *vanished!*) and merely fun to an older one (they know you're still there). It's one of the first great achievements of the human mind.

</Step>

<Step title="Egocentrism and conservation" kind="explain">

The **preoperational** child (roughly 2–7) can now use symbols and language, but two limits define this stage.

**Egocentrism** is the inability to take another's point of view — not selfishness, but a genuine assumption that everyone sees what *they* see. Ask a preoperational child hiding behind their own hands "Can I see you?" and they may say no — *they* can't see you, so surely you can't see them.

**Failure to conserve** is the inability to grasp that quantity stays the same despite a change in appearance. This is Piaget's most famous demonstration — and you can run it yourself.

</Step>

<Step title="The conservation task" kind="interactive">

Pour the same water from a short, wide glass into a tall, thin one. The water hasn't changed — but watch what a preoperational child concludes:

<ConservationTask />

The child fixates on the single most obvious feature (the height of the column) and ignores the rest. They can't yet mentally *reverse* the pour or coordinate height *and* width at once. Around age 7, in the **concrete operational** stage, conservation clicks into place — and the child can no longer be fooled.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Piaget** argued children don't just know less — they *think differently*, progressing through four universal **stages**.
- Knowledge is organised into **schemas**, grown by **assimilation** (fitting new input into an old schema) and **accommodation** (revising the schema).
- The four stages: **sensorimotor** (senses/actions; gains object permanence) → **preoperational** (symbols; limited by egocentrism and no conservation) → **concrete operational** (logic about concrete things; masters conservation) → **formal operational** (abstract, hypothetical reasoning).
- Signature limits reveal the structure of thought: **object permanence**, **egocentrism** and **conservation** mark the great early transitions.

</Step>
