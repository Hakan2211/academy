<Step title="The half of psychology that was missing" kind="explain">

For most of its history, psychology studied what goes *wrong* — anxiety, depression, trauma, disorder. That work matters enormously. But around the year 2000, **Martin Seligman**, then president of the American Psychological Association, pointed out a strange gap: psychology had become very good at moving people from a *minus five* up to *zero*, and almost silent on how to go from *zero* to a *plus five*.

So began **positive psychology** — the rigorous, evidence-based study of what makes life *worth* living. Not happy-face slogans, but the same scientific method you've used all island, turned toward a new question: what actually helps human beings **flourish**?

</Step>

<Step title="What is happiness, anyway?" kind="explain">

Scientists needed something measurable, so they defined **subjective well-being** — how a person evaluates their own life. It has two parts:

- An **emotional** part: feeling more pleasant emotions than unpleasant ones, day to day.
- A **cognitive** part: judging, on reflection, that your life is going well — what researchers call **life satisfaction**.

Notice the word *subjective*. Well-being is assessed from the inside; you are the authority on your own. And it splits a crucial distinction: momentary *pleasure* (a great meal) is not the same as enduring *satisfaction* (a life you're proud of). A good life needs both — and they often come from different places.

</Step>

<Step title="The treadmill that keeps us running" kind="interactive">

Here's the finding that humbles everyone who hears it. We assume big life events — a raise, a new car, even a serious misfortune — will change our happiness *for good*. They almost never do. After a spike, we **adapt** back toward a stable personal baseline, called a **set point**. This is the **hedonic treadmill**: we keep running after the next good thing, and keep ending up roughly where we started.

Trigger some life events and watch happiness jump — then drift home.

<HedonicTreadmill />

Lottery winners, studied a year on, are barely happier than before. Even people who suffer some serious setbacks often return, over time, closer to baseline than anyone predicts. Adaptation is mostly a *gift* — it lets us recover from the worst — but it's also why chasing circumstances is a treadmill: the effect keeps fading.

</Step>

<Step title="PERMA: the shape of flourishing" kind="interactive">

If happiness isn't one thing you can buy, what *is* it? Seligman's answer is that well-being is built from **five distinct pillars**, captured by the acronym **PERMA**:

**P**ositive emotion · **E**ngagement · **R**elationships · **M**eaning · **A**ccomplishment.

The beauty of the model is that each pillar is something you can deliberately *build* — and they don't all depend on feeling cheerful. Explore them:

A flourishing life rarely maxes out every pillar; people lean on different ones. But a life starved of *all* five is the recipe for languishing. Across this world, you'll learn to strengthen each pillar on purpose.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Positive psychology** is the evidence-based study of what makes life flourish — the long-missing other half of the field, launched by **Martin Seligman**.
- **Subjective well-being** = pleasant emotions + **life satisfaction**; momentary pleasure and enduring satisfaction are not the same.
- The **hedonic treadmill**: after a spike, we adapt back toward a personal **set point**, so chasing circumstances brings only fading gains.
- **PERMA** names five buildable pillars of flourishing: **P**ositive emotion, **E**ngagement, **R**elationships, **M**eaning, **A**ccomplishment.

</Step>
