<Step title="A ladder of needs" kind="explain">

In 1943 the humanistic psychologist **Abraham Maslow** offered one of psychology's most famous pictures of motivation. He argued that our needs are not a flat list but a **hierarchy** — a ladder, often drawn as a pyramid. We are pulled to satisfy the most basic needs first; only once those are reasonably met do the higher ones start to motivate us strongly.

It's an intuitive idea. A starving person isn't thinking about self-esteem or poetry — they're thinking about food. But give them food, safety and warm relationships, and questions of meaning and purpose come alive. The pyramid captures how motivation *shifts upward* as lower needs are filled.

</Step>

Reading from the bottom up: **physiological** (food, water, sleep), **safety** (security, stability), **love and belonging** (friendship, intimacy), **esteem** (respect, achievement), and at the peak **self-actualisation** — realising your full potential. The order isn't iron law, but the general pull is real: it's hard to chase a calling while cold, hungry and afraid.

</Step>

<Step title="Deficiency needs vs growth needs" kind="explain">

Maslow drew a crucial line through the pyramid. The bottom four tiers are **deficiency needs** (D-needs): they arise from a *lack*, and we're motivated to fill the gap and then stop. A satisfied D-need goes quiet — once you're full, food stops pulling you.

The top tier is different. **Self-actualisation** is a **growth need** (B-need, for "being"). It isn't about filling a hole; it's about *becoming more*. And unlike deficiency needs, it doesn't switch off when fed — the more you grow, create and pursue meaning, the more you want to. Growth needs are satisfied by expanding, not by topping up.

<Callout type="info" title="Not a rigid staircase">
Maslow never meant the order to be absolute. People go hungry for a cause, find belonging in hardship, or create great art in poverty. The hierarchy describes a general tendency in how needs *compete for attention* — not a law that lower needs must be perfectly met first.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Why we do things: intrinsic vs extrinsic" kind="explain">

The pyramid raises a deeper question about *why* we act. Psychologists distinguish two sources:

- **Intrinsic motivation** — doing something for its own sake, because it's interesting, satisfying or meaningful. A child drawing for the sheer joy of it.
- **Extrinsic motivation** — doing something for an outside reward or to avoid punishment. Drawing to earn a gold star.

Both get behaviour going. But they aren't simply additive — and that leads to one of psychology's most surprising findings.

</Step>

<Step title="The overjustification effect" kind="explain">

Here's the twist. In a classic study, children who already *loved* to draw were split into groups. Some were promised a reward for drawing; others weren't. Afterwards, the children who'd been **paid** drew *less* on their own than before. Rewarding a behaviour someone already enjoys can **undermine** the intrinsic interest — this is the **overjustification effect**.

The explanation: when an external reward is added, the mind quietly re-explains the behaviour. *"I'm not drawing because I love it — I'm drawing for the prize."* Once the prize disappears, so does the reason. The original joy has been crowded out.

The practical lesson is sharp: tangible rewards are great for boring tasks no one would do otherwise, but for things people already find meaningful, heavy external incentives can backfire — turning play into work.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Maslow's hierarchy** arranges needs in a pyramid: **physiological → safety → belonging → esteem → self-actualisation**, with lower needs generally pulling first.
- The bottom four are **deficiency needs** (fill a lack, then go quiet); the top is a **growth need** that expands the more it's pursued.
- **Intrinsic** motivation comes from within (the activity itself); **extrinsic** motivation comes from outside rewards.
- The **overjustification effect**: rewarding an already-enjoyed activity can replace intrinsic interest with extrinsic dependence — and reduce the behaviour once the reward stops.

</Step>
