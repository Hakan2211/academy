<Step title="The oldest question" kind="explain">

Are you who you are because of your **genes** or because of your **upbringing**? It's psychology's most famous tug-of-war: **nature** versus **nurture**.

For centuries people picked a side. Some held that we arrive as a *blank slate*, written on entirely by experience. Others insisted that character is *in the blood*, fixed at birth. Both extremes are wrong — and the truth is far more interesting. The modern verdict is decisive: **always both, always interacting.** The real question was never "which one" but "**how do they work together?**"

</Step>

<Step title="What genes actually do" kind="explain">

A **gene** is a stretch of DNA — a recipe. Genes don't code for "shyness" or "musical talent" directly; they build proteins that build brains and bodies, which then behave. A single trait like height or temperament usually involves **many genes**, each nudging the outcome a little.

Crucially, genes set a **range of possibilities**, not a fixed destiny. Think of your genes as supplying the *seed*; the environment is the *soil, water, and light* that determine which of its possibilities actually grow. The same seed becomes a stunted or a flourishing plant depending on the conditions — and the same genotype can yield very different people in different worlds.

</Step>

<Step title="Heritability — and its biggest trap" kind="interactive">

Psychologists measure how much of a trait's **variation across a population** traces to genetic differences. That number is **heritability**. Use the demo: pick a trait, see the genetic vs environmental split, and watch the twin-study logic that estimates it.

<Heritability />

The trap — and it catches almost everyone — is thinking heritability applies to *you*. It does not. "Height is 80% heritable" does **not** mean 80% of your height came from genes. It means 80% of why *people differ* in height, in this population, traces to their genes. Heritability is a fact about **groups and their variation**, never about a single individual.

</Step>

<Step title="How we know: twins and adoptees" kind="explain">

How can we possibly untangle nature from nurture, when families share both? Two clever natural experiments:

- **Twin studies.** *Identical* (monozygotic) twins share ~100% of their genes; *fraternal* (dizygotic) twins share ~50%, like ordinary siblings. If identical twins are far more alike on a trait than fraternal twins, that gap points to **genes**. The rare gold standard — identical twins **raised apart** — is even more striking: they often share startling similarities despite different homes.
- **Adoption studies.** Adopted children share genes with their *biological* parents but environment with their *adoptive* ones. Whichever they resemble more, on which traits, helps separate the two influences.

Together these designs reveal that almost every psychological trait — intelligence, personality, even interests — is *partly* heritable, and *partly* environmental. Never 0%. Never 100%.

</Step>

<Step title="It's an interaction, not a sum" kind="explain">

The deepest point: nature and nurture don't simply *add up* — they **multiply and shape each other**.

- **Gene × environment interaction.** A genetic tendency may only show up in certain environments. A child genetically prone to aggression might grow up gentle in a calm, supportive home but volatile in a harsh one. The gene's effect *depends on* the environment.
- **Nature affecting nurture.** Your genes even help *build* your environment. A naturally outgoing child draws more social interaction; a bookish one is handed more books. We partly select and create the very experiences that then shape us.

<Callout type="tip" title="Genes can switch on and off">
Experience can literally change which genes are active — turning them up or down without altering the DNA itself. This is **epigenetics**, and it's the molecular handshake where nurture reaches in and adjusts nature. The two aren't separate forces; they're one continuous conversation.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- The **nature–nurture** debate is settled as **both, always interacting** — the live question is *how*, not *which*.
- **Genes** set a *range of possibilities*; the **environment** determines which are realised. Most traits involve many genes.
- **Heritability** measures how much of a trait's variation *across a population* traces to genes — it is **not** about any individual.
- **Twin** and **adoption** studies separate the influences; almost every psychological trait turns out partly heritable, partly environmental.
- Nature and nurture **interact** (gene × environment), and genes even shape the environments we end up in — with **epigenetics** letting experience switch genes on and off.

</Step>
