<Step title="Are moral facts part of the furniture of the universe?" kind="explain">

Here is a striking question: when you say "torturing innocents is wrong," are you reporting a *fact* — something true independently of whether anyone believes it — or are you doing something else, like expressing a feeling, voicing a social norm, or issuing a command?

**Moral realists** say moral facts are real, objective features of the world, the same way mathematical truths or physical laws are. The wrongness of torture isn't just our opinion — it would be wrong even if every human being on Earth thought it was fine. Some realists ground moral facts in natural properties (pleasure, flourishing), others in non-natural moral properties, and others in reason itself.

**Anti-realists** disagree. Moral claims, they say, don't describe objective facts:

- **Error theorists** (J.L. Mackie) say moral claims *look* like factual claims but are all *false*, because no objective moral facts exist.
- **Expressivists** say moral claims don't assert facts at all — "murder is wrong" really means something like "boo to murder!" — an expression of attitude, not a truth-apt statement.
- **Relativists** say moral claims are true or false, but only relative to a culture or individual's standards — there is no culture-independent truth.

Each position has serious defenders and serious objections. The debate is genuinely open.

</Step>

<Step title="Hume's guillotine: the is-ought gap" kind="explain">

David Hume noticed something that sounds simple but is devastating once you see it. In moral arguments, people often move from factual premises — claims about what *is* — to moral conclusions — claims about what we *ought* to do. And they do it without any logical justification.

Hume wrote in *A Treatise of Human Nature* (1739): he had observed every moral system he encountered making this slip. Writers begin with ordinary statements of fact, and then suddenly switch, without explanation, from "is" and "is not" to "ought" and "ought not." He insisted this move requires justification — and none is ever given.

This is known as **Hume's guillotine** (or the is-ought gap, or the naturalistic fallacy in G.E. Moore's later formulation). The logical point is simple: **you cannot derive a value from facts alone.** No matter how many true descriptive facts you assemble, they cannot entail a moral conclusion without at least one normative premise doing the work.

Consider: "Humans evolved to be tribal" — that's a fact. Does it follow that tribalism is *good*? No — you need the additional premise "whatever is natural is good," which is itself a normative claim, not a fact.

<Callout type="info" title="Hume's own words">
"In every system of morality, which I have hitherto met with, I have always remarked, that the author proceeds for some time in the ordinary way of reasoning … when of a sudden I am surprised to find, that instead of the usual copulations of propositions, *is*, and *is not*, I meet with no proposition that is not connected with an *ought*, or an *ought not*."
</Callout>

<Step title="Why this matters" kind="explain">

The is-ought gap has teeth. Once you see it, you notice how often moral arguments illicitly presuppose value premises as if they were obvious facts:

- "It's natural, so it must be right." (But nature is full of cruelty — naturalness doesn't entail goodness.)
- "It maximises happiness." (True — but the premise that we *ought* to maximise happiness is itself a normative claim, not derived from mere facts about happiness.)
- "Evolution shaped us to care about kin." (A biological fact — but nothing follows about whether we *should* care about kin, without adding a value premise.)

Identifying the hidden ought-premise doesn't automatically refute an argument — the premise might be a good one. But making it explicit is the beginning of honest moral reasoning.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Moral realism**: there are objective moral facts, independent of human belief.
- **Anti-realism**: no such facts exist — moral claims express attitudes, are all false, or are relative to cultures.
- **Hume's is-ought gap**: you cannot derive a moral conclusion from purely factual premises — you always need at least one normative premise.
- This is a point about logical form, not about whether moral facts exist.
- Making hidden value premises explicit is essential to honest moral argument.

</Step>
