<Step title="The gadfly of Athens" kind="explain">

Socrates (470–399 BCE) wrote nothing. Everything we know about him comes from others — chiefly his student Plato. Yet he is arguably the most influential philosopher who ever lived, and the pivot on which Western philosophy turns.

He was ugly, famously poor, and barefoot in all seasons. He spent his days in the Athenian agora (marketplace) stopping people and asking them uncomfortable questions. What is courage? What is justice? What is piety? The powerful and the confident would give him an answer — and he would, with maddening politeness, dismantle it.

This is why his fellow citizens called him the **gadfly**: the fly that stings a horse into action. Athens, he said, was a great and noble horse, but sluggish. He was sent to sting it awake.

</Step>

<Step title="The Socratic method in action" kind="interactive">

Socrates' method was not lecturing. He claimed to know nothing — the famous **Socratic irony** — and asked questions until his interlocutor's confident definition collapsed. The collapse was the point: **aporia** (productive confusion) is the beginning of genuine inquiry.

<SocraticMethod />

The method has a structure: elicit a definition → find a counterexample → refine → repeat until the easy answer fails. Real understanding begins where false certainty ends.

</Step>

<Step title="The examined life — and its price" kind="explain">

At his trial in 399 BCE, Socrates was charged with impiety and corrupting the youth of Athens. He was 70 years old. He could have fled, or proposed exile, or simply stopped philosophising. Instead he delivered the **Apology** — his defence — and made the most famous claim in philosophy:

> *"The unexamined life is not worth living."*

He was convicted by a narrow margin and sentenced to death. He drank hemlock calmly, still debating the immortality of the soul with his friends. He refused to compromise his philosophical mission even to save his life — the first great martyr to the life of reason.

<Callout type="info" title="Socratic Irony">
Socrates claimed to know nothing while visibly knowing a great deal about how to dismantle others' supposed knowledge. This "irony" — feigned ignorance used as a tool — is the source of the literary device of the same name. He genuinely believed that wisdom begins with acknowledging how little one knows.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- Socrates pivoted philosophy from "what is reality made of?" to **"how should a person live?"**
- His method: **elicit a definition → counterexample → refine → aporia** (productive confusion).
- He claimed to know nothing — **Socratic irony** — and used this humility as a philosophical tool.
- His maxim: **"The unexamined life is not worth living."**
- He was tried, convicted, and executed in Athens in 399 BCE — refusing to abandon philosophy even to save his life.
- He wrote nothing; we know him through Plato, Xenophon, and Aristophanes.

</Step>
