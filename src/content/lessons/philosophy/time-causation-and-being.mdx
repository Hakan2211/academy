<Step title="Does the past still exist?" kind="explain">

Right now, you are reading this sentence. A moment ago, you were reading the one before it. A moment from now, you will be reading the next one. We speak naturally of the past, present, and future as if they are three regions — but only one of them seems to be *here*.

This intuition drives the **Presentist** view: only the present moment is real. The past has ceased to exist; the future does not yet exist. Time is a moving spotlight that illuminates only NOW.

The rival view is **Eternalism** (sometimes called the block universe): past, present, and future all exist equally. The distinction between them is perspectival — like the distinction between *here* and *there* in space. The year 1776 exists just as surely as 2025; it's just that we are temporally located in 2025, not 1776. On this view, "the present" is like "here" — it marks where you are, not what uniquely exists.

A third position, **Growing Block theory**, says the past and present exist but the future does not yet. The universe is a block that grows as time passes, with new moments crystallising out of the open future.

</Step>

<Step title="Hume's problem with causation" kind="explain">

We think we see causation everywhere: the cue ball *causes* the eight ball to move; the sun *causes* the flower to grow; your coffee *causes* you to wake up. David Hume (1711–1776) looked more carefully and found something disturbing: **we never actually observe causation itself**.

What you see when the cue ball hits the eight ball is: the cue ball moving, then *contiguity* (the balls touching), then *priority* (the cue ball moves first), then *constant conjunction* (balls have always behaved like this in the past). What you never see is the *necessary connection* between cause and effect — the force of causation itself.

Hume concluded that what we call "causation" is just the mind's habit of expecting B after A, built up from repeated observation. There is no metaphysical glue connecting causes to effects in the world; there is only **constant conjunction** in experience and a **psychological habit** of expectation.

<Callout type="warn" title="Hume's challenge is serious">
If Hume is right, science does not discover causes — it discovers reliable correlations. The "laws of nature" are just exceptionally well-confirmed regularities. This is called the **regularity theory of causation**, and it has been enormously influential — and deeply contested — ever since.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Responses to Hume" kind="explain">

Philosophers have resisted Hume's deflationary conclusion in several ways.

**Necessary connection in the world (anti-Humean)**: some philosophers argue there really are causal powers, dispositions, and necessities in nature — not visible in any single instance, but genuinely present. A fragile glass has the *power* to break; a magnet has the *power* to attract iron. These are real features of the world, not just regularities.

**Counterfactual analysis**: David Lewis proposed that "A caused B" means "if A had not occurred, B would not have occurred." This cashes out causation in terms of what *would* have happened in nearby possible worlds — no mysterious metaphysical glue required.

**Interventionist account**: Judea Pearl and others argue that causation is best understood through **manipulation**: A causes B if intervening to change A changes B. This is operationally very useful and underlies the modern theory of causal inference in statistics and AI.

</Step>

<Step title="The deepest question: why is there something?" kind="explain">

Leibniz asked what many consider the most fundamental question in all of philosophy: **"Why is there something rather than nothing?"**

Any scientific explanation takes *something* as given — particles, fields, laws, the Big Bang. But you can always ask: why those particles? Why those laws? Why a Big Bang at all rather than pure nothing?

Several responses are worth knowing:

**Necessary being**: perhaps something must exist necessarily — it would be a contradiction for there to be nothing. Leibniz himself thought God was this necessary being. Modern versions argue that mathematical objects, logical truths, or the laws of logic themselves necessarily exist.

**Brute fact**: perhaps there is no explanation. The existence of the universe is just a **brute fact** — a stopping point where explanation runs out. Not everything can be explained; someone always has to accept some starting point.

**The universe explains itself**: some cosmologists (Sean Carroll, Lawrence Krauss) argue that a universe with no matter, no energy, but the right quantum vacuum can spontaneously produce something. But philosophers object: quantum laws themselves count as "something" — you haven't escaped the question.

**The question is ill-formed**: perhaps "nothing" is not a coherent state that could *obtain*, and the question rests on a grammatical illusion. On this view, there couldn't have been nothing, so the question "why isn't there?" dissolves.

</Step>

<Step title="Revisit the cave: where these questions end up" kind="interactive">

Plato thought these questions — about time, causation, and existence — all point back to the same answer: the **Form of the Good**, the ultimate ground of being and intelligibility. Step through the allegory again with this in mind. The prisoners debating which shadow will come next are doing proto-science; the philosopher who walks out of the cave and sees the sun is grasping the *source* of causation and existence itself.

<CaveAllegory />

Most contemporary metaphysicians do not follow Plato all the way to the Forms. But the structure of the move — from appearances and regularities to deeper explanatory grounds — is exactly what drives modern debates about causation, time, and the existence of the universe.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Time**: Presentism (only the present exists), Eternalism (block universe — all times exist), Growing Block (past and present exist; future is open).
- **Causation**: Hume argued we never observe necessary connection — only constant conjunction. The regularity theory vs anti-Humean powers vs counterfactual analysis vs interventionism.
- **Why is there something?** Necessary being (Leibniz/God), brute fact, self-explaining universe, or the question is ill-formed.
- Plato's move — from regularities up to ultimate explanatory grounds — echoes through all these debates, even in secular form.
- Metaphysics doesn't always give definitive answers; its value is in making implicit assumptions explicit and forcing us to think clearly.

</Step>
