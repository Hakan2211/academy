<Step title="History has a logic — and reason drives it" kind="explain">

Kant had shown that the mind structures experience. His successor, **G. W. F. Hegel** (1770–1831), took that idea and gave it a historical, dynamic twist. The mind is not a fixed set of categories; it develops through time. And not just individual minds — **Spirit** (*Geist*), the collective self-consciousness of humanity, unfolds through history according to a definite logical pattern.

That pattern is the **dialectic**. Every idea or social arrangement contains an internal tension — a contradiction it cannot resolve within itself. The tension generates its opposite. The clash of opposites produces a higher synthesis that preserves the truth of both while transcending their limitations. Then the synthesis itself becomes a new starting point with its own tensions. History is this endless spiral upward, driven not by chance or the will of individuals, but by the inner logic of **ideas**.

Hegel called the driving force the **Absolute Spirit**: reality itself is a kind of cosmic mind becoming conscious of itself through human history. Art, religion, law, philosophy — these are all stages in Spirit's self-understanding. The owl of Minerva (goddess of wisdom) spreads its wings only at dusk: we can understand a historical era only once it has passed.

</Step>

<Step title="Marx turns Hegel on his head" kind="explain">

**Karl Marx** (1818–1883) had a deep but combative relationship with Hegel. He admired the dialectical method — but thought Hegel had it exactly backwards. For Hegel, ideas drive history; material and economic conditions are secondary. For Marx, it's the reverse: **material/economic conditions are the base; ideas, culture, religion, and law are the superstructure built upon them**.

This "inversion" of Hegel is the core of **historical materialism**. The key claim: the way a society organises its production — who owns the tools, land, and factories; how labour is divided; what class relations exist — determines its culture, laws, and ideas, not the other way around. The dominant ideas of any era are the ideas of the dominant class.

Marx applies the dialectic to history itself. Each mode of production (feudalism, capitalism) contains internal contradictions that generate class conflict. Feudalism's internal tensions produced capitalism; capitalism's internal contradictions — the clash between the bourgeoisie (owners of capital) and the proletariat (owners only of their labour) — would, Marx believed, eventually produce a further synthesis beyond both.

Marx also introduced the concept of **alienation**: under capitalism, workers become estranged from the products of their labour (owned by the employer), from the act of production (controlled by the employer), from other workers (in competition), and from their own human potential. What should be a creative, fulfilling activity becomes a means of mere survival.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Hegel's dialectic**: history advances through thesis → antithesis → synthesis, driven by the internal tensions within ideas and social forms.
- **Absolute Spirit** (*Geist*): Hegel's name for the collective self-consciousness of humanity unfolding through history.
- **Marx's inversion**: kept the dialectic but flipped the driver — material and economic conditions shape ideas, not vice versa. This is **historical materialism**.
- **Class conflict** is the engine of historical change: feudal lords vs. serfs → bourgeoisie vs. proletariat → (Marx predicted) a classless society.
- **Alienation**: capitalism estranges workers from their labour, their product, each other, and their human potential.
- Both thinkers saw history as having a *direction* — not random, but driven by an internal logic.

</Step>
