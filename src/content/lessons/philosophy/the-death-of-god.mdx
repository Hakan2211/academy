<Step title="The madman in the marketplace" kind="explain">

In 1882 Friedrich Nietzsche wrote one of the most arresting passages in philosophy: a madman runs into a marketplace carrying a lantern at noon, crying "I seek God!" The crowd laughs. Then he silences them: **"God is dead. God remains dead. And we have killed him."**

Nietzsche was not writing theology. He was writing cultural diagnosis. The "death of God" means the collapse of the entire edifice of values, meanings, and certainties that European civilization had erected on a religious foundation — not just church attendance, but the whole framework: objective moral order, cosmic purpose, a built-in answer to "why does any of this matter?"

The madman then asks the crowd: do they realise what they have done? **"Is there still any up or down? Are we not straying as through an infinite nothing?"** He is not celebrating. He is terrified at what the loss of that foundation means.

</Step>

<Step title="The danger: nihilism" kind="explain">

The immediate philosophical consequence of the death of God, Nietzsche argued, is **nihilism** — the view that life has no meaning, no value, no purpose. If the old framework is gone and nothing replaces it, we are left in a valueless void.

Nietzsche did not *endorse* nihilism. He diagnosed it as the great disease of modernity — a sickness that would sweep through the coming centuries. "What I relate," he wrote, "is the history of the next two centuries." He saw nihilism as a problem to be *overcome*, not a resting place.

He distinguished two forms:
- **Passive nihilism** — giving up, retreating into comfort, "last men" who have abandoned greatness and blink in the sun.
- **Active nihilism** — the creative destruction that clears away old values precisely *in order to* build new ones.

The diagnosis is dark; the intended response is not.

</Step>

<Step title="The answer: create your own values" kind="explain">

Nietzsche's response to nihilism is not despair but transformation. He calls for the **revaluation of all values** — a radical act of creation. His vision is the **Übermensch** (often translated "overman" or "superhuman"): a figure who does not mourn the lost God but takes up the task of creating meaning from the ground up, through strength and affirmation rather than resentment and flight.

Central to this is the **will to power** — not domination over others, but the drive toward self-mastery, growth, and creative expression. The will to power is what pushes the artist to create, the philosopher to think harder, the human being to become more fully alive.

Nietzsche also contrasts **master morality** (values created by the strong, celebrating excellence and life-affirmation) with **slave morality** (values born of resentment, reframing weakness as virtue — meekness, humility, pity). He argues that much of conventional morality is slave morality in disguise, and that truly free individuals must create their own scale of values.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **"God is dead"** — Nietzsche's diagnosis that the religious foundation of Western values has collapsed, leaving a void.
- **Nihilism** — the dangerous consequence: the sense that nothing has meaning or value. Nietzsche presents this as a problem to overcome, not a conclusion to embrace.
- **Revaluation of all values** — his answer: clear away inherited values and *create* new ones from a position of strength.
- **The Übermensch** — the vision of a human being who creates meaning rather than inheriting it.
- **Will to power** — the drive toward self-mastery and creative growth.
- **Master vs slave morality** — life-affirming excellence vs resentment-born meekness.

</Step>
