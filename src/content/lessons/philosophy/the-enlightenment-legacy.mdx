<Step title="Dare to know" kind="explain">

In 1784 Kant asked: "What is Enlightenment?" His answer became its motto: *Sapere aude* — **dare to know**. Have the courage to use your own understanding without the guidance of another. The Enlightenment (roughly 1680–1800) was not a single doctrine but a shared commitment: reason, observation, and open inquiry can liberate humanity from superstition, tyranny, and ignorance.

The movement stretched across Europe's greatest minds. In France, the *philosophes* — Voltaire, Montesquieu, Diderot — subjected religion, monarchy, and tradition to merciless rational scrutiny. Voltaire attacked Church corruption and religious persecution with wit so sharp it could draw blood. Diderot and d'Alembert compiled the *Encyclopédie* — an attempt to gather all human knowledge in one place, the project of Enlightenment in a box.

In Scotland, David Hume and Adam Smith applied empirical method to the human world itself: Hume to human nature and morality, Smith to the economy. Across the Atlantic, Enlightenment ideas poured directly into political documents: Jefferson's "all men are created equal" and are endowed with inalienable rights is Locke's political philosophy rephrased for a Declaration.

The Enlightenment's core commitments:

- **Reason** over authority and revelation as the arbiter of truth.
- **Science** as the method for understanding nature — and eventually human nature and society.
- **Progress**: humanity can improve its condition through knowledge and reform.
- **Liberty**: individuals have natural rights that governments may not override; legitimate authority requires the consent of the governed.
- **Universalism**: these truths apply to all human beings, not just to particular nations or faiths.

</Step>

<Step title="The social contract and political liberty" kind="explain">

The Enlightenment's most explosive political legacy was the theory of the **social contract** — the idea that legitimate government rests on the consent of the governed, not on divine right or hereditary privilege.

The tradition runs from Hobbes through Locke to Rousseau, but each theorist tells a different story.

**Hobbes** (1588–1679): in the state of nature — life without government — existence is "solitary, poor, nasty, brutish, and short." Rational self-interest drives us to surrender our natural freedom to a sovereign who can enforce peace. The contract is stark: obedience in exchange for security.

**Locke** (1632–1704): in the state of nature, people already have rights — to life, liberty, and property. Government is formed to protect these pre-existing rights more effectively. If it fails — if it tramples those rights — the people have the right to revolt. This is the philosophical DNA of the American and Glorious Revolutions.

**Rousseau** (1712–1778): here the story turns darker. Rousseau agreed that legitimate government requires consent — the "general will" of the people — but he was the Enlightenment's fiercest internal critic. Civilisation, he argued, far from freeing us, has corrupted us. "Man is born free, and everywhere he is in chains." The accumulation of property creates inequality; arts and sciences produce vanity and dependence, not virtue. The primitive (pre-social) human was healthier and freer than the "civilised" European.

Rousseau's critique planted the seed of the Enlightenment's discontents.

</Step>

<Step title="The critics: Romanticism and the limits of reason" kind="explain">

Even as the Enlightenment reached its peak, a counter-movement was gathering: **Romanticism**. Where the Enlightenment valued reason, universality, and scientific method, the Romantics valued **feeling, imagination, particularity, and organic wholeness**.

The Romantic critique of Enlightenment took several forms:

**Rousseau's legacy**: civilisation corrupts natural virtue; reason divorced from feeling produces cold, mechanical human beings. The heart has its reasons that reason knows nothing of.

**Burke's conservatism**: Edmund Burke (1729–1797), horrified by the French Revolution, argued that abstract rational principles are a dangerous guide to political life. Inherited institutions — the common law, tradition, the accumulated wisdom of generations — carry more practical knowledge than any system built from scratch by philosophers.

**The Romantics proper** (Wordsworth, Coleridge, Schiller, the Schlegels): nature is not a machine to be analysed but a living whole to be experienced and reverenced. Reason fragments; imagination unifies. The scientist's cold dissection misses what matters most about a flower. Art and poetry grasp truths reason cannot.

**Hegel himself** was partly a Romantic response to Enlightenment atomism: reality is not a collection of isolated facts but a living whole — an organic process. And his student Marx carried a critique of Enlightenment liberalism further: the "rational" individual of Locke and Smith is a historical abstraction, born of capitalism, not a universal truth.

The debate was never fully resolved. Later thinkers — Nietzsche, Freud, the Frankfurt School — would raise it again. Does reason liberate, or does it also dominate? Can "progress" be turned toward catastrophe? The Enlightenment gave modernity its confidence; its critics gave modernity its conscience.

</Step>

<Step title="Explore the a priori/a posteriori divide" kind="interactive">

One of the Enlightenment's central intellectual contributions was clarifying the difference between truths of reason and truths of observation — a distinction that sits at the heart of debates between rationalists and empiricists, and between Kant and his critics.

<RationalismEmpiricism />

This distinction matters for Enlightenment politics too. Are there truths about human rights knowable by reason alone — or are they contingent facts about particular cultures and histories? The Enlightenment said the former; its critics increasingly said the latter.

</Step>
