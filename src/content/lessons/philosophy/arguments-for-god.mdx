<Step title="A philosophical note before we begin" kind="explain">

<Callout type="info" title="These are philosophical arguments, not verdicts">
This lesson presents the classical arguments for God's existence as philosophical arguments — structures of reasoning to evaluate, not conclusions to accept or reject. These arguments have been found compelling by thoughtful people across many traditions, and have been challenged by thoughtful people equally. The goal is philosophical literacy: understanding how the arguments work, where their force lies, and where they are contested. This lesson holds deep respect for people of faith and for those without it alike.
</Callout>

The existence of God is one of the oldest and most intensively debated questions in philosophy. The three arguments explored here — the cosmological, the teleological, and the ontological — represent centuries of careful reasoning. Each has been defended and challenged by philosophers of the first rank. Evaluating them well is itself a philosophical skill.

</Step>

<Step title="The cosmological argument" kind="explain">

The **cosmological argument** begins from the existence of the universe and asks: why is there something rather than nothing?

The most influential version is Thomas Aquinas's **argument from contingency**. Everything that exists is *contingent* — it might not have existed, and its existence depends on prior causes. But an infinite chain of contingent causes explains nothing: if everything in the chain is borrowed existence, where does the existence come from? There must be, Aquinas argues, a **necessary being** — something whose existence is not borrowed from anything else, but which exists of its own nature. This being, which is the uncaused cause of all contingent things, is what we mean by God.

Leibniz offered a similar argument from the **principle of sufficient reason**: everything must have a sufficient explanation for its existence or non-existence. The universe as a whole needs an explanation. That explanation cannot be within the universe (since the universe is what we are explaining), so it must be external — a transcendent necessary being.

The main objections: Why can the universe itself not be the necessary being? Why does the first cause need to be the God of any particular tradition — omnipotent, personal, loving? And David Hume questioned whether the principle of sufficient reason applies to existence at all.

</Step>

<Step title="The teleological argument and its replies" kind="interactive">

The **teleological argument** (from the Greek *telos* — purpose, end) argues from the apparent order and design in the universe to a designer. William Paley's watchmaker analogy (1802) made it famous; contemporary versions focus on the extraordinary **fine-tuning** of the universe's physical constants. <Step title="The ontological argument" kind="explain">

The **ontological argument** is the most philosophically daring — it tries to prove God's existence from the concept of God alone, without any appeal to observation.

Anselm of Canterbury (1033–1109) defined God as "that than which nothing greater can be conceived." Such a being, if it existed only in our minds (only as a concept), would not be the greatest conceivable being — because a being that existed in reality would be greater. Therefore, the greatest conceivable being must exist in reality as well as in the mind. *That* being is God.

Descartes offered a similar version: existence is a perfection; a supremely perfect being must have all perfections; therefore a supremely perfect being exists.

The most famous objection is Kant's: **existence is not a predicate**. When we say "dogs are mammals," we say something about the nature of dogs. But when we say "dogs exist," we are not adding a new property to the concept of a dog — we are saying that the concept is instantiated. Existence cannot be a "perfection" added to a concept, because it is logically prior to properties. If this is right, the ontological argument confuses logic and metaphysics.

Gaunilo (a contemporary of Anselm) offered a parody: I can conceive of a "greatest conceivable island." Does that island therefore exist? If not, something is wrong with the argument.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Cosmological argument**: everything contingent requires a cause; an infinite regress explains nothing; therefore a necessary, uncaused first cause (God) must exist.
- **Teleological argument**: the universe exhibits complex purposive order (Paley's watchmaker; fine-tuning of physical constants); such order is best explained by a designer.
- Main replies to design: Hume's weak analogy objection; Darwin's natural selection as an undesigned source of apparent design; the anthropic/multiverse reply to fine-tuning.
- **Ontological argument**: God is the greatest conceivable being; a being that existed only in the mind would be surpassed by one that also existed in reality; therefore God exists in reality.
- Kant's objection to the ontological argument: existence is not a predicate — you cannot add existence to a concept as a perfection.
- All three arguments remain live topics in philosophy of religion, with serious defenders and serious critics.

</Step>
