<Step title="The harm principle" kind="explain">

Freedom is universally valued — but it is also limited. Every law restricts what someone can do. The question political philosophy must answer is: **when is restricting freedom justified?**

John Stuart Mill, in his 1859 essay *On Liberty*, gave one of the most influential answers ever written. The **harm principle** states:

> "The only purpose for which power can be rightfully exercised over any member of a civilised community, against his will, is to prevent harm to others."

The crucial word is *others*. Mill drew a sharp line between two kinds of conduct:

- **Self-regarding acts** — things you do that affect only yourself. Here the state has no business interfering, no matter how unwise or unhealthy your choice might be.
- **Other-regarding acts** — things you do that harm people who have not consented. Here the state may legitimately step in.

This is a radical position. It rules out **paternalism** (restricting your freedom "for your own good") and **moralism** (restricting your freedom because others find it offensive or immoral). Offence at an idea or lifestyle is not harm under Mill's principle. Only actual harm to a non-consenting other triggers legitimate restriction.

<Step title="Negative vs positive liberty" kind="explain">

Isaiah Berlin's famous 1958 lecture introduced a distinction that sharpens the debate:

**Negative liberty** is **freedom from interference** — the absence of external obstacles, coercion, or restraint. When no one stops you from doing something, you have negative liberty to do it. This is Mill's primary concern.

**Positive liberty** is **freedom to** — the actual power or capacity to realise your goals. A starving person is not prevented by law from eating at a fine restaurant, but they are not *really* free to do so. Positive liberty asks whether you have the real conditions — resources, capabilities, education — to exercise your choices meaningfully.

The distinction matters enormously in practice:

- A strict **negative-liberty** view limits government to removing interference: repeal bad laws, prevent coercion.
- A **positive-liberty** view may justify government action to *provide* conditions for flourishing: fund education, healthcare, housing — because without them, formal freedom is hollow.

Critics of positive liberty worry it can be weaponised: authoritarian regimes have claimed to liberate people from "false consciousness" or poverty in order to justify imposing their preferred way of life. Berlin saw this risk and was cautious. Defenders reply that ignoring real barriers to freedom is itself a political choice that happens to favour the already-advantaged.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- Mill's **harm principle**: the only legitimate basis for restricting liberty is to prevent **harm to others** — not self-harm, not offence, not immorality.
- **Self-regarding acts** (affecting only yourself) are protected; **other-regarding acts** (harming non-consenting others) may be restricted.
- **Paternalism** — restricting freedom for someone's own good — is ruled out by the harm principle.
- **Negative liberty** = freedom from interference; **positive liberty** = real capacity to act on your choices.
- Positive liberty can justify state provision of conditions for flourishing; it can also be misused to justify imposition — hence the ongoing debate.

</Step>
