<Step title="The relativist's starting observation" kind="explain">

Travel far enough and you encounter radically different moral codes. In some societies, leaving elders to die alone is the kindest way to honour them; in others it is abandonment. In some traditions, premarital sex is a grave moral wrong; in others it is morally neutral. Practices that count as hospitality here count as rudeness there.

**Cultural relativism** takes this observation seriously and turns it into a theory: what is morally right or wrong is *relative to a culture*. There is no culture-independent standpoint from which to judge. "Female circumcision is wrong" — said by an outsider — is just "female circumcision is wrong *by my culture's standards*." And by the local standards it is right. Neither verdict is more correct than the other, because there is no neutral place to stand.

The appeal is real. Relativism seems to:

- Honour humility: acknowledging that we might be wrong, not just different.
- Promote tolerance: restraining the urge to impose our values on others.
- Reflect the anthropological fact of moral diversity.

</Step>

<Step title="The reformer's dilemma" kind="explain">

Cultural relativism runs into a sharp problem: it makes moral reform incoherent.

Suppose you lived in a slaveholding society in 1800. The dominant moral code endorses slavery. If morality is relative to culture, then *by the culture's own standards* the abolitionists were morally *wrong* — they were violating the prevailing code. Yet we strongly feel the abolitionists were *right*, even though they were in the minority.

This is the **reformer's dilemma**: relativism cannot coherently honour its own heroes. Every great moral reformer — abolitionists, suffragists, those who resisted genocide — was, by definition, acting against the dominant moral code of their own culture. If that code constitutes what is right, reformers are always, by definition, wrong.

A further problem: relativism seems to entail that we *cannot* criticise any cultural practice from the outside. But taken seriously, this means we cannot call slavery, torture, or genocide wrong when they occur in cultures that endorse them — only "wrong by our standards." Most people find this conclusion unacceptable.

**Universalism**, by contrast, holds that at least some moral truths hold across cultures — not because one culture's traditions are superior, but because there are reasons (suffering, dignity, fairness) that don't depend on local convention.

</Step>

<Step title="A middle path?" kind="explain">

Most contemporary moral philosophers are neither pure relativists nor arrogant universalists. Several nuanced positions have emerged:

**Minimal universalism** holds that some moral claims are universal — prohibitions on gratuitous torture, for instance — while much of morality is legitimately culturally variable (etiquette, role-based duties, sexual norms within consenting communities).

**Strong evaluation** (Charles Taylor) says we are always already embedded in moral frameworks, but we can critically reflect on them and compare frameworks in ways that are not simply projections of our own.

**Cross-cultural overlapping consensus** (inspired by Rawls) seeks moral principles that diverse cultures can converge on for different reasons — a shared conclusion without requiring a shared foundation.

Relativism's insights — humility, attention to diversity, caution about cultural imperialism — are genuine. But the full relativist thesis, that there are *no* cross-cultural moral truths, seems to prove too much. The challenge is to honour the insights without accepting the most damaging conclusions.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Cultural relativism**: what is right or wrong is relative to a culture — there is no neutral standpoint.
- Appeals: humility, tolerance, recognition of genuine diversity.
- **The reformer's dilemma**: relativism makes moral reform incoherent — reformers are always "wrong by their own culture's code."
- **Cannot call anything universally wrong**: on strict relativism, no practice in any culture can be condemned from the outside.
- **Universalism**: some moral truths hold across cultures — grounded in reasons (suffering, dignity) that transcend local convention.
- Most philosophers seek a nuanced middle: humility about cultural variation, with a minimal floor of universal moral constraints.

</Step>
