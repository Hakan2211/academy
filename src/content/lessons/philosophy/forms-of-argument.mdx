<Step title="The conditional and its four children" kind="explain">

Much of everyday reasoning pivots on a **conditional statement**: "If P, then Q." Conditionals encode causal links, rules, and dependencies — "if it rains, the ground gets wet," "if you press the button, the alarm sounds," "if all humans are mortal and Socrates is human, Socrates is mortal."

A conditional has two parts:
- **P** — the *antecedent* (the "if" part)
- **Q** — the *consequent* (the "then" part)

The conditional alone doesn't tell you whether P or Q are true. It only says: *whenever P, you get Q.* To draw a conclusion, you need a second premise. And crucially, **which second premise you add determines which of four argument forms you get** — two valid, two fallacious.

</Step>

<Step title="The two valid forms" kind="explain">

**Modus Ponens** ("the way that affirms by affirming"):

<Formula tex="P \rightarrow Q \quad P \quad \therefore \; Q" block />

You have the conditional. You confirm the antecedent. You conclude the consequent. If it rains, the ground gets wet. It rained. Therefore the ground is wet. Airtight.

**Modus Tollens** ("the way that denies by denying"):

<Formula tex="P \rightarrow Q \quad \neg Q \quad \therefore \; \neg P" block />

You have the conditional. You discover the consequent is false. You conclude the antecedent must also be false. If it rains, the ground gets wet. The ground is *not* wet. Therefore it did not rain. This runs the conditional *backwards* — a contrapositive move, and equally valid.

<Callout type="tip" title="Mnemonic">
Modus **P**onens affirms **P**. Modus **T**ollens **t**olls (negates) — the consequent, then the antecedent.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="The two formal fallacies" kind="explain">

**Affirming the Consequent** (invalid):

<Formula tex="P \rightarrow Q \quad Q \quad \therefore \; P \;\; \text{(fallacy!)}" block />

The ground is wet, therefore it rained. But the ground could be wet because of a sprinkler, dew, or a spilled bucket. Knowing Q doesn't pin down P as the cause. The *form* is broken regardless of the content.

**Denying the Antecedent** (invalid):

<Formula tex="P \rightarrow Q \quad \neg P \quad \therefore \; \neg Q \;\; \text{(fallacy!)}" block />

It didn't rain, therefore the ground isn't wet. But again — the ground could be wet from other sources. The conditional says rain *suffices* for wet ground; it doesn't say rain is the *only* way to get wet ground.

<Callout type="warn" title="These fallacies feel valid — that's what makes them dangerous">
Both fallacies appear in real debates constantly. Politicians, advertisers, and even scientists sometimes argue: "if our policy works, crime falls; crime fell; therefore our policy worked." That's affirming the consequent. Learn to spot it.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- A **conditional** (P → Q) pairs an antecedent P with a consequent Q.
- **Modus Ponens**: P → Q, P ∴ Q — **valid**. Affirm the antecedent, get the consequent.
- **Modus Tollens**: P → Q, ¬Q ∴ ¬P — **valid**. Deny the consequent, deny the antecedent.
- **Affirming the Consequent**: P → Q, Q ∴ P — **fallacy**. Q can have many causes.
- **Denying the Antecedent**: P → Q, ¬P ∴ ¬Q — **fallacy**. Other paths to Q may exist.
- Both fallacies *feel* valid because they resemble valid forms — which makes them the most dangerous reasoning errors in practice.

</Step>
