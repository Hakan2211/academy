<Step title="The puzzle of persistence" kind="explain">

You are not the same collection of atoms you were ten years ago — most of your cells have been replaced. You don't share many of the beliefs, desires, or memories of your childhood self. In some sense you are radically different from the person who shared your name at age five.

And yet: we treat you as the same person. You are held responsible for debts you incurred five years ago. Your future self will receive the pension you are saving for today. Courts punish people for crimes committed long before their trial. All of this assumes personal identity over time is real and robust.

So: **what makes you the same person across time?** This is the problem of personal identity, and it turns out to be genuinely difficult. The three main answers — body, brain, and psychological continuity — each face serious challenges.

</Step>

<Step title="Locke's memory theory" kind="explain">

John Locke proposed that **memory** is the key to personal identity. You are the same person as your past self insofar as you can remember that past self's experiences as your own. The soldier who remembers the battle as a battle he fought is the same person as the young boy who was flogged for stealing — if he remembers the flogging.

This captures something important: when we think about whether someone should be held responsible for a past act, we naturally ask whether they remember it. Severe amnesia seems to sever the connection between a person and their past self in a practically relevant way.

But Locke's theory faces a problem Reid pointed out: imagine an old general who remembers winning a battle as a young officer, and that same young officer remembered being flogged as a boy — but the old general has no memory of being flogged. By Locke's theory, the general IS the officer (he remembers it) and the officer IS the boy (he remembers it) — but the general is NOT the boy. Personal identity becomes non-transitive, which seems absurd.

Derek Parfit refined this to **psychological continuity** (not just memory, but continuity of beliefs, desires, intentions, personality) — which avoids some problems but faces others.

</Step>

<Step title="The teleporter thought experiment" kind="interactive">

Science-fiction thought experiments are powerful tools for testing theories of personal identity, because they peel apart factors (psychological continuity, physical continuity) that normally go together.

<TeleporterPuzzle />

The teleporter experiment asks: if you are destroyed and a psychologically identical copy is created elsewhere, have *you* survived? The malfunction variant — where both the original and the copy exist — is especially revealing. If psychological continuity is sufficient for identity, both copies are equally "you," which leads to contradiction (identity is a one-one relation). This suggests psychological continuity cannot be the whole story.

</Step>

<Step title="What Parfit concluded" kind="explain">

Derek Parfit took the teleporter-style puzzles seriously and drew a radical conclusion: **identity is not what matters**. What matters for our practical concerns — caring about our future selves, being held responsible — is **psychological continuity and connectedness**, not strict numerical identity.

Parfit suggested that personal identity might be like heap-of-sand (Sorites) cases: there may be no sharp fact of the matter about whether you are the "same" person across time. What there are are facts about degrees of psychological connectedness — and those are what ground our practices of concern and responsibility.

This view is liberating in one way (we should care less about personal survival and more about the wellbeing of psychologically connected future people generally) and unsettling in another (the self we instinctively protect is less robust than it seems).

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- Personal identity asks what makes you the *same person* across time — crucial for responsibility, prudence, and care for one's future self.
- **Body / brain continuity**: you persist as long as your body (especially brain) persists. Problem: normal biological change means no sharp cutoff; the teleporter shows this can come apart from psychology.
- **Locke's memory theory**: you are the same person as past selves you remember. Problem: fails transitivity (Reid's objection).
- **Psychological continuity** (Parfit): broader — continuity of memories, beliefs, desires, personality. Problem: duplication — two psychologically continuous successors can't both be strictly identical to you.
- **Parfit's radical conclusion**: identity may not be what matters; what matters is psychological continuity, which can come in degrees and survive splitting.

</Step>
