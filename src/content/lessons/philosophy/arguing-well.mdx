<Step title="Argument is not combat" kind="explain">

In everyday English, "having an argument" usually means a conflict — raised voices, hurt feelings, someone "winning" by grinding the other person down. Philosophy uses the word differently. An **argument** is a set of reasons offered in support of a conclusion. Arguing, properly understood, is the most cooperative intellectual activity humans do: two minds, each contributing what they know, trying to get closer to the truth together.

That framing immediately transforms what counts as success. In the combat model, success means your opponent goes quiet. In the collaborative model, success means **the best idea wins** — even if it was your opponent's.

Three principles underpin genuinely good argument:

**1. The principle of charity** — when interpreting someone else's position, assume the strongest, most reasonable version they could mean, not the weakest. Before you respond, ask: *"Is there a reading of this that makes it genuinely interesting or defensible?"* Charity isn't about being soft. It's about ensuring you're actually engaging with the idea rather than a shadow puppet.

**2. Steelmanning vs strawmanning** — the straw man is the weakest, easiest-to-attack version of an opposing argument. The **steel man** is the *strongest, most sophisticated, most well-evidenced version*. You don't have to agree with the steel man. But if you can't defeat the steel man, you should update your beliefs, not declare victory over the straw version.

**3. Epistemic humility** — genuine argument requires holding your own conclusions provisionally. If you've decided in advance that you are right and the goal is to force the other person to admit it, you're not arguing — you're performing. Humility doesn't mean thinking all views are equally valid; it means remaining genuinely open to being surprised by evidence.

</Step>

<Step title="Fallacies in the wild" kind="explain">

Once you can spot logical fallacies, real-world rhetoric looks different. A few patterns recur constantly:

**In political debate** — politicians rarely engage the strongest version of opposing policy. They straw-man, pivot via red herring, and use emotional language to short-circuit evaluation. None of this proves their policies are wrong — but it does mean you should look elsewhere for the actual arguments.

**In advertising** — appeals to authority (celebrity endorsements, white-coated "experts"), false dilemmas ("the only deodorant that really works"), and emotional appeals are the stock-in-trade. Advertising is not designed to help you reason; it's designed to move you to action.

**In social media** — confirmation bias and the outrage economy combine lethally. Algorithms favour the emotionally arousing, which means straw men and ad hominem attacks spread faster than careful analysis. The viral quote is rarely the charitable interpretation.

**In your own head** — motivated reasoning means you are most vulnerable to accepting bad arguments on topics you care about deeply. The person who can spot every fallacy in opposing political speeches and miss the same fallacies in their own side is the rule, not the exception.

<Callout type="tip" title="The reversal test">

When you evaluate an argument, ask: *"Would I accept this line of reasoning if it pointed to a conclusion I found uncomfortable?"* If yes — it's probably legitimate. If no — you may have a double standard. This is the single most reliable personal check on motivated reasoning.

</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="How to disagree productively" kind="explain">

Philosopher Daniel Dennett adapted Graham's **Hierarchy of Disagreement** into a set of practical norms. Here they are, from weakest to strongest:

- **Name-calling** — the lowest form. Attacking the person, not the idea.
- **Ad hominem** — attacking the arguer's character or credentials.
- **Contradiction** — simply asserting the opposite with equal confidence.
- **Counterargument** — offering reasons the claim is false. Better, but you need to engage the *strongest* version.
- **Refutation** — finding a specific flaw in the argument's logic or evidence, and showing why it matters.
- **Refutation of the central point** — identifying *the core* of the argument and specifically dismantling it.

The top of the hierarchy requires you to understand the argument you're attacking so well that you can identify its load-bearing assumption. That requires the principle of charity and the steelman.

A practical implication: when you're in a heated disagreement, try to spend thirty seconds stating the opposing view *in its best form* before responding. This is uncomfortable — but it consistently leads to better outcomes, including discovering that you were wrong about something important.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Argument is cooperative**, not combative — the goal is that the best idea wins, not that one person wins.
- The **principle of charity**: always assume the strongest reasonable interpretation of an opposing view before responding.
- **Steelmanning**: engage the *strongest* version of an argument, not the weakest (the straw man).
- **Epistemic humility**: hold your own conclusions provisionally — genuine openness to being wrong is a feature, not a weakness.
- Fallacies appear everywhere: political speeches use straw men and red herrings; advertising deploys false dilemmas and emotional appeals; social media amplifies the outrage-maximizing version of any claim.
- The **reversal test**: ask whether you'd accept the same reasoning if it pointed toward a conclusion you disliked. That asymmetry is motivated reasoning in action.
- Productive disagreement means engaging the steel man — and updating your beliefs if you can't defeat it.

</Step>
