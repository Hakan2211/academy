<Step title="It is not about the act — it is about the person" kind="explain">

Consequentialism asks: *what are the outcomes?* Deontology asks: *what are my duties?* Virtue ethics asks a different question entirely: **what kind of person should I be?**

This approach, rooted in **Aristotle** (384–322 BCE), starts not from rules or calculations but from **character**. Morality, for Aristotle, is about cultivating the traits that allow a human being to live and function excellently — to flourish.

The key concept is **eudaimonia** — often translated as happiness, but better rendered as *flourishing* or *living well*. Eudaimonia is not a feeling; it is a condition of a life lived in accordance with virtue. A person who is courageous, just, honest, and practically wise is flourishing — even in hardship — because they are being what a human being is meant to be.

</Step>

<Step title="Virtues, vices, and the golden mean" kind="explain">

A **virtue** is an excellence of character. It is a settled disposition — a stable tendency to feel, choose, and act in the right way, developed through habit. Aristotle's key insight is the **doctrine of the mean**: every virtue lies between two corresponding vices, one of excess and one of deficiency.

Courage, for example, is the mean between **cowardice** (too little risk-acceptance) and **recklessness** (too much). Generosity lies between **stinginess** and **extravagance**. Honesty lies between reticence and boastfulness.

The virtuous person does not merely act correctly — they feel the right emotions at the right time in the right degree. The brave person does not feel no fear (that would be recklessness); they feel appropriate fear and act well despite it. Moral education is precisely the process of training one's feelings to align with one's judgments.

Crucially, virtues are not hit by following a rule or running a calculation. They are acquired by **practice**: you become courageous by doing courageous things; honest by telling the truth; just by making just decisions, again and again, until these become second nature.

</Step>

Notice that the mean is not simply the mathematical midpoint. It is the *right amount for the right person in the right circumstances* — which requires **practical wisdom** (phronesis) to identify. The virtuous person knows not just the general principle but how to apply it to this situation, with this person, at this time.

</Step>

<Step title="Practical wisdom and community" kind="explain">

The central intellectual virtue in Aristotle's ethics is **phronesis** — practical wisdom. It is the capacity to discern what is genuinely good in concrete situations, and to act accordingly. A moral rulebook cannot replace it; it is a skill developed through experience and reflection.

Aristotle also stressed that virtue does not arise in isolation. Humans are by nature **political animals** — social beings who flourish in community. The *polis* (city-state) is not a contract entered for self-interest; it is the environment in which virtue is cultivated, exercised, and recognised.

**Objections.** Virtue ethics can seem too vague — "be courageous" does not tell you exactly what to do on Tuesday. It can also seem culturally relative: different societies prize different virtues. And Aristotle's own catalogue included virtues that reflect the biases of his time (the great-souled man is proud and aristocratic). Modern virtue ethicists try to give a more universal account.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Virtue ethics** asks: what kind of person should I be? It focuses on character, not rules or outcomes.
- **Eudaimonia**: flourishing — the goal of a life lived in accordance with virtue.
- **Virtue**: a settled disposition to feel, choose, and act well; developed through habit.
- **The golden mean**: every virtue lies between a vice of deficiency and a vice of excess.
- **Phronesis** (practical wisdom): the capacity to discern the right course in concrete situations — irreducible to any formula.
- Virtues are acquired by practice — you become courageous by acting courageously.

</Step>
