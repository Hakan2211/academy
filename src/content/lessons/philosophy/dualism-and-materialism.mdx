<Step title="Descartes' radical split" kind="explain">

René Descartes is the philosopher most associated with **substance dualism**: the view that reality contains two fundamentally different kinds of substance. There is *res extensa* — extended matter, everything that occupies space, including the brain. And there is *res cogitans* — thinking substance, the mind or soul, which has no size, no location, no weight.

Descartes thought this was forced on him by pure reflection. He could doubt that he had a body — maybe it was all an illusion. But he could not doubt that *something* was doing the doubting. That something — the pure thinking thing — must be distinct from the body it doubts.

This gives us the famous **Cogito**: *I think, therefore I am*. But it also gives us a brutal problem. If mind and body are truly distinct substances, how do they ever interact? When you decide to raise your arm, how does an immaterial thought cause a physical arm to move? Descartes suggested the pineal gland as the interface — an answer nobody found convincing, and which only pushes the question back.

</Step>

<Step title="The materialist counter" kind="explain">

**Physicalism** (or materialism) says there is only one kind of substance — physical matter — and the mind is entirely part of it. Mental states just *are* brain states. Pain is the firing of C-fibres. Believing that it's raining is a particular pattern of neural activation.

This is the identity theory: mental-state types are identical to physical-state types. It is elegant, scientifically respectable, and sidesteps the interaction problem entirely. Mind and body don't need to "communicate" because they are the same thing described two ways.

The challenge: it seems to leave something out. Even if pain = C-fibre firing, that physical description doesn't capture what pain *feels like*. The felt quality — the "what it's like" — appears to slip through the net of any purely physical account.

</Step>

<Step title="Explore the positions" kind="interactive">

Beyond dualism and basic physicalism, philosophers developed two further positions that try to capture mind naturalistically without reducing it to simple brain states.

<MindBodyPositions />

**Behaviorism** (Gilbert Ryle's version is philosophical, not psychological) holds that mental talk is just talk about dispositions to behave. To believe it is raining is to be disposed to carry an umbrella, say "it's raining," act accordingly. There is no inner mental entity — just patterns of potential behavior.

**Functionalism** goes further: mental states are defined by what they *do* — their causal role — not what they are made of. Pain is whatever state is caused by tissue damage, causes distress, causes avoidance behavior, and interacts with beliefs and desires in the right way. If silicon chips could play that role, they'd be in pain.

</Step>

<Step title="The multiple realizability argument" kind="explain">

Functionalism's most powerful argument is **multiple realizability**. If the identity theory is right, then pain = C-fibre firing *means* any creature without C-fibres cannot feel pain. But that seems wrong — we naturally attribute pain to animals with very different neural architectures, and arguably could build machines that feel pain.

Functionalism lets pain be multiply realised: the same mental state can be implemented in neurons, silicon, or whatever plays the right functional role. The mind is like software — what matters is the program, not the specific hardware.

The functionalist's residual problem is the **absent qualia** objection: imagine a system that plays all the right functional roles — inputs trigger the right outputs, states interact as pain and discomfort should — but there is no inner experience at all. Functionally perfect, experientially empty. Is that really a mind?

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Substance dualism** (Descartes): mind and body are distinct substances — res cogitans and res extensa. Problem: how do they interact?
- **Physicalism / identity theory**: mental states are identical to brain states. Problem: seems to miss the felt quality of experience (qualia).
- **Behaviorism**: mental states are dispositions to behave. Problem: behavior can be faked; inner states seem to matter.
- **Functionalism**: mental states are defined by their causal role. Allows **multiple realizability** — the same mind could run on different hardware. Problem: absent qualia — functional role without experience.
- No view has won the debate; each makes a trade-off between naturalising the mind and honoring subjective experience.

</Step>
