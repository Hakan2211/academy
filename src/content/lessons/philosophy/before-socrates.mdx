<Step title="The first question" kind="explain">

Around 585 BCE, on the coast of what is now Turkey, a man named Thales did something no one had quite done before. He looked at the bewildering variety of things in the world — earth, water, fire, flesh, bone, cloud — and asked: **what is all of this, ultimately, made of?**

Not "which god made it?" but "what is the underlying stuff?" This single move — replacing myth with natural explanation — marks the birth of philosophy and of science. The thinkers who followed Thales before Socrates are called the **Presocratics**, and their question was the arche question: what is the fundamental stuff (the *arche*) of reality?

Each gave a bold, confident answer. Each was wrong about the specifics. Yet together they discovered the right way of asking.

</Step>

<Step title="Meet the Presocratics" kind="interactive">

Five thinkers, five answers, one question that still drives physics today. The Heraclitus–Parmenides debate — **does reality change, or is change an illusion?** — is the headline clash that every philosopher after them had to resolve.

</Step>

<Step title="Where they stood in time" kind="explain">

The Presocratics worked across roughly 200 years, from Thales to Democritus. After them came Socrates — who pivoted from "what is reality made of?" to "how should a person live?" — and the axis of philosophy shifted from cosmology to ethics and epistemology.

<PhilosophyTimeline era="ancient" />

Notice how compressed the sequence is: Thales, Heraclitus, Parmenides, Democritus, Socrates, Plato, Aristotle, Epicurus — all within two and a half centuries, in a corner of the Mediterranean.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- The Presocratics were the first to give **natural explanations** for nature — replacing myth with reason.
- Each sought the **arche**: the fundamental substance or principle underlying all things.
- Thales said water; Anaximenes said air; Heraclitus said fire / flux; Parmenides said changeless being; Democritus said atoms + void.
- The **Heraclitus–Parmenides clash** (change is fundamental vs. change is illusion) set the agenda for Plato, Aristotle, and beyond.
- Democritus's atoms are the most eerily correct prediction in ancient philosophy — confirmed experimentally ~2,200 years later.

</Step>
