This lesson examines euthanasia and abortion as philosophical debates — questions about which serious thinkers have disagreed for centuries. We present the strongest version of each position, the arguments philosophers actually make, without advocating for any particular conclusion. These are live, difficult debates that thoughtful people approach differently, often rooted in deep values about human life, autonomy, and dignity. All positions are treated with respect.

Two questions in applied ethics are harder to avoid than most: what obligations do we have at the end of life, and what moral status does a developing human have before birth? Both force us to engage with concepts we have been building throughout this island — personhood, autonomy, rights, the difference between killing and letting die — and to see how different ethical frameworks pull in different directions.

</Step>

<Step title="Euthanasia: autonomy and the sanctity of life" kind="explain">

**Euthanasia** (from the Greek: "good death") refers to intentionally ending a life to relieve suffering. Philosophers distinguish several kinds:

- **Voluntary**: the person consents, and requests death.
- **Non-voluntary**: the person cannot consent (e.g., is in a coma).
- **Involuntary**: the person could consent but has not been asked.
- **Active**: a direct act causes death (e.g., a lethal injection).
- **Passive**: withdrawing treatment allows death to occur.

Most ethical debate centres on **voluntary active euthanasia** (VAE) — whether a competent adult with terminal or unbearable suffering has the right to request, and receive, a medically assisted death.

**The autonomy argument** (in favour): A competent adult is the authority over their own life and body. If they judge their continued existence to involve suffering they find intolerable and without sufficient compensating goods, preventing them from dying with assistance denies them sovereignty over the most intimate decision of their life. John Stuart Mill's harm principle implies that the state has no right to prevent choices that harm only the chooser.

**The sanctity-of-life argument** (against): Human life has intrinsic worth that cannot be undermined by the person's own assessment of their quality of life. For many religious traditions, and for some secular ones, life is not merely an asset to be disposed of by its possessor; it is a value whose protection others have reason to uphold. This view resists the idea that a person can "consent" away their own fundamental dignity.

**The doctrine of double effect** features here too: most traditions that oppose VAE permit administering pain relief that may hasten death, if the intention is to relieve pain rather than to kill. The intention, not the outcome, marks the moral line.

**Killing vs letting die**: Is there a moral difference between actively ending a life and withdrawing treatment that allows natural death? Many argue yes — the doctor who administers a lethal injection is the cause of death in a way that the doctor who withdraws a ventilator is not. Critics, including James Rachels, argued this distinction is not morally fundamental: if the outcome and the intention are the same, the method should not determine the verdict.

</Step>

<Step title="Abortion: personhood and bodily autonomy" kind="explain">

The philosophical debate about abortion turns on two largely separate questions — and the position one takes on each determines where one lands.

**Question 1: What is the moral status of the fetus?**

Moral status, as we saw in the expanding-circle discussion, is not all-or-nothing. Positions include:

- **Full moral status from conception**: the fertilised egg has the same moral weight as a born person. This view typically holds that a unique human life begins at the moment of fertilisation. It is associated with the **sanctity-of-life** tradition.
- **Gradual acquisition**: moral status increases as the fetus develops morally relevant capacities — sentience (the ability to feel pain), viability outside the womb, or higher-order consciousness. On this view, a very early embryo has little or no moral status; a fetus at 28 weeks has substantial (though perhaps not full) status.
- **No moral status until birth**: full personhood, on some accounts, requires actual psychological continuity, interests in one's future, or social recognition — none of which a fetus possesses. This is a minority view in philosophy.

Don Marquis argued that what makes killing wrong is that it deprives the victim of a **future like ours** — a future of experiences, projects, and relationships. If so, a fetus, which has such a future, has the same reason not to be killed as any person.

**Question 2: Even granting fetal personhood, does abortion remain permissible?**

Judith Jarvis Thomson's famous 1971 essay "A Defense of Abortion" granted, for the sake of argument, that the fetus is a person — and argued that abortion can still be permissible in many cases. Her **Violinist argument**: Imagine waking up connected to a famous violinist whose kidneys need your body for nine months. You did not consent. Do you have an obligation to remain connected for nine months, even to save a life?

Thomson's argument is that **bodily autonomy** — the right to control what uses your body — can override the right to life of another, at least in cases where use of your body was not consented to. Critics argue the analogy has limits: if the pregnancy resulted from voluntary action, one cannot disavow responsibility in the same way.

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Euthanasia**: the central debate is between **autonomy** (competent adults may choose how to die) and **sanctity of life** (life has intrinsic worth that cannot be alienated by the individual's assessment).
- **Killing vs letting die**: a contested but widely invoked distinction; passive withdrawal of treatment is generally more accepted than active termination.
- **Doctrine of double effect**: pain relief that foreseeably hastens death may be permissible if death is not the intended means.
- **Abortion — two separate questions**: (1) What is the moral status of the fetus? (2) Even granting full status, does bodily autonomy override it?
- **Marquis**: killing wrongs us by depriving us of a future like ours — a fetus has such a future.
- **Thomson**: even granting fetal personhood, bodily autonomy may justify abortion in cases of unconsented use of the body.
- No ethical framework delivers a simple verdict on either debate — they illuminate tensions, not resolutions.

</Step>
