<Step title="The ship that sailed for a thousand years" kind="explain">

The legendary hero Theseus sailed back to Athens after slaying the Minotaur. The Athenians preserved his ship as a monument. Over the centuries, planks rotted and were replaced with new ones. Eventually every single plank of the original ship had been swapped out.

Then someone had a further idea: what if we had kept all the old planks and rebuilt the original ship from them? Now there would be **two ships** in the harbour — the continuously maintained vessel with an unbroken history stretching back to Theseus, and a freshly built vessel made from the very planks Theseus actually sailed on.

Which one is the *real* Ship of Theseus?

This is the **Ship of Theseus paradox**, first recorded by Plutarch (c. 75 CE). It is philosophy's sharpest instrument for prying apart different theories of **numerical identity** — what makes something the *same* object over time.

</Step>

<ShipOfTheseus />

There is no universally agreed answer. The puzzle exposes that our ordinary concept of "same object" bundles together several criteria — **material continuity** (same stuff), **spatial-temporal continuity** (unbroken history), **causal continuity** (cause-and-effect chain linking the original to the present) — that normally travel together but here come apart.

</Step>

<Step title="Personal identity: the same puzzle, higher stakes" kind="explain">

Now apply the same logic to yourself. Your body replaces most of its atoms over roughly seven years. The neurons that encoded your memories have different molecular components than they did in childhood. The beliefs, desires, and personality you have now differ substantially from those of the six-year-old you once were.

**Are you the same person?** If so, what grounds that sameness?

John Locke argued it is **psychological continuity**: memory chains connect your present self to your past. You are the same person as the six-year-old because you can (directly or indirectly) remember being them, and there is an overlapping chain of memories and intentions linking each stage of your life to the next.

Thomas Reid objected with a famous counterexample: suppose a general can remember being a young officer, and the young officer could remember being a boy flogged for stealing. But the general has no memory of the flogging. By Locke's criterion, the general is identical to the officer, and the officer to the boy — but by transitivity, the general should be identical to the boy. Yet the general cannot remember being the boy, so by Locke's criterion, he is not identical to the boy. Contradiction.

</Step>

<Step title="Four theories of personal identity" kind="explain">

Philosophers have proposed four main types of answer:

**Biological/animalism**: you are a human animal. You persist as long as your animal body persists — memory and psychology are important, but what grounds identity is the continued life of the organism.

**Psychological continuity**: you persist through overlapping chains of psychological connections — memories, intentions, beliefs, desires — even across radical physical change. Parfit's refinement: what matters isn't strict identity but *degrees* of connectedness.

**Soul theory**: you are an immaterial soul, and it persists through physical change. Most secular philosophers find this unsatisfying because the soul is not independently observable.

**Bundle theory** (Hume): there is no persistent self at all. The self is just a bundle of experiences and perceptions succeeding each other rapidly. "I" is a grammatical fiction tracking a process, not a thing.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Ship of Theseus paradox**: when every plank is replaced, is it the same ship? If the old planks are rebuilt into a second ship, which is the original?
- The puzzle separates **material continuity** from **spatial-temporal continuity** from **causal continuity**.
- **Personal identity** faces the same puzzle: psychological continuity (Locke), animalism, soul theory, bundle theory (Hume).
- Reid's sergeant paradox shows that memory-based psychological continuity must be formulated carefully to avoid violating the transitivity of identity.
- These questions matter practically: legal identity, moral responsibility across time, survival of death.

</Step>
