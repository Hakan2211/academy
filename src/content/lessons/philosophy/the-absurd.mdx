<Callout type="info" title="This lesson explores the absurd philosophically">
Albert Camus asked one of philosophy's most serious questions: given that the universe offers no built-in meaning, how should we live? His answer is a resounding, life-affirming YES to life — rebellion, passion, and joy in the face of silence. This lesson explores that question philosophically, in the tradition of existentialism.

If at any point these ideas feel heavy in a personal way rather than an intellectual one, that is a sign of the depth of the questions — and reaching out to someone you trust is always a strength, not a weakness.
</Callout>

Albert Camus is sometimes grouped with the existentialists, though he resisted the label. His central concept — the **absurd** — begins from a simple observation: human beings are creatures who desperately crave meaning, pattern, and purpose. We want to know that our lives *matter*, that the world makes sense, that there is some answer to "why are we here?"

The universe does not answer. It is silent, indifferent, stubbornly unreasonable. Stars do not care. History has no plot. The cosmos offers no comfort.

Camus calls the collision between these two facts — our craving and the universe's silence — **the absurd**. It is not a property of either side alone. The absurd is a *relationship*, a perpetual confrontation between what we demand and what we receive.

</Step>

<Step title="Sisyphus and the human condition" kind="interactive">

Camus finds his image for the absurd in Greek myth. Sisyphus was condemned by the gods to roll a boulder up a hill, watch it roll back down, and repeat this forever. It sounds like a picture of futility and despair.

Camus sees something else: **the human condition in miniature**. We work, we build, we create, we love — and then it ends, erased, to be done again. Our projects, our civilizations, even our memories are temporary. Yet we cannot stop striving.

The question is not whether the rock rolls back. It always will. The question is how we respond to that fact.

<AbsurdSisyphus />

Camus identifies three possible responses to the absurd. Only one is honest and life-affirming.

</Step>

<Step title="Revolt — the only honest answer" kind="explain">

Camus' answer to the absurd is neither to deny it nor to flee it. It is **revolt**: to hold the absurd clearly in view, refuse false comfort, and embrace life with passionate intensity anyway.

This produces three intertwined values:

- **Revolt** — a perpetual refusal to resign, to accept the absurd as final, to surrender to it. Not anger, but clear-eyed defiance.
- **Freedom** — freed from the illusion of a higher meaning, each moment is liberated. You are free to fill your life on your own terms.
- **Passion** — without eternal significance, the *quantity* of experience matters. Live as fully and intensely as possible.

And then the famous conclusion: **"One must imagine Sisyphus happy."** Not because the boulder gets lighter, not because the gods relent, not because a God waits at the top. But because Sisyphus owns his fate. He has made it his. On the descent, returning to his rock, he is lucid and fully alive. The struggle toward the heights is enough. It does not need to end in triumph to have been worth it.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **The absurd** = the collision between our craving for meaning and the universe's silence. Neither side alone; it is a *relationship*.
- **Myth of Sisyphus** — the human condition: endless striving with no final resolution.
- Camus rejects two escapes: physical despair (capitulation) and the philosophical leap to transcendent meaning (intellectual dishonesty).
- His answer: **revolt, freedom, passion** — hold the absurd in view and live with full intensity anyway.
- **"One must imagine Sisyphus happy"** — the rock does not get lighter, but Sisyphus owns his fate. The struggle itself is enough.
