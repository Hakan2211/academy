<Step title="The pull of the gut" kind="explain">

Before you have thought carefully about ethics, you already have strong moral reactions. Push a stranger into traffic — obviously wrong. Break a promise to save time — feels bad. Give to charity — seems good. These reactions arrive fast, unbidden, and with great force. They are **moral intuitions**: immediate, pre-theoretical judgments about right and wrong.

Where do they come from? They are shaped by evolution (cooperation, kin protection), culture, upbringing, and emotional systems laid down long before language. David Hume argued that reason is, and must be, the *slave of the passions*: reason alone can never motivate action or generate values — our emotional responses come first, and reasoning merely serves them.

Immanuel Kant disagreed sharply. For Kant, *reason* is the source of morality. A moral principle is valid only if it can be endorsed by reason without contradiction — and reason operates independently of feeling. Emotions can mislead; only the rational will is trustworthy.

Who is right matters enormously, because it determines how much weight to give intuitions in moral argument.

</Step>

<Step title="When intuitions conflict with reasoning" kind="explain">

The trolley problem — devised by Philippa Foot and extended by Judith Jarvis Thomson — is a philosophical instrument designed to reveal exactly this tension. The arithmetic is identical in two scenarios; what changes is how the harm is brought about. And that change produces strikingly different intuitive responses in most people.

Why does framing the same arithmetic differently feel so different? Moral psychologist Jonathan Haidt argues this reveals that our intuitions are tracking something beyond pure outcome — perhaps the distinction between harm as a **side effect** of an action versus harm as the **means** by which you act. Using a person as an instrument (pushing them) triggers a different emotional system than redirecting a harm that will happen anyway (the lever).

This tells us something important: our intuitions are not random. They encode moral principles — even when we cannot articulate them. The philosophical task is to surface those principles and evaluate them.

</Step>

<Step title="The role of intuitions in ethics" kind="explain">

Philosophers disagree about the epistemic status of intuitions. Three views:

**Intuitions are evidence.** On this view (associated with intuitionism and reflective equilibrium), strong, widely-shared moral intuitions are data points about moral reality — not infallible, but defeasible evidence. Just as perceptual experience is evidence about the physical world, intuitions are evidence about the moral world.

**Intuitions can be debunked.** Some philosophers argue that if an intuition is explained by evolutionary pressures or cultural conditioning that have nothing to do with moral truth, it loses evidential force. Our disgust responses evolved for hygiene, not ethics — they shouldn't drive moral conclusions.

**Reflective equilibrium** (Rawls): we should seek a coherent fit between our intuitions and our principles, adjusting each in the light of the other. When a principle entails a deeply repugnant conclusion, we revise the principle; when intuitions survive theoretical scrutiny, they carry evidential weight.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Moral intuitions** are fast, pre-theoretical moral reactions — shaped by evolution, culture, and emotion.
- **Hume**: reason is the slave of the passions — emotions drive morality; reason serves them.
- **Kant**: reason alone generates valid moral principles — emotion can mislead.
- The **trolley problem** reveals that our intuitions track hidden principles (e.g., harm as means vs. side effect) that go beyond mere arithmetic.
- Intuitions are not infallible — they can be biased or misfire — but they are defeasible moral evidence.
- **Reflective equilibrium**: adjust principles and intuitions in light of each other, seeking coherence.

</Step>
