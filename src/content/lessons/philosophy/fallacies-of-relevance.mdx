<Step title="Arguments that change the subject" kind="explain">

**Fallacies of relevance** are informal fallacies where the premises are simply *not relevant* to the conclusion — even though the argument is designed to feel like they are. Instead of addressing the actual claim, the argument quietly redirects your attention to something emotionally compelling, personally embarrassing, or simply different.

They are among the most common fallacies in political debate, advertising, and everyday disagreement. Five varieties are especially worth knowing:

**Ad hominem** ("against the person") — attacking who is making the argument rather than the argument itself. *"You can't take her seriously; she failed economics in university."* Whether or not that's true tells us nothing about whether her economic analysis is correct.

**Straw man** — misrepresenting your opponent's position as something weaker or more extreme, then defeating *that* invented version. *"He wants stricter emissions standards — apparently he'd rather destroy jobs than breathe clean air."* The exaggerated version was never the claim.

**Appeal to illegitimate authority** — citing someone's status or prestige as a substitute for evidence, especially when they're speaking outside their area of expertise. A celebrity endorsement and a Nobel laureate in the wrong field both commit this fallacy.

**Appeal to emotion** — using fear, pity, pride, or outrage to drive a conclusion rather than evidence. Powerful emotional language isn't inherently fallacious — but it becomes fallacious when emotion *replaces* rather than *accompanies* a valid argument.

**Red herring** — introducing an irrelevant topic to divert the conversation. The classic political move: respond to a question about policy failure by pivoting to patriotism, the suffering of another group, or the opponent's past.

<Step title="Why relevance matters" kind="explain">

You might wonder: isn't it sometimes legitimate to ask about the speaker's credibility? To appeal to genuine expertise? To point out that someone's personal stake in a debate might bias their judgment?

Yes — with important distinctions. The relevant question isn't "who is speaking?" but **"does this fact about the speaker change the evidential weight of what they've said?"** A medical researcher's financial ties to a pharmaceutical company *can* be relevant — not because it proves they're wrong, but because it's a reason to seek independent replication. That's a different move from simply dismissing their published data.

<Callout type="info" title="The charitable test">

Before labelling something a fallacy of relevance, ask: **"Is there any scenario in which this fact would actually matter to the argument?"** If yes, the move might be legitimate. If you can't construct such a scenario, the relevance is illusory — you're looking at a fallacy.

</Callout>

Similarly, emotional appeals can be appropriate in ethics and policy: pointing out that real people are suffering isn't a fallacy if the argument is about their wellbeing. It becomes a fallacy when emotion is used to *end* rational scrutiny rather than inform it.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Fallacies of relevance** redirect attention away from the actual argument using emotionally loaded but logically irrelevant information.
- **Ad hominem**: attack the person, not the argument.
- **Straw man**: defeat a weakened, exaggerated version of the opponent's real position.
- **Appeal to illegitimate authority**: cite status or prestige instead of evidence, especially outside the authority's area of expertise.
- **Appeal to emotion**: use fear, pity, or outrage to *replace* rather than accompany a valid argument.
- **Red herring**: introduce an unrelated topic to derail the actual discussion.
- The diagnostic question: *does this premise actually bear on whether the conclusion is true?*

</Step>
