<Step title="The discipline of living well" kind="explain">

Ethics — also called **moral philosophy** — is the branch of philosophy that asks the most personal of all questions: *what is right and wrong, and how should I live?* The word comes from the Greek *ethos* (character), and for the ancient Greeks it was inseparable from the question of what it means to flourish as a human being.

But "ethics" is not one question — it is a whole family of questions, and philosophers have found it useful to distinguish three levels at which those questions can be asked.

The first level is **descriptive ethics**: the empirical study of what people and cultures *actually* believe to be right or wrong. A sociologist documenting the moral codes of different societies is doing descriptive ethics. It makes no judgment — it just reports.

The second level is **normative ethics**: the philosophical project of working out what we *should* do. Consequentialists, deontologists, and virtue ethicists are all doing normative ethics, offering rival accounts of what makes an action right.

The third level — the deepest and strangest — is **metaethics**: the inquiry into what moral claims even *mean* and whether they can be objectively *true*. When you ask "is 'murder is wrong' a fact about the universe, or just an expression of feeling?", you have stepped into metaethics.

</Step>

<Step title="Three levels in one example" kind="explain">

Take the claim: **"It is wrong to lie."**

A **descriptive** ethicist asks: do most people believe this? In which cultures and contexts? They discover enormous variety — lying to protect a friend is condemned in some moral codes and praised in others.

A **normative** ethicist asks: *is* lying wrong, and under what conditions? A consequentialist might say lying is wrong when it causes more harm than truth; a Kantian might say lying violates a duty that holds regardless of consequences.

A **metaethicist** asks a prior question: what does it mean to say lying is "wrong"? Is that claim *true* in the same way "water is H₂O" is true? Or is it a cultural norm, a personal feeling, or a social convention? Could it be objectively true even if everyone on Earth disagreed?

These are not the same question. Failing to distinguish them leads to a lot of confused arguments — people talk past each other because they are operating at different levels without realising it.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Ethics** is the philosophical study of right, wrong, and how to live well.
- **Descriptive ethics**: what do people *actually* believe? (empirical)
- **Normative ethics**: what *should* we do? (philosophical prescription)
- **Metaethics**: what do moral claims *mean*, and can they be objectively *true*? (foundational)
- **Intrinsic value**: good in itself (an end). **Instrumental value**: good as a means to something else.
- Distinguishing these levels is essential to avoid talking past each other in moral debates.

</Step>
