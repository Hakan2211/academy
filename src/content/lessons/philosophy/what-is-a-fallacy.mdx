<Step title="Mistakes that feel convincing" kind="explain">

A **fallacy** is a flaw in reasoning — a step in an argument that doesn't actually hold up. What makes fallacies dangerous is the keyword *feel*: fallacious arguments can be persuasive, emotionally compelling, even backed by real facts, while still being **logically broken at their core**.

Spotting a fallacy is not the same as knowing who's right. An argument can commit a fallacy and accidentally reach a true conclusion. What the fallacy shows is that *this particular path to that conclusion* doesn't work. The conclusion still needs a better argument.

Philosophers divide fallacies into two broad families:

- **Formal fallacies** — the *structure* of the argument is broken. Even if every premise were true, the conclusion wouldn't follow. Example: "If it rains, the ground gets wet. The ground is wet. Therefore it must have rained." The form looks like modus ponens but isn't — the ground could be wet for dozens of other reasons. (This particular form is called "affirming the consequent.")
- **Informal fallacies** — the structure could be valid, but something is wrong with the *content*, relevance, or framing. The premises are misleading, irrelevant, or assume what they're trying to prove.

The distinction matters because the remedy is different. A formal fallacy requires you to fix the argument's logical shape. An informal fallacy requires you to question the premises or the relevance of the evidence.

<Step title="Why we fall for them" kind="explain">

Fallacies survive because they exploit real cognitive tendencies. We care about who is saying something (ad hominem works because trust matters in daily life). We fear extreme outcomes (slippery slope works because sometimes chains of events really do cascade). We defer to experts (appeal to authority works because expertise is usually a reliable signal).

The problem arises when we apply these heuristics *automatically*, without checking whether they actually apply. A person's character doesn't determine whether their argument is sound. One step doesn't guarantee the next ten. An expert outside their field isn't more reliable than a non-expert inside it.

<Callout type="tip" title="A simple diagnostic">

When you feel convinced by an argument but can't quite say *why*, pause and ask: **"What specific evidence or logic makes this conclusion follow?"** If the answer is "well, they're a bad person" or "it just seems obvious," a fallacy may be doing the work.

</Callout>

The goal isn't to become a pedantic fallacy-labeller who refuses to engage. It's to develop the habit of **slowing down at the persuasive moments** and asking what's actually holding the argument together.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- A **fallacy** is a flaw in reasoning that can still feel convincing — making them genuinely dangerous.
- **Formal fallacies** have a broken logical *structure*; the conclusion doesn't follow from the premises even if they're true.
- **Informal fallacies** have flawed *content* — irrelevant premises, misleading framing, or hidden assumptions.
- Identifying a fallacy doesn't prove the conclusion is false — it shows the argument doesn't justify it.
- Fallacies exploit real cognitive tendencies; the remedy is pausing to ask what's actually supporting the conclusion.

</Step>
