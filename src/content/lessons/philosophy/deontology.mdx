<Step title="Rules that hold regardless" kind="explain">

Consequentialism says the morality of an action depends entirely on its results. But many of us have a powerful intuition that some things are simply wrong — not because of what happens, but because of what they are. Torturing an innocent person seems wrong even if it would prevent greater suffering. Lying to a friend seems wrong even if the truth would hurt.

**Deontology** is the family of moral theories that grounds right action in **duties and rules**, not consequences. From the Greek *deon* (duty), deontological ethics says: certain actions are intrinsically required or forbidden, full stop. The outcome is morally secondary — or irrelevant.

The greatest deontologist is **Immanuel Kant** (1724–1804). Kant argued that morality must be grounded not in happiness or consequences — which vary — but in **reason**, which is universal. The supreme principle of morality is what he called the **categorical imperative**: a command that holds unconditionally, regardless of your desires or the situation.

</Step>

<Step title="The categorical imperative" kind="explain">

Kant formulated the categorical imperative in several equivalent ways. The two most important:

**Formula 1 — Universalizability:**
*"Act only according to that maxim by which you can at the same time will that it should become a universal law."*

To test whether your action is moral, identify the maxim (the rule) you are acting on, then ask: could you rationally will that everyone acted on that maxim? If universalising it leads to a contradiction — or to a world you could not rationally want — the action is impermissible.

**Formula 2 — Humanity:**
*"Act so that you treat humanity, whether in your own person or that of another, always as an end and never as a means only."*

Every person is a rational agent with inherent dignity. You must never use a person merely as an instrument for your goals — as an object in your calculus. You may involve others in your plans, but you must always respect their rational agency.

These two formulas, Kant claimed, are equivalent. The universalizability test and the prohibition on treating people as mere means both flow from the same source: the special status of rational, autonomous beings.

</Step>

<Step title="Test a maxim" kind="interactive">

Apply Kant's universalizability test. Pick a maxim — a personal rule of action — and walk through what happens when you universalise it. Does it lead to a self-defeating contradiction, or can it coexist with a rational world?

<CategoricalImperative />

Notice the pattern: maxims that involve deception or promise-breaking collapse when universalised, because they depend on a background of honest communication that they simultaneously undermine. This is not a contingent fact about consequences — it is a logical contradiction in the very idea.

</Step>

<Step title="Strengths and objections" kind="explain">

**Strengths.** Deontology captures the intuition that people have rights that cannot simply be traded away for better aggregate outcomes. It gives morality a stable, unconditional character — you cannot do wrong merely because the consequences looked good. It respects persons as ends, not resources.

**The lying murderer objection.** Kant famously held that lying is always impermissible — even to a murderer asking where your friend is hiding. Most people find this counterintuitive: surely consequences matter *here*? This objection reveals a tension between rule-following and common-sense moral judgment.

**Conflicting duties.** What if two duties clash? You promised to meet a friend, but someone needs urgent help on the way. Kant's framework is less helpful when duties conflict — it can struggle to provide a decision procedure in hard cases.

**Whose reason?** Kant assumed rational beings would all converge on the same categorical imperative. Critics question whether different rational agents might universalise different maxims without contradiction — making the test less determinate than Kant hoped.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Deontology**: morality is grounded in duties and rules — not consequences.
- **Kant's categorical imperative**: the supreme moral principle, expressed in two key forms.
- **Formula 1 (Universalizability)**: act only on a maxim you could will to be a universal law.
- **Formula 2 (Humanity)**: treat persons always as ends, never merely as means.
- Lying and promise-breaking fail the categorical imperative because universalising them produces self-defeating contradictions.
- **Key objections**: the lying murderer case, conflicting duties, ambiguity in the universalizability test.

</Step>
