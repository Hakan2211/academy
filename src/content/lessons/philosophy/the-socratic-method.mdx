<Step title="The man who only asked questions" kind="explain">

Socrates (c. 470–399 BCE) wrote nothing and claimed to teach nothing. He simply walked the streets of Athens and **asked people questions** — usually the experts who were most sure of themselves. *You're a general — so tell me, what is courage? You judge legal cases — so what is justice?*

Again and again, the confident answer fell apart under a few gentle follow-up questions. Socrates' trick was to take someone's definition seriously and then find a single **counterexample** that it couldn't handle.

He insisted he was no wiser than anyone else, with one exception: he *knew that he did not know*. Everyone else mistook their opinions for knowledge.

</Step>

Each round follows the same shape: **propose a definition → test it against a counterexample → refine or abandon it.** The goal isn't to humiliate anyone. It's to clear away false certainty so genuine inquiry can begin.

</Step>

<Step title="Why confusion is progress" kind="explain">

The dialogues often end in *aporia* — a Greek word for being stuck, at a loss. To the person being questioned it can feel like failure. Socrates saw it as the **first real step toward wisdom**.

Why? Because you can't learn what you think you already know. As long as you believe your shaky opinion is solid knowledge, the door is closed. The moment you honestly admit *"I don't actually know what justice is,"* the door swings open.

This is why the method survives 2,400 years later — in classrooms, courtrooms, and therapy. It's powered by two humble questions you can use today: **"What exactly do you mean?"** and **"How do you know that?"**

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Socrates** taught by relentless questioning, never lecturing — the *Socratic method*.
- The move: take a definition seriously, then test it with a **counterexample**; refine or abandon it.
- His one claim to wisdom: he *knew that he did not know*.
- Reaching ***aporia*** (productive confusion) is progress — it clears false certainty so inquiry can start.
- Two questions carry the whole method: **"What do you mean?"** and **"How do you know?"**

</Step>
