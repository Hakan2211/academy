<Step title="What are rights — and where do they come from?" kind="explain">

Rights are claims that give their holder a protected sphere — a domain within which others (including the state) may not intrude without justification. But this raises an immediate philosophical puzzle: **where do rights come from?**

Three main answers compete:

**Natural rights**: rights exist independently of any law or government, grounded in human nature, reason, or morality. Locke held that life, liberty, and property are natural rights; the American founders spoke of rights that are "unalienable." The difficulty: if rights are natural, why do people disagree about which ones there are?

**Legal rights**: rights are whatever a legal system declares them to be. This makes rights concrete and enforceable, but it implies that whatever a government legislates is automatically a right — a conclusion most find troubling. It offers no basis for criticising unjust laws.

**Human rights / moral rights**: a middle path — rights grounded in the dignity of personhood that any legitimate legal system *should* recognise, even if some do not. This allows moral criticism of legal systems that fail to protect them.

The question matters practically: if rights are only legal, then minorities have no grounds to object when a majority votes to strip them of protections. If rights are natural or moral, they function as a check on democratic majorities.

</Step>

<Step title="Equality of opportunity vs equality of outcome" kind="explain">

All modern political philosophies claim to value equality — but they disagree sharply about what equality demands.

**Formal equality** (equality before the law): every person is subject to the same rules and may not be discriminated against on arbitrary grounds. This is widely accepted but widely seen as insufficient.

**Equality of opportunity**: every person should have a fair chance to compete for advantages, regardless of their starting point. This requires removing formal barriers (legal discrimination) and arguably more — equalising the conditions under which competition takes place (access to education, healthcare, decent nutrition). Rawls called the stronger version "fair equality of opportunity."

**Equality of outcome**: the goal is for people to end up with similar — or identical — shares of wealth, status, or well-being, regardless of starting points or contributions. Strict egalitarians favour this; critics argue it conflicts with freedom (achieving it requires constant redistribution) and with merit (it severs the link between contribution and reward).

Most contemporary debates do not take all-or-nothing positions but argue over *where* on this spectrum a just society should aim — and which inequalities, if any, are legitimate.

</Step>

<Step title="Democracy and its critics" kind="explain">

Democracy is widely regarded as the best available system of government — but political philosophy does not give it an easy pass.

**Democracy's strengths**: distributes power broadly, provides a mechanism to remove bad rulers peacefully, encourages accountability, and expresses respect for each person's equal standing as a political participant. Theorists like Rousseau and Dewey see participation itself as part of a flourishing civic life.

**The tyranny of the majority**: Mill worried that democracy can become a tool for the majority to oppress minorities. If 51% vote to strip a minority of rights, is that just? This is why most liberal democracies combine majority rule with **constitutional rights** that even majorities cannot override — precisely to protect minorities from democratic majorities.

**Plato's epistocracy objection**: Plato argued in the *Republic* that governing is a skill, like medicine or navigation. You wouldn't let passengers vote on how to sail a ship — you'd hire a skilled navigator. Why let everyone vote on complex questions of policy? The wise, Plato argued, should rule. Modern defenders of epistocracy echo this: uninformed votes may produce worse outcomes than expert governance. Critics reply that "who decides who is wise?" is itself a political question, and concentrating power in an "expert" class creates enormous risks of abuse.

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Rights** can be understood as natural (pre-political), legal (created by law), or moral/human (grounded in dignity and applicable to all legitimate legal systems).
- **Formal equality** (same rules for all) is the minimal baseline; **equality of opportunity** and **equality of outcome** are more demanding and more contested.
- **Democracy** distributes power and ensures accountability — but majorities can oppress minorities, which is why constitutional rights matter.
- **Plato's epistocracy** objection: governing is a skill — should experts rule rather than majorities? Critics worry: who guards the guardians?
- These tensions — rights vs majorities, freedom vs equality, expertise vs participation — are live and unresolved in every real democracy.

</Step>
