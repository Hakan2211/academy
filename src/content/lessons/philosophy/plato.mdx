<Step title="The student who transformed grief into a system" kind="explain">

Plato (428–348 BCE) was a young aristocrat who became a devoted student of Socrates. When Athens executed his teacher in 399 BCE, the 29-year-old Plato left the city in disgust, travelled widely, and returned to found the **Academy** — the first university in the Western world, which ran for over 900 years.

He then did something remarkable: he wrote all of his philosophy as **dialogues**, with Socrates as the main character, so that we are never quite sure where the historical Socrates ends and Plato's own views begin. This ambiguity was probably deliberate.

His central invention is the **Theory of Forms** — a view so counterintuitive and so influential that the philosopher Alfred North Whitehead said all of Western philosophy is "a series of footnotes to Plato."

</Step>

<Step title="The Theory of Forms" kind="explain">

Look at a circle drawn in the sand. It is not a perfect circle — the lines waver, the proportions are off. Yet you know immediately that it is *trying* to be a circle. What is it imitating?

Plato's answer: the **Form of Circle** — a perfect, eternal, non-physical template that exists in a separate realm of being. Every imperfect circle in the world is a *copy* of this Form, more or less faithfully instantiated in matter.

He extended this to everything:
- Every beautiful thing participates in the **Form of Beauty** — but only imperfectly.
- Every just act participates in the **Form of Justice** — the eternal template.
- The highest Form, the source of all truth and goodness, is the **Form of the Good** — which Plato compares to the sun.

This explains the Heraclitus–Parmenides clash: Heraclitus was right that the **sensory world changes constantly**. Parmenides was right that **true being is eternal and changeless**. They were both right — about different levels of reality.

<Callout type="info" title="Two worlds">
Plato's metaphysics divides reality into two: the **world of appearances** (changing, imperfect, accessible through the senses) and the **world of Forms** (eternal, perfect, accessible through reason). Sense-perception gives opinion (doxa); rational inquiry gives knowledge (episteme).
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="The Allegory of the Cave" kind="interactive">

Plato dramatises his metaphysics in the most famous thought experiment in philosophy. The cave represents ordinary human life — mistaking shadows (appearances) for reality. Philosophy is the painful journey toward the light.

<CaveAllegory />

The allegory also carries a political message: the philosopher who reaches the sun — who sees the Form of the Good — has an *obligation* to return to the cave and help others. Yet they will be mocked and disbelieved, because their eyes no longer see the shadows as well as those who never left. (Socrates: tried and executed.)

</Step>

<Step title="The Republic: the tripartite soul and the philosopher-king" kind="explain">

In the *Republic*, Plato argued that the **soul has three parts**:

- **Reason** (logos) — seeks truth; the highest part; should rule.
- **Spirit** (thumos) — the seat of honour, anger, ambition; allied with reason when well-trained.
- **Appetite** (epithumia) — desires food, sex, money; must be governed by reason.

A just person is one where reason governs spirit and appetite. Plato maps this onto the **just city**: philosophers (reason) rule; warriors (spirit) defend; producers (appetite) provide. The ideal ruler is the **philosopher-king** — someone who has seen the Form of the Good and therefore knows what is truly good for the city.

This is deeply anti-democratic: Plato distrusted the demos precisely because most people live in the cave, led by appetite and unreliable opinion.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- Plato founded the **Academy** (~387 BCE) and wrote his philosophy as Socratic dialogues.
- The **Theory of Forms**: behind every imperfect particular thing (beauty, justice, circles) is a perfect, eternal **Form** — the true reality.
- This reconciles Heraclitus and Parmenides: the sensory world changes; the world of Forms is eternal.
- The **Allegory of the Cave**: ordinary people mistake appearances for reality; philosophy is the painful ascent toward truth (the Form of the Good = the sun).
- The **tripartite soul**: reason, spirit, and appetite — justice is reason's rule over the others.
- The **philosopher-king**: only those who have seen the Good are fit to govern.

</Step>
