<Lesson>

<Step title="The question everyone ignores" kind="explain">

You use the word "know" dozens of times a day. *I know her name. I know it will rain. I know 7 × 8 = 56.* But what exactly does it mean to **know** something rather than merely believe it, guess it, or suspect it?

This is one of the oldest questions in epistemology — the branch of philosophy that studies knowledge, justified belief, and the grounds for claiming to know anything. And the answer turns out to be surprisingly hard to pin down.

</Step>

<Step title="Three ingredients — one by one" kind="explain">

Philosophers since Plato have proposed that knowledge requires three things at once:

**1. Belief** — You have to actually hold the proposition in your mind as true. If you've never considered whether there are an even number of hairs on your head, you don't *know* it, even if the number happens to be even. Knowledge requires believing.

**2. Truth** — The belief has to be correct. If you believe the Battle of Hastings was in 1067 but it was in 1066, you don't know it — you're simply wrong. A false belief cannot be knowledge, no matter how confident you feel.

**3. Justification** — Luck doesn't count. If you flip a coin, decide "heads means it will rain tomorrow," and it rains — you were right, but you didn't *know* it would rain. A lucky guess that turned out to be true is still just a lucky guess. Genuine knowledge requires good *reasons* for holding the belief.

Put them together and you get the **Justified True Belief** (JTB) analysis: knowledge is a true belief that you hold for good reasons.

<Formula tex="K(p) \iff B(p) \land T(p) \land J(p)" block />

</Step>

<Step title="Explore the three circles" kind="interactive">

Each condition rules out a different kind of failure. A true belief without justification is a lucky guess. A justified belief that's false is a reasonable mistake. A true, justified proposition you've never considered is just a fact — not your knowledge.

<JTBVenn />

Only in the central intersection — where all three overlap — do we find genuine knowledge.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Epistemology** is the philosophical study of knowledge and justified belief.
- The **JTB analysis**: knowledge = a Justified True Belief.
- **Belief alone** isn't enough — it could be false.
- **Truth alone** isn't enough — you might have landed on it by luck.
- **Justification alone** isn't enough — a well-reasoned belief can still be wrong.
- All three together, according to the classical analysis, make knowledge.

</Step>
