<Step title="Claims vs arguments" kind="explain">

In everyday speech "argument" often means a quarrel — two people shouting past each other. In philosophy it means something far more precise and useful: **a set of statements where some (the premises) are offered as reasons for another (the conclusion)**.

A **claim** is just an assertion: "The death penalty is unjust." You can agree or disagree, but there's nothing to evaluate logically. An **argument** adds the machinery: it gives *reasons* that are meant to establish that claim.

> **Premise 1:** Punishing the innocent is always unjust.
> **Premise 2:** The death penalty sometimes kills innocent people.
> **Conclusion:** Therefore, the death penalty is sometimes unjust.

Now there's something to work with. The premises could be questioned, the link from premises to conclusion could be examined — and if both survive scrutiny, the argument gives you a *reason* to accept the conclusion.

</Step>

<Step title="Indicator words" kind="explain">

How do you spot an argument in the wild? Look for **indicator words** that flag whether a statement is playing the role of premise or conclusion.

**Conclusion indicators** signal "here comes what I'm trying to establish":
- therefore, thus, hence, so, it follows that, consequently, we can conclude

**Premise indicators** signal "here comes my reason":
- because, since, given that, for, as, this is shown by, the reason is that

<Callout type="tip" title="Not every argument wears its structure on its sleeve">
Many real arguments omit indicator words — the logical structure is implied, not explicit. Part of philosophical skill is learning to reconstruct the hidden premises and spot the intended conclusion even when they aren't labelled.
</Callout>

Consider: *"You should bring an umbrella — rain is forecast."* No explicit indicator, but the structure is clear: "rain is forecast" is the premise, "bring an umbrella" is the conclusion.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- An **argument** = one or more **premises** offered as reasons for a **conclusion**.
- A bare **claim** is not an argument — it gives no reasons.
- **Conclusion indicators**: therefore, thus, hence, so, it follows that …
- **Premise indicators**: because, since, given that, for …
- Real arguments often omit indicator words; reconstruct the structure yourself.
- Without premises, a conclusion has no logical support — even if it sounds right.

</Step>
