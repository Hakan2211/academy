<Step title="The skeptic's challenge" kind="explain">

Most of the time we go about our lives assuming we know a great deal. We know our own names, where we are, what day it is, whether we're awake. But the philosophical skeptic asks a disquieting question: **how, exactly, do you know any of that?**

Skepticism is not simply pessimism or cynicism. It is a rigorous demand for justification: **prove that you know what you claim to know**, and prove it in a way that rules out even the most far-fetched possibilities of error. The skeptic does not assert we know nothing — they challenge us to establish that we know *something*.

</Step>

<Step title="Descartes' method of doubt" kind="explain">

René Descartes (1596–1650) was not himself a skeptic, but he deliberately deployed skeptical arguments as a **method** — a tool for finding beliefs so certain that no doubt could touch them. His goal was to build knowledge on an absolutely secure foundation.

He proceeded in waves, each more radical than the last:

**Wave 1 — The senses deceive.** The senses sometimes mislead us (sticks look bent in water; distant objects look small). Could they always deceive? This casts doubt on beliefs based on sensory appearances.

**Wave 2 — The dreaming argument.** When you dream, you believe you are awake — yet you're not. Right now, can you prove you're not dreaming? If you cannot, everything you "know" about your body and surroundings is in doubt.

**Wave 3 — The evil demon.** This is the most radical step. Suppose an omnipotent evil demon is dedicating all its power to deceiving you — not just about the senses or the external world, but about everything, including mathematics. Then even *2 + 3 = 5* might be a deception.

The modern version of the evil demon is the **brain-in-a-vat** thought experiment: a disembodied brain floating in a tank, receiving artificially generated signals that create a perfect illusion of a normal life. You could be that brain right now.

</Step>

<Step title="Cogito ergo sum" kind="explain">

After applying all three waves of doubt, Descartes found one belief that survived: **I think, therefore I am** (*cogito ergo sum*).

The argument is not really a syllogism — it is a direct, undeniable insight. To be deceived, you must exist. To doubt, you must be thinking. And to be thinking, you must exist. The very attempt to doubt your own existence refutes itself.

<Formula tex="\text{Cogito ergo sum}: \text{I think} \Rightarrow \text{I exist}" block />

From this single certain point, Descartes tried to rebuild all of knowledge — proving first that God exists and would not systematically deceive him, and from there restoring science and the external world. Whether his reconstruction succeeds is hotly debated; what is not debated is the power of the cogito itself as philosophy's most famous argument.

<Callout type="info" title="Responses to skepticism">
Later philosophers challenged whether Descartes' skeptical scenarios are even coherent. Wittgenstein argued that doubt only makes sense against a background of certainty — you can't doubt everything at once. G. E. Moore famously replied to global skepticism by holding up his hand and saying "Here is one hand" — claiming that his certainty about the external world was more secure than any philosophical argument against it.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Philosophical skepticism** demands rigorous proof that we know what we claim to know.
- **Descartes** used doubt as a *method* — not to deny knowledge but to find beliefs immune to doubt.
- **Wave 1**: senses deceive → perceptual appearances are in doubt.
- **Wave 2**: dreaming argument → the external world and one's own body are in doubt.
- **Wave 3**: evil demon (brain in a vat) → even mathematics is in doubt.
- **Cogito ergo sum** — "I think, therefore I am" — survives all three waves: to doubt, you must think; to think, you must exist.

</Step>
