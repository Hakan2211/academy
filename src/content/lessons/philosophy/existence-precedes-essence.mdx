<Step title="The craftsperson's paper-cutter" kind="explain">

Jean-Paul Sartre opens his famous 1945 lecture "Existentialism is a Humanism" with a deceptively simple example. A craftsperson makes a paper-cutter. Before any steel is cut, the craftsperson has a **concept** in mind: what the paper-cutter is *for*, what shape it must have, what its ideal form is. The **essence** — the purpose, the design, the definition — comes *first*. Only then does the object come into existence.

This is the classical picture, and it extends naturally to a religious worldview: God creates humanity the way a craftsperson creates tools. God conceives of "human nature" — what we are for, what the good life looks like, what our purpose is — and then creates us to match that design. **Essence precedes existence.** The meaning is built in.

Sartre's entire existentialism is a rejection of this picture.

</Step>

<Step title="Existence first — then self-definition" kind="interactive">

Sartre's slogan — **"existence precedes essence"** — is the most compressed statement of existentialism possible. Humans are not made to a pre-existing design. We are thrown into the world first, and only then — through what we do, choose, commit to, and refuse — do we *create* what we are.

The import of this reversal is enormous. If there is no pre-given human nature, then there is no built-in purpose to fulfil, no designer's specification to live up to. You are not *for* anything in advance. You become what you are through your choices — and there is nowhere to hide from that responsibility.

</Step>

<Step title="Condemned to be free" kind="explain">

Sartre's most quoted line follows directly: **"Man is condemned to be free."**

"Condemned" because you did not choose to exist, did not choose to be a being who must choose. You were thrown into this situation without consent. And yet from the moment of existence, you cannot *not* choose. Even inaction is a choice. Even following the crowd is a decision you make, moment by moment.

This is more radical than it first sounds. It means:

- There is no fixed human nature to which you can appeal to excuse yourself: "I'm just like that."
- There is no God whose plan assigns you a role: "This is my destiny."
- There is no social role that fully defines you: "I'm only a worker/a parent/a citizen."

You always *transcend* your current situation. You are always projecting toward a future that is not yet fixed. Sartre calls this human reality **"being-for-itself"** — conscious, free, never fully coincident with itself.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- For **objects**, essence (purpose/design) precedes existence: the craftsperson imagines the knife before making it.
- For **humans** (on Sartre's view), **existence precedes essence**: we arrive with no fixed nature or purpose, and create our own through choices.
- **"Condemned to be free"** — we did not choose to exist or to be free, yet we cannot escape the responsibility of choosing.
- No fixed human nature, no divine plan, no social role exhausts what you are — you always transcend your current facticity.
- This is the starting point for all of existentialist ethics: freedom is not a gift, it is a burden and a calling.
