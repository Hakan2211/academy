<Step title="The question beneath all the others" kind="explain">

Throughout this world we have asked: what is knowledge? What justifies a belief? How can doubt be defeated? But lurking behind all of these questions is an even more fundamental one: **what does it mean for a belief to be *true* in the first place?**

When you say "the cat is on the mat," what are you claiming? Most people would say you're claiming that the cat really is there — that there is a fact about the world and your sentence matches it. But this intuitive picture turns out to be surprisingly slippery when examined closely.

Philosophy offers three major rival theories. Each starts from something obvious and pushes it further than common sense usually goes.

</Step>

<Step title="Theory 1 — Correspondence" kind="explain">

The **correspondence theory** says that a proposition is true if and only if it *corresponds to* — or accurately represents — reality.

This is the most intuitive view. "Snow is white" is true because there is something out there called snow, and it has the property of being white. The statement matches the world.

The appeal is strong: truth should track the world, not just our beliefs. But the theory faces two hard challenges:

**What is "correspondence"?** It is easy to say a sentence "matches reality," but hard to give a precise account of what this matching relation is. Sentences are linguistic objects; facts are... what exactly? How can words and the world stand in a "correspondence" relation?

**What about abstract or moral claims?** "7 is a prime number" — is there something in the external physical world it corresponds to? "Cruelty is wrong" — is there a moral fact out there to match? The correspondence theory works most naturally for concrete empirical claims and gets strained elsewhere.

</Step>

<Step title="Theory 2 — Coherence" kind="explain">

The **coherence theory** says that a proposition is true if and only if it *coheres with* — fits consistently with — a system of beliefs.

For the coherentist, truth is not about matching some external world we can never access directly. Instead, a belief is true when it fits with the rest of our knowledge: when accepting it doesn't contradict other things we believe, when it reinforces and is reinforced by the web of beliefs we hold.

This view is attractive in science: we don't verify each scientific claim by directly checking it against "raw reality." We check it against everything else we know — other theories, experimental results, background assumptions. A claim earns its place by cohering.

But coherentism faces a devastating objection: **a perfectly coherent but completely false story would count as true.** A well-crafted novel is internally consistent, but nobody thinks its fictional claims are true. And two radically different but internally coherent systems of belief could both be "true" by coherentist standards — but they can't both be right.

</Step>

<Step title="Theory 3 — Pragmatic" kind="explain">

The **pragmatic theory of truth**, associated with William James and C. S. Peirce, says that a proposition is true if it *works* — if believing it is useful, if it guides successful action, if it helps us navigate the world.

James put it provocatively: "True ideas are those that we can assimilate, validate, corroborate, and verify." Truth is not a static property a belief has in isolation; it is something a belief *earns* through its usefulness in practice.

This view has real appeal: science works because its claims are true, and "working" is at least evidence of truth. Pragmatism is also democratic — it judges beliefs by their consequences, not by abstract metaphysical matching with an inaccessible reality.

But the objection is sharp: **useful beliefs can be false, and true beliefs can be useless.** A placebo works — does that make "this pill cures migraines" true? A false historical belief ("we've always been at war with them") might be politically convenient. And "true" and "useful" just seem like different properties — the pragmatist seems to be changing the subject.

<Callout type="info" title="Deflationism">
A fourth family of views — *deflationism* — says that there is no deep property of truth to theorize about at all. "It's true that snow is white" just means "snow is white." The word "true" is a grammatical tool, not a name for a real property. On this view, the whole debate between correspondence, coherence, and pragmatism is a pseudo-problem — there's no "truth" to explain, only sentences.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Correspondence theory**: true = the proposition matches or corresponds to reality. Intuitive, but "correspondence" is hard to define precisely.
- **Coherence theory**: true = fits consistently with a system of beliefs. Explains knowledge as a web, but allows for coherent falsehoods.
- **Pragmatic theory**: true = works / is useful to believe. Keeps truth practical, but conflates truth with usefulness.
- **Deflationism**: "true" is a logical/grammatical device, not a name for a deep property — the debate is a pseudo-problem.
- Each theory captures something real; no theory has won the debate. The question of what truth *is* remains one of philosophy's deepest open problems.

</Step>
