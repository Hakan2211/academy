<Step title="The love of wisdom" kind="explain">

The word **philosophy** comes from the Greek *philosophia* — *philo* (love) and *sophia* (wisdom). A philosopher is, literally, a **lover of wisdom**: not someone who claims to have all the answers, but someone who can't stop asking the deepest questions.

And the questions are the deepest there are. *What can I really know? Is there a right way to live? Do I have free will? What makes me the same person I was as a child? Why is there anything at all?* These aren't idle puzzles — they sit underneath everything else you think and do.

What makes a question *philosophical* is that you can't settle it just by looking. You can't weigh "justice" on a scale or point a telescope at "the meaning of life." These questions are answered — when they can be answered — by careful **reasoning and argument**.

</Step>

<Step title="Philosophy, science, and religion" kind="explain">

Three great traditions try to make sense of the world. They're easy to confuse, so it helps to see how each one answers a question:

- **Science** asks what *is* the case and tests its answers by **observation and experiment**. *How old is the universe?*
- **Religion** often answers life's biggest questions through **faith, revelation, and tradition**. *What is sacred? Is there a God?*
- **Philosophy** uses **reason and argument** to examine questions that evidence alone can't settle — including questions *about* science and religion themselves. *What counts as good evidence? Can we prove God exists?*

In fact, every science was once a branch of philosophy. Physics was "natural philosophy"; psychology split off barely 150 years ago. Philosophy is where questions live **before** we have a reliable method to answer them — and some questions never leave home.

</Step>

<Step title="Why it can feel slippery" kind="explain">

Newcomers often complain that philosophy "never reaches an answer." But notice what's really happening. In most subjects you memorise conclusions. In philosophy you learn to **examine the reasons** behind conclusions — including your own.

That's why a philosopher's favourite move is to ask, *"What exactly do you mean?"* and *"How do you know?"* Progress isn't always a final answer; often it's a **better question**, a hidden assumption dragged into the light, or an argument that no longer works once you see its flaw.

Done well, philosophy is the most practical skill there is: it teaches you to think clearly, spot bad reasoning, and hold your own beliefs up to the light.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Philosophy** = the *love of wisdom*: the disciplined asking of life's deepest questions.
- Its questions can't be settled just by looking — they're answered through **reasoning and argument**.
- **Science** uses observation, **religion** often uses faith, **philosophy** uses reason — and it can examine science and religion too.
- Every science began as philosophy; philosophy is where questions live before we have a method to answer them.
- Its real payoff is a habit of mind: ask *"What do you mean?"* and *"How do you know?"*
