<Step title="Before government — the state of nature" kind="explain">

Imagine a world with no laws, no police, no courts, and no government of any kind. Philosophers call this the **state of nature** — not a historical fact but a thought experiment: what would life be like if there were no political authority at all?

The answer reveals *why* we need a state in the first place.

Thomas Hobbes, writing in 1651 amid the chaos of the English Civil War, painted the darkest picture. Without an authority capable of enforcing peace, every person is a potential threat to every other. There is no property (anyone can take what you have), no justice (no courts to appeal to), and no trust. Life in the state of nature, Hobbes concluded, would be **"solitary, poor, nasty, brutish, and short."**

John Locke disagreed. Writing just decades later, he thought people were rational enough to follow a natural law that protected life, liberty, and property even without a government. The state of nature is inconvenient — disputes arise and no one can settle them impartially — but it is not a war zone. Rousseau went further still: he thought the pre-social human ("the noble savage") was naturally good and compassionate, and that it was *civilisation* — private property, inequality, competition — that corrupted us.

</Step>

<Step title="Compare the three social-contract thinkers" kind="interactive">

The social-contract tradition asks a single driving question: why do rational people consent to give up some of their freedom and submit to political authority? Step through the argument for each thinker.

<SocialContract />

Notice that the answer to "what kind of government follows?" depends entirely on your answer to "what is human nature?" Hobbes' frightened individuals need an absolute sovereign. Locke's rights-bearing individuals need only a limited, accountable one. Rousseau's naturally good citizens need to participate directly in the laws they follow.

</Step>

<Step title="What the contract establishes" kind="explain">

All three thinkers share a crucial structural move: legitimate political authority must rest on **consent**, not mere force. A tyrant who rules by violence is not a government in the morally relevant sense — they are just a more powerful threat.

This idea has enormous practical consequences:

- If authority comes from the consent of the governed, citizens may **withdraw consent** when government violates its purpose (Locke's basis for the American Declaration of Independence).
- If authority protects natural rights, it cannot demand that citizens surrender those rights entirely (the foundation of constitutional limits on government power).
- If the "general will" is the only legitimate authority, then laws you helped make — even ones that restrict you — are expressions of your own freedom (Rousseau's paradox: "forced to be free").

The social-contract tradition didn't just describe government — it set a standard by which actual governments could be judged and, if necessary, resisted.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- The **state of nature** is a thought experiment: what would life be like without any political authority?
- **Hobbes**: it would be a war of all against all — an absolute sovereign is the only solution.
- **Locke**: it is imperfect but not hellish — a limited government protecting natural rights is sufficient.
- **Rousseau**: civilisation corrupts natural goodness — legitimate government must express the "general will" through citizen participation.
- All three agree: legitimate authority requires **consent**, which sets a standard for evaluating and — when necessary — resisting actual governments.
