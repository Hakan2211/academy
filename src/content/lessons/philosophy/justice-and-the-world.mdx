<Step title="The drowning child" kind="explain">

In 1972, Peter Singer published a short paper that became one of the most cited — and most uncomfortable — essays in applied ethics. The argument begins with a vivid scenario:

You are walking past a shallow pond. A small child has fallen in and is drowning. You can wade in and save them at the cost of ruining your expensive shoes. Almost everyone agrees: **you must wade in.** The shoes are a trivial cost compared to a child's life. To walk past would be monstrous.

Singer then notes that children are dying of preventable diseases and starvation at this moment — in numbers vastly larger than the one child in the pond. Effective medical interventions and direct nutritional support can save a child's life for roughly the cost of those shoes (in 1972 terms) or a relatively modest donation (in modern ones).

**Singer's argument**: If you ought to ruin your shoes to save the child in front of you, what morally relevant difference does distance make? The child across the world is equally real, equally capable of suffering, equally dead if no one helps. The only difference is that you cannot see them — and that, Singer insists, is not a morally relevant difference. It is a psychological one.

His principle: *"if it is in our power to prevent something bad from happening, without thereby sacrificing anything of comparable moral importance, we ought, morally, to do it."*

</Step>

<Step title="Vary the distance and the cost" kind="interactive">

Singer's argument is not just a thought experiment — it is interactive. Change the distance to the child in need and the cost of helping. Notice how your intuition shifts, then ask whether the shift tracks any genuine moral difference.

The argument is not that you must impoverish yourself. Singer distinguishes a **strong** version (give until you approach equal sacrifice) from a **moderate** version (give until the marginal cost to you begins to matter more than the marginal benefit to the recipient). Even the moderate version implies giving far more than most people in wealthy countries currently do.

</Step>

<Step title="The limits of obligation" kind="explain">

Singer's argument is powerful, but it has attracted equally serious objections.

**The demandingness objection** is the most common: if we are obligated to give until we are approaching equal deprivation with the worst-off, morality becomes impossibly demanding. We are permitted to have lives, projects, relationships, and pursuits that consume resources without producing global-welfare gains. A moral theory that leaves no room for personal projects seems to misunderstand what a human life is.

Singer's reply: the argument shows that our obligations are much greater than common sense allows, but common sense is not automatically correct. Moral progress often feels "too demanding" before it is accepted. He accepts a moderate version: give until giving more would harm something of genuine moral importance in your own life.

**The special-obligations objection**: We have stronger duties to our family, our community, and our compatriots than to distant strangers. A parent who spent their child's education fund on famine relief, because more QALYs per dollar could be bought there, would be a worse parent — not a better person. Special relations generate real moral weight.

Singer grants special obligations exist, but argues they cannot simply extinguish the general obligation to prevent easily preventable death at modest cost. The question is not whether special obligations exist but how they weigh against general ones.

**The systemic-change objection**: Individual charity treats symptoms while leaving unjust structures in place. What is needed is political change — fairer trade rules, debt cancellation, institutional reform. Directing moral energy toward charity distracts from advocacy.

Singer's response: we can do both, and the people who most need help now cannot wait for systemic change. The systemic objection is often used to rationalise inaction on both fronts.

</Step>

<Step title="Rawls and global justice" kind="explain">

Singer's argument is essentially **utilitarian**: the obligation follows from welfare arithmetic and the absence of a morally relevant distinction between near and far suffering.

**John Rawls** offers a different framework. Behind the "veil of ignorance" — not knowing your place in society — rational agents would choose principles that protect the worst-off. Rawls applied this domestically, generating the **difference principle**: inequalities are only just if they benefit the least-advantaged members of society.

**Thomas Pogge** extended Rawlsian thinking globally: wealthy nations actively maintain international institutions (trade rules, patent regimes, debt structures) that keep poor countries poor. This is not merely a failure to help; it is active harm. The obligation to stop harming is even stronger than the obligation to help.

**Michael Walzer** pushed back: justice is always embedded in a community's particular history and self-understanding. There is no universal, culturally neutral standard. Obligations run most strongly within communities.

The debate between these positions is, at bottom, about how far moral impartiality extends — and whether distance, culture, and community membership are morally relevant features or morally irrelevant contingencies of birth.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Singer's drowning-child argument**: if you'd ruin your shoes to save a child in front of you, distance alone cannot remove the obligation to save a child far away.
- **The principle**: prevent bad things from happening when you can, without sacrificing anything of comparable moral importance.
- **Demandingness objection**: morality cannot require us to give until we approach equal deprivation. Singer's reply: obligations are greater than common sense admits; a moderate version is still demanding.
- **Special-obligations objection**: stronger duties run toward family, community, compatriots. Singer: these do not extinguish general obligations.
- **Systemic-change objection**: charity treats symptoms; structural change is needed. Singer: we can do both; the urgently poor cannot wait.
- **Rawls and Pogge**: wealthy nations do not merely fail to help — they actively maintain unjust global structures, generating strong obligations.
- The debate turns on how far moral impartiality extends — whether proximity and membership are morally relevant or morally arbitrary.

</Step>
