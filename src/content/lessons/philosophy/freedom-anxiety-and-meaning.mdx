<Step title="The first existentialist" kind="explain">

Before Sartre, before Heidegger, before Camus — there was **Søren Kierkegaard** (1813–1855), the melancholy Danish philosopher who is widely regarded as the father of existentialism, though he would have found the label as perplexing as any other label.

Kierkegaard's central conviction was that **truth is subjective**: the most important questions — how to live, what to commit to, what gives life meaning — cannot be answered by detached, objective reasoning alone. They must be *lived*. "The task is to become a subject," he wrote.

He described three **stages of existence**:
- **The aesthetic stage** — living for pleasure, novelty, and sensation; drifting from experience to experience without commitment. Ultimately produces boredom and despair.
- **The ethical stage** — living by duty, reason, and social norms; taking responsibility and committing to principles. Stable, but perhaps still insufficient.
- **The religious stage** — a *leap of faith* beyond what reason can justify; a personal, passionate relationship with God or the transcendent. Kierkegaard himself embraced this stage; later existentialists (Sartre, Camus) refused the leap but kept the passion.

</Step>

<Step title="The dizziness of freedom" kind="explain">

In *The Concept of Anxiety* (1844), Kierkegaard offers one of the most precise accounts of a feeling we all recognise but rarely name.

Stand at the edge of a high cliff. You feel fear — but fear has an *object* (the fall). Yet you might notice something else: a strange pull, a vertiginous awareness that you *could* jump. You won't. You don't want to. But the fact that nothing physically stops you, that freedom extends even here, produces a peculiar dizziness.

Kierkegaard calls this **"the dizziness of freedom"** — anxiety (*Angst*) in the face of infinite possibility. Unlike fear, anxiety has no determinate object. You are not afraid *of* something. You are anxious *at* freedom itself — at the recognition that you must choose, that the future is open, and that you bear responsibility for what you make of it.

This anxiety is not a pathology to be cured. It is the *signal* of genuine freedom. The person who feels no anxiety about their choices has not grasped the weight of what they are doing.

</Step>

<Step title="The hidden choices — freedom made tangible" kind="interactive">

One of the most practically useful insights of existentialism is this: the situations where we feel most "forced" are often the ones where our freedom is most hidden from us. Revealing the hidden choices is the first step toward owning them.

Kierkegaard's prescription for the anxiety this produces: not to escape it, but to commit — to leap into a choice and own it fully. Commitment does not eliminate anxiety; it transforms it into the productive tension of a life being actually lived.

<Step title="The existentialists in their moment" kind="explain">

<PhilosophyTimeline era="existential" />

What unites these thinkers across their differences? Each was responding to the same crisis: the collapse of the old frameworks (divine order, rational progress, fixed human nature) that had given European civilization its sense of meaning and direction. Each asked: *now what?*

- **Kierkegaard** (1843): leap into passionate personal commitment.
- **Nietzsche** (1883): create your own values; the will to power.
- **Heidegger** (1927): confront your mortality; live authentically rather than in the crowd.
- **Sartre** (1943): existence precedes essence; own your radical freedom.
- **Camus** (1942): hold the absurd with clear eyes and live fully anyway.

They disagree profoundly — especially on whether the religious leap is honest (Kierkegaard: yes; Camus: no). But they share the insistence that philosophy is not an academic exercise: it is the very urgent question of how to live when no one else is going to answer it for you.

</Step>

<Step title="Creating meaning" kind="explain">

If there is no built-in meaning — no God who designed us, no fixed human nature to fulfil, no cosmic script — then the existentialists agree: **meaning is created, not found.**

This is not the same as meaning being arbitrary or merely subjective in a dismissive sense. Sartre argued that our choices always *imply* a picture of what is worth choosing — and in choosing, we in some sense choose for all humans ("what I ought to do"). The weight of freedom is also its dignity.

Practical takeaways from existentialism:

- **Own your choices** — even the default ones, even the "forced" ones. You are always authoring your life.
- **Commit** — Kierkegaard's lesson: authentic existence requires leaping into projects you cannot fully justify in advance.
- **Be present to mortality** — Heidegger's lesson: finitude is not a problem; it is what makes each choice genuinely yours.
- **Rebel and live** — Camus' lesson: the universe's silence is not the last word; your response is.

Existentialism is, in the end, a philosophy of responsibility — and a bracing form of hope.

</Step>
