<Step title="What is intrinsically good?" kind="explain">

Moral philosophy asks not just "what should we do?" but a prior question: **what is good?** What things are worth having, pursuing, or preserving — and *why*?

The key distinction is one we have already encountered: **intrinsic value** versus **instrumental value**.

Something has **intrinsic value** when it is good *in itself* — valuable as an end, independently of what it leads to. Something has **instrumental value** when it is good *as a means* — worth having only because it produces something else that is valuable.

Money is the paradigm case of pure instrumental value: it is worth nothing in itself; it is valuable only because of what you can get with it. Happiness, by contrast, is a candidate for intrinsic value: even if happiness led to *nothing else*, it would still seem worth having.

Major theories disagree about what is intrinsically good:

- **Hedonism** (Bentham, Mill): only pleasure/happiness has intrinsic value; pain is the only intrinsic disvalue.
- **Desire satisfactionism**: what is intrinsically good is the satisfaction of one's preferences — even if satisfaction brings no conscious pleasure.
- **Objective list theories**: certain things are intrinsically good regardless of whether anyone desires them — knowledge, friendship, achievement, health.

Whatever the correct theory, the question of intrinsic value shapes everything in normative ethics.

</Step>

<Step title="Moral status: who counts?" kind="explain">

Even if we know what is intrinsically good, we face a second question: **whose good counts morally?** This is the question of **moral status** (or moral patiency).

An entity has **moral status** if its interests count directly in moral reasoning — if its wellbeing can make a moral claim on how we act. Entities with full moral status are sometimes called **moral patients**.

The contested question is: what *grounds* moral status? Candidates include:

- **Rationality/autonomy** (Kant): only rational agents — beings capable of reason and self-governance — have full moral status.
- **Sentience** (Bentham, Singer): any being capable of suffering and enjoyment has interests that count. "The question is not, Can they reason? nor, Can they talk? but, Can they suffer?"
- **Being human** (speciesism, natural law): membership in the human species confers status, independently of capacities.
- **Relationships and social standing** (care ethics): moral status is partly constituted by webs of relationship and social recognition.

The answer matters profoundly. If rationality is required, severely cognitively impaired humans and higher animals have reduced status — a troubling conclusion. If sentience is sufficient, factory farming becomes one of the most morally important issues on Earth.

</Step>

<Step title="The expanding circle" kind="interactive">

Philosopher Peter Singer uses the image of the **expanding circle**: through history, the range of entities accorded moral status has widened — from kin to tribe to nation to all humans, and now to the debate about animals and future generations. Each expansion was once considered radical. Where do you draw the line — and why?

<MoralStatusCircle />

Notice that wherever you draw the boundary, the entities just outside it make a claim on you: they have interests, they suffer, they exist in time. The question is always: what morally relevant difference justifies exclusion?

</Step>

<Step title="Future generations and beyond" kind="explain">

One of the most contested expansions concerns **future generations**. People who do not yet exist cannot advocate for themselves, cannot vote, cannot sue. Yet our decisions about climate change, nuclear waste, and biodiversity will profoundly affect billions of future people.

Do future people have moral status now? If sentience grounds moral status, then a person who will exist and will suffer has interests that are morally relevant *now*, even before they are born. Philosopher Derek Parfit devoted much of his career to this question, arguing that the sheer number of potential future people makes their welfare enormously morally important — perhaps the most important moral consideration of all.

The **non-identity problem** (Parfit) adds a puzzle: the very people who will be affected by our decisions would not have existed but for those decisions. Can you harm someone by bringing them into a life worth living? This seemingly academic question has real implications for climate policy, reproductive decisions, and intergenerational justice.

The debate about **animal rights** raises similar structure: animals cannot consent, cannot participate in moral discourse, yet they clearly suffer. Singer argues that failing to give animal suffering equal weight is **speciesism** — a prejudice as arbitrary as racism — because species membership is not itself a morally relevant difference.

<Callout type="info" title="Peter Singer on the expanding circle">
"At any given moment, the prevailing morality of a society is accepted by those who benefit from that society. Change comes when those on the boundaries of the circle — or those outside it — make their voices heard, or when others speak for them."
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Intrinsic value**: good in itself, as an end. **Instrumental value**: good as a means.
- Major theories of the good: hedonism (pleasure), desire satisfactionism, objective list theories.
- **Moral status**: whose interests count directly in moral reasoning.
- Grounds proposed: rationality, sentience, humanity, relationships.
- **The expanding circle** (Singer): historically, moral concern has widened — kin → tribe → all humans → animals → future generations.
- **Speciesism**: the arbitrary privileging of species membership over sentience as the criterion for moral status.
- Future generations and animals are the live frontier of debates about moral status.

</Step>
