<Step title="Beyond the simple story" kind="explain">

Popular culture often presents science and religion as locked in permanent warfare: superstition retreating before the advance of reason, with each new scientific discovery shrinking the space available for God. This picture is historically distorted and philosophically crude — but it is also not entirely without foundation. The relationship is more complex, more interesting, and more contested than any simple slogan captures.

The **conflict narrative** was largely constructed in the late nineteenth century — particularly by John William Draper (*History of the Conflict between Religion and Science*, 1874) and Andrew Dickson White (*A History of the Warfare of Science with Theology*, 1896). Historians of science have since shown that both books systematically distorted the historical record. The Galileo affair was as much about politics and personality as about religion versus science. Medieval universities — largely church institutions — were the primary homes of natural philosophy. Many founders of modern science (Copernicus, Kepler, Newton, Mendel, Lemaître) were devout believers.

Yet genuine conflicts have occurred. Young-Earth creationism is in direct conflict with geology, astronomy, and biology. And some religious claims — miracles, answered prayer, divinely determined events — are at least in principle in tension with a closed causal account of the natural world.

The question for philosophy is not "who won?" but "what kind of relationship do these domains actually have?"

<Step title="What science can and cannot answer" kind="explain">

Science is extraordinarily powerful within its domain. It can answer questions about the mechanisms of the natural world: how species arise, how galaxies form, how neurons process information, how diseases spread. Its methods — controlled experimentation, peer review, falsifiable prediction — are the most reliable tools humans have developed for producing reliable knowledge about empirical reality.

But there are questions science does not answer — not because of its current limitations, but because of what kind of questions they are:

**Why is there something rather than nothing?** Science can describe the processes by which the universe evolves from an initial state; it cannot explain why there is an initial state (or a universe at all) rather than nothing. This is not a gap in our knowledge — it is a question that appears to lie at the boundary of empirical inquiry.

**Why does anything matter?** Science describes the mechanisms of suffering, but it does not tell us that suffering is bad. Normative claims — that pain matters, that kindness is better than cruelty — are not derivable from empirical facts alone (Hume's is-ought gap). Moral philosophy addresses these questions; science provides their empirical context.

**What is the meaning of a life?** Science can tell you how long you will live, what will make you happier or less happy, what kinds of relationships your brain responds to. It cannot tell you what you should be living *for*.

These are not non-questions. They are among the most important questions human beings face. Religion, philosophy, literature, and the arts address them — from different angles and with different tools.

</Step>

<Step title="Where the boundary really lies" kind="explain">

The philosophical insight at the heart of this debate is a clarification about domains and methods:

**Science** is the systematic empirical inquiry into the mechanisms of the natural world, using observation, experiment, and falsifiable theory. Its authority is properly strongest in exactly this domain.

**Religion** (in its philosophical dimensions) addresses questions of ultimate origin, meaning, value, and what it is to live well in relation to what is ultimately real. These questions are not answered by controlled experiment — not because they are irrational, but because they call for different modes of inquiry.

The conflict arises when either side overreaches: when religion makes specific empirical claims that contradict well-established science (the Earth is six thousand years old; evolution did not occur), or when science claims authority over questions of meaning and value that lie outside its methods ("science shows that life has no purpose").

Richard Dawkins arguing that God's existence is a scientific hypothesis, and therefore disconfirmed by the evidence, is overreaching in exactly the way that Young-Earth creationists overreach — both assume the question belongs entirely to science. The more careful position is to be precise about what each domain can claim, and honest about where genuine conflicts (vs apparent ones) lie.

<Callout type="tip" title="Gould's NOMA">
Stephen Jay Gould's "Non-Overlapping Magisteria" (NOMA) is the most compact statement of the independence model: science gets the empirical domain; religion gets meaning, value, and purpose. The attraction is its clarity; the challenge is that the magisteria may overlap more than Gould admits — both science and religion make some claims that bear on the other.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="The scientists who were believers, and the believers who did science" kind="explain">

The conflict narrative is also undermined by the sheer number of major scientists who have held religious beliefs — not as a compartmentalised private matter, but as an integrated part of their intellectual life:

**Georges Lemaître** (1894–1966): the Belgian priest who first proposed the Big Bang theory, derived from Einstein's equations. He was emphatic that his cosmology was science, not theology, but he saw no contradiction between the two.

**Gregor Mendel** (1822–1884): the Augustinian friar whose experiments with pea plants founded modern genetics.

**Francis Collins** (born 1950): director of the Human Genome Project and former director of the NIH; a convert to Christianity who has written extensively on integrating faith and scientific practice.

**Freeman Dyson** (1923–2020): one of the great physicists of the twentieth century, who wrote thoughtfully about the relationship between science and religion without compartmentalising either.

None of this proves God's existence. But it does complicate the picture of science and religion as inherently at war. Many of the most rigorous scientific minds have found the two commitments compatible — and have thought carefully about how.

The philosopher Alvin Plantinga argued (in *Where the Conflict Really Lies*, 2011) that the deepest conflict is not between science and theism, but between science and *naturalism* (the view that the natural world is all there is). If naturalism is true, our cognitive faculties were selected for survival, not truth — which gives us reason to doubt them, including our scientific reasoning. Theism, by contrast, grounds the reliability of reason in a God who created rational beings to apprehend truth.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- The **conflict narrative** was largely constructed in the late nineteenth century and is historically distorted, though genuine conflicts have occurred when either domain overreaches.
- **Three models** of the science-religion relationship: Conflict (they compete for the same territory), Independence/NOMA (they address different domains), Dialogue (they can inform each other).
- **What science answers**: empirical mechanisms of the natural world — how things work, what causes what.
- **What science does not answer by its methods**: why there is something rather than nothing; why anything matters morally; what meaning a life should have.
- The conflict arises from **overreach** — when religion makes specific empirical claims contradicted by science, or when science claims authority over meaning and value.
- Many founders of modern science were devout believers (Mendel, Lemaître, Newton, Kepler); scientific rigor and religious commitment have coexisted throughout the history of science.
- **Plantinga's inversion**: the deepest conflict may be between science and *naturalism* — if our faculties were selected for survival not truth, scientific reasoning itself is in question on the naturalist picture.

</Step>
