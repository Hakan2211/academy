<Step title="The puzzle of the one and the many" kind="explain">

Look at three objects: an apple, a rose, a red car. Each is a distinct, particular thing — *this* apple, *this* rose, *this* car. Yet something strikes us as **the same** in all three. We use the same word: *red*.

What exactly is that sameness? This is the **Problem of Universals**, one of the oldest and most contested questions in metaphysics. Is there some one thing — **Redness** — that all red objects share? Or is there nothing beyond the particular objects themselves, and "red" is just a convenient label we attach to a loosely similar group?

The stakes are higher than they look. Your answer here ripples out into questions about science (are laws of nature universal?), mathematics (do numbers exist?), morality (is there a universal property of goodness?), and language (what does a general term actually refer to?).

</Step>

<Step title="Realism: the Forms are real" kind="explain">

Plato's answer was unambiguous. The many particular red things are red because they each **participate** in, or *instantiate*, the universal Form of Redness — an abstract, eternal, unchanging entity that exists independently of any physical thing. If every red object in the universe were destroyed, the Form of Redness would still exist in the Platonic realm.

Later philosophers called this position **universals realism** (or just *realism*). The core claim: **universals are real, mind-independent entities.** Aristotle modified the view: he accepted universals but located them *in* particular things rather than a separate realm. The redness exists in the apple, not in some detached Platonic heaven — but it is still a genuine universal shared across instances.

This is an attractive view. It explains why scientific laws hold everywhere: "force equals mass times acceleration" is a universal truth because universal properties (force, mass, acceleration) really do exist.

</Step>

<Step title="Nominalism: names all the way down" kind="explain">

The rival position says there is no abstract entity called Redness. There are only particular things — this apple, this rose, this car. When we say they are all "red" we are simply applying the same **name** (*nomen* in Latin) to things that resemble each other in a certain way. The name is useful shorthand; it does not point to any extra object in the world.

William of Ockham (14th century) was the great medieval champion of nominalism. His famous **Ockham's Razor** — *don't multiply entities beyond necessity* — cuts away the whole Platonic realm at a stroke. If we can explain our use of general terms without positing abstract universals, we should.

Nominalism keeps ontology (the inventory of what exists) lean and tidy. The cost is explaining *why* resemblance works — why the apple, rose, and car resemble each other in a way that tables and triangles do not. Some nominalists say resemblance is a primitive, basic fact. Others give accounts in terms of sets of similar particulars (class nominalism) or paradigm cases (resemblance nominalism).

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- The **Problem of Universals**: what is shared by many distinct particulars that fall under the same concept?
- **Realism**: universals (Redness, Triangularity, Justice…) are real, mind-independent entities — either in a separate Platonic realm or immanent in particulars.
- **Nominalism**: there are only particulars. General words are names we attach to resembling things; no abstract universal exists.
- Key figure for realism: Plato (and later Aristotle, in a moderate form). Key figure for nominalism: William of Ockham.
- The debate matters for science, mathematics, ethics, and the philosophy of language.

</Step>
