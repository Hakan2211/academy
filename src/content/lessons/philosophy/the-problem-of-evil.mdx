<Step title="The challenge" kind="explain">

The **problem of evil** is widely considered the most powerful philosophical argument against the existence of a God who is simultaneously omnipotent (all-powerful), omnibenevolent (all-good), and omniscient (all-knowing). The argument does not deny that people experience God; it asks whether the existence of evil and suffering is compatible with the existence of such a God.

The ancient version is often attributed to Epicurus: if God is willing to prevent evil but not able, God is not omnipotent. If able but not willing, God is not omnibenevolent. If both able and willing, where does evil come from? If neither able nor willing, why call this being God?

Philosophers distinguish two versions:

**The logical problem of evil** (J. L. Mackie, 1955): God and evil are *logically incompatible*. An omnipotent, omnibenevolent, omniscient God would prevent all evil. Evil exists. Therefore no such God exists. This is presented as a formal contradiction.

**The evidential problem of evil** (William Rowe, 1979): the sheer scale and distribution of suffering — the death of innocents, extreme natural disasters, animal suffering over millions of years of evolution — makes it *highly improbable* that an all-good God exists. Even if there is no formal contradiction, the evidence is strongly against theism.

</Step>

<Step title="Logical and evidential versions" kind="explain">

The distinction matters because theists have a strategy against the logical version: **the free-will defence** (Alvin Plantinga, 1974). Plantinga argues that there is no formal contradiction between God and evil, because God could not create free beings who always chose good. Genuine freedom logically requires the possibility of choosing evil. If this is so, an omnipotent God could not eliminate all evil while also creating genuinely free beings — and a world with free beings may be better than a world of moral automatons.

This response, many philosophers agree, largely dissolves the *logical* problem. It does not, however, obviously address the *evidential* problem. Even if some moral evil follows from human freedom, natural evil — earthquakes, cancer, the suffering of animals before any human being existed — does not. And even among human-caused suffering, the scale seems disproportionate to any plausible purpose.

The evidential problem therefore remains a live debate: even granting Plantinga's point, does the world we actually observe — with its particular distribution of suffering — look like the creation of an all-good, all-powerful God?

</Step>

No theodicy has produced a consensus. The free-will defence addresses moral evil powerfully but not natural evil; soul-making extends further but faces the problem of gratuitous suffering; the limits-of-understanding response is epistemically modest but may come at the cost of positive reasons for belief. The debate is genuinely open.

</Step>

<Step title="The wider significance" kind="explain">

The problem of evil is significant beyond its technical philosophical dimensions. It is existentially live: people confronting profound suffering — their own or others' — have always asked whether it is compatible with a loving God. Philosophical theodicies are not simply academic exercises; they are responses to real human experience.

Both the problem and the responses deserve serious engagement:

The problem is pressing. The scale of suffering — including the suffering of those who did nothing to deserve it, including children, including animals — is a genuine datum that any complete theology must address.

The responses are serious. The free-will defence, the soul-making theodicy, and epistemic humility about God's reasons are not evasions — they are substantive positions defended by rigorous philosophers, many of them people of deep personal faith who have confronted the problem honestly.

The philosopher Marilyn McCord Adams proposed focusing on *horrendous evils* — evils so severe that participation in them seems to defeat any positive meaning a person's life could have — and arguing that only a God of infinite value could redeem such evils by integrating them into a larger narrative of meaning. Whether this succeeds is itself debated.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- The **problem of evil** argues that an omnipotent, omnibenevolent, omniscient God is incompatible with the existence of evil and suffering.
- **Logical version** (Mackie): God and evil are formally contradictory.
- **Evidential version** (Rowe): the scale and distribution of suffering makes God's existence highly improbable.
- **Free-will defence** (Plantinga): genuine freedom requires the possibility of evil; God could not create free beings who always choose good. Largely answers the logical problem.
- **Soul-making theodicy** (Hick): suffering is the medium of moral and spiritual growth; a challenge-free world could not produce virtuous persons.
- **Limits of understanding**: we may not be in a position to assess God's reasons; finite minds cannot expect to grasp infinite purposes.
- The evidential problem — especially natural evil and gratuitous suffering — remains the most challenging for theism. The debate is live and serious on both sides.

</Step>
