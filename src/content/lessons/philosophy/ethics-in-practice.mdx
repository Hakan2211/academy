<Step title="Why theories matter in practice" kind="explain">

A theory that cannot engage with concrete decisions is useless. But a concrete decision made without theoretical resources is liable to be confused, inconsistent, or swayed by bias. The point of ethical theory is not to replace practical wisdom — it is to sharpen it.

In this lesson we take one of the oldest and most studied dilemmas in moral philosophy — **the trolley problem** — and run it through all three major frameworks. The goal is not to produce a definitive answer (moral philosophers still debate this) but to make vivid how the frameworks diverge, where they converge, and what each illuminates that the others miss.

</Step>

<Step title="The dilemma in full" kind="explain">

**The trolley problem** (originally due to Philippa Foot, 1967; elaborated by Judith Jarvis Thomson):

A runaway trolley is hurtling down a track. Ahead, tied to the track, are five people who will certainly be killed if the trolley reaches them. You stand at a lever. If you pull it, the trolley diverts onto a side track — where one person is tied. If you pull the lever, one person dies and five are saved. If you do nothing, five people die.

What should you do?

A second version — the footbridge case — sharpens the analysis: now you are standing on a bridge above the track. A large stranger stands next to you. The only way to stop the trolley and save the five is to push the stranger off the bridge; his body is the only thing large enough to stop it. Should you push him?

Most people say: pull the lever; do not push the man. Both cases involve one death to save five. Why do so many people treat them differently?

</Step>

<Step title="Three frameworks analyse the trolley" kind="explain">

**Consequentialism** analyses both cases the same way: five lives saved versus one life lost. The utilitarian arithmetic points clearly in one direction — pull the lever, and push the man. Any hesitation is mere squeamishness; outcomes are what matter, and five lives outweigh one.

The consequentialist may acknowledge that the footbridge case feels different — but insists that feeling is an evolutionary bias (a reluctance to direct physical force at a person), not a morally relevant distinction. The numbers are the numbers.

**Deontology** draws a sharp line. In the lever case, you divert a threat already in motion — you are redirecting harm, not initiating it. The one person on the side track is killed by the trolley, not by you in any direct sense. Many deontologists, applying the **doctrine of double effect**, permit this: you intend to save the five; the death of the one is foreseen but not intended as a means.

In the footbridge case, you use the large man's body as a means — as a trolley-stopper. You intend his death as part of your plan. This violates the Formula of Humanity: you treat him as an instrument, not as a person. The deontologist says: do not push. Five deaths is a tragedy; murder is a different kind of wrong.

**Virtue ethics** resists the clean calculation. The virtuous person feels the full weight of both cases — not with cold calculation, but with appropriate moral gravity. Practical wisdom (phronesis) is required: what would a person of genuinely good character do, and be, in this moment?

Most virtue ethicists would permit pulling the lever; the action is not one of malice or indifference, and the character it expresses is that of someone willing to bear a terrible responsibility to prevent greater harm. The footbridge case is harder: actively pushing a person to their death requires a kind of deliberate violence that seems inconsistent with the character of a just, compassionate person. The virtue ethicist emphasises **moral remainder** — even the permitted action leaves a residue of grief and responsibility that a good person takes seriously.

</Step>

<Step title="Run the dilemmas yourself" kind="interactive">

Now apply the three ethical lenses yourself to several different dilemmas — including the trolley-style scenario. Notice how each framework frames the question differently, what it emphasises, and what it tends to miss.

<EthicalLenses />

Pay close attention to where the frameworks converge (some actions look wrong from every angle) and where they diverge (the trolley footbridge is a case of maximal divergence). Divergence is philosophically productive: it tells you which features of the case are morally contested.

</Step>

<Step title="Convergence, divergence, and moral residue" kind="explain">

After running a dilemma through multiple frameworks, three patterns emerge:

**Convergence.** When all three frameworks agree — consequentialist, deontological, and virtue-ethical analysis all point the same way — that is a strong reason to act accordingly. Gratuitous cruelty, for example, is condemned from every angle. Convergence gives moral confidence.

**Divergence.** When frameworks disagree, you face a genuine hard case. You cannot simply "apply the right theory" because the question of which theory is right is precisely what is contested. What you can do is identify which features of the situation each framework is highlighting, and make a judgment about which features seem most morally relevant in this case.

**Moral residue.** Even when you act rightly, some actions leave a **moral remainder** — a residue of regret, grief, or responsibility that a good person acknowledges. If you pull the lever and one person dies, you did the right thing — but something terrible happened and you were its proximate cause. Bernard Williams called this "agent-regret": the proper response of a morally serious person who caused harm even in the course of doing right.

Recognising moral residue is not weakness. It is a mark of moral seriousness: the recognition that tragic situations involve real losses that cannot be cancelled out by correct calculations.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- Ethical theories are tools for moral thinking — not algorithms that replace judgment.
- **Consequentialism** evaluates both trolley cases the same: pull the lever and push the man. Five lives outweigh one.
- **Deontology** distinguishes them: redirecting harm (lever) may be permissible; using a person as a means (footbridge) is not. The doctrine of double effect matters.
- **Virtue ethics** asks what a person of practical wisdom and good character would do — acknowledging the full moral weight of both choices.
- **Convergence** across frameworks gives moral confidence; **divergence** reveals which features of a case are genuinely contested.
- **Moral residue**: even right action in tragic situations leaves grief and responsibility — a mark of moral seriousness, not weakness.
