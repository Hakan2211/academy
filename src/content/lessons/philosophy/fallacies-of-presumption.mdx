<Step title="Arguments that smuggle in assumptions" kind="explain">

**Fallacies of presumption** are a subtler breed. Unlike fallacies of relevance — which distract you — fallacies of presumption work by *hiding an unwarranted assumption inside the argument itself*. On the surface, the reasoning looks airtight. But buried in the premises is something the argument assumes to be true without any justification.

Four patterns dominate this family:

**False dilemma** (false dichotomy) — presenting a choice as if exactly two options exist when in fact there are more. *"Either you support this policy completely, or you don't care about children."* Forcing a binary erases every nuanced middle position, making it seem like refusing the false choice means endorsing the extreme.

**Begging the question** (circular reasoning) — slipping the conclusion into the premises. *"We know the ancient texts are reliable because reliable ancient texts say so."* The argument goes in a circle: you can only accept the premise if you already believe the conclusion. The Latin name is *petitio principii* — requesting what is at stake.

**Slippery slope** — asserting that one step will inevitably lead to an extreme outcome through a chain of events, without evidence that each step must follow. The error is treating the chain as *inevitable* rather than *possible*. *"If we allow mild censorship, we'll end up in a totalitarian state within a generation."* Perhaps — but asserting the chain doesn't establish it.

**Hasty generalisation** — drawing a broad conclusion from an unrepresentative or too-small sample. *"I asked three students whether the test was fair and they all said no — clearly the whole class thinks it was unfair."* Three students, even if honest, can't speak for hundreds.

Notice that each fallacy leaves a real concern in its shadow: sometimes the options really are binary; sometimes a slippery slope really does happen; sometimes a small sample is the best available evidence. The fallacy isn't in the concern — it's in treating the assumption as already established.

<Step title="Diagnosing hidden assumptions" kind="explain">

The best tool for catching fallacies of presumption is to make their hidden assumptions explicit and then ask: *has anyone shown this is true?*

For a false dilemma, ask: *"Are those really the only options? What lies between them?"*

For begging the question, ask: *"Does accepting this premise require me to already believe the conclusion?"*

For a slippery slope, ask: *"What specific mechanism makes each step follow from the one before? Has anyone demonstrated that it's likely?"*

For a hasty generalisation, ask: *"How large and representative is the sample? Could it be cherry-picked?"*

<Callout type="warn" title="Not all slopes are slippery">

A slippery-slope argument is not automatically fallacious. If someone can show *why* each step in the chain is probable — not just possible — the argument may be legitimate. The fallacy lies in asserting inevitability without that evidence. Historians studying the path to authoritarian regimes have documented real slippery slopes; the difference is they show the mechanism.

</Callout>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Fallacies of presumption** smuggle an unjustified assumption into the premises of an argument.
- **False dilemma**: presents only two options when many more exist.
- **Begging the question**: the conclusion is hidden inside the premise — the argument goes in a circle.
- **Slippery slope**: treats a chain of events as *inevitable* without evidence for each link.
- **Hasty generalisation**: draws a broad conclusion from too small or unrepresentative a sample.
- The fix: surface the hidden assumption and ask whether anyone has actually established it.

</Step>
