<Step title="Philosophy as a way of life" kind="explain">

When Alexander the Great died in 323 BCE, the Greek city-states lost their independence and were absorbed into vast empires. The old civic world — in which Socrates and Plato had assumed that the good person needed the good city — was gone. Ordinary people found themselves in a cosmopolitan, unpredictable world ruled by distant kings.

Philosophy responded to this. The great Hellenistic schools — **Stoicism**, **Epicureanism**, and **Skepticism** — all asked the same practical question: **how can an individual live well in a world they cannot control?** Their answers are less abstract than Plato's or Aristotle's and more urgently applicable to daily life.

At roughly the same moment, in India and China, philosophers were asking the same question — and arriving at answers that are strikingly parallel and sometimes eerily similar.

</Step>

<Step title="Epicureanism: modest pleasures, freedom from fear" kind="explain">

**Epicurus** (341–270 BCE) is one of philosophy's most misunderstood figures. The word "epicurean" now conjures images of fine dining and luxury. The historical Epicurus lived on bread, olives, and water, in a garden outside Athens, with a small community of friends — including, scandalously, women and slaves.

His ethics:
- The good is **pleasure** (*hedone*), but not intense bodily pleasure — that leads to pain and dependency.
- The highest pleasure is **ataraxia**: tranquility, freedom from mental disturbance. The absence of suffering, not the presence of excitement.
- The chief sources of suffering are: **fear of the gods**, **fear of death**, and **unfulfilled bodily desires**. Philosophy dissolves all three.
- His argument against the fear of death is almost mathematical: *"When death is, I am not; when I am, death is not."* Death cannot harm you because there is no "you" to be harmed.
- The best life involves **friendship** (he considered it the greatest good), **modest pleasures**, and **withdrawal from politics**.

<Callout type="info" title="Epicurus on death">
Epicurus revived Democritus's atomism: the soul, like the body, is made of atoms that disperse at death. There is no afterlife and no divine punishment. This was meant to be liberating, not bleak — if there is no afterlife to fear, you are free to focus entirely on living well now.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Stoicism: what is in your control?" kind="interactive">

**Stoicism** was founded in Athens around 300 BCE by Zeno of Citium, who taught in the *stoa poikile* (painted porch) — hence the name. Its greatest voices are later: **Epictetus** (a freed slave), **Marcus Aurelius** (a Roman emperor), and **Seneca** (a statesman).

The Stoic key insight — the **dichotomy of control** — may be the single most practically useful idea in all of ancient philosophy:

> *"Some things are in our control and others not. Things in our control are opinion, pursuit, desire, aversion — in a word, whatever are our own actions. Things not in our control are body, reputation, command, and in one word, whatever are not our own actions."* — Epictetus, *Enchiridion* §1

Sort these situations into the two categories — and discover where Stoic peace actually lives.

<Step title="Stoic ethics: virtue, reason, and the cosmopolis" kind="explain">

Stoic ethics goes deeper than the dichotomy of control:

- **Only virtue is truly good.** Wealth, health, reputation — these are "preferred indifferents." Nice to have, but their loss should not touch your core wellbeing.
- **Virtue is living according to reason and nature.** Humans are rational by nature; therefore, the virtuous life is the rational life.
- **All humans share in universal reason (the Logos).** This makes Stoics the first philosophers to argue for a genuine **cosmopolitanism** — we are all citizens of one world-city (cosmopolis), regardless of nationality. Epictetus, the slave, and Marcus Aurelius, the emperor, were both citizens of the same cosmic community.
- The Stoic sage — the person of perfect virtue — is "free" even in chains. No external circumstance can take away the freedom of the inner citadel: your own mind.

<Callout type="tip" title="Stoicism in the modern world">
Cognitive Behavioural Therapy (CBT), developed in the 20th century, was explicitly influenced by Stoicism. The CBT insight that it is not events but our *interpretations* of events that disturb us is a direct descendant of Epictetus: "Men are disturbed not by things, but by their opinions about things."
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Skepticism and the Eastern echoes" kind="explain">

**Pyrrhonian Skepticism** (Pyrrho, ~360–270 BCE) offered a third answer to how to live well: **suspend all judgment** (*epoché*). For every argument, there is an equally strong counter-argument. Since we cannot know which is true, we should neither assert nor deny anything — and in this suspension, tranquility (*ataraxia*, the same word as Epicurus!) follows naturally.

Meanwhile, 5,000 kilometres away, three other great traditions were asking the same questions:

**Confucius** (551–479 BCE) — a near-contemporary of the Presocratics — taught that the good life requires **virtue** (*ren*, benevolence/humaneness), **proper relationships**, and the cultivation of **harmony** through ritual and education. His ethics is deeply social: you become a person through relationships, not in isolation from them.

**The Buddha** (c. 563–483 BCE) diagnosed the human condition as *dukkha* (suffering, unsatisfactoriness) caused by craving and clinging — including clinging to a fixed self. The path to liberation (*nirvana*) is through the **Eightfold Path**: right view, right intention, right speech, right action, right livelihood, right effort, right mindfulness, right concentration. The doctrine of **anatta** (no-self) dissolves the anxious ego the Stoics were trying to discipline.

**The Daoist** tradition (Laozi, *Tao Te Ching*, c. 6th century BCE) taught that the good life aligns with the **Tao** — the fundamental, unnameable principle of reality. The practice is **wu-wei**: effortless action, not forcing, going with the grain of things. Where Aristotle urges cultivated virtue and the Stoics urge rational self-control, the Daoist sage lets go of control altogether.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- Hellenistic philosophy arose after the Greek city-states fell: how does the **individual live well** in an uncontrollable world?
- **Epicureanism**: seek **ataraxia** (tranquility) through modest pleasures, friendship, and freedom from fear (of gods, death, pain).
- **Stoicism**: focus only on what is **up to you** (your judgments, effort, character); release the rest. Virtue is the only genuine good.
- **Pyrrhonian Skepticism**: suspend all judgment (*epoché*) → tranquility follows.
- **Confucius**: virtue (*ren*) and social harmony through proper relationships.
- **Buddhism**: suffering arises from craving; the Eightfold Path leads to liberation; the self is not a fixed thing (*anatta*).
- **Daoism**: align with the Tao; practise *wu-wei* (effortless action, non-forcing).
- Despite geographical separation, all these traditions converge on one insight: **inner life — character, attention, disposition — is more in your control than outer circumstances**, and that is where wisdom begins.
