<Step title="The waiter who is too much a waiter" kind="explain">

In *Being and Nothingness* (1943), Sartre offers an unforgettable sketch: a waiter in a Parisian café whose every gesture is slightly too precise, whose voice is a touch too attentive, whose whole manner is a performance of *Waiter* with a capital W. He is playing at being a waiter the way an actor plays a role.

Why? Sartre's diagnosis: the waiter is in **bad faith** (*mauvaise foi*). He is identifying completely with his social function, pretending that being a waiter is what he simply *is* — a fixed, given fact about his nature — rather than what he is *doing* by choice. He is using the role to escape the anxiety of freedom.

This is bad faith's essential move: **treating yourself as a thing** (a fixed object with a given nature) rather than as a consciousness (a free being who is always more than any role). You tell yourself "I can't help it — I'm just a waiter / a hothead / a loyal friend / a rule-follower." But you can always choose otherwise. You are lying to yourself.

</Step>

<Step title="Bad faith vs authenticity" kind="interactive">

Bad faith takes many forms. The common thread is self-deception about your own freedom — either pretending you are fully determined ("I had no choice") or pretending you are entirely free with no constraints at all ("I can do anything, so nothing matters"). Authenticity threads the needle: owning your freedom within your real situation.

<Step title="Heidegger: being-toward-death" kind="explain">

Martin Heidegger approaches authenticity from a different angle. In *Being and Time* (1927), he argues that everyday human life is dominated by **das Man** — "the They" — the anonymous social norm that tells us what one does, what one thinks, what one values. We flee into the crowd to avoid confronting our own existence.

What breaks through this comfortable inauthenticity? Heidegger's answer: **death**.

Death is the one event that cannot be delegated, shared, or avoided. I can have someone else drive me to work, cook my dinner, take my exam — but no one can die for me in my place. Death is my **ownmost possibility**, non-relational and unavoidable. Confronting this — what Heidegger calls **being-toward-death** — cuts through the noise of das Man and forces the question: *what do I, as this particular existing being, actually want to do with the finite time I have?*

This is not morbidity. It is the sharpening of life. The awareness of mortality makes each choice feel genuinely mine rather than a smooth continuation of what "one" simply does.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Bad faith** (*mauvaise foi*) — Sartre's term for self-deception about your own freedom: pretending you "had no choice," or that your role/personality/circumstances fully define you.
- **Authenticity** — owning your freedom and responsibility fully, within your actual situation. Not heroism — honesty.
- Sartre's waiter: the paradigm case of someone who treats himself as a fixed thing, fleeing the anxiety of freedom into a role.
- **Heidegger's being-toward-death** — confronting mortality individualises existence and breaks the grip of "the They," pointing toward authentic living.
- Both thinkers agree: authentic existence requires facing what is uncomfortable — your freedom (Sartre) or your finitude (Heidegger).

</Step>
