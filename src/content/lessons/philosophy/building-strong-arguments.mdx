<Step title="The principle of charity" kind="explain">

When you encounter an argument — especially one you disagree with — the first instinct is often to find its weakest version and knock it down. This is called a **straw man**, and it's both intellectually dishonest and unproductive.

The antidote is the **principle of charity**: always engage with the *strongest reasonable interpretation* of an argument, not the weakest. If an argument is ambiguous, assume the most defensible reading. If a premise seems implausible, ask whether there's a charitable version that is more defensible.

Why? Two reasons:

1. **You learn more.** Defeating a strong argument teaches you something. Defeating a weak one teaches you nothing.
2. **You earn the right to disagree.** If you can show that even the best version of an argument fails, your objection carries real weight.

Charitable reading is not the same as agreeing. It is the discipline of engaging with the real argument before judging it.

</Step>

<Step title="Reconstructing hidden premises" kind="explain">

Real-world arguments almost never spell out all their premises. People omit steps they consider obvious. The philosopher's job is to **make the implicit explicit** — to surface hidden premises (also called *suppressed* or *unstated* premises) so they can be examined.

Consider: *"We should ban sugary drinks because obesity is rising."* The visible structure is:

> P1: Obesity is rising.
> Conclusion: We should ban sugary drinks.

But as stated, P1 doesn't connect to the conclusion. There must be hidden premises, something like:

> P2 (hidden): Sugary drinks significantly contribute to obesity.
> P3 (hidden): Banning them would reduce obesity.
> P4 (hidden): Governments should act to reduce obesity.

Once surfaced, each hidden premise becomes a target for scrutiny. P3 might be disputed (do bans work?). P4 raises questions about state paternalism. Revealing the hidden premises transforms a slogan into an argument that can actually be evaluated.

<Callout type="info" title="Enthymemes">
An argument with a missing premise is called an **enthymeme** (from Greek for "in mind"). Most ordinary arguments are enthymemes — the speaker assumes the audience will supply the missing step. Philosophers make the assumed step explicit.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Testing validity with counterexamples" kind="explain">

How do you check whether a deductive argument is valid? The most powerful method is to try to **construct a counterexample**: a scenario in which all the premises are true yet the conclusion is false.

If you can describe such a scenario — even a hypothetical or fantastic one — the argument is **invalid**. If you cannot, the argument is (likely) valid.

Example:

> P1: All politicians are liars.
> P2: Maria is a liar.
> Conclusion: Maria is a politician.

Counterexample: imagine a world where Maria is a con artist — not a politician — who lies habitually. In that scenario P1 and P2 are both true, but the conclusion is false. The argument is **invalid** (this is the affirming-the-consequent fallacy dressed up as a syllogism).

The counterexample method works because validity is about all *possible* worlds, not just the actual one. One possible counterexample is enough to break it.

</Step>

When evaluating any argument, work through this checklist:

- **Step 1: Identify** — What are the explicit premises? What is the conclusion? Where are indicator words?
- **Step 2: Reconstruct** — What hidden premises does the argument rely on?
- **Step 3: Check validity** — Is the form valid? Try to construct a counterexample.
- **Step 4: Check truth** — Are the premises (including hidden ones) actually true?
- **Step 5: Assess soundness** — Valid + all premises true = sound. Only then does the conclusion follow with certainty.

<Step title="Good arguments in practice" kind="explain">

Building a good argument means attending to both the logical structure and the factual content. Here are the key habits of a strong philosophical reasoner:

**Be precise about your terms.** Many apparent disagreements dissolve when you define your terms carefully. "Freedom" means different things to different people; an argument about freedom that never defines it is arguing in fog.

**Separate validity from truth.** You can concede that an argument is valid while disputing a premise. Saying "your logic is fine, but premise 2 is false" is far more powerful than a vague objection.

**Look for the weakest link.** In a multi-premise argument, the conclusion is only as strong as the weakest premise. Identifying that link is where the real work happens.

**Distinguish absence of counter-evidence from positive evidence.** "No one has proved it false" does not mean it is true. This is the fallacy of *argument from ignorance* — a common pitfall in both everyday life and philosophy.

<Callout type="tip" title="The dialectical method">
Socrates taught by **elenchus** — systematically exposing the hidden assumptions in a position until the questioner sees a contradiction. Every time you ask "but what justifies *that* premise?", you are doing philosophy in the oldest tradition.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Principle of charity**: always engage with the strongest version of an argument.
- **Hidden premises (enthymemes)**: make implicit assumptions explicit before evaluating.
- **Counterexample method**: to test validity, try to construct a scenario where premises are true but the conclusion is false; one counter-example is enough to defeat validity.
- **Full checklist**: identify → reconstruct → check validity → check truth → assess soundness.
- **Weakest link**: a chain of premises is only as strong as its shakiest link.
- Good philosophical reasoning separates the question of *valid form* from the question of *true premises* — both matter, but they are distinct.

</Step>
