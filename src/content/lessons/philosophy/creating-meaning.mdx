<Step title="Meaning is made, not found" kind="explain">

The great existentialist insight is that meaning is not a treasure hidden somewhere in the universe, waiting to be discovered by those who look hard enough. It is something human beings *create* — through commitment, through love, through the projects they throw themselves into, through the stories they tell about their own lives.

Jean-Paul Sartre put this with characteristic bluntness: **existence precedes essence.** For a manufactured object — a knife, a chair — the essence (what it is for, what it is designed to do) exists before the object itself: the craftsman has a plan before cutting the wood. Sartre claimed that for human beings, it is the reverse. We exist first — we are thrown into the world without a predetermined purpose — and then we forge our essence through what we choose to do. There is no human nature that dictates what you must become. You are radically free, and that freedom is also a responsibility.

This is terrifying. It is also liberating.

Simone de Beauvoir added an important dimension: freedom is not just inner — it becomes real through *projects* that reach out into the world. The person who does nothing, commits to nothing, loves nothing, is not exercising their freedom; they are fleeing from it. Authentic freedom is enacted freedom: freedom that takes on projects, relationships, and responsibilities.

</Step>

<Step title="Susan Wolf — the formula for meaning" kind="explain">

Contemporary philosopher Susan Wolf offers one of the most precise and convincing accounts of personal meaning. She agrees with the existentialists that meaning must be partly subjective — a life cannot be meaningful if the person living it feels no engagement with it. But she disagrees that meaning is *purely* subjective — that any project, however trivial, becomes meaningful just because you choose it passionately.

Wolf's formula: **Meaning arises from active engagement in projects of worth.**

Two conditions are both necessary:

1. **Subjective attraction** — you must actually care about the project, be engaged by it, find yourself drawn to it. Joyless duty is not meaning.

2. **Objective worth** — the project must genuinely *be* worthwhile, not just feel worthwhile to you. Passionate dedication to something trivial or harmful does not generate meaning, however intensely it is felt.

Meaning lives where these two axes meet: **loving something genuinely worth loving.** This is why Wolf says the most meaningful activities are neither pure pleasure (high subjective, low objective) nor pure duty (high objective, low subjective) but the activities where deep personal engagement meets genuine worth.

</Step>

<Step title="Relationships and purpose — the core of a meaningful life" kind="explain">

Across very different philosophical traditions, two sources of meaning emerge as especially important.

**Relationships.** Deep, genuine connection with other people — friendship, love, family, community — is consistently ranked among the most meaning-generating of human goods. Viktor Frankl, who survived Auschwitz, wrote that love is "the only way to grasp another human being in the innermost core of his personality" — and that in the camps, those who survived often had a person, a love, a child, waiting for them. Aristotle called deep friendship "a single soul dwelling in two bodies" and placed it at the centre of the flourishing life. Susan Wolf's framework supports this: genuine relationships score high on both axes — deeply engaging AND genuinely worthwhile.

**Purpose — projects that reach beyond the self.** A life organised around projects that matter — teaching, creating, healing, building, discovering — provides the kind of sustained engagement and worth that generates meaning over time. These need not be grand or public. A teacher who genuinely changes a student's relationship with learning, a parent who gives a child the secure base from which to explore the world, a craftsperson who makes beautiful things with care — all of these are lives of purpose in Wolf's sense.

The existentialists were right that meaning is not given — it must be built. But they were also right that it *can* be built, and that building it is the most human thing we do.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Existentialism:** meaning is not found but created — through commitment, projects, and relationships. Existence precedes essence: you forge your own nature.
- **De Beauvoir:** freedom becomes real through projects that reach into the world. Authentic freedom is enacted, not merely inner.
- **Susan Wolf's formula:** meaning = active engagement + objective worth. Both conditions are necessary. Meaning lives where subjective attraction meets genuine worthwhileness.
- Pure pleasure (subjective without objective worth) and pure duty (objective without subjective engagement) are not sufficient for meaning — meaning requires both.
- **Relationships** consistently emerge as among the most meaning-rich goods — deeply engaging AND genuinely worthwhile.
- **Purpose** — projects that reach beyond the self — provides sustained meaning over a lifetime.

</Step>
