<Step title="Philosophy begins in wonder" kind="explain">

Both Plato and Aristotle said the same thing: **philosophy begins in wonder**. Not the polite "huh, neat" kind — the deeper jolt you feel when something ordinary suddenly looks strange.

You've felt it. Lying awake wondering why there's *anything* at all. Catching yourself thinking *"Is the red I see the same red you see?"* Realising you can't prove you're not dreaming right now. Children do this constantly — *"But why?"* — until they're taught to stop.

Wonder is philosophy's fuel. The philosopher is just someone who refuses to let the questions go.

</Step>

<Step title="The unexamined life" kind="explain">

At his trial, facing a death sentence, Socrates was offered a deal: stop philosophising and live. He refused, with one of history's most famous lines:

> "The unexamined life is not worth living."

It sounds extreme. But his point was serious. To drift through life never asking *why* you believe what you believe, *why* you want what you want, or *whether* your way of living is good — that, Socrates thought, is to sleepwalk. You'd be living on autopilot, running on values you absorbed without ever choosing.

The **examined life** is the opposite: living awake. You don't have to reach final answers. You just have to keep asking — to make your beliefs *yours*, held for reasons you can actually stand behind.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Philosophy begins in wonder** — the jolt when the ordinary suddenly looks strange.
- The **great questions** (free will, knowledge, mind, why anything exists) are ancient and still open.
- Socrates: **"the unexamined life is not worth living"** — drifting unreflectively is a kind of sleepwalking.
- The **examined life** means living awake: questioning your beliefs and values so they're truly yours.
- You don't need final answers — you need to keep asking.

</Step>
