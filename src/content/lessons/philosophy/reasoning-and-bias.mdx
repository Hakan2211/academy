<Step title="The thinker who tricks herself" kind="explain">

Knowing fallacies in the abstract is one thing. The uncomfortable truth is that **we are the ones most likely to fall for them** — not because we're unintelligent, but because the human mind didn't evolve for formal logic. It evolved for speed, social survival, and pattern recognition. These tendencies are generally useful, but they make us systematically vulnerable to certain errors.

Cognitive psychology has mapped dozens of these tendencies — usually called **cognitive biases**. Two are especially relevant to fallacy-spotting:

**Confirmation bias** — the tendency to seek out, interpret, and remember information in ways that confirm what we already believe, while discounting information that challenges it. We don't just *prefer* agreeable evidence; we actively work harder to find flaws in evidence we don't like. This means fallacies that support our existing views slide past our critical radar almost invisibly.

**Motivated reasoning** — reasoning backwards from a desired conclusion. Instead of following evidence to wherever it leads, motivated reasoning starts with "I want X to be true" and then constructs a case for X, cherry-picking evidence and treating every objection as an obstacle to be overcome rather than a data point to be weighed. This is why smart, knowledgeable people can make worse arguments about topics they care about than about topics they don't.

Together, these biases explain why knowing about fallacies doesn't automatically immunise you against them. The straw man you commit feels like a fair summary. The slippery slope you accept feels like a realistic chain of consequences. The ad hominem that confirms your view of someone feels like relevant context.

</Step>

<Step title="The Wason 2-4-6 task" kind="interactive">

Peter Wason's classic experiment from 1960 is the cleanest laboratory demonstration of confirmation bias ever designed. You're told the sequence **2, 4, 6** obeys a rule. Your job is to discover the rule by testing your own triples. Almost everyone generates triples that *already fit their hypothesis* — and never tries to break it.

<ConfirmationBias />

The insight isn't just academic. Every time you only read news sources you agree with, only notice evidence that supports your theory, and only remember the times your prediction was right — you're playing the same game as the people who never tested outside "even numbers, +2."

</Step>

<Step title="Anchoring and the first number you hear" kind="explain">

A third bias worth knowing is **anchoring**: the first piece of information you encounter about a quantity disproportionately influences your final estimate — even when that anchor is arbitrary. Ask someone whether Gandhi died before or after age 9, then ask his actual age at death, and they'll guess younger than someone first asked whether it was before or after age 140.

This matters for reasoning because many real arguments begin with an anchor — a price, a statistic, a number — that wasn't chosen neutrally. A negotiator who opens with an extreme offer, a pundit who quotes an outlier statistic, or an advertiser who shows a crossed-out "original" price are all deploying anchoring deliberately.

<Callout type="info" title="Bias ≠ stupidity">

Cognitive biases are not signs of low intelligence. In fact, research suggests they're sometimes *stronger* in high-IQ individuals who are better at generating post-hoc rationalizations. Intelligence is a tool for reasoning; motivation and self-awareness determine whether that tool is aimed carefully.

</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Confirmation bias**: we seek, interpret, and remember evidence that fits our existing beliefs, and discount evidence that doesn't.
- **Motivated reasoning**: starting from a desired conclusion and working backwards — intelligence amplifies the problem, not reduces it.
- **Anchoring**: the first number or frame we encounter biases every subsequent estimate, even if the anchor was arbitrary.
- These biases make fallacies feel invisible when they support our views — the straw man feels like fair characterisation; the slippery slope feels like realism.
- The antidote isn't suppressing emotion — it's deliberately seeking **disconfirming evidence** before concluding you're right.

</Step>
