<Step title="The demarcation problem" kind="explain">

Not everything that calls itself science is science. Astrology, homeopathy, creation science, and psychoanalysis have all been presented as scientific at various times. The **demarcation problem** is the philosophical question of what criterion, if any, separates genuine science from non-science.

This matters beyond mere label-disputes. Science enjoys enormous epistemic authority — we trust vaccines, satellites, and bridges because the science behind them is reliable. If that authority is to be warranted, we need to understand what earns it. And if we cannot demarcate science from non-science, we cannot distinguish warranted from unwarranted claims to scientific credibility.

The logical empiricists of the early twentieth century proposed **verificationism**: a claim is scientifically meaningful if and only if it is, at least in principle, verifiable by observation or experiment. But this immediately ran into problems — statements about the distant past, or about all electrons everywhere, cannot be directly verified.

</Step>

<Step title="Popper and falsifiability" kind="explain">

Karl Popper (1902–1994) proposed the most influential solution: a theory is **scientific** if and only if it is **falsifiable** — if there exists some observation or experiment that, if it occurred, would demonstrate the theory to be false.

Notice the asymmetry. You cannot verify "all ravens are black" by checking every raven in the universe — there are too many. But a single white raven *falsifies* it immediately. Science, on Popper's view, is the perpetual attempt to prove theories wrong. Theories that survive rigorous attempts at falsification gain credibility — not because they are proven, but because they have been tested and not broken.

The hallmark of *pseudo-science*, for Popper, is the opposite: its practitioners explain away every anomaly without ever allowing a result to falsify the theory. Freudian psychoanalysis and Adlerian psychology, he argued, could explain any human behaviour no matter what occurred — which meant they predicted nothing and risked nothing. A theory that is compatible with every possible outcome is compatible with no particular outcome.

<Callout type="info" title="Falsifiability vs falsification">
Popper's criterion is about *in-principle* falsifiability, not actual falsification. A theory can be scientific (falsifiable) and still be true. Newton's laws were falsifiable — and survived for centuries of testing before being refined by Einstein. The criterion sorts scientific from non-scientific *claims*, not true from false ones.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Test your intuitions" kind="interactive">

Popper's criterion is deceptively simple: can this claim, even in principle, be proven wrong by observation? Try sorting these claims — the tricky ones reveal why the criterion matters.

<Falsifiability />

The key pattern: claims with built-in escape clauses ("but only if you truly believe," "in ways indistinguishable from chance") are unfalsifiable because they can absorb any result. A claim that can accommodate every possible observation makes no prediction about which observations to expect — and so it cannot be science in Popper's sense.

</Step>

<Step title="Kuhn: science doesn't just grow, it revolutions" kind="explain">

Thomas Kuhn (1922–1996) offered a different but complementary picture in *The Structure of Scientific Revolutions* (1962). Kuhn argued that science does not advance by smooth accumulation of facts. Instead it operates under **paradigms** — shared frameworks of concepts, methods, standards, and assumptions that define what counts as a legitimate question and a good answer.

Under a paradigm, scientists do **normal science**: solving puzzles within the accepted framework, not challenging the framework itself. When anomalies accumulate — results that the paradigm cannot explain — the field eventually undergoes a **paradigm shift** (a scientific revolution): Ptolemy to Copernicus; phlogiston theory to oxygen chemistry; Newtonian mechanics to relativity and quantum theory.

The shift is not simply a matter of new evidence. The old paradigm's defenders can always add epicycles, qualifications, and auxiliary hypotheses. Revolution happens when the weight of anomalies and the attractions of the new paradigm create a gestalt switch — a moment where the new framework simply makes more sense. Kuhn controversially suggested that paradigms are not fully rationally comparable ("incommensurable"), though he later softened this claim.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- The **demarcation problem**: what separates science from non-science? Not merely a label dispute — scientific authority is at stake.
- **Verificationism** (logical empiricists): a claim is meaningful if verifiable. Problem: many scientific claims cannot be directly verified.
- **Popper's falsifiability criterion**: a theory is scientific if there is a possible observation that would show it to be false. Pseudo-science explains away anomalies without ever risking the theory.
- **Induction**: science relies on generalising from observations to universal laws — but no finite number of observations logically guarantees the next case. This is the **problem of induction** (Hume).
- **Kuhn's paradigms**: normal science operates within a paradigm; revolutions occur when anomalies overwhelm it and a new paradigm takes hold. Science is not merely cumulative — it is periodically discontinuous.

</Step>
