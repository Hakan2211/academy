<Step title="Beyond the big three" kind="explain">

Consequentialism, deontology, and virtue ethics are the dominant theories — but they are not the only ones. Several other frameworks capture important moral insights that the big three can underemphasise. Three of the most significant: **care ethics**, **contractarianism**, and **divine command theory**.

Each starts from a different question about the source and nature of moral obligations. Knowing these alternatives sharpens your ability to apply any framework thoughtfully.

</Step>

<Step title="Care ethics" kind="explain">

Developed by Carol Gilligan (b. 1936) and Nel Noddings (b. 1929), **care ethics** emerged partly as a critique of traditional ethics, which it saw as overly focused on abstract, universal principles — the domain of what Gilligan called a "justice orientation."

Care ethics argues that morality is fundamentally grounded in **relationships and caring**. We are not isolated rational atoms choosing principles behind a veil; we are embedded in networks of particular relationships — family, friendship, community — and those relationships generate genuine moral obligations.

What matters morally is **attending to the needs of others** in your particular relationships, responding with empathy, and maintaining connections. The virtues of care — attentiveness, responsiveness, empathy, responsibility — are central, not peripheral.

Care ethics does not reject justice; it insists that an ethics of care complements and contextualises it. A doctor caring for a patient should not think only of abstract impartial duties — the particular relationship generates particular obligations of attentiveness.

**Objection:** Critics ask whether grounding morality in particular relationships risks excluding those outside them — strangers, distant others, or future generations. Care ethics has responded by developing more expansive accounts of who deserves care.

</Step>

<Step title="Contractarianism" kind="explain">

**Contractarianism** grounds morality in what rational agents would agree to from a position of self-interest or fairness. There are two main versions:

**Hobbes (1588–1679):** In the state of nature — without society or government — life is "solitary, poor, nasty, brutish, and short." Rational self-interest leads people to agree to a social contract: we give up some freedom in exchange for order and security. Morality consists of the rules of this contract. Its authority comes from its mutual benefit to self-interested agents.

**Rawls (1921–2002):** A more egalitarian version. Rawls asks: what principles of justice would rational people choose from behind a "veil of ignorance" — not knowing their place in society, their talents, wealth, gender, or religion? He argues they would choose two principles: (1) equal basic liberties for all; (2) inequalities are permitted only if they benefit the least advantaged. This is a preview — we will explore Rawls at length in the world on justice.

Contractarianism gives morality an intersubjective foundation — it is about what we owe each other as parties to a shared social arrangement.

**Objection:** Both versions struggle with beings outside the contract: children, animals, future generations, and severely disabled people cannot participate as rational contractors. If moral status requires contracting, these beings seem to fall outside its scope.

</Step>

<Step title="Divine command theory" kind="explain">

**Divine command theory** holds that morality is grounded in God's commands. An action is right because God commands it; wrong because God forbids it. This grounds ethics in a personal relationship with a divine lawgiver and provides absolute moral authority.

Plato identified the central problem in the *Euthyphro* dialogue, 2,400 years ago: **"Is the pious loved by the gods because it is pious, or is it pious because it is loved?"** Translated into monotheism: is something right because God commands it, or does God command it because it is right?

If the former: morality is arbitrary — God could have made torturing children obligatory and it would be right. The content of morality depends entirely on God's will, making it seem contingent and potentially terrifying.

If the latter: morality is independent of God's will — God recognises pre-existing moral truths. Divine command theory collapses: God's commands track morality but don't constitute it. This is the **Euthyphro dilemma**.

Religious moral philosophers have responded in various ways — appealing to God's nature rather than bare commands — but the dilemma remains an important challenge.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Care ethics** (Gilligan, Noddings): morality is rooted in relationships and caring — particular obligations of attentiveness and empathy, not just abstract principles.
- **Contractarianism** (Hobbes, Rawls): morality consists of rules rational agents would agree to; Hobbes emphasises mutual advantage, Rawls emphasises fairness under a veil of ignorance.
- **Divine command theory**: right = commanded by God; challenged by the Euthyphro dilemma, which makes morality either arbitrary or independent of divine will.
- Each tradition captures something real: the importance of relationships, the role of social agreement, and the possibility of grounding ethics in something beyond human preference.

</Step>
