<Step title="Right by results" kind="explain">

What makes an action morally right? One compelling answer: **whatever produces the best consequences**. If the world will go better as a result of your action than of any alternative, then that action is the one you ought to perform.

This family of views is called **consequentialism**. Its most influential form is **utilitarianism**, developed by Jeremy Bentham (1748–1832) and refined by John Stuart Mill (1806–1873). The core principle: an action is right if and only if it produces the greatest happiness for the greatest number.

Bentham proposed a **hedonic calculus** — literally an arithmetic of pleasure and pain. Add up all the pleasure an action produces (across everyone affected), subtract all the pain, and compare that net sum against the alternatives. The action with the highest net utility wins.

This is a radical democratic idea: every person counts equally in the calculation. A king's pleasure counts for no more than a beggar's. Pain is pain whoever feels it.

</Step>

<Step title="Act vs. rule utilitarianism" kind="explain">

Utilitarianism comes in two important variants:

**Act utilitarianism** says: in each situation, do whichever specific act produces the most utility. Consult the calculus every time.

**Rule utilitarianism** says: follow the set of rules that, if generally followed, would produce the most utility. For example, a rule like "always keep your promises" generates more utility when universally followed than case-by-case promise-breaking would — even if in some individual case breaking a promise might yield slightly more good.

Mill leaned toward rule utilitarianism. He also introduced a famous refinement: some pleasures are **higher** than others. Intellectual and moral pleasures are better in kind than merely physical ones — not just more intense. It is "better to be Socrates dissatisfied than a fool satisfied."

</Step>

<Step title="The classic objections" kind="explain">

Consequentialism captures something important — outcomes matter — but it faces serious challenges:

**The rights objection.** If torturing one innocent person would prevent great suffering for a thousand, the calculus says do it. Many find this monstrous: some things are simply wrong regardless of consequences. Consequentialism seems to allow any act, however horrifying, if the numbers add up.

**The justice objection.** Utility might be maximised by systems that are deeply unfair — enslaving a minority for the comfort of the majority, for instance. The distribution of happiness seems to matter morally, not just the total.

**The utility monster.** Philosopher Robert Nozick imagined a being that gets vastly more utility from resources than anyone else. Classical utilitarianism implies we should give everything to this monster, depriving everyone else — a reductio ad absurdum.

**The demandingness objection.** If you should always maximise utility, you are always obligated to give until giving more would cost you more than it gains others. This seems to demand sainthood from everyone.

**The integrity objection.** Bernard Williams argued that utilitarianism requires you to alienate yourself from your own projects and commitments whenever a better utility calculation comes along — destroying personal integrity.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Consequentialism**: an action is right if it produces the best consequences.
- **Utilitarianism**: maximise the greatest happiness for the greatest number (Bentham, Mill).
- **Hedonic calculus**: add up all pleasure and pain across all persons — the action with highest net utility is right.
- **Act vs. rule utilitarianism**: evaluate each act on its own vs. follow the rules that would yield the most utility if universally followed.
- **Key objections**: rights violations, injustice, the utility monster, demandingness, loss of personal integrity.

</Step>
