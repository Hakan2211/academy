<Step title="A 3-page earthquake" kind="explain">

For roughly 2,400 years — from Plato to the mid-twentieth century — most philosophers accepted some version of the Justified True Belief analysis of knowledge. It seemed obviously right. What more could knowledge possibly require beyond a belief that's true and well-supported?

In 1963, Edmund Gettier published a paper just three pages long that demolished the whole edifice. It became one of the most cited philosophy papers ever written — and it raised a problem that has not been fully solved to this day.

Gettier's insight was elegant: **you can have a justified true belief that is true only by luck**, and in such a case we would *not* say you have knowledge. JTB is therefore not *sufficient* for knowledge, even if it may be necessary.

</Step>

The case shows that JTB can be satisfied in a way that involves an accident or coincidence. When truth and justification are only accidentally connected to each other — when the belief is true "for the wrong reasons" — it feels wrong to call it knowledge.

</Step>

<Step title="Why this matters" kind="explain">

Gettier cases share a common structure:

1. You form a justified belief on the basis of some evidence.
2. By coincidence, something unrelated makes the belief happen to be true.
3. The justification and the truth are therefore disconnected — the belief is true, but not because of your reasons.

Philosophers have proposed many "fourth conditions" to fix the JTB analysis:

- **No false lemmas** — your reasoning shouldn't pass through any false intermediate steps. (The clock case violates this: you reason via the false belief that the clock is running.)
- **Causal connection** — your belief should be caused by the very fact that makes it true.
- **Reliabilism** — knowledge requires that your belief-forming process be reliably accurate.
- **Safety** — your belief couldn't easily have been false in nearby possible situations.

None of these fixes is universally accepted. The Gettier problem remains open — a vivid reminder that our most intuitive concepts can be harder to define than they look.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- Edmund **Gettier (1963)** showed with a 3-page paper that JTB is not *sufficient* for knowledge.
- A **Gettier case**: a belief that is true and justified, but true only by **luck** — the justification and the truth are accidentally connected.
- The **stopped clock**: Henry reads the correct time from a clock that stopped 12 hours ago — JTB, but not knowledge.
- Philosophers have proposed many **fourth conditions** (no false lemmas, causal connection, reliabilism, safety) — none is universally accepted.
- The search for a complete analysis of knowledge continues.

</Step>
