<Lesson>

<Step title="Two ways to reason" kind="explain">

Not all arguments offer the same kind of support. Philosophy distinguishes two fundamentally different relationships a conclusion can have with its premises.

**Deductive arguments** claim that if the premises are true, the conclusion *must* be true — with absolute certainty. There is no possible world in which all the premises hold yet the conclusion fails. Mathematics and formal logic run on deductive reasoning.

**Inductive arguments** claim only that if the premises are true, the conclusion is *probably* true — or *well-supported*. The premises don't guarantee the conclusion; they raise its probability. Nearly all empirical science, everyday decision-making, and historical reasoning is inductive.

The key distinction is not which kind of argument is "better" — both are indispensable. It is about the *type of support* on offer.

</Step>

<Step title="Certainty vs strength" kind="explain">

Because they make different promises, deductive and inductive arguments are evaluated by different standards.

A deductive argument is either **valid** (the conclusion absolutely follows) or **invalid** — there's no middle ground. Even one tiny logical flaw breaks the guarantee.

An inductive argument is evaluated by its **strength**: how probable does it make the conclusion? A strong inductive argument might give you 95% confidence; a weak one only 55%. Evidence, sample size, and relevance all affect strength.

<Callout type="info" title="The asymmetry of induction">
No matter how many times you observe that the sun rises — a billion mornings in a row — you haven't *deduced* that it will rise tomorrow. David Hume called this the **problem of induction**: past regularity, however extensive, never logically guarantees future regularity. Yet inductive reasoning is still our most powerful tool for learning about the world.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Deductive** arguments: if premises are true, the conclusion *must* be true — certainty.
- **Inductive** arguments: premises make the conclusion *probable* — strength varies.
- Deductive arguments are either valid or invalid; there is no partial credit.
- Inductive arguments are strong or weak, rated by how much they raise the probability of the conclusion.
- Most real-world reasoning — science, medicine, history — is inductive.
- Hume's **problem of induction**: past observations never logically guarantee future events.

</Step>
