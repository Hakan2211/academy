<Step title="Beyond the physical world" kind="explain">

Physics studies how things move and interact. Chemistry studies what they are made of. But there is a deeper layer of questions that no laboratory can settle by experiment: **What is it for anything to exist at all? What is real, and what is mere appearance?** These are the questions of **metaphysics** — the branch of philosophy that asks about the fundamental structure of reality itself.

The word comes from Aristotle's editor, who placed his treatise on "first philosophy" *after* (meta) the Physics. The name stuck — though the questions are anything but optional extras. They underlie every other inquiry. Before you can ask *how* things work, you have to have some view — even an implicit one — about *what kinds of things* exist.

</Step>

<Step title="Appearance and reality" kind="explain">

The central tension in metaphysics is between **appearance** — how things seem to us — and **reality** — how they actually are. You see the table as solid and at rest, but physics tells you it is mostly empty space with vibrating particles. You see the sun move across the sky, but astronomy tells you the Earth is moving around the sun.

Plato pushed this tension to its extreme. He argued that the world we perceive with our senses is not truly real — it is a shifting, imperfect shadow of a higher realm of **Forms**: eternal, perfect, unchanging archetypes of which ordinary things are merely copies. A beautiful painting is beautiful because it *participates* in the Form of Beauty; it is not beautiful in itself.

His most vivid way of expressing this is the **Allegory of the Cave** — one of the most influential thought experiments in the history of ideas.

</Step>

<Step title="The cave: walk through it" kind="interactive">

Step through Plato's allegory and see what each stage represents about the journey from opinion to knowledge.

<CaveAllegory />

The allegory maps a complete **epistemological journey**: from doxa (mere opinion, the shadows) through pistis (confidence about sensory things) to dianoia (mathematical reasoning) and finally noesis (direct apprehension of the Forms, symbolised by the sun). The sun — the Form of the Good — is both what makes everything else visible and the source of their very existence.

</Step>

<Step title="What metaphysics actually asks" kind="explain">

Metaphysics today is divided into several core questions:

- **Ontology** — what kinds of things exist? (Numbers? Minds? Properties? God? Possible worlds?)
- **Identity and persistence** — what makes something the same thing over time?
- **Causation** — what is it for one event to *cause* another?
- **Free will** — do we originate our actions, or are they fixed by prior causes?
- **Time** — does the past still exist? Does the future yet exist?

Each of the next lessons in this world takes one of these questions and opens it up. But they all share Plato's root impulse: **don't accept the shadows on the wall as the final story.**

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Metaphysics** asks about the fundamental structure of reality: what exists, what is real, how things persist through time.
- The key tension: **appearance** (how things seem) vs **reality** (how things are).
- Plato's **Allegory of the Cave** maps a journey from shadows (opinion) to the sun (the Form of the Good — ultimate truth).
- Core metaphysical questions: ontology, identity, causation, free will, time.
- Every inquiry presupposes some metaphysics — it is better to think about it explicitly.

</Step>
