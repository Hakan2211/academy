<Step title="The mind as a blank slate" kind="explain">

While the Rationalists were building grand systems from pure reason, a rival tradition was taking shape across the English Channel. The British Empiricists — Locke, Berkeley, and Hume — agreed on one fundamental point: **all knowledge begins with experience**. The mind, far from containing innate ideas, is a *tabula rasa* — a blank slate — before experience writes on it.

**John Locke** (1632–1704) launched the tradition in his *Essay Concerning Human Understanding*. Where do ideas come from? From two and only two sources: **sensation** (the five senses reporting on the external world) and **reflection** (the mind noticing its own operations — thinking, doubting, willing). There is nothing in the intellect that was not first in the senses. No innate ideas; no a priori content.

**George Berkeley** (1685–1753) took Locke's empiricism and pushed it to a startling conclusion: if we only ever know our own perceptions, what justification do we have for positing a "material world" behind them? His answer: none. For Berkeley, *esse est percipi* — **to be is to be perceived**. What we call "physical objects" are simply stable collections of ideas in minds. The world doesn't disappear when you leave the room only because God perceives it continuously.

**David Hume** (1711–1776) was the most radical. He accepted the empiricist premise and followed it relentlessly to its conclusions — undermining not just the rationalist programme but much of what we ordinarily take for granted about causation, the self, and induction.

</Step>

<Step title="A priori or a posteriori? Sort the claims." kind="interactive">

The great rationalist/empiricist debate turns on the question: which truths require experience, and which don't? Sort these claims and see where the two camps diverge.

<RationalismEmpiricism />

Hume's famous "fork" divided all meaningful claims into exactly these two categories — *relations of ideas* (a priori, necessary) and *matters of fact* (a posteriori, contingent). Anything that doesn't fit into either, he argued, should be thrown into the fire.

</Step>

<Step title="Hume's most dangerous arguments" kind="explain">

Hume's radicalism went far beyond sorting knowledge into two types. He made three arguments that shook philosophy to its foundations.

**The problem of induction.** We assume the future will resemble the past — that the sun will rise tomorrow because it always has. But this inference is itself inductive. You can't justify induction without using induction. The argument is circular. Hume's conclusion: our confident predictions about the future rest on *custom and habit*, not on reason.

**We never observe causation.** When one billiard ball strikes another and the second moves, we see two events in sequence — contact, then motion. We never see *causation* itself: the necessary connection, the "must." Hume argued that our idea of necessary connection is just a feeling of expectation built up by repeated experience. Causation, as it appears in philosophy and science, is a projection of the mind, not a feature of the world directly observed.

**The self is a bundle.** Look inward, Hume said. Do you find a persisting "self" — a unified, unchanging subject of experience? No. You find a stream of individual perceptions — this thought, that sensation, this memory — but no observer standing behind them. The self is just a "bundle" of perceptions with no unified owner.

Together these arguments constitute *Hume's fork* and its aftermath: reason and experience together leave us with far less certain knowledge than we imagined.

</Step>

<Step title="Will the sun rise tomorrow?" kind="interactive">

Hume's problem of induction is easy to feel for yourself. Pile up observations and watch your confidence soar — then notice the gap Hume points to between *"it always has"* and *"it must."*

<ProblemOfInduction />

No quantity of past sunrises *logically* guarantees the next one. Induction works — we couldn't live without it — but Hume showed it rests on habit and custom, not on reason that could prove itself.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Empiricism**: all knowledge originates in experience; the mind is a blank slate (*tabula rasa*).
- **Locke**: ideas come from sensation and reflection; no innate ideas.
- **Berkeley**: *esse est percipi* — to be is to be perceived; there is no mind-independent matter.
- **Hume**: the most radical — his "fork" divides all meaning into relations of ideas and matters of fact; everything else is meaningless metaphysics.
- **Hume's problem of induction**: inductive inference cannot be rationally justified without circularity.
- **Causation**: we observe constant conjunction, not necessary connection — causation is a habit of mind, not a directly observed feature of reality.

</Step>
