<Step title="Who counts, morally?" kind="explain">

The expanding circle of moral concern — which we explored in an earlier world — has been expanding throughout human history: from kin, to tribe, to all of humanity. But two of its most contested current frontiers are **animals** and **the environment**, including future generations.

The question is not merely sentimental. It is philosophical: **what is it that makes something worthy of moral consideration?** If we can answer that, we can determine who — or what — belongs inside the circle.

The most influential modern answer was given by **Peter Singer**, drawing on Jeremy Bentham: the criterion is not species membership, rationality, or language. It is the capacity to **suffer**. "The question is not, Can they reason? nor, Can they talk? but, Can they suffer?" Singer argued that excluding animals from moral consideration on the basis of species alone is **speciesism** — a bias as arbitrary as racism or sexism. Just as the colour of a person's skin is not morally relevant to how much their suffering matters, the species of a creature is not morally relevant to how much its suffering matters.

</Step>

<Step title="Animal rights and welfare" kind="explain">

Singer's argument leads to **animal welfarism**: because animals can suffer, their suffering must be weighed in our moral calculations. This does not require treating all creatures identically — a shrimp and a chimpanzee are different in morally relevant ways — but it does require that we not dismiss animal suffering as morally zero.

A stronger position is **animal rights**, associated with philosopher Tom Regan: animals are not merely sentient (capable of suffering), but are **subjects of a life** — they have beliefs, desires, memories, and a welfare that matters to them. As subjects of a life, they are rights-bearers, not merely welfare-objects. On this view, using animals as mere resources — regardless of how painlessly — is a violation of their rights.

The practical implications differ: welfare theory permits using animals if suffering is minimised and the gains are large enough; rights theory prohibits use even in painless conditions.

**Objections**: Critics argue that extending moral rights to animals requires that they also bear moral duties — but animals cannot. The asymmetry (rights without responsibilities) is philosophically awkward. Others argue species membership is not arbitrary: our obligations run differently across species because of our evolved social structures, not mere prejudice. Carl Cohen argued that rights belong to beings capable of moral reasoning — and animals are not.

</Step>

<Step title="Environmental ethics and future generations" kind="explain">

**Environmental ethics** asks whether nature — ecosystems, species, rivers, forests — has moral value beyond its value to humans. Two broad positions:

**Anthropocentrism**: the environment matters morally only insofar as it matters to humans (and sentient animals). Destroying a rainforest is wrong because it harms human welfare, disrupts ecosystems, and causes suffering to sentient creatures — not because the trees have intrinsic worth.

**Ecocentrism** (Aldo Leopold, Arne Naess): living systems and ecosystems have intrinsic value independent of their usefulness to humans. Leopold's **Land Ethic**: "A thing is right when it tends to preserve the integrity, stability, and beauty of the biotic community. It is wrong when it tends otherwise."

**Future generations** pose a different puzzle: we can harm people who do not yet exist and cannot consent or protest. Climate change, soil degradation, and resource depletion impose costs on those not yet born. Derek Parfit's work in *Reasons and Persons* opened this field: if we have obligations to people who exist, and those obligations stem from the fact that they have interests, then future people — who will exist — have interests that we can currently damage. The "non-identity problem" complicates this: in many cases, the people who will exist in a damaged future are not the same people who would have existed in a better future; so we cannot say we "harmed" a specific person.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Singer's speciesism argument**: the capacity to suffer, not species membership, determines moral status. Excluding animals on species grounds is arbitrary.
- **Welfarism vs rights**: welfare theory requires minimising animal suffering in our calculations; rights theory (Regan) forbids using animals as mere resources regardless of how they are treated.
- **Environmental ethics**: anthropocentrism holds that nature matters because of its value to humans; ecocentrism holds that ecosystems have intrinsic value.
- **Leopold's Land Ethic**: right and wrong are defined by what preserves the integrity and stability of the biotic community.
- **Future generations**: we can harm people who do not yet exist; Parfit's non-identity problem complicates but does not eliminate the obligation.
- The expanding circle of moral concern is a description of moral history — and a prescription for its next frontier.

</Step>
