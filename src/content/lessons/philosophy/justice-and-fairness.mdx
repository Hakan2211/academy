<Step title="What is a just society?" kind="explain">

Political philosophy's central question is deceptively simple: **what makes a society just?** Two towering twentieth-century answers stand in productive opposition: John Rawls' theory of justice as fairness, and Robert Nozick's libertarian reply.

John Rawls believed the right way to discover the principles of a just society is to imagine choosing them from a position of perfect impartiality. His device for achieving this is the **veil of ignorance**.

Rawls asks you to enter an **"original position"** — a hypothetical moment before society is set up — behind a veil that conceals everything about you: your intelligence, gender, race, wealth, natural talents, family, and even your conception of what makes life good. You must choose the basic rules of society without knowing which position you will occupy.

Rawls argues that rational people in this situation would choose two principles:

1. **Equal basic liberties**: every person has the same fundamental rights and freedoms (free speech, conscience, association, the vote).
2. **The difference principle**: social and economic inequalities are permissible *only* if they benefit the least-advantaged members of society.

The difference principle is the crucial and controversial one. It does not demand equality of outcome — some inequality may be perfectly just, if (and only if) it makes the worst-off better off than they would be under strict equality. A surgeon earning more than a cleaner is just, if the incentive structures that produce it result in better healthcare for the poorest.

<Step title="Nozick's libertarian reply" kind="explain">

Robert Nozick, in *Anarchy, State, and Utopia* (1974), mounted a powerful challenge. He agreed that a just social arrangement is necessary, but disagreed entirely about what makes it just.

For Nozick, justice is not about **patterns** (distributions of wealth) but about **processes** (how holdings are acquired). His **entitlement theory** holds that a distribution is just if:

1. Everyone's holdings were **justly acquired** (through labour, gift, or voluntary trade — not theft or fraud).
2. Any **transfers** were voluntary.

If both conditions hold, the resulting distribution — however unequal — is just. Nozick famously used the example of basketball player Wilt Chamberlain: if a million fans each voluntarily give 25 cents to watch him play, he ends up with $250,000 while they have less. No one was coerced. How can the resulting inequality be unjust?

Nozick's deeper objection to Rawls is that maintaining a *pattern* (like the difference principle) requires **ongoing interference** with free choices. Taxing people to redistribute wealth, he argued, is morally equivalent to forcing them to work for others — a violation of self-ownership.

The Rawls–Nozick debate is not merely academic. It underlies real disagreements about taxation, welfare states, inheritance, and the proper limits of government.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Rawls** uses the **veil of ignorance** — choosing society's rules without knowing your own position — to derive two principles: equal basic liberties, and the difference principle.
- The **difference principle**: inequalities are just only if they benefit the least-advantaged.
- **Nozick** argues that justice is about *process*, not *patterns*: a distribution is just if it arose through voluntary, non-coercive transactions.
- Nozick objects that maintaining patterns requires ongoing interference with free choice — a violation of self-ownership.
- The debate defines a real fault line: is justice about *what you end up with*, or *how you came to have it?*
