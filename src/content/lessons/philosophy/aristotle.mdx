<Step title="The student who disagreed" kind="explain">

Aristotle (384–322 BCE) joined Plato's Academy at age 17 and stayed for twenty years — until Plato died. He was Plato's most brilliant student. He was also his most important critic.

Where Plato believed that true reality is a separate, non-physical realm of Forms accessible only to pure reason, Aristotle turned his gaze firmly back toward the physical world. He collected specimens of animals, catalogued plants, classified constitutions of 158 Greek city-states, wrote on logic, physics, metaphysics, ethics, politics, rhetoric, poetry, and biology.

His guiding intuition: **knowledge comes from observation**. The Forms are not "up there" in a separate realm — they are embodied *in* the things themselves. You understand a horse not by contemplating a Platonic Form of Horse but by dissecting actual horses and grasping the principles that make them what they are.

<Callout type="info" title="Plato vs Aristotle at a glance">
Plato: reality = the eternal Forms; the sensory world is a shadow. Aristotle: reality = the natural world; understanding comes through careful observation of it. Both insights are partially right — and this tension between rationalism and empiricism runs through all of philosophy after them.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="The four causes" kind="interactive">

To fully explain *why* something is the way it is, Aristotle says you must answer four different questions — the four causes. "Cause" here means "explanatory factor," not just efficient physical cause. For anything in the world, ask:

<FourCauses />

The **final cause** is Aristotle's most original contribution. Everything in nature, he argued, has a purpose (*telos*) — even if it was not consciously designed. The eye is for seeing. The heart is for pumping blood. This is **teleology**: explanation by purpose, not just mechanism.

</Step>

<Step title="Teleology and virtue as the golden mean" kind="explain">

Teleology reaches its height in Aristotle's ethics. **What is the telos of a human being?** Not mere survival, not pleasure, not wealth — these are means to an end. The highest human end, Aristotle argues, is **eudaimonia**: often translated as "happiness" but better rendered as **flourishing** — a life fully realised, lived in accordance with human nature.

Humans are rational social animals. Our telos is exercised in the community, through virtuous activity over a complete life.

**Virtue** (*arete*) is not a feeling but a **stable disposition** to act and feel appropriately. And for every virtue, Aristotle identifies two vices — one of deficiency, one of excess. Courage is the mean between cowardice and recklessness. Generosity is the mean between stinginess and extravagance.

<GoldenMean />

Virtues are not rules to follow but **habits to cultivate**. We become courageous by practising courageous acts, just as we become skilled at the lyre by playing it. Character is formed by repeated action, not by knowing the right theory.

</Step>

<Step title="The father of formal logic" kind="explain">

Aristotle also invented formal logic — a complete system for evaluating the validity of arguments. His key tool was the **syllogism**: an argument with two premises and a conclusion.

<Formula tex="\text{All humans are mortal.}\quad \text{Socrates is human.}\quad \therefore\ \text{Socrates is mortal.}" block />

This isn't just about Socrates. Aristotle showed that the form of the argument — All A are B; x is A; therefore x is B — is valid regardless of its content. He catalogued valid and invalid forms, laying the foundation for all logic until the 19th century.

His logic, collected in the *Organon*, was so influential that Kant wrote (in 1781!) that logic had made no essential advances since Aristotle. (He was almost right — Frege's symbolic logic only arrived in 1879.)

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- Aristotle rejected Plato's separate realm of Forms: **Forms are in things, not above them**. Knowledge comes from observation.
- The **four causes**: material (what it's made of), formal (its structure/design), efficient (what produced it), final (its purpose/telos).
- **Teleology**: everything has a telos; the telos of humans is **eudaimonia** (flourishing).
- **Virtue ethics**: virtue is a stable disposition to act at the **golden mean** between excess and deficiency — cultivated by habit, not rule-following.
- He invented **formal logic** (the syllogism) and systematised it in the *Organon*.
- He founded the **Lyceum** and wrote foundational texts in logic, biology, physics, ethics, politics, and more.

</Step>
