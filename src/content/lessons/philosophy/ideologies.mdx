<Step title="What is a political ideology?" kind="explain">

A **political ideology** is a coherent system of ideas about how society should be organised — who should hold power, how wealth should be distributed, what role the state should play, and what kind of freedom and equality matter most.

Ideologies are not random bundles of opinions. Each one has an internal logic: a view of human nature, a diagnosis of what is wrong with society, and a prescription for how to fix it. Understanding that logic is the key to understanding why intelligent people who share many values can end up with radically different political conclusions.

Five ideologies have dominated modern Western political thought. They are not monoliths — each has many variants and internal disputes — but their broad outlines are recognisable and worth knowing in depth.

A note on fairness: political philosophy's job is to understand each ideology from the inside, presenting its *strongest* case. The goal here is not to tell you which ideology is correct, but to give you the tools to engage with each honestly.

</Step>

<Step title="Liberalism — the ideology of individual rights" kind="explain">

**Liberalism** is perhaps the most influential political philosophy of the last three centuries. At its core is the belief that **individuals are the basic unit of moral concern**, each with inherent dignity and rights that no state may override.

Liberalism began as a challenge to feudal hierarchy, absolute monarchy, and religious authority. John Locke argued that government derives its legitimacy from the consent of the governed and exists to protect natural rights. Later liberals like Mill extended this to freedom of thought and expression. Rawls reformulated liberalism for the twentieth century: a just liberal state must be *neutral* among competing visions of the good life, letting individuals pursue their own ends within a framework of equal basic rights.

Modern liberalism comes in two main strands:

- **Classical liberalism** (sometimes called libertarianism lite): emphasises free markets, limited government, and formal equality. The state should protect rights and enforce contracts but not redistribute wealth or regulate the economy beyond preventing force and fraud.
- **Social (or progressive) liberalism**: accepts that formal freedom is hollow without real opportunity. Accepts regulation, welfare states, and redistribution as consistent with — indeed required by — a genuine commitment to individual flourishing.

Both strands agree that the state must not impose a particular religious, moral, or cultural vision on its citizens. They disagree about how much equality of condition is needed for freedom to be real.

</Step>

<Step title="Conservatism — the wisdom of tradition" kind="explain">

**Conservatism** does not begin with a utopian blueprint. Edmund Burke, its founding father, argued against the French Revolution not because he denied the need for reform, but because he believed that abstract reason alone is a dangerously inadequate guide to political life. Inherited institutions — the common law, the church, the family, established customs — encode the accumulated wisdom of generations. Destroying them wholesale and rebuilding from first principles is an act of hubris that produces chaos.

The core conservative intuitions:

- **Tradition matters**: institutions that have survived carry reasons for their survival, even if those reasons are not fully articulable.
- **Human nature is imperfect**: people are not infinitely malleable or perfectible. Attempts to engineer a perfect society typically ignore human limitations.
- **Order is fragile**: the conditions for civil society — trust, rule of law, shared norms — are hard to build and easy to destroy.
- **Change should be gradual**: reform is sometimes necessary, but it should proceed carefully, learning from what works, not imposing untested theories.

Conservatism is not a defence of any particular status quo — Burke himself supported American independence. It is more a disposition toward proven arrangements over novel experiments. This makes it a moving target: what conservatives seek to conserve changes over time.

</Step>

<Step title="Socialism — equality and collective power" kind="explain">

**Socialism** emerges from the diagnosis that capitalism — private ownership of the means of production and market distribution — systematically generates inequality, exploitation, and alienation. The solution, in various forms, is greater collective or public control over the economy.

Karl Marx offered the most influential socialist analysis: capitalists extract "surplus value" from workers who have no choice but to sell their labour. This class conflict is the engine of history; socialism is the next stage beyond capitalism, in which the working class takes control.

But socialism is a broad tent:

- **Revolutionary socialism / Marxism-Leninism**: capitalism must be overthrown; the state must seize the commanding heights of the economy. In practice, this produced the Soviet Union and similar regimes — outcomes that most socialists today disown.
- **Democratic socialism**: pursue socialist ends through democratic means — elect governments that will nationalise key industries, guarantee strong workers' rights, and expand public services.
- **Social democracy**: accept a market economy but use regulation, strong unions, progressive taxation, and a generous welfare state to correct its worst outcomes. Scandinavia is often cited as the closest real-world approximation.

The core socialist values are **solidarity** (we rise or fall together), **equality** (deep economic inequality is a moral problem, not just an efficiency problem), and **collective power** to correct markets that generate those outcomes.

</Step>

<Step title="Libertarianism and Anarchism — liberty against the state" kind="explain">

**Libertarianism** and **anarchism** both prioritise liberty above the claims of community or collective welfare — but they differ significantly in their conclusions.

**Libertarianism** accepts the need for a minimal state — one that protects property rights, enforces contracts, and prevents force and fraud — but insists that virtually all other functions of modern governments are illegitimate. Robert Nozick's argument is characteristic: taxation beyond the minimum required for protection is morally equivalent to forced labour. Social welfare programmes, regulations, and redistribution all involve coercing some people to benefit others.

Libertarians are often socially liberal (no state interference in personal life) and economically conservative (free markets, minimal regulation). What unites these positions is the non-aggression principle: the state may never initiate force against a peaceful person, whatever the intended social benefit.

**Anarchism** goes further: the state itself is illegitimate. Even the minimal state Nozick defends rests on a territorial monopoly of violence that cannot be justified. Anarchists favour voluntary associations — mutual-aid societies, workers' cooperatives, community councils — over any coercive authority. Anarchism is not chaos; it is the belief that coordination and community are achievable without hierarchy.

Anarchists on the economic left (like Kropotkin) imagine communities sharing resources cooperatively. Anarcho-capitalists on the right imagine markets replacing all state functions.

</Step>
