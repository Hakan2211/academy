<Step title="When reason trumps the senses" kind="explain">

In the seventeenth century, a cluster of brilliant thinkers across Europe converged on a radical conviction: the senses are unreliable guides to truth, but **reason alone can give us certain knowledge**. We call them the Rationalists — and their leading figures, Descartes, Spinoza, and Leibniz, share the view that some truths can be known *a priori*, independently of experience, simply by thinking carefully.

Think of geometry. No one needs to measure every triangle in the world to know that its angles sum to 180°. The truth follows from definitions and logical deduction alone. The Rationalists believed this power of pure reason could be extended far beyond geometry — to God, mind, substance, and the ultimate structure of reality.

Each Rationalist built a grand system from first principles:

- **Descartes** (1596–1650) started from systematic doubt, found the one certainty that resisted it (*cogito ergo sum*), and rebuilt knowledge on that foundation using reason and the existence of a non-deceiving God.
- **Spinoza** (1632–1677) carried the geometric method furthest: his *Ethics* is literally written in the style of Euclid, with definitions, axioms, and proofs. He concluded that God and Nature are one substance — *Deus sive Natura*.
- **Leibniz** (1646–1716) argued for innate ideas and pre-established harmony: the soul and the body don't interact — they run in perfect synchrony like two clocks wound by God at the creation.

What unites them is the conviction that the deepest truths are **innate or derivable by reason**, not learned from the senses.

</Step>

<Step title="Doubt your way to certainty" kind="interactive">

Descartes' method is not scepticism for its own sake — it's a tool for *finding* certainty. He applies waves of doubt, stripping away everything that can be questioned, until he hits bedrock: the one thing no deceiver can take away.

<SkepticismLab />

Notice what survives: not a fact about the world, not a sensory experience, but a truth of pure reason — the existence of the thinking thing itself. This is the Rationalist move: reason, not experience, delivers the most secure knowledge.

</Step>

<Step title="Innate ideas and the a priori" kind="explain">

The Rationalists disagreed with each other on many things, but shared the claim that the mind brings something to experience — it is not a blank slate waiting to be written on by the senses. Call this the doctrine of **innate ideas**.

Leibniz put it memorably: the mind is like a block of marble with veins already in it. The sculptor (experience) reveals the figure hidden in the stone, but the figure was always there. Pure concepts — unity, substance, identity, cause, God — are not derived from any perception. They are part of the mind's own structure.

This has a striking consequence. Hume would later challenge it head-on. But for now, notice the ambition: if some truths are knowable by reason alone and are *necessarily* true, they hold in all possible worlds — not just the actual one. Mathematics and logic aren't contingent facts that happen to be true; they couldn't possibly be otherwise. The Rationalists wanted to extend that necessity to metaphysics itself.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Rationalism**: the view that reason, not the senses, is the primary or most reliable source of knowledge.
- **A priori** knowledge: knowledge independent of experience — provable by reason alone (e.g. mathematics).
- **Descartes** used systematic doubt to find one indubitable truth: *cogito ergo sum*.
- **Spinoza** extended the geometric method to all of reality; **Leibniz** defended innate ideas and pre-established harmony.
- The shared claim: the mind is not a passive blank slate — it contributes structure, concepts, or ideas that no sensory experience could supply.

</Step>
