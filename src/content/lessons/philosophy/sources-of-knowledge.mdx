<Step title="The oldest debate in epistemology" kind="explain">

Even if we agree that knowledge requires justified true belief, there is a deeper question: **where does justification come from in the first place?** What are the ultimate sources of our knowledge of the world?

Two grand traditions have dominated this debate for centuries:

**Rationalism** holds that **reason is the primary source of knowledge**. Some truths can be known by pure thought, independent of any experience. Descartes, Leibniz, and Spinoza are the classic rationalists. They pointed to mathematics and logic: you don't need to go outside and measure triangles to know that the angles sum to 180°. You can prove it from pure reasoning.

**Empiricism** holds that **experience is the source of all genuine knowledge about the world**. The mind begins as a *tabula rasa* — a blank slate — and everything we know is written on it by the senses. Locke, Hume, and Berkeley are the classic empiricists. For Hume, if you can't trace a concept back to some sensory impression, it's meaningless.

</Step>

<Step title="Two kinds of knowledge" kind="explain">

The debate hinges on a crucial distinction:

**A priori** knowledge is knowledge that can be justified *independently of experience* — by reason, reflection, or analysis of concepts alone. The paradigm cases are logic and mathematics.

- *"All bachelors are unmarried"* — true by the meaning of the words; no survey needed.
- *"2 + 2 = 4"* — provable from definitions; you don't need to count pairs of apples.

**A posteriori** knowledge requires experience to justify. You have to go out and look, measure, or observe.

- *"Water boils at 100 °C at sea level"* — no amount of armchair reflection tells you this.
- *"There is a cat on the mat"* — you need your eyes.

Rationalists emphasize a priori knowledge; empiricists argue it covers far less than rationalists think, and that all knowledge of the external world is ultimately a posteriori.

<Callout type="info" title="Kant's synthesis">
Immanuel Kant famously tried to bridge the two camps. He agreed with empiricists that all knowledge *begins with* experience, but agreed with rationalists that some knowledge is *not derived from* experience — the mind actively structures experience using built-in concepts (space, time, causation). This produced a third category: *synthetic a priori* knowledge, which is both informative about the world and knowable by reason.
</Callout>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Rationalism**: reason is the primary source of knowledge; some truths are knowable without experience.
- **Empiricism**: experience is the source; the mind begins as a blank slate (*tabula rasa*).
- **A priori** knowledge: justified by reason alone (logic, maths, analytic truths).
- **A posteriori** knowledge: requires experience or observation (science, history, everyday facts).
- **Kant** synthesised both: knowledge begins with experience but the mind's built-in structures shape what we know.

</Step>
