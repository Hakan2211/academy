<Step title="The boldest guess in science" kind="explain">

Cut a piece of gold in half, then in half again, and again. Is there a smallest piece you can't divide further? The ancient Greek Democritus said yes, and named it *atomos* — "uncuttable."

For two thousand years it was just a guess. Then, around 1803, John **Dalton** turned it into a scientific theory that actually explained measurements: elements combine in fixed, whole-number ratios because they're made of indivisible **atoms** that simply rearrange.

</Step>

<Step title="Four models, one century" kind="interactive">

The atom was then rebuilt again and again as new experiments revealed more. Step through the models and see what each one got right — and what forced the next revision.

</Step>

<Step title="How a model gets overturned" kind="explain">

Notice the pattern: each model wasn't *wrong* so much as *incomplete*. A new experiment revealed something the old picture couldn't explain, and the model was upgraded — never thrown away entirely.

- Dalton's solid ball → **Thomson** found electrons inside it.
- Thomson's pudding → **Rutherford's** gold-foil experiment found a tiny dense nucleus.
- Rutherford's orbits → **Bohr** fixed electrons into specific energy shells to explain the light atoms emit.

<Callout type="info" title="This is how science works">
A scientific model survives only as long as it predicts what we observe. The atom's story is a perfect example: better instruments → surprising results → a sharper model.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="The modern picture" kind="explain">

Even Bohr's neat orbits were later refined into the **quantum model**, where electrons live in fuzzy clouds of probability rather than tidy circles (we'll meet that at the end of this world). But for most of chemistry, the Bohr-style shell picture is exactly the right tool — and it's the one we'll build on next.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- The **atom** is the smallest piece of an element; Dalton made it a testable theory.
- The model was rebuilt as experiments revealed more: **Dalton → Thomson → Rutherford → Bohr**.
- **Rutherford** discovered the tiny dense **nucleus**; **Bohr** added fixed electron **shells**.
- A model survives only while it predicts observations — the modern **quantum** picture refines Bohr further.

</Step>
