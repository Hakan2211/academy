<Step title="One map for everything" kind="explain">

You've now met every category of matter. Let's put them on one map — a decision tree that any material in the universe falls onto.

Start at the top with a sample of matter and ask two questions:

1. **Is it uniform — the same throughout?** If not, it's a **heterogeneous mixture**. If yes, keep going.
2. **Can it be physically separated into different substances?** If yes, it's a **homogeneous mixture** (a solution). If no, it's a **pure substance**.

Then for a pure substance: **can it be broken down chemically?** If yes, it's a **compound**; if no, it's an **element**.

</Step>

</Step>

<Step title="The particle logic underneath" kind="explain">

Every box on the map is really a statement about particles:

- **Element** — one kind of atom (copper: only Cu atoms; oxygen gas: only O atoms, paired as O₂).
- **Compound** — two or more elements **bonded** in a fixed ratio (H₂O is always 2 hydrogen : 1 oxygen).
- **Homogeneous mixture** — more than one substance, **unbonded**, blended evenly (salt water).
- **Heterogeneous mixture** — more than one substance, **unbonded**, unevenly distributed (sand and iron).

The single deepest divide is **bonded vs unbonded**: bonding makes a compound (a pure substance), mere mixing makes a mixture.

</Step>

<Step title="Composition and state are independent" kind="interactive">

Here's a subtlety worth pinning down: *composition* (element / compound / mixture) and *state* (solid / liquid / gas) are two separate axes. Water is a compound whether it's ice, liquid, or steam — melting it doesn't change what it's made of.

<StatesOfMatter />

So a full description of a material names **both**: "liquid water" (compound + liquid), "solid copper" (element + solid), "gaseous air" (mixture + gas).

</Step>

<Step title="Why the map matters" kind="explain">

This classification isn't bookkeeping for its own sake — it tells you *what tools to reach for*:

- See a **mixture**? You can separate it physically (filter, distil, chromatograph) — no chemistry needed.
- See a **compound**? Splitting it needs a **chemical change** (a reaction).
- See an **element**? It's already as simple as ordinary matter gets — the next world zooms inside the atom itself.

<Callout type="info" title="Where we're headed">
Everything from here builds downward. Elements are made of **atoms**; the way atoms are structured (next world) explains the **periodic table**; and the way atoms **bond** explains why compounds form at all. The map of matter is the doorway to the rest of chemistry.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- All matter maps onto a tree by two questions: **uniform?** and **physically separable?**
- **Element** (one kind of atom) and **compound** (elements bonded in a fixed ratio) are **pure substances**; everything else is a **mixture** (homogeneous or heterogeneous).
- The deepest divide is **bonded (compound) vs unbonded (mixture)**.
- **Composition** and **state** are independent axes — describe a material with both.
- The classification tells you the tools: physical separation for mixtures, reactions for compounds.

</Step>
