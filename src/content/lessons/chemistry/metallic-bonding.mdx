<Step title="A sea of electrons" kind="explain">

When metal atoms pack together, they do something neither ionic nor covalent: they let their valence electrons **roam free**. Each atom releases its outer electrons into a shared pool, becoming a fixed **positive ion**.

The result is a lattice of positive ions sitting in a **sea of delocalised electrons**. The attraction between the positive ions and that mobile electron sea is the **metallic bond**.

</Step>

<Step title="Watch the sea flow" kind="interactive">

The electrons aren't tied to any one atom — they drift everywhere. Apply a voltage and the whole sea flows in one direction.

<MetallicBond />

</Step>

<Step title="Free electrons explain everything" kind="explain">

Almost every classic metal property comes straight from those free electrons:

- **Electrical conductivity** — mobile electrons drift to carry current.
- **Thermal conductivity** — they also shuttle heat energy quickly through the metal.
- **Lustre (shine)** — the loose electrons reflect light.

</Step>

<Step title="Malleable, not brittle" kind="explain">

Here's the contrast with ionic crystals. Hammer a metal and the layers of positive ions slide over each other — but the electron sea simply flows along and keeps holding everything together. So metals **bend and flatten** (malleable) instead of shattering.

In an ionic lattice the same blow would line up like charges and crack it. The mobile glue of the electron sea is what makes metals so workable.

<Callout type="info" title="Stronger and weaker metals">
More valence electrons donated and smaller ions mean a stronger metallic bond — which is why magnesium is harder and higher-melting than sodium. Alloys (like steel) tweak these bonds by mixing in atoms of different sizes.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Metallic bonding**: positive metal ions in a shared **sea of delocalised electrons**.
- The free electrons give metals their **electrical and thermal conductivity** and **shine**.
- Layers of ions can **slide** while the electron sea holds them together → metals are **malleable**, not brittle.
- Bond strength rises with more donated electrons and smaller ions.

</Step>
