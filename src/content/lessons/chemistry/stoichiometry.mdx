<Step title="The recipe ratios" kind="explain">

A balanced equation is a recipe. Just as "2 eggs make 1 omelette" lets you scale a meal, the coefficients in **2H₂ + O₂ → 2H₂O** tell you that 2 moles of hydrogen react with 1 of oxygen to make 2 of water.

**Stoichiometry** is using these **mole ratios** to calculate how much reactant you need or how much product you'll get. The coefficients are the conversion factors.

</Step>

<Step title="The mole-ratio bridge" kind="explain">

The heart of every stoichiometry problem is the mole ratio from the equation. For N₂ + 3H₂ → 2NH₃, going from hydrogen to ammonia:

<Formula tex="\text{mol NH}_3 = \text{mol H}_2 \times \dfrac{2}{3}" block />

The fraction comes straight from the coefficients (2 NH₃ for every 3 H₂). Multiply by it and you've crossed from one substance to another.

</Step>

<Step title="Mass to mass" kind="explain">

Most real problems start and end in **grams**, because that's what we weigh. The full path strings together everything from this world:

1. **mass of A → moles of A** (divide by molar mass)
2. **moles of A → moles of B** (multiply by the mole ratio)
3. **moles of B → mass of B** (multiply by molar mass)

Grams → moles → *mole ratio* → moles → grams. The mole is always the middle step.

<Callout type="tip" title="You can't skip the ratio">
It's tempting to compare masses directly, but reactions count *particles*, not grams. Always convert to moles, apply the coefficient ratio, then convert back. The mole ratio is non-negotiable.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Stoichiometry** uses the balanced equation's **mole ratios** to relate amounts of reactants and products.
- The **mole ratio** (from the coefficients) converts moles of one substance to moles of another.
- Mass-to-mass path: **grams → moles → mole ratio → moles → grams**.
- Always work through **moles** — reactions count particles, not grams.

</Step>
