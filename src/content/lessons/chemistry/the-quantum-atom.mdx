<Lesson>

<Step title="Beyond tidy orbits" kind="explain">

Bohr's neat circular shells explain a lot — but they're not quite the truth. Electrons don't trace clean circles. They obey **quantum mechanics**, where you can never pin down exactly where an electron is, only the *probability* of finding it in a region.

So the modern atom replaces orbits with **orbitals**: 3D clouds showing where an electron is *likely* to be. The shells you already know are still there — they're just made of these fuzzier orbitals.

</Step>

<Step title="The shape of probability" kind="interactive">

An orbital isn't a path; it's a cloud. The denser the cloud, the more likely the electron is there. Different orbitals have different shapes — spheres, dumbbells, and more.

<ProbabilityCloud />

</Step>

<Step title="Sub-shells: s, p, d, f" kind="explain">

Each main shell is divided into **sub-shells**, each a set of orbitals with a characteristic shape:

- **s** — one spherical orbital (holds 2 electrons)
- **p** — three dumbbell orbitals (holds 6)
- **d** — five orbitals (holds 10)
- **f** — seven orbitals (holds 14)

Each individual orbital holds at most **two** electrons, and they must have opposite "spin." This is why the shells hold 2, 8, 18… — it's just the orbitals adding up.

</Step>

<Step title="The filling rules" kind="interactive">

Electrons fill orbitals by three simple rules:

1. **Aufbau** — fill the lowest-energy sub-shell first.
2. **Pauli** — max two electrons per orbital, opposite spins.
3. **Hund** — spread out singly across equal-energy orbitals before pairing up.

Slide the atomic number and watch the boxes fill in order.

<ElectronConfig />

</Step>

<Step title="Configuration explains the table" kind="explain">

Write out where the electrons go and you get an **electron configuration** like oxygen's 1s² 2s² 2p⁴. This notation is the deep key to the periodic table:

- The **s-block, p-block, d-block** regions of the table are exactly the sub-shell being filled.
- An element's **valence configuration** (its outer s and p electrons) explains its column and its chemistry.

<Callout type="info" title="Why 4s fills before 3d">
Energy levels overlap as they get higher: the 4s sub-shell sits slightly *below* 3d in energy, so it fills first (potassium and calcium start the 4th shell before the 3d block of transition metals begins). The filling order, not simple shell number, sets the structure of the table.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- Quantum mechanics replaces orbits with **orbitals** — probability clouds for an electron's location.
- Shells split into **sub-shells** (s, p, d, f) holding 2, 6, 10, 14 electrons; each orbital takes 2 with opposite spin.
- Electrons fill by **Aufbau, Pauli, and Hund's** rules, giving an **electron configuration**.
- Configuration explains the periodic table's **blocks** and each element's valence behaviour — including why 4s fills before 3d.

</Step>
