<Step title="Opposites cancel" kind="explain">

Mix an acid and a base and they cancel each other out — **neutralization**. The acid's H⁺ and the base's OH⁻ combine to form plain water, while the leftover ions form a **salt**:

<Formula tex="\text{acid} + \text{base} \rightarrow \text{salt} + \text{water}" block />

For example: HCl + NaOH → NaCl + H₂O. The dangerous acid and base produce harmless salt water.

</Step>

<Step title="Toward neutral" kind="interactive">

Neutralization drives the pH toward 7. Watch how acidity changes as you move across the scale.

<PHScale />

</Step>

<Step title="The heart of it: H⁺ + OH⁻ → water" kind="explain">

Strip away the spectator ions and every neutralization has the same core reaction:

<Formula tex="\text{H}^+ + \text{OH}^- \rightarrow \text{H}_2\text{O}" block />

The H⁺ from the acid and OH⁻ from the base join to make water. Whatever positive ion came from the base and negative ion from the acid are left dissolved as the salt.

</Step>

<Step title="Naming the salt" kind="explain">

The salt's name comes from the acid and base used:

- Hydrochloric acid → **chloride** salts (NaCl)
- Sulfuric acid → **sulfate** salts (Na₂SO₄)
- Nitric acid → **nitrate** salts (KNO₃)

<Callout type="info" title="Neutralization all around you">
Antacids (a weak base) neutralise excess stomach acid. Lime (a base) is spread to fix acidic soil. Toothpaste neutralises the acids that cause decay. Even a bee sting (acidic) versus a wasp sting (alkaline) calls for opposite remedies. Neutralization is everywhere.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Neutralization**: acid + base → **salt + water**, driving pH toward 7.
- The core reaction is always **H⁺ + OH⁻ → H₂O**; leftover ions form the salt.
- The **salt's name** comes from the parent acid (chloride, sulfate, nitrate…).
- Everyday uses: antacids, soil treatment, toothpaste, sting remedies.

</Step>
