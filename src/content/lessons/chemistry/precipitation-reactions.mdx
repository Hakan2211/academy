<Step title="A solid from two liquids" kind="explain">

Mix two clear solutions and sometimes — surprisingly — a solid suddenly appears, swirling out of the liquid. That solid is a **precipitate**, and the reaction is a **precipitation** (a kind of double replacement).

It happens when the ions swap partners and one of the new combinations is **insoluble** — it can't stay dissolved, so it drops out as a solid.

</Step>

<Step title="Solubility rules" kind="explain">

Whether a precipitate forms is decided by **solubility rules** — a set of patterns for which ionic compounds dissolve:

- Most **nitrates (NO₃⁻)** and **group 1 / ammonium** salts are *always* soluble.
- Most **chlorides** are soluble — *except* silver, lead, and mercury.
- Most **carbonates, hydroxides, and sulfides** are *insoluble* — except with group 1 and ammonium.

If swapping partners creates one of the insoluble combinations, that's your precipitate.

</Step>

<Step title="Predicting the product" kind="explain">

To predict a precipitation reaction:

1. Identify the ions present in both solutions.
2. Swap the partners (double replacement) to get two possible new compounds.
3. Check each against the solubility rules — any insoluble one is the precipitate.

<Callout type="info" title="Precipitation in the real world">
This is how hard-water scale forms, how kidney stones grow, how minerals are extracted from solution, and how chemists test for specific ions (a yellow PbI₂ precipitate flags lead). The same swap-and-check logic runs all of it.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- A **precipitation reaction** forms an insoluble solid (a **precipitate**) when ions swap partners.
- **Solubility rules** predict which combinations stay dissolved and which drop out.
- Predict by: list ions → swap partners → check solubility → the insoluble one precipitates.
- Used in water treatment, ion testing, mineral extraction, and explaining scale and stones.

</Step>
