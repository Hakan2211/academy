<Lesson>

<Step title="Balance charge as well as atoms" kind="explain">

Redox equations must balance **two** things: the atoms (as always) *and* the electrons transferred. Every electron lost in oxidation must be gained in reduction — none can vanish.

The neatest way to handle this is to split the reaction into two **half-reactions** — one for oxidation, one for reduction — balance each, then recombine.

</Step>

<Step title="The electrons must match" kind="interactive">

Recall the core idea: electrons released by the oxidised species are exactly the electrons taken up by the reduced one.

<RedoxLab />

The number lost must equal the number gained — that's the rule that makes balancing work.

</Step>

<Step title="The half-reaction method" kind="explain">

1. **Split** into oxidation and reduction half-reactions.
2. **Balance** the atoms in each half.
3. **Add electrons** to balance the charge in each half.
4. **Multiply** the halves so the electrons lost = electrons gained.
5. **Add** them back together; the electrons cancel.

For Zn + Cu²⁺:

<Formula tex="\text{Zn} \rightarrow \text{Zn}^{2+} + 2e^- \quad\text{(oxidation)}" block />
<Formula tex="\text{Cu}^{2+} + 2e^- \rightarrow \text{Cu} \quad\text{(reduction)}" block />

Both involve 2 electrons, so they add directly: Zn + Cu²⁺ → Zn²⁺ + Cu.

</Step>

<Step title="When the electrons don't match" kind="explain">

Often the two halves involve different electron counts. Then you **scale** each half-reaction to a common multiple so the electrons cancel exactly — just like finding a common denominator.

<Callout type="tip" title="In acidic solution">
For reactions in water, you also balance oxygen by adding H₂O and hydrogen by adding H⁺ (and OH⁻ in basic solution). But the heart of it is always the same: make the electrons lost equal the electrons gained.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- Redox equations balance **atoms and electrons** (charge).
- Use the **half-reaction method**: split, balance atoms, add electrons, scale so electrons match, recombine.
- Electrons **lost = gained**, so they cancel in the final equation.
- In solution, also balance O with H₂O and H with H⁺ — but matching electrons is the core.

</Step>
