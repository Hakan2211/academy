<Step title="Defining 'acid' more deeply" kind="explain">

"Releases H⁺" is a good start, but chemists sharpened the definition over time. Two theories matter:

- **Arrhenius:** an acid produces **H⁺** in water; a base produces **OH⁻**. Simple, but only works in water.
- **Brønsted–Lowry:** an acid is a **proton (H⁺) donor**; a base is a **proton acceptor**. This is broader — it works beyond water and explains far more.

A lone H⁺ *is* a proton (hydrogen with its electron stripped away), so "donating H⁺" and "donating a proton" mean the same thing.

</Step>

<Step title="Conjugate pairs" kind="explain">

The Brønsted–Lowry view reveals a beautiful symmetry. When an acid donates a proton, what's left behind can accept one back — it's a base. They form a **conjugate acid–base pair**:

<Formula tex="\text{HA} \rightleftharpoons \text{H}^+ + \text{A}^-" block />

Here HA (acid) and A⁻ (its **conjugate base**) are a pair, differing by just one proton. Every acid has a conjugate base, and every base a conjugate acid.

</Step>

<Step title="Water plays both roles" kind="explain">

Water is **amphoteric** — it can act as an acid *or* a base. With an acid it accepts a proton (becoming H₃O⁺); with a base it donates one (becoming OH⁻). This flexibility is exactly why water is the natural home of acid–base chemistry.

<Callout type="info" title="Why broaden the definition?">
Ammonia (NH₃) is a base, yet it contains no OH⁻ — Arrhenius can't explain it. But it clearly *accepts* a proton (NH₃ + H⁺ → NH₄⁺), so Brønsted–Lowry handles it easily. The broader theory captures bases the simple one misses.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Arrhenius**: acid makes H⁺, base makes OH⁻ (in water only).
- **Brønsted–Lowry**: acid = **proton donor**, base = **proton acceptor** (broader, works beyond water).
- Acids and bases come in **conjugate pairs** differing by one proton (HA / A⁻).
- Water is **amphoteric** — it can donate or accept a proton.

</Step>
