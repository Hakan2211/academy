<Step title="Two opposite families" kind="explain">

Acids and bases are among the most important — and most opposite — classes of compounds. You meet them daily: the citric acid in lemons, the acetic acid in vinegar, the ammonia and baking soda in cleaning products.

The simplest picture, in water:

- **Acids** release **hydrogen ions (H⁺)** into solution.
- **Bases** release **hydroxide ions (OH⁻)** (or otherwise accept H⁺).

These ions give each family its characteristic behaviour — and let them neutralise each other.

</Step>

<Step title="Indicators reveal them" kind="explain">

Because you should never taste or touch lab chemicals, chemists use **indicators** — dyes that change colour with acidity:

- **Litmus**: red in acid, blue in base.
- **Universal indicator**: a full rainbow from red (strong acid) to purple (strong base).

A quick dip of indicator paper tells you instantly which family you're dealing with.

<Callout type="warn" title="Corrosive, both ends">
Strong acids *and* strong bases are dangerous — both are corrosive and can cause severe burns. "Base" doesn't mean "safe." Concentrated drain cleaner (a strong base) is every bit as hazardous as a strong acid.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Why they matter" kind="explain">

Acid–base chemistry runs your life: stomach acid digests food, your blood is held at a precise pH, soil acidity controls what crops grow, and countless industrial processes depend on it. Understanding it starts with measuring *how* acidic something is — the pH scale, next.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Acids** release **H⁺** in water; **bases** release **OH⁻** (or accept H⁺) — opposite families.
- They have characteristic properties (taste, feel, reactions) and flip **indicators** (litmus, universal indicator).
- Both strong acids and strong bases are **corrosive** — base ≠ safe.
- Acid–base chemistry is central to digestion, blood, soil, and industry.

</Step>
