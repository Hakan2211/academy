<Step title="Electrons want their space" kind="explain">

Molecules are 3D, and their shape isn't random. It comes from one simple idea called **VSEPR** (Valence Shell Electron Pair Repulsion):

> Electron pairs around a central atom all repel each other, so they spread out as **far apart as possible**.

Count the regions of electrons around the central atom — bonds and lone pairs alike — and the geometry follows automatically.

</Step>

<Step title="Counting regions" kind="explain">

Just count the **regions of electron density** (each single, double, or triple bond counts as one region, and each lone pair counts as one):

- **2 regions** → linear (180°)
- **3 regions** → trigonal planar (120°)
- **4 regions** → tetrahedral (109.5°)

So methane (CH₄), with four bonds and no lone pairs, is a perfect tetrahedron.

</Step>

<Step title="Lone pairs bend the shape" kind="explain">

Lone pairs are greedy — held by just one nucleus, they spread wider than bonding pairs and **squeeze the bonds closer together**.

Ammonia (NH₃) has four regions (3 bonds + 1 lone pair). The lone pair pushes the three N–H bonds down into a **pyramid** and shrinks the angle to ~107°. Water (H₂O) has *two* lone pairs, bending it further to ~104.5°.

<Callout type="info" title="Shape decides function">
Geometry is destiny in chemistry. Water's bent shape makes it polar (next lesson), which is why it dissolves so much. The precise shapes of enzymes and drugs let them lock onto exactly the right target. VSEPR is the first step to predicting all of it.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **VSEPR**: electron regions around a central atom repel and spread **as far apart as possible**, setting the shape.
- **2 / 3 / 4 regions** → linear / trigonal planar / tetrahedral.
- **Lone pairs** repel more than bonds, bending molecules (NH₃ pyramidal, H₂O bent).
- Molecular **shape** determines polarity, solubility, and biological function.

</Step>
