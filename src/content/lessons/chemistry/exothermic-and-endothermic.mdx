<Lesson>

<Step title="Two directions of energy flow" kind="explain">

Every reaction is one of two types, by which way energy flows:

- **Exothermic** — *releases* energy to the surroundings, which **warm up**. Burning, explosions, respiration, hand-warmers.
- **Endothermic** — *absorbs* energy from the surroundings, which **cool down**. Photosynthesis, cooking an egg, instant cold packs, melting ice.

"Exo" = out, "endo" = in. Feel the beaker: warmer means exothermic, colder means endothermic.

</Step>

<Step title="Enthalpy change, ΔH" kind="explain">

The energy released or absorbed at constant pressure is the **enthalpy change**, ΔH, with a tidy sign convention:

- **Exothermic: ΔH is negative** (the system *loses* energy — products are lower).
- **Endothermic: ΔH is positive** (the system *gains* energy — products are higher).

So ΔH = −890 kJ/mol for burning methane (releases energy), but ΔH is positive for melting ice (absorbs energy).

</Step>

<Step title="Why products' energy decides it" kind="explain">

The direction comes straight from the energy diagram: if the products are **more stable** (lower energy) than the reactants, the leftover energy is released — exothermic. If the products are **less stable** (higher energy), energy had to be supplied — endothermic.

<Callout type="tip" title="The sign tells the story">
A negative ΔH means "downhill" energetically — the reaction tends to release heat and run readily. A positive ΔH means "uphill" — it needs a continuous energy input. (Whether a reaction actually *proceeds* also depends on entropy — the capstone.)
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Exothermic** reactions release energy (surroundings warm, **ΔH negative**); **endothermic** absorb it (surroundings cool, **ΔH positive**).
- **ΔH** is the enthalpy change — energy released/absorbed at constant pressure.
- Lower-energy (more stable) products → exothermic; higher-energy products → endothermic.
- The sign of ΔH signals "downhill" (releases) vs "uphill" (needs input).

</Step>
