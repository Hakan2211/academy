<Lesson>

<Step title="A shortcut over the hill" kind="explain">

A **catalyst** speeds up a reaction without being used up itself. It works by offering an alternative pathway with a **lower activation energy** — a smaller hill to climb — so a far greater fraction of collisions succeed.

Crucially, a catalyst is **regenerated** at the end: it takes part, then is released unchanged, ready to help again. A tiny amount can process huge quantities.

</Step>

<Step title="Lower the barrier" kind="interactive">

Add a catalyst and watch the activation-energy hump drop. The reactants and products stay put — only the barrier between them shrinks.

<EnergyDiagram />

</Step>

<Step title="What a catalyst does and doesn't do" kind="explain">

A catalyst:

- **Lowers** the activation energy → speeds the reaction.
- Speeds the forward **and** reverse reaction equally (it helps reach equilibrium faster).
- Is **not consumed** — it comes out unchanged.

A catalyst does **not** change ΔH (the energy released or absorbed) or the position of equilibrium. It only changes how *fast* you get there, not *where* you end up.

</Step>

<Step title="Catalysts everywhere" kind="explain">

Catalysts run the modern world and your body:

- **Enzymes** are biological catalysts — they make life's reactions fast enough to sustain you (your digestion would take years without them).
- **Catalytic converters** (platinum, palladium) clean car exhaust.
- The **Haber process** uses an iron catalyst to make ammonia for fertiliser, feeding billions.

<Callout type="info" title="A connection ahead">
Because a catalyst speeds forward and reverse equally, it changes nothing about *where* equilibrium sits — only how fast it's reached. That distinction matters enormously for the equilibrium lessons coming next.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- A **catalyst** speeds a reaction by lowering the **activation energy** — and is **regenerated** unchanged.
- It speeds forward and reverse equally; it does **not** change ΔH or the equilibrium position.
- **Enzymes** are biological catalysts essential to life.
- Industrial catalysts (catalytic converters, the Haber process) are economically vital.

</Step>
