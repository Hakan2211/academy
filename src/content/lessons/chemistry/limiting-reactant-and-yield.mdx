<Step title="What runs out first?" kind="explain">

Mix reactants and rarely are they in the exact ratio the equation demands. One will run out before the others — and the moment it does, the reaction **stops**. That one is the **limiting reactant**: it caps how much product you can make.

Whatever is left over is the **excess reactant**. It's like making sandwiches with 10 slices of bread and 3 fillings — you get 3 sandwiches and 4 slices of bread are wasted. The filling limits you.

</Step>

<Step title="How to identify it" kind="explain">

To find the limiting reactant:

1. Convert each reactant to **moles**.
2. Divide each by its **coefficient** in the balanced equation.
3. The **smallest** result is the limiting reactant — it determines the product amount.

Then run normal stoichiometry from the *limiting* reactant to find the maximum product (the **theoretical yield**).

</Step>

<Step title="Theoretical vs actual yield" kind="explain">

The **theoretical yield** is the most product the equation allows. In reality you almost always get less — spills, side reactions, incomplete conversion. The **percent yield** measures how well it went:

<Formula tex="\%\text{ yield} = \dfrac{\text{actual yield}}{\text{theoretical yield}} \times 100" block />

<Callout type="info" title="Why it matters in industry">
Chemical plants obsess over yield and the limiting reactant. They often run the *cheaper* reactant in excess to squeeze the most product from the expensive one — turning stoichiometry directly into money.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- The **limiting reactant** runs out first and caps the product; the rest is **excess**.
- Find it: moles ÷ coefficient for each reactant → the **smallest** is limiting.
- Calculate the **theoretical yield** from the limiting reactant.
- **% yield** = actual ÷ theoretical × 100 — real reactions fall short, and industry optimises it.

</Step>
