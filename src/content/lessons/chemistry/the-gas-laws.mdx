<Step title="Linking pressure, volume, and temperature" kind="explain">

For a fixed amount of gas, pressure, volume, and temperature are tied together. Change one and another must respond. The **gas laws** capture these links, each holding two quantities steady:

- **Boyle's Law** — at constant temperature, **P × V = constant**.
- **Charles's Law** — at constant pressure, **V ÷ T = constant**.
- **Gay-Lussac's Law** — at constant volume, **P ÷ T = constant**.

</Step>

<Step title="Boyle: squeeze and the pressure rises" kind="explain">

**Boyle's Law** (P × V = constant) gives an inverse curve: halve the volume and you double the pressure. The particles, crammed into less space, hit the walls twice as often.

<Formula tex="P_1 V_1 = P_2 V_2" block />

It's why a syringe gets harder to push as you block the tip, and why your ears pop as air pressure changes.

</Step>

<Step title="Charles and Gay-Lussac: heat expands and pressurises" kind="explain">

**Charles's Law** (V ÷ T = constant): heat a gas at constant pressure and it **expands** in direct proportion to its kelvin temperature — the principle behind a hot-air balloon.

<Formula tex="\dfrac{V_1}{T_1} = \dfrac{V_2}{T_2}" block />

**Gay-Lussac's Law** (P ÷ T = constant): heat a gas in a *sealed* container and the **pressure** climbs — which is why an aerosol can warns "do not incinerate."

<Callout type="tip" title="One combined law">
All three merge into the combined gas law: P₁V₁/T₁ = P₂V₂/T₂. Hold any quantity constant and it collapses back to Boyle, Charles, or Gay-Lussac.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- For fixed gas, **P, V, and T** are linked by the gas laws.
- **Boyle** (constant T): P × V = constant — inverse (squeeze → higher P).
- **Charles** (constant P): V ÷ T = constant — heat expands.
- **Gay-Lussac** (constant V): P ÷ T = constant — heat a sealed gas → higher P. All combine into P₁V₁/T₁ = P₂V₂/T₂.

</Step>
