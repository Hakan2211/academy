<Lesson>

<Step title="The table is a graph" kind="explain">

Properties don't jump around the table randomly — they slide smoothly in predictable directions called **periodic trends**. Learn the arrows once and you can estimate an element's behaviour just from its position. Two forces explain almost everything:

- **More protons** pull electrons in harder (across a period).
- **More shells** push the outer electrons further out and shield them (down a group).

</Step>

<Step title="Atomic radius — how big is the atom?" kind="interactive">

**Atomic radius** *decreases* across a period (more protons pull the same shell tighter) and *increases* down a group (each row adds a whole new shell). So the biggest atoms sit bottom-left, the smallest top-right.

<PeriodicTable mode="radius" />

</Step>

<Step title="Ionization energy — how hard to remove an electron?" kind="interactive">

**Ionization energy** is the energy needed to pull an electron off. It runs *opposite* to radius: small, tightly held atoms (top-right) cling hardest, so ionization energy **increases across and up**. Big atoms bottom-left give up electrons easily.

<PeriodicTable mode="ionization" />

</Step>

<Step title="Electronegativity — how strongly does it pull shared electrons?" kind="interactive">

**Electronegativity** measures how hard an atom tugs on electrons it's *sharing* in a bond. Same direction as ionization energy: highest top-right (**fluorine** is the champion), lowest bottom-left. This trend will be the key to understanding bonding in the next world.

<PeriodicTable mode="electronegativity" />

<Callout type="info" title="One pattern, three faces">
Radius, ionization energy, and electronegativity are really three views of the same tug-of-war between nuclear pull and electron distance. Pin down the radius trend and the other two follow — they just point the opposite way.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Periodic trends** slide smoothly with position, set by nuclear pull (across) vs electron distance/shielding (down).
- **Atomic radius**: biggest bottom-left, smallest top-right.
- **Ionization energy** and **electronegativity**: highest top-right (fluorine), lowest bottom-left — opposite to radius.
- These trends predict reactivity and, next, how atoms **bond**.

</Step>
