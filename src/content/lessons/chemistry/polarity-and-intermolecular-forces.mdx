<Lesson>

<Step title="Unequal sharing" kind="explain">

Covalent atoms share electrons — but rarely *equally*. The more **electronegative** atom pulls the shared pair closer, building up a small negative charge (δ⁻) while its partner goes slightly positive (δ⁺). A bond with separated charge like this is **polar**.

The bigger the electronegativity gap, the more polar the bond — and at a large enough gap, it tips over into fully ionic.

</Step>

<Step title="From non-polar to ionic" kind="interactive">

Slide the electronegativity difference and watch the shared electrons drift toward the greedier atom.

<Polarity />

A bond between identical atoms (like O₂) is perfectly **non-polar** — no gap, even sharing.

</Step>

<Step title="Polar bonds vs polar molecules" kind="explain">

A molecule can have polar bonds yet be non-polar overall — if its **shape** makes the pulls cancel. CO₂ has two polar C=O bonds, but they point exactly opposite, so they cancel to a non-polar molecule.

Water is different: its **bent** shape means the two O–H pulls don't cancel — they add up. So water is a **polar molecule** with a distinct positive end (the H side) and negative end (the O side). Shape, from VSEPR, decides the outcome.

</Step>

<Step title="Forces between molecules" kind="explain">

These partial charges create attractions *between* separate molecules — **intermolecular forces**. From weakest to strongest:

- **London dispersion forces** — fleeting, in *all* molecules; stronger in bigger ones.
- **Dipole–dipole** — between permanently polar molecules.
- **Hydrogen bonding** — an extra-strong attraction when H is bonded to N, O, or F.

<Callout type="tip" title="Why water is weird">
Hydrogen bonding is the reason water has such a high boiling point, sticks to itself (surface tension), and expands when it freezes so ice floats. These forces aren't bonds *within* molecules — they're the attractions *between* them, and they govern melting points, boiling points, and solubility.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- Unequal electron sharing makes a **polar bond** (δ⁺/δ⁻); a big enough gap becomes ionic.
- A molecule's overall polarity depends on **bond polarity + shape** — symmetric shapes can cancel (CO₂), bent ones add up (H₂O).
- **Intermolecular forces** (dispersion < dipole–dipole < hydrogen bonding) act *between* molecules.
- These forces set **melting/boiling points and solubility** — and explain water's unusual behaviour.

</Step>
