<Step title="Columns are families" kind="explain">

Because a group shares the same valence-electron count, its members act like a **family** — same kind of reactions, same kinds of compounds, with properties trending smoothly down the column. A few families are so distinctive they have names.

</Step>

<Step title="Group 1 — the alkali metals" kind="interactive">

The **alkali metals** (lithium, sodium, potassium…) each have **one** valence electron they're desperate to lose. That makes them soft, shiny, and *violently* reactive — drop sodium in water and it fizzes and may ignite. Reactivity **increases** down the group as that lone electron sits further from the nucleus and is lost more easily.

<PeriodicTable highlightGroup={1} />

</Step>

<Step title="Group 17 — the halogens" kind="interactive">

The **halogens** (fluorine, chlorine, bromine, iodine) each have **seven** valence electrons — one short of full — so they're aggressive electron-*grabbers* and highly reactive non-metals. Here reactivity **decreases** down the group (the incoming electron lands further out and is held less strongly).

<PeriodicTable highlightGroup={17} />

</Step>

<Step title="Group 18 — the noble gases" kind="interactive">

The **noble gases** (helium, neon, argon…) already have a **full** outer shell. With nothing to gain or lose, they are almost completely **unreactive** — the chemical loners. Their full-shell stability is the very target every *other* atom is chasing when it bonds.

<PeriodicTable highlightGroup={18} />

<Callout type="tip" title="Two more named families">
**Group 2** are the **alkaline earth metals** (Mg, Ca…) — reactive, but less so than Group 1 (two electrons to lose). The central block (groups 3–12) are the **transition metals** — hard, dense, often colourful, and great catalysts.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- A **group** is a chemical **family** — same valence count, similar reactions, smooth trends down the column.
- **Group 1 alkali metals** (1 valence e⁻): soft, violently reactive, reactivity rises down.
- **Group 17 halogens** (7 valence e⁻): reactive electron-grabbers, reactivity falls down.
- **Group 18 noble gases** (full shell): unreactive — the stability everything else seeks.

</Step>
