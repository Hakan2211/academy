<Step title="Chemistry is a measuring science" kind="explain">

Chemistry runs on numbers. Before you can predict how much product a reaction makes, you have to *measure* what you started with. Three quantities show up everywhere:

- **Mass** — how much matter is present, in grams (g) or kilograms (kg). Measured on a balance.
- **Volume** — how much space it takes up, in litres (L) or cubic centimetres (cm³). Measured with glassware.
- **Temperature** — how hot it is, usually in degrees Celsius (°C) or kelvin (K).

Chemists use the **metric (SI) system** because its units scale by neat powers of ten.

</Step>

<Step title="Density — mass packed into space" kind="interactive">

Combine mass and volume and you get one of the most useful properties of all: **density**, how much mass is packed into a given volume.

<DensityColumn />

An object floats on any liquid that is *denser* than itself, and sinks through any liquid that is *less* dense. That single rule explains the whole column.

</Step>

<Step title="The density formula" kind="explain">

Density is mass divided by volume:

<Formula tex="\rho = \dfrac{m}{V}" block />

Measured in grams per cubic centimetre (g/cm³) or kilograms per litre. Water is almost exactly **1 g/cm³** — the handy reference that decides what floats in it.

Density is an **intrinsic** property: it doesn't depend on how much you have. A gold ring and a gold bar have the same density, so the same chunk of "is it really gold?" test works on both.

</Step>

<Step title="Honest numbers: significant figures" kind="explain">

A measurement is only as good as the instrument behind it. If your balance reads to 0.01 g, you can't claim a result to 0.0001 g — the extra digits are fiction.

**Significant figures** are the digits you actually know. A good rule: a calculated answer shouldn't look more precise than the least precise measurement that went into it.

<Callout type="tip" title="Precision vs accuracy">
**Accurate** means close to the true value. **Precise** means your repeated measurements agree with each other. A scale that always reads 5 g too high is precise but not accurate — and significant figures are how you avoid overstating either one.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- Chemistry measures **mass** (g), **volume** (L, cm³), and **temperature** (°C, K), using the metric/SI system.
- **Density** = mass ÷ volume (ρ = m/V); water ≈ 1 g/cm³ sets the float/sink line.
- Density is **intrinsic** — independent of how much substance you have.
- **Significant figures** keep your answers as precise as your measurements — and no more.

</Step>
