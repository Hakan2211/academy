<Step title="No collision, no reaction" kind="explain">

For particles to react, they must first **collide**. But not just any collision works — **collision theory** says a collision leads to reaction only if two conditions are met:

1. **Enough energy** — the collision must clear the **activation energy** (the minimum energy to break the existing bonds).
2. **Correct orientation** — the particles must hit at the right angle for the new bonds to form.

A collision that's too gentle or badly aimed just bounces off, unchanged.

</Step>

<Step title="Watch collisions succeed and fail" kind="interactive">

Particles are constantly colliding. Raise the temperature and more of those collisions carry enough energy to react (the green flashes).

</Step>

<Step title="Activation energy: the barrier" kind="explain">

Even an energy-releasing reaction needs a starting push — the **activation energy (Eₐ)**. It's the hill the reactants must climb before they can roll down to products. A match doesn't light itself; the friction strike supplies the activation energy.

The fraction of particles with enough energy to clear Eₐ rises steeply with temperature — which is the deep reason heating speeds reactions so dramatically.

</Step>

<Step title="The unifying idea" kind="explain">

Collision theory ties every rate factor to one question: *how often do successful collisions happen?*

- More frequent collisions → faster.
- More energetic collisions (clearing Eₐ) → faster.

<Callout type="tip" title="Frequency × success">
Rate ≈ (collision frequency) × (fraction that succeed). Anything that raises either term speeds the reaction. That's exactly the lens for the next lesson's factors — temperature, concentration, surface area, and catalysts.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Collision theory**: particles react only when they collide with **enough energy** and the **right orientation**.
- The **activation energy (Eₐ)** is the minimum energy barrier every reaction must clear.
- Raising temperature sharply increases the fraction of particles that can clear Eₐ.
- Rate ≈ collision **frequency** × fraction that **succeed** — the lens for all rate factors.

</Step>
