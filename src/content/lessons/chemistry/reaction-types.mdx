<Step title="A few master patterns" kind="explain">

Millions of reactions exist, but most fit a handful of **patterns**. Spot the pattern and you can predict the products. The big four (plus combustion):

- **Synthesis**: A + B → AB (combine)
- **Decomposition**: AB → A + B (break apart)
- **Single replacement**: A + BC → AC + B (one element swaps in)
- **Double replacement**: AB + CD → AD + CB (partners swap)

</Step>

<Step title="How to recognise them" kind="explain">

Look at how many reactants and products there are, and whether they're elements or compounds:

- **Two things → one** is synthesis.
- **One → two or more** is decomposition.
- **An element + a compound**, with the element swapping places, is single replacement.
- **Two compounds swapping ions** is double replacement.

</Step>

<Step title="Why patterns help" kind="explain">

Classifying a reaction is the first step to **predicting its products**. If you know that "metal + acid" is a single replacement that releases hydrogen gas, you can predict the products before ever running it.

<Callout type="tip" title="Patterns can overlap">
The categories aren't perfectly exclusive — combustion of a hydrocarbon is also an oxidation, and many reactions could be labelled in more than one way. The patterns are a thinking tool, not rigid boxes. What matters is using them to predict what forms.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- Most reactions fit master **patterns**: synthesis, decomposition, single replacement, double replacement (and combustion).
- Identify by counting reactants/products and whether they're elements or compounds.
- **Two → one** = synthesis; **one → many** = decomposition; element swaps = single replacement; partner swaps = double replacement.
- Patterns are a tool for **predicting products** — not rigid, mutually exclusive boxes.

</Step>
