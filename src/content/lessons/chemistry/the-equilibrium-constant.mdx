<Lesson>

<Step title="Putting a number on the balance" kind="explain">

Le Chatelier tells you *which way* equilibrium shifts. The **equilibrium constant (K)** puts a precise **number** on where it sits. For a reaction aA + bB ⇌ cC + dD:

<Formula tex="K = \dfrac{[C]^c [D]^d}{[A]^a [B]^b}" block />

It's the ratio of product concentrations to reactant concentrations at equilibrium, each raised to its coefficient. K is a fixed value for a given reaction at a given temperature.

</Step>

<Step title="What K tells you" kind="explain">

The size of K reveals where equilibrium lies:

- **K ≫ 1** — products dominate; equilibrium lies far to the **right** (reaction goes nearly to completion).
- **K ≈ 1** — significant amounts of both.
- **K ≪ 1** — reactants dominate; equilibrium lies far to the **left** (barely proceeds).

K is a snapshot of the *destination*, independent of how fast you get there or what amounts you start with.

</Step>

<Step title="K stays put — unless temperature changes" kind="interactive">

Add or remove substances and the system shifts (Le Chatelier) — but it settles back to the **same K**. Only changing the **temperature** actually changes K's value.

<EquilibriumLab />

This is the deep reason temperature is special: concentration and pressure changes shift the *position* within a fixed K, but temperature changes K itself.

</Step>

<Step title="Q vs K: predicting the direction" kind="explain">

For a mixture that *isn't* at equilibrium yet, compute the same ratio and call it the **reaction quotient, Q**. Compare:

- **Q < K** → too few products → reaction goes **forward**.
- **Q > K** → too many products → reaction goes **reverse**.
- **Q = K** → already at equilibrium.

So K and Q together let you predict not just where equilibrium lies, but which way any mixture will move to get there.

<Callout type="info" title="The two halves of this world, united">
Rate (kinetics) governs *how fast* equilibrium is reached; K (thermodynamics) governs *where* it ends up. A catalyst changes the first, never the second. Together they explain everything from the Haber process to the chemistry inside your cells — and they close the central chapters of chemistry.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- The **equilibrium constant K** = [products] ÷ [reactants] (each to its coefficient) — a fixed number at a given temperature.
- **K ≫ 1** → products favoured; **K ≪ 1** → reactants favoured.
- Concentration/pressure changes shift position but keep K; only **temperature** changes K.
- Compare the quotient **Q to K** to predict which way a mixture moves. Rate = how fast; K = where it ends.

</Step>
