<Step title="Breaking and making bonds" kind="explain">

A **chemical reaction** is the heart of chemistry: substances (the **reactants**) transform into new substances (the **products**) by breaking and re-forming bonds between atoms.

The atoms themselves are never created or destroyed — they're just **rearranged** into new combinations. We capture this with an equation, reactants on the left, products on the right:

<Step title="How to spot a reaction" kind="explain">

Because new substances appear, reactions usually announce themselves with visible **signs**:

- A **colour change**
- **Bubbles** of gas
- A **solid** (precipitate) forming from liquids
- **Heat or light** given off (or absorbed)
- An **odour** appearing

Any of these hints that bonds have been rearranged into something new.

</Step>

<Step title="Reactants, products, and conditions" kind="explain">

Equations often note the **conditions** above the arrow — heat (Δ), a catalyst, or light — and the **state** of each substance: (s) solid, (l) liquid, (g) gas, (aq) dissolved in water.

<Callout type="info" title="Energy is part of the story">
Rearranging bonds always involves energy: breaking bonds costs it, forming bonds releases it. The net difference is why some reactions warm their surroundings and others cool them — a whole world we'll explore later. For now, focus on what turns into what.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- A **reaction** turns **reactants** into **products** by breaking and forming bonds; atoms are **rearranged**, never created or destroyed.
- Written as **reactants → products**, often with conditions and states noted.
- Signs of a reaction: colour change, gas, precipitate, heat/light, odour.
- Reactions always involve an **energy** change (explored in a later world).

</Step>
