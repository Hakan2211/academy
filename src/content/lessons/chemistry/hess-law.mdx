<Lesson>

<Step title="The route doesn't matter" kind="explain">

Some reactions are hard to measure directly — they're too slow, too violent, or make side products. **Hess's Law** is the clever workaround:

> The total enthalpy change of a reaction is the same no matter what path it takes — one step or many.

Because enthalpy depends only on the **start and end states**, you can add up the ΔH values of easy-to-measure steps to find the ΔH of a hard one.

</Step>

<Step title="One path or two — same total" kind="interactive">

Compare making CO₂ from carbon directly, versus going through carbon monoxide first. The two-step ΔH values add up to exactly the direct ΔH.

<HessLaw />

</Step>

<Step title="Adding reactions" kind="explain">

The method: arrange known reactions (reversing or scaling them as needed) so they add up to your target equation, then add their ΔH values:

- **Reverse** a reaction → flip the sign of its ΔH.
- **Multiply** a reaction by a number → multiply its ΔH by the same number.

Add the adjusted equations (cancelling anything appearing on both sides) and the adjusted ΔH values give your answer.

</Step>

<Step title="Why it works" kind="explain">

Enthalpy is a **state function** — it cares only about where you start and finish, not the journey. Like altitude on a mountain: the height difference between base and summit is fixed, whether you take the steep direct trail or a long winding one.

<Callout type="info" title="Energy can't be created by a detour">
If different paths gave different total energy, you could loop around and make free energy from nothing — impossible. Hess's Law is really energy conservation in disguise, applied to chemical routes.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Hess's Law**: total ΔH is the same by any path — so add step ΔH values to find a hard-to-measure one.
- **Reverse** a step → flip ΔH's sign; **scale** a step → scale ΔH.
- It works because enthalpy is a **state function** (depends only on start and end).
- It's energy conservation applied to reaction routes.

</Step>
