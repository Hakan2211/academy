<Step title="Four ways to speed things up" kind="explain">

Collision theory predicts exactly what speeds a reaction — anything that makes **successful collisions more frequent**. There are four main levers:

- **Temperature** — faster, more energetic particles.
- **Concentration** (or pressure for gases) — more particles packed in.
- **Surface area** — more exposed particles to collide with.
- **Catalyst** — lowers the activation energy.

</Step>

<Step title="Why each one works" kind="explain">

Trace each back to collisions:

- **Temperature ↑** — particles move faster (more collisions) *and* more of them clear the activation energy. This is the biggest lever — rate roughly doubles for every 10 °C.
- **Concentration ↑** — more particles in the same space means more frequent collisions.
- **Surface area ↑** — breaking a solid into powder exposes far more particles; a flour dust cloud can explode while a flour bag won't.
- **Catalyst** — provides an easier route with lower activation energy, so more collisions succeed.

</Step>

<Step title="Everyday rate control" kind="explain">

You manage these constantly without thinking:

- A **fridge** slows food decay (lower temperature).
- **Chopping** vegetables cooks them faster (more surface area).
- **Pressurising** gases in industry speeds production (higher concentration).
- **Catalytic converters** clean exhaust fumes fast enough to matter (catalyst).

<Callout type="warn" title="Surface area can be dangerous">
The same powdered-solid speed-up that helps cooking causes **dust explosions** in flour mills, grain silos, and sawmills — a fine, highly flammable powder reacts with air explosively fast. Rate factors cut both ways.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- Four factors speed a reaction: **temperature, concentration/pressure, surface area, catalyst**.
- Each works by making **successful collisions more frequent** (temperature also raises their energy).
- Temperature is the strongest lever (~doubles rate per 10 °C); a catalyst lowers activation energy.
- These are used daily — and surface area's power makes **dust explosions** a real hazard.

</Step>
