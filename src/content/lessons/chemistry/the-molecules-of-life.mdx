<Step title="Chemistry, alive" kind="explain">

**Biochemistry** is where everything you've learned meets life. Living things are built and run by organic molecules — and they fall into just **four great families**:

- **Carbohydrates** — sugars and starches (energy and structure).
- **Lipids** — fats and oils (energy store and membranes).
- **Proteins** — the workhorses (enzymes, structure, transport).
- **Nucleic acids** — DNA and RNA (the information).

Four families, built mostly from carbon, hydrogen, oxygen, nitrogen — assembling all of life's complexity.

</Step>

<Step title="Polymers of life" kind="explain">

Three of the four are **polymers** — exactly the giant repeating-unit molecules from the organic world:

- Carbohydrates are polymers of **sugars**.
- Proteins are polymers of **amino acids**.
- Nucleic acids are polymers of **nucleotides**.

(Lipids are the exception — they're assembled but not true repeating polymers.) Life builds its big molecules by linking small monomers, just like industrial plastics — through **condensation**, losing a water molecule at each join.

</Step>

<Step title="Built and broken with water" kind="explain">

Two opposite reactions run constantly in every cell:

- **Condensation** joins monomers, releasing water — building polymers.
- **Hydrolysis** uses water to split them back into monomers — digestion is hydrolysis.

<Callout type="info" title="Just organic chemistry, scaled up">
Nothing here breaks the rules you already know — biochemistry is organic chemistry running inside cells, with functional groups, polymers, and acid–base and redox reactions all at work. Life is chemistry, organised.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Biochemistry** is the chemistry of life, built on four families: **carbohydrates, lipids, proteins, nucleic acids**.
- Three are **polymers** (of sugars, amino acids, nucleotides); lipids are the exception.
- Cells build polymers by **condensation** (losing water) and break them by **hydrolysis** (adding water).
- It's all organic chemistry — functional groups, polymers, acid–base and redox — running inside cells.

</Step>
