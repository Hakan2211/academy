<Step title="Two halves of one process" kind="explain">

**Redox** reactions are all about the **transfer of electrons**. They come in two inseparable halves:

- **Oxidation** = **loss** of electrons.
- **Reduction** = **gain** of electrons.

You can't have one without the other — every electron lost by one species is gained by another. The classic memory aid is **OIL RIG**: *Oxidation Is Loss, Reduction Is Gain.*

</Step>

<Step title="Watch the electrons move" kind="interactive">

When zinc meets copper ions, zinc hands over electrons and the copper ions accept them. That transfer *is* the reaction.

<RedoxLab />

</Step>

<Step title="Oxidising and reducing agents" kind="explain">

There's a slight twist in the naming:

- The **oxidising agent** is the species that *causes* oxidation — by accepting electrons, so it is itself **reduced**.
- The **reducing agent** *causes* reduction — by donating electrons, so it is itself **oxidised**.

In Zn + Cu²⁺, zinc is the reducing agent (it gets oxidised) and Cu²⁺ is the oxidising agent (it gets reduced). The agent does the opposite of its name.

</Step>

<Step title="Redox is everywhere" kind="explain">

Electron transfer powers an astonishing range of processes:

- **Combustion** and **respiration** (fuels and food oxidised, releasing energy).
- **Corrosion** — iron oxidised to rust.
- **Batteries** — redox harnessed to deliver electricity.
- **Photosynthesis** and **metabolism** in living things.

<Callout type="tip" title="Beyond oxygen">
"Oxidation" originally meant reacting with oxygen, and it still does — but the modern, broader meaning is simply *losing electrons*, with or without oxygen involved. That generalisation is what makes redox so universal.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Redox** = electron transfer: **Oxidation Is Loss, Reduction Is Gain** (OIL RIG) — always paired.
- The **oxidising agent** gets reduced; the **reducing agent** gets oxidised (each does the opposite of its name).
- Redox powers combustion, respiration, corrosion, batteries, and photosynthesis.
- Modern "oxidation" means **losing electrons** — oxygen optional.

</Step>
