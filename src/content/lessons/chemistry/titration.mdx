<Step title="Finding an unknown concentration" kind="explain">

How much acid is in this solution? **Titration** answers it precisely. You add a base of *known* concentration, drop by drop, to a measured volume of the acid until it's exactly neutralised. The volume needed reveals the unknown concentration.

It's one of chemistry's most reliable lab techniques — accurate, simple, and used everywhere from quality control to medicine.

</Step>

<Step title="Run a titration" kind="interactive">

Add base gradually and watch the pH. It barely moves at first, then leaps through neutral at the **equivalence point** — where moles of acid exactly match moles of base.

<TitrationLab />

</Step>

<Step title="The equivalence point and indicators" kind="explain">

The **equivalence point** is where neutralization is exactly complete. Because the pH changes so sharply there, an **indicator** that flips colour in that range pinpoints it — a single drop turns the flask pink (phenolphthalein) and you stop.

The volume of base added at that moment is the **end point**, read straight off the burette.

</Step>

<Step title="The calculation" kind="explain">

At the equivalence point, the moles of acid and base match (for a 1:1 reaction):

<Formula tex="M_a V_a = M_b V_b" block />

Knowing the base's concentration (M_b), the volume added (V_b), and the acid's volume (V_a), you solve for the acid's unknown concentration (M_a). Stoichiometry, applied with a burette.

<Callout type="tip" title="Mind the ratio">
The simple M_aV_a = M_bV_b assumes a 1:1 mole ratio. If the equation isn't 1:1 (say H₂SO₄ needs two NaOH), include the coefficient — it's just the mole-ratio idea from the stoichiometry world applied here.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Titration** finds an unknown concentration by adding a known solution to neutralization.
- The **equivalence point** is where acid and base moles match — the pH leaps and an **indicator** flips colour.
- Calculate with **MₐVₐ = M_bV_b** (adjusting for the mole ratio if not 1:1).
- It's a precise, everyday lab method built on stoichiometry.
