<Lesson>

<Step title="Resisting change" kind="explain">

Some solutions barely change pH even when you add acid or base — they're **buffers**. Your blood is a superb one: it stays at pH 7.4 despite the constant acids your metabolism dumps into it. Stray even a few tenths and you'd be in serious danger.

A buffer is made of a **weak acid and its conjugate base** together (or a weak base and its conjugate acid). The pair works as a team to absorb shocks.

</Step>

<Step title="Why weak acids hold the key" kind="interactive">

Buffers rely on a weak acid only partly ionising — leaving a reservoir of both the acid (HA) and its conjugate base (A⁻) present at once.

<Dissociation />

</Step>

<Step title="A two-way reserve" kind="explain">

The buffer pair sits in **equilibrium**, ready to neutralise whatever is added:

<Formula tex="\text{HA} \rightleftharpoons \text{H}^+ + \text{A}^-" block />

- Add **acid** (extra H⁺)? The conjugate base A⁻ mops it up, forming HA.
- Add **base** (extra OH⁻)? The weak acid HA donates H⁺ to neutralise it.

Either way the free [H⁺] barely moves, so the pH holds steady. The double arrow — a reversible **equilibrium** — is the heart of how it works (and the subject of a whole world ahead).

</Step>

<Step title="Buffers flatten the titration curve" kind="interactive">

A buffered region resists pH change, showing up as a flat stretch on a titration curve — the pH only lurches once the buffer is overwhelmed at the equivalence point.

<TitrationLab />

</Step>

<Step title="pH, equilibrium, and life" kind="explain">

Buffers are everywhere it matters:

- **Blood** uses a carbonic-acid / bicarbonate buffer to defend pH 7.4.
- **Cells, oceans, and shampoos** are buffered to stay in their working range.
- **Enzymes** only function within a narrow pH, so buffers keep their environment just right.

<Callout type="info" title="A bridge to equilibrium">
Notice the reversible arrow ⇌ doing the heavy lifting — the buffer constantly shifts to counter any disturbance. That's a preview of **Le Chatelier's principle** and chemical **equilibrium**, which govern far more than acids. Acids and bases are your first deep encounter with reactions that run both ways at once.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- A **buffer** resists pH change; it's a **weak acid + its conjugate base** working together.
- It sits in **equilibrium** (HA ⇌ H⁺ + A⁻), neutralising added acid *or* base from a two-way reserve.
- Buffers create the **flat regions** of a titration curve.
- They keep **blood, cells, and enzymes** at their vital pH — and preview chemical equilibrium ahead.

</Step>
