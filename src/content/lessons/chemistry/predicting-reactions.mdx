<Lesson>

<Step title="From watching to forecasting" kind="explain">

You've met the reaction types, the activity series, and solubility rules. Together they let you do something genuinely powerful: look at a set of reactants and **predict the products before mixing anything**.

The strategy is always the same three questions:

1. **What type** of reaction is this?
2. **Will it happen at all?** (Is there a driving force?)
3. **What are the products?**

</Step>

<Step title="Step 1 — classify the type" kind="interactive">

Match the reactants to a pattern. The pattern tells you the *shape* of the products.

<ReactionTypes />

An element + a compound points to single replacement; two ionic compounds point to double replacement; a fuel + O₂ is combustion.

</Step>

<Step title="Step 2 — check the driving force" kind="explain">

A reaction only proceeds if something *drives* it forward — a reason for the products to be more stable than the reactants. The classic driving forces:

- A **precipitate** forms (an insoluble solid leaves the solution).
- A **gas** is released.
- **Water** (or another weak electrolyte) forms.
- A **more reactive element** displaces a less reactive one.

No driving force? No reaction — the ions just stay as they are.

</Step>

<Step title="Step 3 — apply your tools" kind="interactive">

For single replacement, the **activity series** decides whether the swap happens at all.

<ActivitySeries />

For double replacement, the **solubility rules** tell you whether a precipitate forms.

<PrecipitationLab />

</Step>

<Step title="A worked prediction" kind="explain">

*Will zinc metal react with copper(II) sulfate solution? If so, what forms?*

1. **Type:** element (Zn) + compound (CuSO₄) → single replacement.
2. **Driving force:** zinc is *above* copper in the activity series, so it can displace it. Yes, it reacts.
3. **Products:** zinc takes copper's place → ZnSO₄ + Cu.

<Formula tex="\text{Zn} + \text{CuSO}_4 \rightarrow \text{ZnSO}_4 + \text{Cu}" block />

<Callout type="info" title="The thread through this world">
Notice how every tool fed into one prediction. Classification narrowed the possibilities; the activity series and solubility rules confirmed the driving force and the products. This is how a chemist reasons. Next we'll look at the *conditions* reactions happen under — gases, solutions, energy, and speed.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- Predict reactions with three questions: **what type? will it happen? what products?**
- The **type** sets the product shape; a **driving force** (precipitate, gas, water, displacement) decides if it proceeds.
- Use the **activity series** for single replacement and **solubility rules** for double replacement.
- Combining these tools turns chemistry from observation into **prediction**.

</Step>
