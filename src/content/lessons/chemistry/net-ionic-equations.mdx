<Step title="Who actually reacts?" kind="explain">

In solution, ionic compounds split into free ions. When two solutions mix, often only *some* of those ions actually do anything — the rest just drift around unchanged. A **net ionic equation** strips the reaction down to the ions that genuinely react.

The bystanders are called **spectator ions**: present before and after, but unchanged.

</Step>

<Step title="Spot the spectators" kind="interactive">

Mix solutions and notice which ions combine into a precipitate (the real reaction) and which stay floating (spectators).

</Step>

<Step title="Three levels of equation" kind="explain">

The same reaction can be written three ways, each more focused:

1. **Molecular:** AgNO₃ + NaCl → AgCl + NaNO₃
2. **Full ionic** (everything soluble split into ions): Ag⁺ + NO₃⁻ + Na⁺ + Cl⁻ → AgCl(s) + Na⁺ + NO₃⁻
3. **Net ionic** (cancel the spectators): **Ag⁺(aq) + Cl⁻(aq) → AgCl(s)**

The net ionic equation shows the *real chemistry* — silver meeting chloride — without the Na⁺ and NO₃⁻ that never changed.

</Step>

<Step title="How to write one" kind="explain">

1. Write the balanced **molecular** equation.
2. Split all **soluble** ionic compounds (aq) into their ions; leave solids (s), liquids, and gases intact.
3. **Cancel** ions that appear identically on both sides (the spectators).
4. What remains is the **net ionic equation**.

<Callout type="tip" title="Why bother?">
The net ionic equation reveals that wildly different starting materials can drive the *same* core reaction. Any soluble silver salt plus any soluble chloride gives the same net ionic equation — Ag⁺ + Cl⁻ → AgCl. It's chemistry's way of seeing the essential reaction beneath the surface.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- A **net ionic equation** shows only the ions that actually react, dropping the **spectator ions**.
- Three forms: **molecular → full ionic → net ionic** (cancel spectators).
- Write it by splitting soluble (aq) compounds into ions, keeping solids/gases whole, then cancelling.
- It reveals the **essential reaction** shared by many different starting materials.

</Step>
