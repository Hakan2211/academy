<Step title="Why is anything solid, liquid, or gas?" kind="explain">

Here's a question that ties this world to the bonding world: at room temperature, why is oxygen a gas, water a liquid, and salt a solid? The answer is a tug-of-war between two things:

- **Thermal energy** — particle motion, trying to fling them apart.
- **Intermolecular forces** — attractions, trying to hold them together.

Strong attractions win → solid. Weak attractions → gas. In between → liquid. The state of any substance is just *which side is winning at that temperature.*

</Step>

Recall how the states differ only in how tightly particles are held and how much they move.

<Step title="The strength ladder" kind="explain">

The intermolecular forces (from the bonding world), weakest to strongest:

- **London dispersion** — present in everything; stronger for bigger molecules.
- **Dipole–dipole** — between polar molecules.
- **Hydrogen bonding** — the strong one, when H bonds to N, O, or F.

The stronger a substance's forces, the more thermal energy is needed to pull its particles apart — so the **higher its melting and boiling points**.

</Step>

<Step title="Polarity sets the forces" kind="interactive">

And polarity (which comes from shape and electronegativity) is what determines those forces in the first place.

<Polarity />

A non-polar molecule has only weak dispersion forces, so it's often a gas (O₂, CH₄). A polar, hydrogen-bonding molecule like water clings hard to its neighbours — so it's a liquid at room temperature, with an unusually high boiling point for its size.

</Step>

<Step title="The full chain, states edition" kind="explain">

Put it all together and you can explain the state of almost any substance from its structure:

- **Salt (NaCl):** giant ionic lattice, enormous electrostatic forces → **solid**, melts at 801 °C.
- **Water (H₂O):** small polar molecule with hydrogen bonding → **liquid**, boils at 100 °C.
- **Oxygen (O₂):** small non-polar molecule, only dispersion forces → **gas**, boils at −183 °C.

Same physics, three states — decided entirely by the strength of the forces between particles.

<Callout type="info" title="Where chemistry's first half lands">
This closes the loop from atoms → bonds → forces → the tangible states of matter you started with in World 1. From here, chemistry shifts from *what things are* to *what they do*: dissolving, reacting in acids and bases, releasing energy, and reaching equilibrium.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- A substance's **state** is a contest between **thermal energy** (apart) and **intermolecular forces** (together).
- Stronger forces → higher melting/boiling points → solid; weaker → gas.
- **Polarity** (shape + electronegativity) sets which forces a substance has.
- This links **atoms → bonds → forces → states**, closing the first half of chemistry.

</Step>
