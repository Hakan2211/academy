<Step title="Equilibrium pushes back" kind="explain">

Disturb a system at equilibrium and it responds. **Le Chatelier's principle** captures how:

> If you apply a change (a "stress") to a system at equilibrium, it shifts in the direction that **counteracts** the change.

Push it one way and it leans back the other way to partly undo what you did. It's chemistry's version of a stubborn balance.

</Step>

<Step title="Apply some stresses" kind="interactive">

Add reactant, add product, heat, or cool — and watch the equilibrium shift to oppose your change.

</Step>

<Step title="The three main stresses" kind="explain">

- **Concentration:** add a substance → the system shifts *away* from it (consuming it). Remove one → it shifts *toward* that side to replace it.
- **Temperature:** heat shifts equilibrium in the **endothermic** direction (absorbing the added heat); cooling shifts it the **exothermic** way. This one actually changes the equilibrium constant.
- **Pressure (gases):** increasing pressure shifts toward the side with **fewer gas molecules**.

In each case, the system moves to **partly cancel** the disturbance.

</Step>

<Step title="Why it matters: the Haber process" kind="explain">

Le Chatelier turns equilibrium into a tool for maximising yield. The **Haber process** (N₂ + 3H₂ ⇌ 2NH₃) makes the ammonia that fertilises the world. Engineers tune the conditions:

- **High pressure** — shifts toward the fewer gas molecules (2 NH₃ vs 4 reactant molecules) → more ammonia.
- **Lower temperature** favours the exothermic forward reaction (more ammonia), but too low is too slow — so a **compromise temperature** plus a catalyst balances yield against speed.

<Callout type="tip" title="Yield vs rate, balanced">
Le Chatelier maximises *how much* product you can get; kinetics governs *how fast*. Real industrial processes are always a careful compromise between the two — the deep lesson of this whole world.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Le Chatelier's principle**: a system at equilibrium shifts to **counteract** any applied change.
- **Add** a substance → shift away from it; **remove** → shift toward it.
- **Heat** → shifts endothermic direction; **pressure ↑** → shifts toward fewer gas molecules.
- It's used to maximise industrial yields (e.g. the **Haber process**), always balanced against rate.
