<Step title="Putting the toolkit together" kind="explain">

You've built every tool needed to answer chemistry's most practical question: *how much?* This capstone assembles them into one workflow.

The full chain of a real problem is:

**balance the equation → convert grams to moles → apply the mole ratio → handle the limiting reactant → convert back to grams → compare to actual yield.**

Master this and you can predict the outcome of any reaction quantitatively.

</Step>

<Step title="Step 1 — balance first, always" kind="interactive">

Every calculation rides on a correctly balanced equation, because the coefficients *are* the mole ratios. Get this wrong and everything downstream is wrong.

<Step title="A worked example" kind="explain">

*How much ammonia forms from 28 g of nitrogen (N₂) with excess hydrogen?* (N₂ + 3H₂ → 2NH₃; molar masses N₂ = 28, NH₃ = 17.)

1. **Balance:** done — coefficients 1, 3, 2.
2. **Grams → moles of N₂:** 28 g ÷ 28 g/mol = **1 mol N₂**.
3. **Mole ratio N₂ → NH₃:** 1 mol × (2/1) = **2 mol NH₃**.
4. **Moles → grams:** 2 mol × 17 g/mol = **34 g NH₃** (theoretical yield).
5. If you actually collected 27.2 g, the **percent yield** = 27.2 ÷ 34 × 100 = **80%**.

Grams in, grams out — with moles and the mole ratio as the bridge.

</Step>

<Step title="Why this is the heart of chemistry" kind="explain">

This arithmetic is what turns chemistry from description into **prediction and control**. It tells a pharmacist how much drug a batch yields, a rocket engineer the exact fuel-to-oxidiser mix, and a steelmaker how much ore to charge.

<Callout type="info" title="From counting to changing">
You can now quantify *how much* reacts. Next we turn to *what kinds* of reactions happen and how to predict their products — and later, how fast they go and how much energy they release. The mole stays with you through all of it.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- The full workflow: **balance → grams to moles → mole ratio → limiting reactant → moles to grams → % yield.**
- The balanced equation's **coefficients are the mole ratios** — everything depends on them.
- The **mole** is always the middle step between measurable grams.
- This arithmetic turns chemistry into **prediction and control** — the foundation for every quantitative result.
