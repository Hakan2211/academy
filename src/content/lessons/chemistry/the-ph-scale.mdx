<Step title="Measuring acidity" kind="explain">

The **pH scale** puts a number on how acidic or basic a solution is, from **0 to 14**:

- **0–6** → acidic (lower = stronger acid)
- **7** → neutral (pure water)
- **8–14** → basic / alkaline (higher = stronger base)

pH is really a measure of the **hydrogen-ion concentration [H⁺]**. More H⁺ means lower pH (more acidic).

</Step>

<Step title="It's logarithmic" kind="explain">

The crucial subtlety: pH is a **logarithmic** scale. Each step of 1 is a **tenfold** change in [H⁺]:

<Formula tex="\text{pH} = -\log_{10}[\text{H}^+]" block />

So pH 3 is **ten times** more acidic than pH 4, and **a hundred times** more acidic than pH 5. Small pH differences mean big changes in acidity — which is why your blood is defended so tightly around pH 7.4.

</Step>

<Step title="H⁺ and OH⁻ are linked" kind="explain">

In any water solution, [H⁺] and [OH⁻] are tied together — as one rises, the other falls, and their product is constant. That's why a single number (pH) captures the whole story:

- Acidic: lots of H⁺, little OH⁻.
- Neutral: equal amounts.
- Basic: little H⁺, lots of OH⁻.

<Callout type="info" title="Beyond the ends">
Very concentrated strong acids and bases can actually go below 0 or above 14 — the 0–14 range is just where everyday solutions sit, not a hard wall.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **pH** runs 0–14: below 7 acidic, 7 neutral, above 7 basic — it measures **[H⁺]**.
- It is **logarithmic**: pH = −log[H⁺], so each unit is a **10×** change in acidity.
- [H⁺] and [OH⁻] are linked — one number captures both.
- Small pH shifts mean large changes, which is why biological pH is tightly controlled.

</Step>
