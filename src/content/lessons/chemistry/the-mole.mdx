<Step title="Counting the uncountable" kind="explain">

Atoms are absurdly tiny — a single drop of water holds trillions of trillions of them. Chemists needed a way to count such enormous numbers, so they invented a counting unit, just like a "dozen" means 12.

The chemist's unit is the **mole**. One mole is **6.022 × 10²³** particles — a number so big it's called **Avogadro's number**. A mole of marbles would bury the Earth kilometres deep.

</Step>

<Step title="One mole, by the numbers" kind="interactive">

The mole's real magic: one mole of a substance weighs its **molar mass in grams** and always contains Avogadro's number of particles. Explore different substances.

<MoleConcept />

</Step>

<Step title="Counting by weighing" kind="explain">

You can't count atoms one by one — but you *can* weigh them. That's the entire point of the mole. Because one mole of carbon is exactly 12 g, weighing out 12 g of carbon means you've counted out 6.022 × 10²³ atoms without counting a thing.

This is the bridge that makes quantitative chemistry possible:

<Formula tex="\text{moles} = \dfrac{\text{mass (g)}}{\text{molar mass (g/mol)}}" block />

</Step>

<Step title="Why this exact number?" kind="explain">

Avogadro's number isn't arbitrary — it's defined so that **atomic mass units line up with grams**. A carbon-12 atom has a mass of 12 u; a *mole* of them has a mass of 12 g. The mole is the conversion factor between the invisible atomic world (u) and the lab world (g).

<Callout type="info" title="A mole of anything">
A mole always means 6.022 × 10²³ particles — but of *what*? Of atoms for an element, molecules for a covalent compound, formula units for an ionic one. Always state what you're counting.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- A **mole** = 6.022 × 10²³ particles (**Avogadro's number**) — chemistry's counting unit.
- One mole of a substance weighs its **molar mass in grams**, so we **count by weighing**.
- **moles = mass ÷ molar mass** is the key bridge between grams and particles.
- The number is chosen so one atom's mass in **u** equals one mole's mass in **g**.

</Step>
