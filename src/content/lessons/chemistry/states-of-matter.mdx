<Step title="Solid, liquid, gas" kind="explain">

The same substance can show up in strikingly different forms. Water can be hard ice, flowing liquid, or invisible steam — yet it's the same molecule, H₂O, the whole time.

These forms are the **states of matter**. What separates them isn't *what* the particles are, but *how they're arranged and how fast they move*:

- **Solid** — particles packed tightly in a fixed pattern, only vibrating. Fixed shape and volume.
- **Liquid** — particles still touching but free to slide past each other. Fixed volume, takes the shape of its container.
- **Gas** — particles far apart, flying freely at high speed. Fills any container completely.

</Step>

<Step title="Watch the particles" kind="interactive">

Switch between the three states and watch what the particles do. Everything about a state — whether it flows, whether you can compress it, whether it holds a shape — comes straight from this particle behaviour.

<StatesOfMatter />

Notice that the particles never disappear or change. Only their **spacing and energy** change.

</Step>

<Step title="Changing state" kind="explain">

Add or remove energy (usually heat) and a substance jumps between states. Each transition has a name:

- **Melting** (solid → liquid) and **freezing** (liquid → solid)
- **Boiling / evaporation** (liquid → gas) and **condensation** (gas → liquid)
- **Sublimation** (solid → gas directly, like dry ice) and **deposition** (gas → solid, like frost)

<Callout type="info" title="Energy goes into spacing, not heating">
While ice is melting, its temperature stays at 0 °C even though you're adding heat. That energy isn't raising the temperature — it's pulling the particles apart from their rigid lattice. Temperature only climbs again once melting is complete.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="A fourth state" kind="explain">

Heat a gas hot enough and its atoms start losing electrons, becoming a glowing, electrically charged soup called **plasma** — the stuff of stars, lightning, and neon signs. It's actually the most common state of matter in the universe, even though it's rare on Earth's surface.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- The **states of matter** — solid, liquid, gas (and plasma) — differ in particle **arrangement and energy**, not in what the particles are.
- **Solids** hold shape; **liquids** flow but keep volume; **gases** fill any space.
- Adding or removing **energy** drives changes of state (melting, boiling, condensation…).
- During a change of state, energy goes into rearranging particles, so the temperature pauses.

</Step>
