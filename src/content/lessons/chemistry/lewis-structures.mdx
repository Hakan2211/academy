<Step title="Drawing the electrons" kind="explain">

A **Lewis structure** is a map of every valence electron in a molecule. The rules are simple:

- A **line** between two atoms = a shared (bonding) pair — a covalent bond.
- A pair of **dots** on an atom = a **lone pair** (electrons not used in bonding).
- Every atom should end up surrounded by eight electrons (an **octet**) — except hydrogen, which is happy with two.

</Step>

<Step title="How to build one" kind="explain">

The recipe for a Lewis structure:

1. **Count** all the valence electrons from every atom.
2. Put the least electronegative atom in the **centre** (often carbon); arrange the others around it.
3. Draw a **single bond** from the centre to each outer atom.
4. **Distribute** the remaining electrons as lone pairs to complete octets.
5. If the centre is short of eight, **make double or triple bonds** by sharing lone pairs.

</Step>

<Step title="Why lone pairs matter" kind="explain">

Lone pairs aren't just bookkeeping — they take up space and push the bonds around, which sets the molecule's 3D **shape** (the next lesson). They also make atoms reactive: a lone pair is exactly what a molecule offers when it grabs onto an acid's proton.

<Callout type="tip" title="The octet has exceptions">
Hydrogen wants only 2 electrons. A few atoms (like boron) settle for fewer than 8, and elements from period 3 onward (like sulfur and phosphorus) can hold *more* than 8 by using their d-orbitals. The octet is a powerful guide, not an unbreakable law.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- A **Lewis structure** shows every valence electron: **lines** are bonding pairs, **dots** are lone pairs.
- Aim for an **octet** around each atom (hydrogen: 2).
- Build by counting valence electrons, placing a central atom, bonding, then filling octets (adding multiple bonds if needed).
- **Lone pairs** drive both molecular **shape** and reactivity.

</Step>
