<Step title="Measuring heat directly" kind="explain">

You can't see energy, but you can measure the **temperature change** it causes. **Calorimetry** does exactly that: run a reaction in (or under) water and measure how much the water's temperature changes. From that you calculate the heat transferred.

The key equation:

<Formula tex="q = m \, c \, \Delta T" block />

where **q** is heat (J), **m** is mass of water (g), **c** is its specific heat capacity, and **ΔT** is the temperature change.

</Step>

</Step>

<Step title="Specific heat capacity" kind="explain">

The **specific heat capacity (c)** is how much energy it takes to raise 1 gram of a substance by 1 °C. Water's is unusually high — **4.18 J/g·°C** — meaning it soaks up a lot of energy for a small temperature rise.

That high value is why water is the standard calorimetry fluid, why oceans moderate climate, and why your body uses water to buffer its temperature.

</Step>

<Step title="From q to ΔH" kind="explain">

The heat absorbed by the water equals the heat *released* by the reaction (with opposite sign). Divide by the moles reacting and you get the molar enthalpy change:

<Formula tex="\Delta H = \dfrac{-q}{\text{moles of reactant}}" block />

The minus sign flips perspective: heat leaving the reaction (exothermic) *enters* the water, so a temperature *rise* in the water means a *negative* ΔH for the reaction.

<Callout type="tip" title="Simple but powerful">
A foam cup, a thermometer, and this one equation are enough to measure the energy of combustion, neutralization, or dissolving — turning an invisible energy change into a number you can read off a thermometer.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Calorimetry** finds reaction heat from a **temperature change**: **q = mcΔT**.
- **Specific heat capacity (c)** is energy per gram per °C; water's is high (4.18 J/g·°C).
- The water's heat gain equals the reaction's heat loss → **ΔH = −q ÷ moles**.
- A cup, thermometer, and one equation measure invisible energy changes.

</Step>
