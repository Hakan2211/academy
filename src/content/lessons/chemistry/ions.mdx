<Step title="Atoms that gained or lost electrons" kind="explain">

A neutral atom has equal protons and electrons. But atoms chase a full outer shell — and the easiest route is often to **gain or lose** a few electrons. When they do, the proton/electron balance breaks and the atom becomes a charged **ion**:

- Lose electrons → fewer negatives → **positive ion (cation)**.
- Gain electrons → more negatives → **negative ion (anion)**.

The number of protons never changes, so it's still the same element — just charged.

</Step>

<Step title="Metals give, non-metals take" kind="explain">

A clear pattern emerges:

- **Metals** have few valence electrons, so it's easiest to **lose** them → they form **cations** (Na⁺, Mg²⁺, Al³⁺).
- **Non-metals** are close to a full shell, so they **gain** electrons → they form **anions** (Cl⁻, O²⁻, N³⁻).

The charge tells you how many electrons moved: Mg²⁺ lost two; O²⁻ gained two. In each case the ion ends up with the same stable electron arrangement as a **noble gas**.

</Step>

<Step title="Why ions matter" kind="explain">

Ions are everywhere and they do real work:

- Opposite ions attract, snapping together into **ionic compounds** (next world) — that's how table salt, Na⁺ and Cl⁻, forms.
- Dissolved ions carry electricity — the salts in your nerves fire signals; batteries shuttle ions to deliver current.

<Callout type="tip" title="Polyatomic ions">
Some ions are whole groups of bonded atoms carrying a charge — like sulfate (SO₄²⁻) or ammonium (NH₄⁺). They behave as a single charged unit in reactions.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- An **ion** is an atom that has gained or lost electrons, giving it a net charge — same protons, same element.
- Lose electrons → **cation** (+); gain electrons → **anion** (−).
- **Metals** lose to form cations; **non-metals** gain to form anions — each reaching a noble-gas arrangement.
- Ions form **ionic compounds** and carry **electric current** in solution and in cells.

</Step>
