<Step title="The bond accountant" kind="explain">

Where does ΔH actually *come from*? From bonds. Every reaction is a transaction:

- **Breaking** bonds always **costs** energy (you must pull atoms apart) — energy *in*.
- **Forming** bonds always **releases** energy (atoms snap to a stable arrangement) — energy *out*.

The reaction's enthalpy change is just the balance of this account:

<Formula tex="\Delta H = (\text{energy to break bonds}) - (\text{energy released forming bonds})" block />

</Step>

<Step title="Tally the bonds" kind="interactive">

Compare the energy spent breaking reactant bonds with the energy released forming product bonds. The net is ΔH.

<BondEnergy />

</Step>

<Step title="The sign falls out naturally" kind="explain">

The bond accounting explains the signs from the last lesson:

- If **forming releases more** than breaking costs → net energy out → **exothermic** (ΔH negative). The products have stronger, more stable bonds.
- If **breaking costs more** than forming releases → net energy in → **endothermic** (ΔH positive). The products have weaker bonds.

Combustion is so exothermic because the bonds in CO₂ and H₂O are much stronger (lower energy) than those in the fuel and O₂.

</Step>

<Step title="Bond energy values" kind="explain">

Each type of bond has a characteristic **bond energy** (kJ/mol) — the energy to break one mole of it. Stronger bonds (like the triple bond in N₂, 945 kJ/mol) take far more energy to break than weak ones.

Using tabulated bond energies, you can *estimate* a reaction's ΔH before ever doing it: add up the bonds broken, subtract the bonds formed.

<Callout type="info" title="An estimate, not exact">
Bond energies are averages over many molecules, so this method gives a close estimate, not the exact ΔH. For precise values, chemists measure heat directly (calorimetry, next) or use tabulated enthalpies of formation.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Breaking** bonds costs energy (in); **forming** bonds releases it (out).
- **ΔH = energy to break − energy released forming**.
- Forming releases more → **exothermic**; breaking costs more → **endothermic**.
- **Bond energies** (kJ/mol) let you estimate ΔH; exact values come from calorimetry.

</Step>
