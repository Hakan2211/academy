<Step title="Two ways for matter to change" kind="explain">

When something changes, ask one question: **are new substances made?**

- A **physical change** rearranges matter without creating anything new. The molecules stay the same — you've just changed their state, shape, or spacing. Melting, boiling, dissolving, cutting, and crushing are all physical.
- A **chemical change** turns substances into *different* substances. Bonds between atoms break and re-form, producing materials with new properties. Burning, rusting, cooking, and digesting are chemical.

</Step>

</Step>

<Step title="The tell-tale signs" kind="explain">

You usually can't see molecules, so chemists watch for clues that a **chemical** change has happened:

- A **colour change** that isn't just mixing paints
- **Bubbles** of a new gas forming (not just boiling)
- A **solid forming** when two liquids mix (a precipitate)
- **Heat or light** released or absorbed
- A change that is **hard to reverse**

<Callout type="warn" title="Clues, not proof">
Any single sign can be misleading — boiling water bubbles but is physical; mixing warm and cold water changes temperature but is physical. The only sure test is whether a **new substance with new properties** now exists.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Reversibility" kind="explain">

Physical changes are usually easy to undo: freeze the melted water, evaporate the dissolved salt. The original is right there, just rearranged.

Chemical changes are usually hard to reverse, because you'd have to break the new bonds and rebuild the old ones. You can't easily turn ash and smoke back into wood. (Some chemical changes *can* be reversed by another reaction — but it takes a second chemical change, not just cooling down.)

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- A **physical change** rearranges matter but makes **no new substance** (melting, dissolving, crushing).
- A **chemical change** makes **new substances** by breaking and forming bonds (burning, rusting, cooking).
- Watch for clues — colour, gas, a new solid, heat/light, irreversibility — but the real test is a **new substance**.
- Physical changes are usually easy to reverse; chemical changes usually are not.

</Step>
