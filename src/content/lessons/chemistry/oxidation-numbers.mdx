<Step title="Tracking electrons with a number" kind="explain">

In ionic reactions, electron transfer is obvious. But in covalent compounds, where electrons are *shared*, how do we tell what's oxidised? The answer is the **oxidation number** — a bookkeeping charge each atom *would* have if every bond were fully ionic.

Track how an atom's oxidation number changes across a reaction and you can spot redox even when no obvious ions appear.

</Step>

<Step title="Assign the numbers" kind="interactive">

</Step>

<Step title="The rules" kind="explain">

A short set of rules assigns them:

- A **free element** (O₂, Cu) is **0**.
- A **monatomic ion** equals its charge (Na⁺ is +1, Cl⁻ is −1).
- **Oxygen** is usually **−2**; **hydrogen** usually **+1**.
- **Group 1** = +1, **group 2** = +2, fluorine = −1.
- The numbers **sum to the overall charge** (0 for a neutral compound).

Apply them in order and the unknown atom's number falls out — that's how we get S = +6 in H₂SO₄.

</Step>

<Step title="Redox in terms of numbers" kind="explain">

Now oxidation and reduction get a precise definition:

- **Oxidation** = oxidation number **increases** (more positive — lost electrons).
- **Reduction** = oxidation number **decreases** (more negative — gained electrons).

If any atom's oxidation number changes during a reaction, it's a redox reaction. If none change, it isn't.

<Callout type="info" title="A universal detector">
Oxidation numbers let you identify redox in *any* reaction — even messy organic ones with no obvious ions. Find the atom whose number went up (oxidised) and the one whose went down (reduced), and you've found the electron transfer.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- An **oxidation number** is a bookkeeping charge — what an atom would have if all bonds were ionic.
- Assign with rules (free element 0, O −2, H +1, group charges) so the total equals the species' charge.
- **Oxidation** = number goes **up**; **reduction** = number goes **down**.
- A change in any oxidation number flags a **redox** reaction — even in covalent or organic cases.

</Step>
