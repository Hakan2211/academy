<Step title="Two families you meet daily" kind="explain">

Two functional groups deserve a closer look because they're everywhere:

- **Alcohols** carry the **–OH** group. Ethanol is the alcohol in drinks, an antiseptic, and a biofuel.
- **Carboxylic acids** carry the **–COOH** group. Acetic acid is what makes vinegar sour.

They're also chemically linked: oxidising an alcohol can turn it into a carboxylic acid.

</Step>

<Step title="Alcohols" kind="explain">

The **–OH** group makes alcohols polar, so small ones (like ethanol) mix freely with water and make good solvents. Alcohols:

- **Burn** cleanly — a reason ethanol is a fuel.
- Can be **oxidised** to carboxylic acids (it's why wine left open turns to vinegar — ethanol oxidises to acetic acid).

Naming uses the **-ol** suffix: methanol, ethanol, propanol.

</Step>

<Step title="Carboxylic acids and esters" kind="explain">

The **–COOH** group is acidic — it donates an H⁺, so carboxylic acids turn litmus red and react with bases. Named with the **-oic acid** suffix (ethanoic acid = acetic acid).

React a carboxylic acid with an alcohol and you make an **ester** (plus water) — a reaction called esterification. Esters smell sweet and fruity, and they're the basis of flavourings, perfumes, and (as long-chain versions) fats and oils.

<Callout type="info" title="A chain of transformations">
Alcohol → (oxidise) → carboxylic acid → (+ alcohol) → ester. This little network of interconversions is organic chemistry in miniature: functional groups transforming into one another, each step predictable from the groups involved.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Alcohols** (–OH, -ol) are polar, burn cleanly, and can be **oxidised** to carboxylic acids.
- **Carboxylic acids** (–COOH, -oic acid) are acidic — they donate H⁺ and react with bases.
- Acid + alcohol → **ester** (esterification): the source of fruity smells, flavourings, and fats.
- These families **interconvert**, a small map of organic transformations.

</Step>
