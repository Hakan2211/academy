<Step title="Sharing, not transferring" kind="explain">

When two **non-metals** meet, neither wants to give up electrons — both want *more*. So they compromise: they **share** a pair of electrons, and that shared pair counts toward a full outer shell for *both* atoms. A shared pair is a **covalent bond**.

The shared electrons are attracted to both nuclei at once, which is what holds the atoms together.

</Step>

<Step title="Single, double, triple" kind="interactive">

Atoms can share more than one pair. Toggle between molecules and count the shared pairs in the overlap.

<CovalentBond />

More shared pairs means a **stronger, shorter** bond. A triple bond (like in N₂) is tougher to break than a double, which beats a single.

</Step>

<Step title="Molecules, not lattices" kind="explain">

Covalent bonding usually makes discrete **molecules** — small, definite groups of atoms like H₂O or CO₂. The bond *inside* a molecule is strong, but the attraction *between* separate molecules is weak.

That's why many covalent substances are **gases or liquids** at room temperature (water, oxygen, methane) and have **low melting points** — you only need to overcome the weak forces between molecules, not the strong bonds within them.

</Step>

<Step title="Giant covalent exceptions" kind="explain">

A few covalent substances bond into endless networks instead of small molecules — **giant covalent structures**. Diamond and silicon dioxide (sand, quartz) are entire crystals laced together by covalent bonds.

Because you'd have to break strong covalent bonds throughout, these are extraordinarily hard with very high melting points — diamond is the hardest natural material there is.

<Callout type="tip" title="Don't conduct (usually)">
Most covalent substances don't conduct electricity — their electrons are tied up in bonds, with no free charges to move. (Graphite is the famous exception: each carbon bonds to only three others, leaving free electrons to carry current.)
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Covalent bonding**: two non-metals **share** electron pairs so both reach a full shell.
- Shared pairs can be **single, double, or triple** — more pairs mean stronger, shorter bonds.
- Usually forms discrete **molecules** with weak forces between them → low melting points, often gases/liquids.
- **Giant covalent** networks (diamond, quartz) are exceptions: extremely hard, very high melting points.

</Step>
