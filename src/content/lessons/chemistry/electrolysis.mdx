<Lesson>

<Step title="Running a reaction backwards" kind="explain">

A galvanic cell uses a *spontaneous* reaction to make electricity. **Electrolysis** does the reverse: it uses **electricity to force a non-spontaneous reaction** to happen. Energy goes *in* instead of coming out.

It's how we split stable compounds that would never break apart on their own — like tearing water into hydrogen and oxygen, or molten salt into sodium metal and chlorine gas.

</Step>

<Step title="Ions on the move" kind="interactive">

A power supply charges the electrodes. Positive ions are pulled to the negative cathode; negative ions to the positive anode, where they react.

<ElectrolysisCell />

</Step>

<Step title="The electrodes swap roles" kind="explain">

The definitions of anode and cathode still hold (oxidation at the anode, reduction at the cathode), but the **charges flip** compared with a galvanic cell, because the power supply is now in control:

- **Cathode** is **negative** (the supply pushes electrons onto it) → positive **cations** go there and are **reduced**.
- **Anode** is **positive** → negative **anions** go there and are **oxidised**.

So at the cathode you often deposit a metal or release hydrogen; at the anode you release a non-metal like oxygen or chlorine.

</Step>

<Step title="Why electrolysis is essential" kind="explain">

It does jobs nothing else can:

- **Extracting reactive metals** — aluminium is won from its ore by electrolysis (it's too reactive to displace cheaply).
- **Electroplating** — coating objects with a thin layer of metal (chrome, silver, gold).
- **Purifying copper** for wiring.
- **Producing chlorine, hydrogen, and sodium hydroxide** industrially.

<Callout type="info" title="Galvanic vs electrolytic, mirrored">
A galvanic cell *releases* energy from a spontaneous reaction; an electrolytic cell *consumes* energy to drive a non-spontaneous one. They're mirror images — and a rechargeable battery actually switches between the two as it discharges and charges.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Electrolysis** uses **electricity to force a non-spontaneous** redox reaction (energy in).
- The power supply makes the **cathode negative** (cations reduced there) and the **anode positive** (anions oxidised).
- Uses: extracting reactive metals (aluminium), electroplating, purifying copper, making chlorine/hydrogen.
- It's the mirror image of a galvanic cell — a rechargeable battery does both.

</Step>
