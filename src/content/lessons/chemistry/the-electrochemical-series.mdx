<Step title="Ranking the urge to lose electrons" kind="explain">

This capstone ties the whole world together with one ranking. The **electrochemical series** lists species by their tendency to gain or lose electrons — measured as a **standard electrode potential (E°)** in volts.

It's the quantitative version of the activity series you met in the reactions world: the more eagerly a metal gives up electrons (is oxidised), the more negative its potential, and the higher it sits as a reducing agent.

</Step>

<Step title="The reactivity ranking, revisited" kind="interactive">

You've seen this ordering before — a more reactive metal displaces a less reactive one. The electrochemical series is the same idea with numbers attached.

</Step>

<Step title="Predicting cell voltage" kind="interactive">

The series predicts how any two half-cells combine — and the voltage they produce.

<GalvanicCell />

The cell voltage is the **difference** between the two electrode potentials:

<Formula tex="E^\circ_{\text{cell}} = E^\circ_{\text{cathode}} - E^\circ_{\text{anode}}" block />

The bigger the gap between the two metals in the series, the larger the voltage. Zinc and copper sit far apart, giving 1.10 V.

</Step>

<Step title="What the series predicts" kind="explain">

This single ranking answers a remarkable range of questions:

- **Which reactions are spontaneous** — a more reactive species reduces a less reactive one (positive E°_cell).
- **Cell voltages** — from the difference in potentials.
- **Displacement** — a metal displaces any ion below it.
- **Electrolysis products** — which ions react preferentially at each electrode.

<Callout type="info" title="Where chemistry's reactivity story lands">
From the periodic table's trends, to the activity series, to electrode potentials — it's all one thread: how willingly atoms give up or grab electrons. Electrochemistry turns that willingness into measurable volts, and into the batteries that power the modern world. Next, we leave the inorganic world for the chemistry of carbon.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- The **electrochemical series** ranks species by **standard electrode potential (E°)** — their tendency to gain/lose electrons.
- It's the quantitative form of the **activity series**.
- **E°cell = E°cathode − E°anode**; a bigger gap means a bigger voltage.
- It predicts spontaneity, voltages, displacement, and electrolysis products — the unifying thread of reactivity.

</Step>
