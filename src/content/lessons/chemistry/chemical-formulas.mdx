<Step title="A recipe in symbols" kind="explain">

A **chemical formula** tells you exactly which atoms are in a substance and how many of each. It's a compact recipe:

- The **symbols** name the elements (H, O, Na…).
- The **subscripts** (small numbers) say how many of each atom. H₂O means two hydrogen, one oxygen.
- **Parentheses** group atoms; the subscript after them multiplies everything inside. Ca(OH)₂ means one calcium and *two* OH units — so 2 oxygen and 2 hydrogen.

</Step>

<Step title="Subscripts vs coefficients" kind="explain">

Two kinds of numbers appear in chemistry, and mixing them up is a classic mistake:

- A **subscript** is *inside* a formula and changes the substance. H₂O (water) and H₂O₂ (hydrogen peroxide) are completely different chemicals.
- A **coefficient** sits *in front* of a formula and just counts how many copies. 2 H₂O means two water molecules — same substance, more of it.

You can never change a subscript to balance a reaction (that would change what the substance *is*); you only ever change coefficients.

<Callout type="tip" title="Why ratios are fixed">
A compound's formula is fixed because its atoms are bonded in set ratios. Water is *always* 2 H to 1 O — never 1.5 to 1. This is the law of constant composition, and it's what makes a formula meaningful.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Reading ionic formulas" kind="explain">

For ionic compounds, the formula gives the **ratio** that makes the charges cancel. Calcium (Ca²⁺) and chloride (Cl⁻) combine as CaCl₂ — one +2 needs two −1 ions to balance. The formula is the smallest neutral ratio, not a molecule.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- A **formula** lists elements (symbols) and how many of each (**subscripts**); **parentheses** multiply whole groups.
- A **subscript** changes the substance; a **coefficient** (in front) just counts copies — never swap them.
- Compound formulas are **fixed ratios** (law of constant composition).
- Ionic formulas give the smallest **neutral ratio** of ions.

</Step>
