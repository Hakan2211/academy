<Step title="The universal solvent" kind="explain">

Water dissolves more substances than any other common liquid — so many that it's called the **universal solvent**. That power comes from one thing you've already met: water is a small, strongly **polar**, hydrogen-bonding molecule.

Its bent shape gives it a positive end (the hydrogens) and a negative end (the oxygen), so it can grab onto charged and polar particles and pull them apart.

</Step>

<Step title="Water's polarity, revisited" kind="interactive">

Water's lopsided charge is the secret to everything in this lesson.

<WaterMolecule />

</Step>

<Step title="Electrolytes: solutions that conduct" kind="interactive">

When an ionic compound dissolves, it splits into free-moving ions. A solution full of mobile ions can **carry electric current** — it's an **electrolyte**.

<DissolveLab />

- **Strong electrolytes** (table salt, strong acids) fully split into ions → conduct well.
- **Weak electrolytes** (weak acids) only partly split → conduct weakly.
- **Non-electrolytes** (sugar) dissolve without forming ions → don't conduct at all.

Pure water itself barely conducts — it's the dissolved ions that do the work.

</Step>

<Step title="Why electrolytes matter for life" kind="explain">

Your body runs on electrolytes. Dissolved Na⁺, K⁺, Ca²⁺, and Cl⁻ ions:

- Carry the **electrical signals** in your nerves and the contractions of your muscles (including your heartbeat).
- Balance the **water** moving in and out of cells (osmosis).
- Keep your blood at the right **pH**.

Lose too many through sweat and you cramp or worse — which is why sports drinks replace *electrolytes*, not just water.

</Step>

<Step title="Water, the molecule that enables chemistry" kind="explain">

Step back and see how much rides on water's polarity:

- It **dissolves** nutrients, salts, and gases so they can be transported and react.
- Its **hydrogen bonding** gives it a high boiling point, high heat capacity (stabilising climate and body temperature), and the oddity that **ice floats** (protecting life beneath frozen lakes).
- As an electrolyte solvent, it's the **medium** in which nearly all biological and many industrial reactions happen.

<Callout type="info" title="Where solutions lead">
Most reactions you'll meet from here happen *in solution* — acids and bases, precipitation, electrochemistry. Water is the stage on which the rest of chemistry plays out. Next up: one of the most important solution chemistries of all — acids and bases.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- Water is the **universal solvent** because it is small, polar, and hydrogen-bonding.
- Dissolved ions make a solution an **electrolyte** that conducts electricity (strong / weak / non-electrolytes).
- **Electrolytes** drive nerves, muscles, osmosis, and blood pH in living things.
- Water's polarity underlies dissolving, its unusual physical properties, and its role as the **medium** for most chemistry — including acids and bases, next.

</Step>
