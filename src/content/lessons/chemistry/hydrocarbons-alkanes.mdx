<Step title="The simplest organic molecules" kind="explain">

**Hydrocarbons** contain only two elements: **hydrogen and carbon**. They're the simplest organic compounds — and the world's main fuels, since crude oil is essentially a mixture of them.

The first family is the **alkanes**: hydrocarbons with only **single** bonds. They are **saturated** — each carbon holds the maximum possible hydrogen.

</Step>

<Step title="A homologous series" kind="explain">

The alkanes form a **homologous series** — a family following a pattern:

<Formula tex="\text{C}_n\text{H}_{2n+2}" block />

So methane is CH₄, ethane C₂H₆, propane C₃H₈, butane C₄H₁₀ — each differing by one CH₂. They share similar chemistry, with properties (boiling point, viscosity) changing smoothly as the chain lengthens.

The names use a stem for chain length (meth-, eth-, prop-, but-, pent-) plus the ending **-ane**.

</Step>

<Step title="Properties and uses" kind="explain">

Alkanes are fairly **unreactive** (those strong single bonds), with two important exceptions:

- They **burn** readily — combustion is what makes them fuels (methane in gas, propane in camping stoves, octane in petrol).
- Longer chains have higher boiling points, so crude oil is separated by **fractional distillation** into fuels of different chain lengths.

<Callout type="tip" title="Counting hydrogens">
The formula CₙH₂ₙ₊₂ is your quick check: a 5-carbon alkane has 2(5)+2 = 12 hydrogens, so pentane is C₅H₁₂. If a hydrocarbon has fewer hydrogens than this, it must contain a double or triple bond — the next lesson.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Hydrocarbons** contain only hydrogen and carbon; **alkanes** have only single bonds (**saturated**).
- Alkanes form a **homologous series**: **CₙH₂ₙ₊₂**, named by chain length + **-ane**.
- They're fairly unreactive but **burn** well — the world's main fuels.
- Crude oil is separated into fuels by **fractional distillation** (boiling point rises with chain length).

</Step>
