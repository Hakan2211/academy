<Lesson>

<Step title="Reactions that run both ways" kind="explain">

Many reactions are **reversible** — products can turn back into reactants. We write them with a double arrow:

<Formula tex="A \rightleftharpoons B" block />

In a closed container, the forward reaction starts fast (lots of A) and slows; the reverse starts at zero (no B) and speeds up. Eventually the two rates become **equal** — and the system reaches **dynamic equilibrium**.

</Step>

<Step title="Reach the balance" kind="interactive">

Watch the concentrations of A and B level off as forward and reverse rates equalise. (Ignore the stress buttons for now — just see it settle.)

<EquilibriumLab />

</Step>

<Step title="'Dynamic', not stopped" kind="explain">

The key word is **dynamic**. At equilibrium the concentrations stop changing — but the reactions have *not* stopped. Forward and reverse are both still running, just at the **same rate**, so every B made is matched by a B turning back to A.

It's like an escalator you walk down at the same speed it rises — you appear to stay put, but everything is still moving.

</Step>

<Step title="What equilibrium is and isn't" kind="explain">

At dynamic equilibrium:

- Concentrations are **constant** (but not necessarily equal — the ratio depends on the reaction).
- Forward rate **=** reverse rate.
- It only holds in a **closed system** (nothing escaping).

It does **not** mean the reaction is finished or that there are equal amounts of each — just that the opposing rates are balanced.

<Callout type="info" title="A position, not a wall">
Equilibrium can lie far to the right (mostly products) or far to the left (mostly reactants). Its *position* is what we can shift — the subject of Le Chatelier's principle, next.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- A **reversible** reaction (⇌) can run both ways; in a closed system it reaches **dynamic equilibrium**.
- At equilibrium, forward rate **=** reverse rate, so concentrations stay **constant** — but reactions keep running.
- Equilibrium doesn't mean equal amounts or a stopped reaction.
- Its **position** (toward products or reactants) can be shifted — Le Chatelier, next.

</Step>
