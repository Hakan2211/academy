<Step title="A model for moving particles" kind="explain">

The **kinetic molecular theory** explains gas behaviour with a simple particle model. Its key assumptions:

- Gas particles are **tiny** compared with the space between them (a gas is mostly empty).
- They move **constantly and randomly** in straight lines until they collide.
- Collisions are **elastic** — no kinetic energy is lost.
- There are **negligible forces** between particles.
- Average kinetic energy is **proportional to temperature** (in kelvin).

</Step>

<Step title="Temperature is motion" kind="interactive">

Raise the temperature and watch the particles speed up. Temperature *is* the average kinetic energy of the particles — heat is just faster jiggling.

<ThermalJiggle />

</Step>

<Step title="Explaining the gas laws" kind="explain">

This model explains *why* gases behave as they do:

- **Heat a gas** → particles move faster → hit the walls harder and more often → pressure (or volume) rises.
- **Shrink the volume** → particles hit the walls more often → pressure rises.
- **Add more gas** → more particles, more collisions → pressure rises.

Every gas law in the next lesson falls straight out of "faster or more crowded particles hit the walls more."

</Step>

<Step title="Real vs ideal" kind="explain">

A gas that obeys these assumptions perfectly is an **ideal gas**. Real gases come close at everyday conditions, but deviate at **very high pressure** or **very low temperature**, where particles are forced close enough that their volume and weak attractions start to matter.

<Callout type="info" title="Absolute zero">
If temperature is particle motion, what happens at 0 K (−273 °C)? In this model, motion would stop entirely — **absolute zero**, the coldest possible temperature. It can be approached but never quite reached.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Kinetic molecular theory** models gas as tiny, fast, randomly moving particles with elastic collisions and negligible forces.
- **Temperature** = average **kinetic energy** of the particles.
- The model explains the gas laws: faster or more crowded particles hit the walls harder/more often.
- An **ideal gas** obeys the model perfectly; real gases deviate at high pressure / low temperature.

</Step>
