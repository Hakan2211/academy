<Lesson>

<Step title="Why do some reactions just happen?" kind="explain">

Ice melts on a warm day; iron rusts; gases mix and never un-mix. These changes happen on their own — they're **spontaneous**. But here's the puzzle: not all spontaneous changes release energy. Ice melting *absorbs* heat, yet it still happens.

So energy (ΔH) can't be the whole story. There's a second driving force: **entropy**.

</Step>

<Step title="Entropy: nature's drive toward disorder" kind="interactive">

**Entropy (S)** measures the number of ways a system can be arranged — loosely, its **disorder** or spread of energy. Left alone, systems drift toward higher entropy, because disordered arrangements are overwhelmingly more likely.

<EntropyBox />

Gases spreading to fill a room, a drop of dye dispersing, heat flowing from hot to cold — all are entropy increasing. It almost never reverses on its own.

</Step>

<Step title="Two competing tendencies" kind="explain">

Nature is pulled by two things at once:

- Toward **lower energy** (favouring exothermic, negative ΔH).
- Toward **higher entropy** (favouring more disorder, positive ΔS).

Sometimes they agree, sometimes they fight. Melting ice is uphill in energy (ΔH positive) but strongly uphill in entropy too (liquid is more disordered than solid) — so above 0 °C, entropy wins and it melts.

</Step>

<Step title="Gibbs free energy: the deciding equation" kind="explain">

The two tendencies combine into one master quantity — the **Gibbs free energy**, ΔG:

<Formula tex="\Delta G = \Delta H - T\Delta S" block />

The rule is beautifully simple:

- **ΔG negative → spontaneous** (the reaction can proceed on its own).
- **ΔG positive → not spontaneous** (it needs a continuous push).

Notice the **temperature (T)** multiplying entropy: at high temperature the entropy term dominates, which is exactly why heating can drive reactions (like melting or decomposition) that are stuck when cold.

</Step>

<Step title="The deepest law in chemistry" kind="explain">

ΔG ties the whole world together. A reaction's ΔH (from bonds), its ΔS (order vs disorder), and the temperature together decide whether it goes — and how far.

<Callout type="info" title="Spontaneous ≠ fast">
A crucial caveat: ΔG tells you *whether* a reaction can happen, not *how fast*. Diamond turning to graphite is spontaneous (ΔG negative) — but so slow it takes billions of years. Speed is a separate question, governed by activation energy and kinetics — the very next world.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- A change is **spontaneous** if it happens on its own — driven by both energy *and* **entropy** (disorder).
- **Entropy (S)** rises naturally; systems drift toward more probable, disordered states.
- **Gibbs free energy** ΔG = ΔH − TΔS decides it: **negative ΔG → spontaneous**.
- **Temperature** weights the entropy term — heat can drive otherwise-stuck reactions. But ΔG governs *whether*, not *how fast* (next world: kinetics).

</Step>
