<Step title="Same formula, different molecule" kind="explain">

Here's something carbon makes possible: two molecules with the **identical molecular formula** but **different structures**. These are **isomers**.

Because the atoms are connected differently, isomers are genuinely different substances — with different shapes, boiling points, and sometimes completely different chemistry, despite sharing a formula.

</Step>

</Step>

<Step title="Why more carbons mean more isomers" kind="explain">

With short chains there's little choice, but as carbon chains grow, the number of ways to arrange them **explodes**:

- C₄H₁₀ has **2** isomers (straight or branched).
- C₅H₁₂ has **3**.
- C₁₀H₂₂ has **75**.
- C₃₀ has over **four billion**.

This combinatorial explosion — straight chains, branches, and rings — is a big reason carbon forms so many millions of compounds.

</Step>

<Step title="Structure decides properties" kind="explain">

Isomers remind us of a theme running through all chemistry: **structure determines properties**. A branched isomer packs differently from a straight one, so it has weaker intermolecular forces and a lower boiling point.

<Callout type="info" title="When shape is everything">
In biology, isomerism can be life-or-death. Many drugs have a "mirror-image" isomer that is useless or even harmful, while its twin heals — same formula, opposite effect. Your enzymes recognise one shape and reject the other. Structure is destiny.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Isomers** share a molecular formula but differ in **structure** — making them different substances.
- The number of isomers **explodes** with chain length (C₄ has 2, C₃₀ over four billion).
- **Structure determines properties** — branched isomers boil lower than straight chains.
- Isomerism is critical in biology and medicine, where one shape works and its mirror image doesn't.

</Step>
