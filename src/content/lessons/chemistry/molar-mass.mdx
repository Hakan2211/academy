<Step title="The mass of a mole" kind="explain">

The **molar mass** of a substance is the mass of one mole of it, in grams per mole (g/mol). And it's wonderfully easy to find: just **add up the atomic masses** of every atom in the formula, reading each atomic mass straight off the periodic table.

For water, H₂O: two hydrogens (2 × 1.008) plus one oxygen (16.00) = **18.02 g/mol**.

</Step>

<Step title="The grand conversion hub" kind="explain">

Molar mass is the exchange rate between **grams** (what you weigh) and **moles** (what reactions count in). Rearrange the one relationship three ways:

<Formula tex="n = \dfrac{m}{M} \qquad m = n \times M \qquad M = \dfrac{m}{n}" block />

where *n* = moles, *m* = mass, *M* = molar mass. With molar mass in hand you can convert any mass to a count of particles and back — the everyday workhorse of the chemistry lab.

</Step>

<Step title="Why it matters" kind="explain">

Reactions happen in whole-number ratios of *particles*, not grams. To predict how much of something you need or will make, you must work in moles — and molar mass is how you get there from a measurable mass.

<Callout type="tip" title="Watch the formula">
Always use the full formula. The molar mass of oxygen *gas* is O₂ = 32 g/mol, not 16 — oxygen travels as a diatomic molecule. Forgetting this is one of the most common slip-ups.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Molar mass** (g/mol) = the sum of the **atomic masses** of all atoms in the formula.
- It converts between **mass and moles**: n = m/M, m = n×M, M = m/n.
- Reactions count in **moles**, so molar mass is the bridge from the balance to the equation.
- Use the **full formula** (O₂ is 32, not 16 g/mol).

</Step>
