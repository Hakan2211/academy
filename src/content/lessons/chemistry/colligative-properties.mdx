<Step title="It's the count that matters" kind="explain">

Some properties of a solution depend only on **how many** solute particles are dissolved — not on what they are. These are the **colligative properties**. Dissolve *anything* in water and you change them.

The two big ones:

- **Boiling point elevation** — the solution boils at a *higher* temperature than the pure solvent.
- **Freezing point depression** — it freezes at a *lower* temperature.

</Step>

<Step title="Widen the liquid range" kind="interactive">

Add more solute and watch both points spread apart — pushing the freezing point down and the boiling point up.

<Colligative />

</Step>

<Step title="Why it happens" kind="explain">

Solute particles get in the way:

- **Freezing** requires solvent molecules to lock into an orderly solid. Dissolved particles disrupt that packing, so you must cool *further* before it can freeze.
- **Boiling** requires molecules to escape the surface. Solute particles block some surface spots and lower the escaping tendency, so you must heat *hotter* to boil.

More particles → bigger effect. That's why it's the *number*, not the identity, that counts.

</Step>

<Step title="Everyday colligative effects" kind="explain">

These show up everywhere:

- **Salt on icy roads** lowers water's freezing point, melting the ice.
- **Antifreeze** in a car raises the boiling point and lowers the freezing point of the coolant — protecting the engine in summer *and* winter.
- **Salting pasta water** raises its boiling point slightly.

<Callout type="info" title="Ions count double">
Because the effect counts *particles*, an ionic solute that splits into several ions has a bigger impact. One unit of NaCl makes two particles (Na⁺ + Cl⁻), so it depresses freezing about twice as much as one molecule of sugar — handy to know when de-icing.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Colligative properties** depend on the **number** of dissolved particles, not their identity.
- Solutes cause **boiling point elevation** and **freezing point depression** — widening the liquid range.
- The cause: solute particles **disrupt freezing** and **hinder escape** at the surface.
- **Ionic** solutes count extra (they split into multiple ions) — the basis of de-icing and antifreeze.

</Step>
