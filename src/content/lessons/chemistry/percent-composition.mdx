<Step title="What fraction is each element?" kind="explain">

The **percent composition** tells you what fraction of a compound's mass comes from each element. For any element:

<Formula tex="\%\text{ mass} = \dfrac{\text{mass of that element in 1 mol}}{\text{molar mass}} \times 100" block />

It's how you'd answer "what percentage of this fertiliser is actually nitrogen?" — a genuinely practical question.

</Step>

<Step title="From percentages back to a formula" kind="explain">

Run it in reverse and you can *discover* a compound's formula from lab data. Given the percent (or mass) of each element:

1. Assume 100 g, so percentages become grams.
2. Convert each mass to **moles** (divide by atomic mass).
3. Divide all by the **smallest** mole value to get the simplest whole-number ratio.

That ratio is the **empirical formula** — the simplest whole-number ratio of atoms.

</Step>

<Step title="Empirical vs molecular formula" kind="explain">

The **empirical formula** is the simplest ratio; the **molecular formula** is the actual number of atoms in a molecule — always a whole-number multiple of the empirical one.

Glucose is C₆H₁₂O₆ (molecular), but its simplest ratio is CH₂O (empirical). To find the molecular formula, compare the real molar mass to the empirical formula's mass and multiply.

<Callout type="info" title="Same empirical, different molecule">
Formaldehyde (CH₂O), acetic acid (C₂H₄O₂), and glucose (C₆H₁₂O₆) all share the empirical formula CH₂O — same ratio, wildly different molecules. The molecular formula is what pins down the real substance.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Percent composition** = each element's mass ÷ molar mass × 100.
- Reverse it (mass → moles → simplest ratio) to find the **empirical formula** from lab data.
- The **molecular formula** is a whole-number multiple of the empirical one, found using the real molar mass.
- Different molecules can share an empirical formula (CH₂O), so the molecular formula identifies the actual substance.

</Step>
