<Step title="Electrons live in shells" kind="explain">

Electrons aren't scattered randomly around the nucleus — they occupy **shells** (energy levels) at set distances. Shells fill from the inside out, and each holds a maximum number of electrons:

- 1st shell: up to **2**
- 2nd shell: up to **8**
- 3rd shell: up to **8** (for the first 20 elements)

So sodium's 11 electrons arrange as **2, 8, 1** — two shells full, one electron starting the third.

</Step>

<Step title="Valence electrons run the show" kind="explain">

The electrons in the **outermost** shell are called **valence electrons**, and they are the single most important thing in chemistry. Nearly every chemical property — how reactive an element is, what it bonds with, what charge its ion takes — comes down to how many valence electrons it has.

Atoms "want" a full outer shell (the stable arrangement of the noble gases). An element with one valence electron (like sodium) loses it easily; one needing just a single electron (like chlorine) grabs one fiercely.

</Step>

<Step title="The link to the periodic table" kind="explain">

Here's the payoff: elements in the **same column** of the periodic table have the **same number of valence electrons** — which is exactly why they behave alike. Sodium (2,8,**1**) and potassium (2,8,8,**1**) are both soft, violently reactive metals because both have one electron to give away.

<Callout type="info" title="A preview">
This is the secret behind the whole periodic table — its columns are really columns of matching valence electrons. The next world is built entirely on this idea.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- Electrons fill **shells** from the inside out, holding 2, then 8, then 8 (first 20 elements).
- **Valence electrons** (outer shell) control an element's chemistry — reactivity, bonding, ion charge.
- Atoms tend toward a **full outer shell** (the noble-gas arrangement).
- Elements in the same periodic-table **column** share the same valence count — hence similar behaviour.

</Step>
