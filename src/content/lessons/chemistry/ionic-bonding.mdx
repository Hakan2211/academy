<Step title="Give and take" kind="explain">

When a **metal** meets a **non-metal**, electrons don't get shared — they get *transferred*. The metal, with few loosely held valence electrons, gives them up; the non-metal, hungry for a full shell, takes them.

Now both are **ions**: the metal positive, the non-metal negative. Opposite charges attract, and that electrostatic pull *is* the **ionic bond**.

</Step>

<Step title="A giant lattice, not a molecule" kind="explain">

An ionic bond isn't a private link between two atoms. Each Na⁺ is pulled by *every* nearby Cl⁻ and vice versa, so the ions stack into a vast, repeating 3D grid — an **ionic lattice**.

That's why we don't write "one NaCl molecule." The formula NaCl just gives the *ratio* (one Na⁺ for every Cl⁻) within a crystal of countless ions.

</Step>

<Step title="Properties of ionic compounds" kind="explain">

The strong, rigid lattice explains how ionic compounds behave:

- **High melting points** — it takes a lot of energy to break so many strong attractions.
- **Brittle** — knock the layers and like charges line up, repel, and the crystal cracks.
- **Conduct when molten or dissolved** — the freed ions can move and carry charge (but a solid lattice can't, so solid salt doesn't conduct).

<Callout type="info" title="Why salt dissolves and conducts">
Water pulls the lattice apart into free-floating Na⁺ and Cl⁻ ions. Those moving charges are why salt water conducts electricity — and why the salts in your body carry nerve signals.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Ionic bonding**: a metal **transfers** electrons to a non-metal, forming oppositely charged ions that attract.
- Ions stack into a **giant lattice**; the formula gives the **ratio**, not a molecule.
- The strong lattice → **high melting points** and **brittleness**.
- Ionic compounds **conduct when molten or dissolved** (free ions), but not as solids.

</Step>
