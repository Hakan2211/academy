<Lesson>

<Step title="Crossing between states" kind="explain">

Add or remove energy and a substance switches state. The transitions come in pairs:

- **Melting** ⇄ **freezing** (solid ⇄ liquid)
- **Vaporising / boiling** ⇄ **condensing** (liquid ⇄ gas)
- **Sublimation** ⇄ **deposition** (solid ⇄ gas directly)

Adding energy loosens the particles (toward gas); removing it lets attractions pull them back together (toward solid).

</Step>

<Step title="The heating curve" kind="interactive">

Heat a solid steadily and watch its temperature. It rises — then *pauses* at each phase change, even though heat keeps flowing in.

<PhaseChange />

</Step>

<Step title="Why temperature pauses" kind="explain">

During a phase change the temperature stays flat. The incoming energy isn't speeding the particles up — it's being spent to **overcome the forces holding them together** (breaking the lattice when melting, separating molecules when boiling).

This hidden energy is the **latent heat**. Only once the change is complete does added energy start raising the temperature again. It's why a pot of boiling water stays at 100 °C no matter how high you crank the flame.

</Step>

<Step title="Melting and boiling points" kind="explain">

The temperatures at which these changes happen — the **melting point** and **boiling point** — are fixed properties of a pure substance, set by the strength of its intermolecular forces. Stronger forces mean more energy needed, so higher melting and boiling points.

<Callout type="info" title="Pressure matters too">
Boiling point depends on pressure: lower pressure lets molecules escape more easily, so water boils below 100 °C up a mountain. A pressure cooker does the reverse — higher pressure, higher boiling point, faster cooking.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- Phase changes come in pairs: **melting/freezing, boiling/condensing, sublimation/deposition**.
- On a heating curve, temperature **pauses** during each change.
- That energy is **latent heat** — spent overcoming intermolecular forces, not raising temperature.
- **Melting/boiling points** are fixed by intermolecular force strength, and boiling point shifts with **pressure**.

</Step>
