<Step title="Same element, different mass" kind="explain">

Change the number of protons and you change the element. But change the number of **neutrons** and you get the *same* element with a different mass — an **isotope**.

All carbon atoms have 6 protons (that's what makes them carbon). But carbon-12 has 6 neutrons, carbon-13 has 7, and carbon-14 has 8. Same element, three different masses.

Because neutrons are neutral, isotopes behave **identically in chemical reactions** — chemistry is run by electrons, not neutrons. They differ only in mass and, sometimes, in nuclear stability.

</Step>

<Step title="Add neutrons, make isotopes" kind="interactive">

Keep the proton count fixed and vary the neutrons. Watch the mass number change while the element stays the same — and see when the nucleus becomes unstable (radioactive).

<Step title="Weighted average mass" kind="explain">

This is why periodic-table masses aren't whole numbers. Chlorine exists as about 75% chlorine-35 and 25% chlorine-37. The listed mass is the **weighted average**:

<Formula tex="(0.75 \times 35) + (0.25 \times 37) = 35.5" block />

So "35.5" isn't any single chlorine atom — it's the average you'd measure across a normal sample.

</Step>

<Step title="Useful and unstable isotopes" kind="explain">

Some isotopes have unstable nuclei and decay over time, emitting radiation. These **radioisotopes** are enormously useful:

- **Carbon-14** decays at a known rate, so its leftover amount dates ancient wood and bone (radiocarbon dating).
- **Medical isotopes** trace blood flow or destroy tumours.

<Callout type="tip" title="Chemistry vs nuclear">
Adding neutrons doesn't change how an atom *bonds* — that's chemistry, decided by electrons. It changes how the *nucleus* behaves — that's nuclear physics. Isotopes sit right on that boundary.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Isotopes** are atoms of the same element (same protons) with different **neutron** counts → different mass numbers.
- They are chemically **identical** because chemistry depends on electrons, not neutrons.
- Periodic-table atomic masses are **weighted averages** over an element's natural isotopes.
- Unstable **radioisotopes** (e.g. carbon-14) are used in dating, medicine, and tracing.

</Step>
