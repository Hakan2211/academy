<Step title="Every reaction moves energy" kind="explain">

Reactions don't just rearrange atoms — they move **energy**, almost always as heat. Some reactions warm their surroundings (a fire, a hand-warmer); others cool them (an instant cold pack). Thermochemistry is the study of that energy flow.

The bookkeeping uses two terms:

- The **system** is the reaction itself (the chemicals).
- The **surroundings** are everything else (the beaker, the air, you).

Energy released by the system flows *into* the surroundings, and vice versa.

</Step>

<Step title="Where the energy comes from" kind="explain">

It all traces back to **chemical bonds**. Breaking bonds **absorbs** energy; forming bonds **releases** it. A reaction's net energy change is the difference between the two — energy stored in bonds being repackaged.

This is why fuels matter: their bonds store energy that's released when they burn into more stable products.

</Step>

<Step title="Conservation, always" kind="explain">

Energy is never created or destroyed (the first law of thermodynamics) — it only moves between the system and the surroundings, or between chemical and thermal forms. If a reaction releases 200 kJ, the surroundings gain exactly 200 kJ.

<Callout type="info" title="Energy and matter, side by side">
You've already balanced *atoms* in a reaction. Thermochemistry balances the *energy*. Both are conserved — reactions just shuffle matter and energy into new arrangements.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- Reactions transfer **energy** (usually heat) between the **system** and the **surroundings**.
- The energy comes from **bonds**: breaking absorbs, forming releases — the net is the reaction's energy change.
- **Energy is conserved** — it moves, never vanishes.
- Thermochemistry is the energy counterpart to balancing atoms.

</Step>
