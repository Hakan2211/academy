<Lesson>

<Step title="How fast, not just whether" kind="explain">

The last world asked *whether* a reaction can happen. This one asks **how fast**. Rates span an astonishing range — an explosion finishes in milliseconds, while iron rusts over years and rock weathers over millennia.

The **rate of reaction** is how quickly reactants are used up (or products formed) per unit time. Measure it by tracking a concentration, a mass, or a volume of gas over time.

</Step>

<Step title="Watch a reactant disappear" kind="interactive">

Plot a reactant's concentration over time and the rate is the **steepness** of the curve — steep means fast. Notice the rate also *slows* as reactants run low.

<RateFactors />

</Step>

<Step title="Rate changes as it goes" kind="explain">

A reaction is usually **fastest at the start**, when reactant concentration is highest, and slows as reactants deplete — which is why the curve flattens. The rate at any instant is the slope of the concentration–time graph right there.

The average rate over an interval is simply:

<Formula tex="\text{rate} = \dfrac{\text{change in concentration}}{\text{time taken}}" block />

</Step>

<Step title="Why rates matter" kind="explain">

Controlling rate is everywhere in practice: keeping food fresh (slowing decay), curing concrete (just right), running an engine (fast and controlled), and manufacturing chemicals economically (fast enough to be cheap, slow enough to be safe).

<Callout type="info" title="The question behind rate">
Everything that follows answers one question: *what makes successful collisions between particles more frequent?* That single idea — collision theory, next — explains every factor that speeds a reaction up.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Reaction rate** = how fast reactants are consumed or products formed per unit time.
- On a concentration–time graph, rate is the **slope** — and it usually **slows** as reactants deplete.
- Average rate = change in concentration ÷ time.
- Rate control matters everywhere; it all comes down to the frequency of **successful collisions**.

</Step>
