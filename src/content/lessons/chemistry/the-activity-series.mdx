<Step title="Not all metals are equally eager" kind="explain">

Some metals react violently (sodium fizzes in water), others barely react at all (gold survives for millennia). Rank metals from most to least reactive and you get the **activity series**.

Its power is in **single replacement** reactions: a **more reactive** metal will **displace** a less reactive one from its compound — pushing it out and taking its place.

</Step>

<Step title="Test a displacement" kind="interactive">

Pick a metal strip and dip it into a solution of another metal's salt. A reaction happens only if your strip is *higher* in the series than the dissolved metal.

<ActivitySeries />

</Step>

<Step title="Reactivity only works downhill" kind="explain">

The rule is one-directional: a metal can only displace those **below** it. Drop a zinc strip into copper sulfate and zinc (more reactive) displaces copper — the strip darkens with copper while zinc dissolves. But a copper strip in zinc sulfate does *nothing*: copper can't displace the more reactive zinc.

<Formula tex="\text{Zn} + \text{CuSO}_4 \rightarrow \text{ZnSO}_4 + \text{Cu}" block />

</Step>

<Step title="What the ranking predicts" kind="explain">

The series predicts far more than displacement:

- **Metal + water / acid:** very reactive metals (top) react fast; unreactive ones (bottom) don't react at all.
- **Extraction:** reactive metals (aluminium) need electrolysis to win them from their ores; unreactive ones (gold) are found as the pure metal.
- **Corrosion:** reactive metals tarnish and rust; gold and platinum stay shiny.

<Callout type="tip" title="A measure of 'wanting' to be an ion">
Position in the series reflects how readily a metal gives up electrons to become a positive ion. The more eagerly it ionises, the higher it sits — which is exactly the idea behind batteries, two worlds from now.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- The **activity series** ranks metals from most to least reactive.
- A **more reactive** metal **displaces** a less reactive one from its compound — only ever downhill.
- It predicts reactions with **water/acid**, how metals are **extracted**, and how they **corrode**.
- Position reflects how readily a metal becomes an **ion** — the basis of batteries later.

</Step>
