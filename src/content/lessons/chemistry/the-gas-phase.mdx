<Step title="The freest state of matter" kind="explain">

Gases are unlike solids and liquids in a defining way: their particles are **far apart** and move **fast and freely**, with almost nothing holding them together. This gives gases their signature behaviours:

- They **fill any container** completely, taking its shape and volume.
- They are easily **compressed** — there's mostly empty space to squeeze out.
- They have **low density** — few particles per unit of space.
- They **flow and mix** readily.

</Step>

<Step title="Gas in a container" kind="interactive">

Watch particles bounce around a container. Each collision with the walls is a tiny push — and the sum of all those pushes is **pressure**.

</Step>

<Step title="Pressure: collisions on the walls" kind="explain">

**Pressure** is force spread over an area — and for a gas, it comes from countless particles slamming into the container walls. More frequent or harder collisions mean higher pressure.

That's why squeezing a gas into a smaller space raises the pressure (the same particles hit the walls more often) and why heating it raises the pressure too (the particles move faster and hit harder).

</Step>

<Step title="Measuring gases" kind="explain">

A gas is described by four quantities, and they're all linked:

- **Pressure (P)** — in pascals (Pa) or kilopascals (kPa); atmospheric pressure is about 101 kPa.
- **Volume (V)** — in litres or cubic metres.
- **Temperature (T)** — always in **kelvin** for gas calculations (0 K is absolute zero).
- **Amount (n)** — in moles.

<Callout type="tip" title="Always use kelvin">
Gas laws only work with temperature in **kelvin**, because the relationships are about the *actual* energy of particles. At 0 K particle motion stops; you can't have a "negative" amount of motion, so Celsius (which goes negative) would break the maths. Convert: K = °C + 273.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Gases** have far-apart, fast, free particles — they fill any container, compress easily, and have low density.
- **Pressure** is the result of particles **colliding with the walls**.
- Gases are described by **P, V, T, and n** (amount in moles).
- Always use **kelvin** for temperature in gas calculations (K = °C + 273).

</Step>
