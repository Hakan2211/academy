<Lesson>

<Step title="How crowded is the solute?" kind="explain">

**Concentration** measures how much solute is packed into a given amount of solution — how *strong* it is. A dark, strong coffee is concentrated; a watered-down one is dilute.

Chemists' favourite measure is **molarity (M)** — moles of solute per litre of solution:

<Formula tex="M = \dfrac{\text{moles of solute}}{\text{litres of solution}}" block />

A 2 M solution has 2 moles of solute in every litre. Molarity is handy because reactions count in moles.

</Step>

<Step title="Making and using molarity" kind="explain">

Rearrange the formula to get whatever you need:

<Formula tex="\text{moles} = M \times V \qquad V = \dfrac{\text{moles}}{M}" block />

To make 0.5 L of 2 M salt solution, you need 2 × 0.5 = 1 mole of salt — weigh it out (using molar mass) and add water up to 0.5 L.

</Step>

<Step title="Dilution: same solute, more water" kind="explain">

**Dilution** adds solvent to lower the concentration. The key insight: the *amount of solute doesn't change* — only the volume grows. So:

<Formula tex="M_1 V_1 = M_2 V_2" block />

Both sides equal the (unchanged) moles of solute. To dilute 100 mL of 6 M acid to 2 M, you'd add water up to 300 mL (the volume triples, the concentration drops to a third).

<Callout type="warn" title="Add acid to water">
When diluting a strong acid, always add the **acid to water**, never water to acid. The mixing releases heat, and adding water to concentrated acid can make it spit and splash dangerously.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Concentration** = how much solute per amount of solution; **molarity (M)** = moles ÷ litres.
- Rearrange: **moles = M × V**, so you can prepare a solution of any strength.
- **Dilution** adds solvent — solute stays fixed, so **M₁V₁ = M₂V₂**.
- Safety: always add **acid to water**, never the reverse.

</Step>
