<Lesson>

<Step title="Rows and columns" kind="explain">

The table's power is in its two directions:

- A **period** is a horizontal **row**. Moving across a period, you add one proton (and one electron) at a time, filling the same outer shell.
- A **group** is a vertical **column**. Elements in a group share the same number of **valence electrons**, so they behave alike.

There are 7 periods and 18 groups. An element's address — its group and period — tells you almost everything about how it will act.

</Step>

</Step>

<Step title="What each square tells you" kind="explain">

Every cell packs in data. At minimum you'll see:

- The **atomic number (Z)** — the proton count, which sets the element's identity and its position.
- The **chemical symbol** — one or two letters (H, He, Na, Cl).
- The **name** and, on a full table, the **relative atomic mass**.

Because the table is ordered by Z, the squares simply count up 1, 2, 3… as you read left to right, top to bottom.

</Step>

<Step title="Group numbers and valence" kind="explain">

For the **main-group** elements (the tall columns on the left and right), the group number reveals the valence electrons:

- Group 1 → 1 valence electron
- Group 2 → 2
- Groups 13–18 → 3, 4, 5, 6, 7, 8 valence electrons

<Callout type="tip" title="The transition metals are the exception">
The block in the middle (groups 3–12, the transition metals) fills inner d-orbitals, so the neat "group number = valence electrons" rule doesn't apply there. It's the main-group elements where the shortcut shines.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Periods** are rows (same outer shell filling); **groups** are columns (same valence count).
- Each cell shows the **atomic number, symbol, name** (and mass) — ordered by Z.
- For **main-group** elements, the group number gives the **valence electrons**.
- The **transition metals** (groups 3–12) are the exception — they fill inner d-orbitals.

</Step>
