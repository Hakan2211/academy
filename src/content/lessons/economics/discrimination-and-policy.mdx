<Step title="When markets need a verdict" kind="explain">

The clean supply-and-demand picture of the labor market is powerful, but it leaves out things people care about most: Is the resulting pay *fair*? What about workers paid the same despite different skill, or paid differently despite the same skill? And what can governments do — minimum wages, unions, redistribution — without breaking the very mechanism that makes markets work?

This capstone takes the labor market off the chalkboard and into the messy real world. We'll look at four big questions: **discrimination**, the **minimum wage**, **unions**, and **redistribution**. The thread running through all of them is the central tension of policy: every intervention has both a benefit and a cost, and good policy weighs the two at the margin.

</Step>

<Step title="Discrimination in the labor market" kind="explain">

**Discrimination** means paying or hiring workers differently because of a trait — race, sex, age, religion — unrelated to their productivity. It is both a moral problem and an economic puzzle.

The puzzle: in a fiercely competitive market, discrimination is **costly to the discriminator**. If two workers are equally productive but an employer underpays one group, a rival firm can hire that group cheaply, undercut the prejudiced firm, and win. Competition therefore tends to *erode* wage gaps based on pure prejudice — which is one reason economists are wary of explanations that rest on prejudice alone.

Yet sizable pay gaps persist. Why? Part is **not** discrimination at all but differences in human capital, experience, hours and occupation. But a stubborn part remains even after accounting for those, and economists point to forces competition doesn't easily wash away: **statistical discrimination** (judging an individual by group averages), unequal access to education and networks that build human capital, occupational segregation, and discrimination by customers or co-workers that a firm can't simply ignore. The economic lesson is subtle: competition fights prejudice, but it does not automatically erase inequality rooted in unequal opportunity.

</Step>

<Step title="The minimum wage" kind="explain">

A **minimum wage** is a legal floor on the price of labor — employers may not pay below it. Its goal is to lift the earnings of the lowest-paid. Its risk is the same as any price floor set above the market price: it can create a **surplus** of labor, which in this market means **unemployment**.

The logic: above the equilibrium wage, the high pay tempts *more* people to look for work while firms want *fewer* of them. The gap between workers who want a job and jobs on offer is the surplus. So a binding minimum wage helps the workers who keep their jobs (they earn more) but hurts those who can't find one (priced out).

How big the job loss is depends on how *responsive* employment is to the wage — the elasticity of labor demand. If demand is quite inelastic, or if employers have wage-setting power that was holding pay artificially low, a modest minimum wage can raise pay with little or no job loss. Set it far above equilibrium, though, and the unemployment effect grows. This is exactly why the empirical debate over the minimum wage is so lively: the answer depends on the numbers, not on the diagram alone.

</Step>

<Step title="Unions and bargaining power" kind="explain">

Individual workers often have little bargaining power against a large employer. A **labor union** pools workers so they can negotiate as one, and it can push wages above the level a single worker could get — partly by the credible threat to **strike** (withhold labor as a group).

In supply-and-demand terms, a union acts a lot like a privately set wage floor: it can raise pay for its members, but if that wage sits above equilibrium it tends to reduce the number of union jobs, much like a minimum wage. Unions can also raise productivity — through training, lower turnover, and giving workers a voice that improves how firms are run — which partly offsets the cost. They illustrate the same theme: **bargaining power**, not just productivity, helps determine the wage, and shifting that power has both gains and costs.

</Step>

<Step title="Redistribution and the big trade-off" kind="explain">

When a society decides market outcomes are too unequal, it can **redistribute** — taxing higher incomes and transferring to lower ones through progressive taxes, benefits, the minimum wage, and public services like education and health.

But redistribution faces what economists call the **big trade-off**: the very tools that shrink inequality can also **shrink the size of the pie**. High taxes can blunt the incentive to work, save and take risks; poorly designed benefits can discourage the move from welfare into work. The bucket carrying income from rich to poor leaks along the way.

That does **not** mean redistribution is wrong — many people judge a somewhat smaller, fairer pie better than a larger, harshly unequal one, and well-designed policy minimizes the leak. It means the choice is genuinely hard, and it crosses from **positive** economics (what *will* happen if we tax and transfer) into **normative** economics (what *ought* we to do). Economics can map the trade-offs with precision; it cannot, by itself, tell a society how much equality is worth. That judgment belongs to citizens — informed, ideally, by a clear view of the costs and benefits at the margin.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Discrimination** pays or hires by traits unrelated to productivity; competition penalizes pure prejudice but does not erase inequality rooted in unequal opportunity, networks and statistical discrimination.
- A **minimum wage** is a price floor on labor: above equilibrium it raises pay for those who keep jobs but creates a labor surplus (unemployment); the size of the effect depends on how responsive labor demand is.
- **Unions** pool bargaining power and can raise wages (and sometimes productivity), but a union wage above equilibrium reduces union jobs, much like a minimum wage.
- **Redistribution** can shrink inequality but faces the **big trade-off** — the tools that shrink the gap can shrink the pie — and the right amount is a **normative** judgment economics can inform but not settle.

</Step>
