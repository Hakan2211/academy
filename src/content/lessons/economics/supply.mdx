<Step title="The other side of the market" kind="explain">

Demand is only half the story. For every buyer there's a seller, and sellers follow their own rule — one that points the opposite way. It's the **law of supply**: *holding everything else constant, as the price of a good rises, the quantity producers want to sell rises too; as the price falls, the quantity supplied falls.* Price and quantity supplied move in the **same** direction.

Picture a farmer deciding how many fields to plant, or a factory deciding whether to run a night shift. A higher selling price makes the extra effort, the overtime, the marginal field worth it. So sellers offer more. The supply curve therefore slopes **upward**, mirroring the demand curve's downward slant.

</Step>

<Step title="Why supply slopes up" kind="explain">

There's a deeper reason behind the upward slope, and it's an old friend: **rising marginal cost**. Producing each additional unit tends to cost a little more than the last — you bring in less-suited workers, run machines harder, pay overtime, or farm poorer land.

A producer will only make that next unit if the price covers its (rising) marginal cost. So a *low* price tempts out only the cheap-to-make units; a *higher* price makes it worthwhile to push into the costlier units too. That's exactly why a higher price draws out a larger quantity supplied — the supply curve is really a marginal-cost curve in disguise.

<Callout type="info" title="Supply needs time">
The law of supply is strongest given time to react. In the next hour a baker can't bake more bread no matter the price. Over weeks they can buy more flour; over years, build a bigger bakery. The longer the horizon, the more responsive supply becomes.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Read the supply curve" kind="interactive">

Just as with demand, keep two changes apart. Moving the **price** slides you *along* the curve — a change in **quantity supplied**. A toggle (technology, input costs, the number of sellers) moves the *whole* curve — a change in **supply**. We'll explore those shifters next lesson.

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- The **law of supply**: all else equal, a higher price raises the **quantity supplied** — price and quantity move in the **same** direction.
- The supply curve slopes **upward**, the mirror image of the demand curve.
- The upward slope reflects **rising marginal cost**: each extra unit costs a bit more, so it takes a higher price to coax it out.
- Supply is more **responsive over longer time horizons** — sellers need time to adjust.
- Changing the good's **own price** = movement **along** the curve (change in *quantity supplied*); other changes **shift** the curve (change in *supply*).

</Step>
