<Step title="Deficit is a flow, debt is a stock" kind="explain">

Expansionary fiscal policy has a cost: the government spends more than it takes in. To talk about that cost precisely, you need to keep two ideas apart — one of the most common confusions in all of economics.

A **budget deficit** is a *flow*: how much the government overspends in a *single year*. It is this year's spending minus this year's revenue. A **surplus** is the opposite — revenue beating spending. A **balanced budget** is the two matching exactly.

<Formula tex="\text{deficit} = \text{government spending} - \text{tax revenue}" block />

The **national debt** is a *stock*: the total amount the government owes *right now*, the accumulation of every past deficit (minus every past surplus). The link between them is simple and relentless: each year's deficit is borrowed and added to the debt.

The bathtub image helps. The debt is the water in the tub. The deficit is the tap running in; a surplus is the drain running out. A *smaller* deficit still raises the debt — the water keeps rising, just more slowly. The debt only falls when you actually run a **surplus**.

</Step>

<Step title="Watch deficits pile into debt" kind="interactive">

Set a steady annual budget balance and run the clock forward. A negative balance is a deficit; watch it accumulate, year after year, into a mounting national debt.

<DebtClock />

Leave a deficit running and the debt climbs without pause — every year adds another layer. Slide to a **surplus** and the debt finally falls. Notice the second number too: the **debt-to-GDP ratio**, which we turn to next.

</Step>

<Step title="Why debt-to-GDP is the number that matters" kind="explain">

A debt of a trillion dollars sounds alarming — but is it? It depends entirely on how big the economy carrying it is. A household with a $1m mortgage and a $2m income is comfortable; the same mortgage on a $40,000 income is a crisis. Nations are no different.

That's why economists track the **debt-to-GDP ratio** rather than the raw dollar figure. GDP measures the economy's annual income — its capacity to service and eventually repay debt. A debt equal to 60% of GDP is far more manageable than one at 130%, even if the second country is richer in absolute terms.

This ratio has a hopeful implication: because the economy *grows*, a country can run modest deficits forever and still keep debt-to-GDP stable or falling — as long as GDP grows faster than the debt. The debt rises in dollars, but shrinks relative to the economy that backs it. Trouble comes when debt grows faster than income year after year, because then ever more of the budget must go to **interest payments** instead of schools, defence, or tax cuts — and lenders may demand higher interest rates to keep buying the debt.

</Step>

<Step title="Crowding out: the hidden cost" kind="explain">

Beyond the interest bill, large deficits carry a subtler cost called **crowding out**.

When the government borrows heavily, it competes with everyone else — firms wanting to build factories, families wanting mortgages — for the same finite pool of national savings. That extra demand for borrowing pushes **interest rates up**. Higher rates make private investment more expensive, so some firms shelve projects they would otherwise have funded. The government's borrowing has "crowded out" private investment.

This is the deepest worry about deficit spending: a dollar the government borrows and spends may displace a dollar of private investment that would have been more productive — and since investment builds the economy's future capacity, crowding out can quietly slow long-run growth.

<Callout type="warn" title="Crowding out is not absolute">
Crowding out bites hardest when the economy is *near* potential, where savings are scarce and resources are fully used. In a deep recession, with idle savings and unemployed resources, government borrowing can soak up funds nobody else is using — so crowding out is weak and stimulus is most effective. Context decides how much it matters.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- A **deficit** is a *flow* (one year's overspending); the **national debt** is a *stock* (all past deficits accumulated). Each year's deficit adds to the debt.
- The debt falls only when the government runs an actual **surplus**; a smaller deficit still raises the debt.
- The **debt-to-GDP ratio** matters more than the raw figure: it measures debt against the economy's capacity to carry it, and can fall even as debt grows if GDP grows faster.
- Heavy borrowing means a rising **interest bill** and **crowding out** — government borrowing pushes up interest rates and displaces private investment.
- Crowding out is strongest near potential output and weakest in a deep recession with idle resources.

</Step>
