<Step title="The question that elasticity finally answers" kind="explain">

A government slaps a £2-per-bottle tax on a drink and announces that *sellers* must pay it. Sellers grumble. But do they actually bear the cost? Maybe they just raise the shelf price by £2 and pass the whole thing to shoppers. Or maybe shoppers refuse to pay more and sellers eat it. Who *really* pays?

This is the question of **tax incidence** — the study of who ultimately bears the burden of a tax, as opposed to who is legally charged. It is one of the most counter-intuitive and most important results in microeconomics, and the answer turns out to have almost nothing to do with the law and almost everything to do with **elasticity**.

Everything you have learned this world — elastic versus inelastic, who can walk away, who is stuck — comes together here.

</Step>

<Step title="A tax drives a wedge" kind="explain">

Picture an ordinary market at equilibrium: one price clears it, where supply meets demand. Now impose a **per-unit tax**. The single price splits into two:

- the price **buyers pay** at the till, and
- the price **sellers receive** after handing the tax to the government.

The gap between them — equal to the tax — is the **tax wedge**. Because buyers face a higher effective price and sellers a lower effective one, fewer units trade than before. The market shrinks to a new, smaller quantity, with buyers paying more than the old price and sellers keeping less than the old price.

Crucially, it does not matter whether the law collects the tax from buyers or from sellers. The wedge, the new quantity, and the split of the burden come out *identical* either way. Legal incidence is a distraction; **economic incidence** is what counts.

</Step>

<Step title="The burden falls on the inelastic side" kind="explain">

So what *does* decide the split? The relative elasticity of the two sides. The rule is short and powerful:

> The more **inelastic** side of the market — the side less able to walk away — bears the **larger** share of the tax.

The intuition is pure elasticity. An inelastic buyer keeps buying almost regardless of price, so sellers can pass the tax on with little loss of sales — buyers absorb most of it. An elastic buyer flees the moment the price ticks up, so sellers dare not raise the price much and must swallow the tax themselves. Symmetrically, an inelastic *seller* (one who cannot easily stop producing — a farmer with a perishable crop, a landlord with fixed land) gets stuck holding the tax, while an elastic seller who can simply shut down forces buyers to bear it.

Whoever is more trapped, pays more. The side that can most credibly say *"raise my price and I'm gone"* escapes the tax.

</Step>

<Step title="The deadweight loss" kind="explain">

Look again at that shaded triangle. A tax does not just *move* money from buyers and sellers to the government — it also **destroys** some value outright. Between the old quantity and the new, smaller quantity sit trades that *would have happened* and would have made both buyer and seller better off, but now don't, because the tax wedge makes them unprofitable. The value of those lost mutually beneficial trades is the **deadweight loss**.

It is called a *loss* because nobody captures it — not buyers, not sellers, not the government. It simply vanishes, the price of distorting people's choices. This is why economists judge taxes partly by the deadweight loss they create.

And here elasticity returns one last time, with a twist that guides real tax policy:

> The **more elastic** the market, the **larger** the deadweight loss.

When demand and supply are elastic, a tax scares off lots of trades, so the triangle is fat. When both sides are inelastic, almost the same quantity trades despite the tax, so very little value is lost — the triangle is thin.

<Callout type="warn" title="Efficiency and fairness can collide">
The same inelasticity that minimises deadweight loss (so taxing necessities is 'efficient') also dumps the burden on buyers who cannot escape — often the people who can least afford it. That is exactly why taxes on things like fuel, tobacco, and basic goods raise so much revenue with so little dodging, yet are so often attacked as unfair. Elasticity explains both the appeal and the controversy.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Putting it to work" kind="explain">

This single framework explains a surprising amount of the tax world. Governments love taxing tobacco, alcohol, fuel, and other inelastic goods precisely *because* buyers cannot escape: revenue stays high, the quantity barely falls, and deadweight loss is small — though the burden lands squarely on consumers. By contrast, a heavy tax on a good with close substitutes mostly drives buyers to the alternative, raising little money while destroying a lot of trades.

The same logic reappears throughout economics. A *tariff* is a tax on imports, split between domestic buyers and foreign sellers by their elasticities. A *Pigouvian tax* on pollution works only if you understand who will actually bear it. Even the question of whether a payroll tax falls on workers or employers is, at bottom, a question about the elasticity of labour supply versus labour demand. Master incidence here, and you have a key that unlocks all of them.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Tax incidence** asks who *really* bears a tax, not who is legally charged — and the two can differ completely.
- A per-unit tax drives a **wedge** between the price buyers pay and the price sellers receive, shrinking quantity.
- The burden falls more heavily on the **more inelastic** side — whoever is least able to walk away.
- **Legal incidence is irrelevant**: charging buyers or sellers gives the identical outcome.
- A tax also creates **deadweight loss** — the value of mutually beneficial trades it destroys — and that loss is **larger when the market is more elastic**.
- The framework explains 'sin' taxes, tariffs, Pigouvian taxes, and payroll taxes alike.

</Step>
