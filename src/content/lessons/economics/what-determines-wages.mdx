<Step title="The puzzle of pay" kind="explain">

A surgeon earns many times what a cashier earns. A night-shift nurse out-earns a daytime one. A software engineer in a booming firm out-earns one in a shrinking industry. If a wage is just the price of labor, why are these prices so different?

The answer is that workers and jobs are **not interchangeable**. Each job is really its own little labor market, with its own supply and demand. Two forces explain most of the gaps:

- **Human capital** — the skills, knowledge and training a worker brings.
- **Compensating differentials** — extra pay that makes up for unpleasant or risky working conditions.

Let's take them one at a time.

</Step>

<Step title="Human capital" kind="explain">

**Human capital** is the stock of skills and knowledge a worker carries around in their head and hands. Like a factory's machinery, it makes the worker more productive — and we just saw that more productive workers have a higher marginal revenue product, so firms will pay more for them.

Education and training are investments in human capital. They cost time and money up front (tuition, forgone earnings) and pay off later as higher wages — a clear example of weighing cost against benefit over time. This is why, on average, each step up the education ladder raises pay: the schooling builds skills that make a worker worth more to employers.

It isn't *only* formal schooling. On-the-job experience, apprenticeships, and learning rare or in-demand skills all add human capital. The scarcer and more valuable the skill, the higher the wage it commands.

</Step>

<Step title="See the gaps" kind="interactive">

In the human-capital view, pay climbs steeply with schooling because each level adds productivity. In the compensating-pay view, the nastier and riskier jobs sit higher — not because those workers are more skilled, but because someone has to be paid extra to do unpleasant work.

</Step>

<Step title="Compensating differentials" kind="explain">

Among jobs that demand similar skill, the ones with worse conditions must pay **more** to attract anyone. Economists call this premium a **compensating differential** — extra pay that compensates the worker for danger, dirt, stress, unsocial hours or instability.

A deep-sea welder earns more than an equally skilled welder working safely on land. Night shifts pay a premium over day shifts. Risky, dirty or remote jobs pay more than comfortable office work of the same difficulty. The market has to bid the wage up until enough workers are willing to put up with the downside.

Several other forces add to the picture. **Efficiency wages** — paying above the market rate to attract better workers and discourage them from quitting or slacking. **Bargaining power**, including unions, which can push wages above the competitive level. And plain old **luck and location**: being in a booming industry or a high-cost city. But human capital and compensating differentials are the workhorses that explain most of the everyday gaps in pay.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- Wages differ because workers and jobs aren't interchangeable — each job is its own labor market.
- **Human capital** (skills, education, training, experience) raises productivity, so more-skilled workers earn more; education is an investment that pays off in higher wages.
- **Compensating differentials** are extra pay for unpleasant, risky or unstable jobs — a premium for bad conditions, not for higher skill.
- Other forces — efficiency wages, bargaining power and unions, luck and location — add to the picture, but human capital and compensating differentials explain most everyday pay gaps.

</Step>
