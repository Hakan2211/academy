<Step title="The other side of the diagram" kind="explain">

Aggregate demand tells us how much the economy wants to *buy* at each price level. **Aggregate supply (AS)** tells us how much it can *produce* and sell. But here macroeconomics splits in two, because the answer depends entirely on the **time horizon**. In the short run, producers can be coaxed into making more; in the long run, output is pinned down by the economy's real capacity. So we draw *two* aggregate-supply curves.

This split — a short-run curve that slopes up and a long-run curve that stands vertical — is one of the most important ideas in all of macro. It's the difference between what an economy can do this quarter and what it can sustain forever.

</Step>

<Step title="Short-run aggregate supply slopes up" kind="explain">

**Short-run aggregate supply (SRAS)** slopes **upward**: in the short run, a higher price level coaxes firms to produce more. Why? The key is that some costs are **sticky** — they don't adjust right away.

The clearest case is wages. Workers' pay is often fixed by contract for months or years. So when the price level rises, firms sell their output for more, but their wage bill hasn't caught up. Higher revenue against still-fixed costs means **fatter profit margins**, so firms ramp up production and hire more. The reverse holds when prices fall: with wages stuck, margins get squeezed and firms cut back.

The upward slope, then, comes from a *temporary mismatch*: output prices move faster than input costs. SRAS shifts when those costs themselves change — a spike in oil or wages or a supply disruption shifts SRAS **left** (costlier to produce); cheaper inputs or a productivity jump shift it **right**.

</Step>

<Step title="Long-run aggregate supply stands vertical" kind="explain">

Give it enough time, and the sticky costs unstick. Contracts get renegotiated; wages, rents, and all prices fully adjust. Once everything has caught up, the profit-margin trick disappears — a higher price level no longer buys any extra output.

So **long-run aggregate supply (LRAS)** is a **vertical line**. It sits at the economy's **potential output** (also called full-employment GDP): the most an economy can produce when its labour, capital, and technology are all fully and normally employed. In the long run, the price level can be anything at all, but real GDP is fixed at potential — money is "neutral," affecting only prices, not real output.

What *can* move LRAS? Only things that change the economy's actual productive capacity: more workers, more capital, better technology, improved skills, better institutions. That outward march of LRAS over decades is exactly what we call **economic growth** — the subject of a later world.

<Callout type="info" title="Two curves, two questions">
SRAS answers "how does output respond to prices *right now*, while costs are sticky?" — so it slopes up. LRAS answers "what can the economy *sustain* once everything adjusts?" — a fixed potential, so it's vertical. Demand-side policy can push output along SRAS in the short run, but cannot move LRAS; only growth in real capacity does that.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Aggregate supply** comes in two forms because it depends on the **time horizon**.
- **Short-run aggregate supply (SRAS)** slopes **up**: with **sticky costs** (especially wages), a higher price level fattens profit margins and coaxes out more output.
- SRAS **shifts** with input costs and productivity: cost shocks (oil, wages) push it **left**; cheaper inputs or better tech push it **right**.
- **Long-run aggregate supply (LRAS)** is **vertical** at **potential output**: once all costs adjust, the price level can't change real GDP.
- LRAS moves only when real **capacity** grows — more labour, capital, technology, skills — which is **economic growth**.

</Step>
