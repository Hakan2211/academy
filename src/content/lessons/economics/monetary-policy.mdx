<Step title="The other macro toolkit" kind="explain">

Fiscal policy is the government's tool, run through the budget. But there is a second, often more nimble, way to steer the economy — and it belongs not to the government but to the **central bank**: the Federal Reserve in the United States, the European Central Bank in the eurozone, the Bank of England in the UK.

**Monetary policy** is the central bank's use of **interest rates** and the **money supply** to influence aggregate demand. You already met the central bank's plumbing in the Money & Banking world — it controls the money supply and acts as lender of last resort. Now we see what it does with that power: it manages the economy's overall level of spending by changing how cheap or dear it is to borrow.

The central bank is typically **independent** of the elected government, by design. Politicians face elections every few years and are tempted to juice the economy with cheap money just before a vote, even if inflation pays the price later. Insulating monetary policy from that temptation tends to produce more stable, credible inflation — which is why most modern central banks set rates at arm's length from the government.

</Step>

<Step title="The policy rate and how the bank moves it" kind="explain">

The central bank's main control is a single **policy interest rate** — the rate at which banks lend to one another overnight (the federal funds rate in the US). That rate ripples out through the whole financial system, dragging mortgage rates, business-loan rates, and savings rates along with it.

But the bank doesn't simply *announce* a rate and hope. It moves it through **open-market operations** — buying and selling government bonds:

- To push rates **down**, the bank **buys** bonds from banks, paying with newly created money. That floods the banking system with reserves; with more money to lend and the same demand for loans, the price of borrowing — the interest rate — falls.
- To push rates **up**, the bank **sells** bonds, draining money out of the system. Reserves become scarcer, so the interest rate rises.

So buying bonds = looser policy = lower rates = more money; selling bonds = tighter policy = higher rates = less money. The bond market is the lever; the interest rate is what the lever moves.

</Step>

Push the rate *below* neutral and you get **easy money**: cheap loans fuel borrowing, investment and spending, and inflation heats up — the move to fight a recession. Push it *above* neutral and you get **tight money**: dear loans choke off spending and rein in inflation — the move to fight an overheating economy. The bank is steering aggregate demand with a single dial.

</Step>

<Step title="Following the chain of transmission" kind="explain">

Why does a change in one overnight rate end up moving the price of everything? Trace the **transmission mechanism** step by step. Suppose the bank *cuts* the rate to fight a recession:

- Lower rates make **borrowing cheaper**. Mortgages, car loans, and credit are all priced off the policy rate.
- Cheaper borrowing lifts **interest-sensitive spending**. Firms borrow to invest in equipment and buildings; households buy homes and cars they'd have put off.
- More investment and consumption means **aggregate demand shifts right** — exactly the AD shift you studied last world.
- Higher demand raises **output and employment** in the short run; if pushed too far past potential, it also raises the **price level** — inflation.

Raising the rate runs the same chain in reverse: dearer borrowing, less investment and spending, AD shifts left, the economy cools, inflation falls. This is the same destination fiscal policy aims for — shifting aggregate demand — reached by a completely different route.

<Callout type="tip" title="Two tools, one target">
Fiscal and monetary policy both ultimately move **aggregate demand**, just with different levers. Fiscal policy works through the budget (spending and taxes); monetary policy works through interest rates and the money supply. In a downturn they often pull together — a stimulus package *and* rate cuts — to give AD a bigger push.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Monetary policy** is the **central bank's** use of **interest rates** and the **money supply** to steer aggregate demand; the bank is usually **independent** of elected government.
- The bank moves its **policy rate** through **open-market operations**: buying bonds pushes rates down (looser); selling bonds pushes rates up (tighter).
- **Lower rates → cheaper borrowing → more investment and spending → AD shifts right** (the cure for recession). Higher rates run the chain in reverse to cool inflation.
- This **transmission mechanism** reaches the same target as fiscal policy — aggregate demand — by a different route, and the two tools often work together.

</Step>
