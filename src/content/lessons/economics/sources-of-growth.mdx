<Step title="What actually makes an economy grow?" kind="explain">

We know growth means rising output per person. But *where does the extra output come from?* You can't conjure more goods from thin air — something real has to change. Economists trace growth to a short list of ingredients, the **engines of growth**. Switch them on and an economy produces more from the same population; leave them off and living standards stagnate.

There are four engines, and understanding them tells you why some countries pull ahead and how poor countries can catch up.

</Step>

<Step title="The four engines" kind="explain">

**1. Labour.** More workers — a larger or more fully employed workforce — produce more total output. But by itself, adding people raises *total* GDP without necessarily raising GDP *per person*. Labour fills the engine room; it doesn't, on its own, make each worker richer.

**2. Physical capital.** These are the tools, machines, factories, roads, and computers workers use. Give a farmer a tractor instead of a hoe and output per worker jumps. Building up the **capital stock** is one of the surest ways to raise productivity — though it runs into *diminishing returns* (the tenth tractor adds less than the first).

**3. Human capital.** This is the knowledge, skills, health, and experience embodied in workers themselves — built through education, training, and good health. A literate, skilled, healthy workforce produces far more than an untrained one with the same machines. Human capital is why education is treated as an investment, not just a cost.

**4. Technology.** New ideas and better ways of doing things — the recipe, not the ingredients. Technology is the special engine: it doesn't just add to output, it **multiplies the productivity of every other input**. The same workers and machines, organized around a better idea, simply produce more.

</Step>

<Step title="It all comes down to productivity" kind="explain">

These engines share a common thread: **productivity** — output per worker per hour. Capital, human capital, and technology all matter precisely because they make each hour of work yield more. As one economist quipped, "Productivity isn't everything, but in the long run it is almost everything." A country's living standard depends almost entirely on how much its workers can produce.

In the long run, the heaviest lifting is done by **technology**, because it's the only engine without sharp diminishing returns — there's no obvious limit to better ideas. Capital deepening eventually slows as machines pile up; new knowledge can keep growth going indefinitely.

<Callout type="tip" title="Capital deepening vs. innovation">
Adding more machines to existing workers — *capital deepening* — gives a one-time boost that fades as returns diminish. Sustained, long-run growth comes from *innovation*: a steady stream of new technology that keeps raising what each machine and worker can do.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- Growth comes from four **engines**: labour, physical capital, human capital, and technology.
- **Labour** raises total output but not necessarily output per person; **physical capital** (tools, machines) raises productivity but hits diminishing returns.
- **Human capital** (skills, education, health) and **technology** (better ideas) make the same inputs far more productive.
- **Technology** is the most powerful long-run engine — it multiplies every other input and has no obvious limit.
- All engines work through **productivity** (output per hour), which is what ultimately determines living standards.
- On a PPF, growth from these engines **shifts the whole frontier outward**.

</Step>
