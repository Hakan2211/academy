<Step title="The most reliable rule in economics" kind="explain">

Ask any shopkeeper what happens when they cut a price, and they'll tell you the same thing economists do: people buy more. Raise the price, and they buy less. That stubborn, almost universal pattern has a name — the **law of demand**.

Stated carefully: *holding everything else constant, as the price of a good rises, the quantity people want to buy falls; as the price falls, the quantity rises.* Price and quantity demanded move in **opposite** directions.

Why is it so reliable? Two forces push the same way. First, when something gets cheaper, your money stretches further, so you can afford more of it (the **income effect**). Second, when something gets cheaper relative to its alternatives, you swap toward it and away from the now-pricier substitutes (the **substitution effect**). Together they mean a lower price almost always coaxes out a larger quantity demanded.

</Step>

<Step title="From a schedule to a curve" kind="explain">

Economists capture this with a **demand schedule** — a simple list of how much buyers would purchase at each possible price. Suppose at a price of 90 buyers want 20 units; at 70 they want 40; at 50 they want 60; at 30 they want 80. Read down the list and a pattern jumps out: every time the price drops, the quantity climbs.

Plot those pairs with price on the vertical axis and quantity on the horizontal axis, connect them, and you get the **demand curve** — a line that slopes **downward** from upper-left to lower-right. The downward slope *is* the law of demand, drawn.

<Callout type="info" title="Why price goes on the vertical axis">
It looks backwards — price usually feels like the "cause." But economists have drawn it this way since Alfred Marshall in the 1890s, and the convention stuck. Just remember: quantity along the bottom, price up the side.
</Callout>

</Step>

Notice the crucial distinction. Changing the **price** slides the dot *along a fixed curve* — that's a change in **quantity demanded**. Toggling a shifter (richer buyers, more buyers, a fashion change) moves the *entire curve* sideways — that's a change in **demand** itself. We'll dig into those shifters next lesson; for now, just feel the difference between moving *along* a curve and *shifting* it.

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- The **law of demand**: holding all else equal, a higher price means a lower **quantity demanded**, and vice versa — they move in opposite directions.
- It is driven by the **income effect** (cheaper goods stretch your budget) and the **substitution effect** (you switch toward what got cheaper).
- A **demand schedule** lists quantity at each price; plotting it gives the **demand curve**, which slopes **downward**.
- By convention, **price** is on the vertical axis, **quantity** on the horizontal.
- Changing the good's **own price** = a movement **along** the curve (change in *quantity demanded*); other changes **shift** the whole curve (change in *demand*).

</Step>
