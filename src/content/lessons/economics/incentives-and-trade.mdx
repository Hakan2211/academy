<Step title="People respond to incentives" kind="explain">

If you remember one phrase from all of economics, make it this: **people respond to incentives.** An incentive is anything that rewards or punishes an action — a price, a fine, a subsidy, a bonus, a tax.

Change the incentive and behaviour changes, often in ways nobody intended. Put a tax on sugary drinks and people buy fewer. Offer a cash bonus per sale and watch effort rise. Cap apartment rents below the market and landlords stop building apartments. Economists analyse almost everything by asking: *what are the incentives here, and how will people respond?*

The trickiest part is **unintended consequences**: a well-meant rule that changes incentives in a surprising direction. A law requiring car seats made buckling kids safer — but by making a third child harder to seat, some studies suggest it nudged a few families to have fewer children. Incentives reach further than we expect.

</Step>

<Step title="Why trade leaves everyone richer" kind="explain">

Incentives also explain one of economics' most counter-intuitive truths: **trade can make both sides better off at once.** It isn't a zero-sum fight where one wins only if the other loses.

The secret is **specialisation**. Instead of each person (or country) trying to make everything themselves, each concentrates on what they do at the lowest opportunity cost, then trades for the rest. A doctor *could* mow her own lawn, but her time is far more valuable healing patients — so she hires a gardener and both gain. The gardener gets income; the doctor gets her afternoon back to do what only she can do well.

Multiply that across millions of people and you get the modern economy: nobody is self-sufficient, everybody specialises, and trade weaves it all together — leaving the whole far richer than the sum of isolated parts.

</Step>

<Step title="Consume beyond your own frontier" kind="interactive">

Recall the production possibilities frontier — the limit of what you could make alone. Here's the magic of trade: by specialising and exchanging, you can **consume at a point beyond your own PPF**, something impossible in isolation.

On your own you're stuck on (or inside) this curve. Specialise in what you're relatively best at, sell it, and buy the rest — and you effectively reach combinations the curve says you couldn't produce yourself. Trade is the closest thing economics has to a free lunch.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **People respond to incentives** — the single most reliable idea in economics.
- Changing incentives changes behaviour, sometimes via surprising **unintended consequences**.
- **Trade is not zero-sum**: specialisation plus exchange can make all parties better off.
- Each party specialises where its **opportunity cost is lowest**, then trades for the rest.
- Trade lets you **consume beyond your own production frontier** — impossible alone.

</Step>
