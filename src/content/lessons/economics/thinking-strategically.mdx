<Step title="When your best move depends on theirs" kind="explain">

Almost everything you've learned so far assumed you were a small player in a big market — one shopper among millions, one firm among many — too tiny for your single choice to move the price. But step into the world of the **few**, where what you should do depends entirely on what *someone else* does, and a new kind of reasoning takes over: **strategic** thinking.

A chess player choosing a move, two superpowers deciding whether to disarm, rival airlines setting fares, friends picking a meeting spot — none can plan in isolation. Each must ask: *what will the other do, and how does that change what I should do?* That circular question is the subject of **game theory** — the study of decisions where outcomes depend on the choices of others.

This is the summit of the whole island. Game theory takes the spirit of economics — people respond to incentives — and turns it loose far beyond markets: on cooperation, conflict, trust, evolution, and the design of the rules themselves.

</Step>

<Step title="The anatomy of a game" kind="explain">

Strip any strategic situation down and you find three ingredients — the bones of every **game**:

- **Players** — the decision-makers (two suspects, three firms, a buyer and a seller).
- **Strategies** — the choices available to each player (confess or stay silent; raise prices or hold).
- **Payoffs** — what each player gets for every *combination* of choices. Crucially, your payoff depends not just on your move but on everyone's together.

The defining feature is **interdependence**: no player controls the outcome alone. A "game" here doesn't mean something frivolous — it just means a situation with these three parts. War, marriage, voting, and pollution treaties are all games in this sense.

</Step>

<Step title="Reading a payoff matrix" kind="interactive">

When there are two players who each have two strategies, we can lay the whole game out in a **payoff matrix** — a 2×2 grid. The row player picks a row; the column player picks a column; where they cross sits the pair of payoffs, one for each.

Pick a strategy for each player and read the cell. Then switch between three famous games — each uses the *same* grid machinery but produces totally different logic.

<PayoffMatrix />

In the **Prisoner's Dilemma**, one move (confess) is better no matter what the other does — a **dominant strategy**. In **Coordination**, there's no dominant move at all: you simply want to match. In **Chicken**, you want to do the opposite of the other player. Same skeleton, three different strategic worlds — that's the power of laying a game out this way.

</Step>

<Step title="Dominant strategies and looking ahead" kind="explain">

A **dominant strategy** is one that gives a player a better payoff than the alternative *regardless* of what anyone else does. When you have one, the choice is easy: just play it, no guessing required. Much of game theory is about the harder cases where no dominant strategy exists and you must anticipate the other player.

The core mental move is **putting yourself in the other's shoes**. Before you choose, ask: *if I were them, what would I do? And given that, what's best for me?* Skilled strategists reason several steps deep — "if I do this, they'll respond with that, so then I'd want..." This is exactly how a chess player thinks, and exactly how the firms in an oligopoly size each other up.

</Step>

<Step title="Games are everywhere" kind="explain">

You've already met a game on this island without naming it. Back in **market structures**, two firms in an oligopoly each chose whether to price high or low — and discovered that undercutting was tempting for both, dragging them into a price war neither wanted. That was a payoff matrix in disguise.

Game theory generalises that insight far past pricing. The same few players, strategies, payoffs framework explains arms races, traffic jams, fishing fleets emptying the ocean, vaccination, and why your group project always has one free rider. Once you can spot the structure of a game, you can predict — and sometimes redesign — the outcome.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Game theory** studies decisions where your best move depends on what others do — situations of **interdependence**.
- Every game has three parts: **players**, **strategies**, and **payoffs**, displayed for two-by-two games in a **payoff matrix**.
- A **dominant strategy** is best no matter what others do; when none exists, you must anticipate the other player.
- The key skill is putting yourself in the other player's shoes and reasoning several steps ahead.
- The same structure recurs everywhere — pricing, arms races, traffic, the commons — which is why game theory reaches far beyond markets.

</Step>
