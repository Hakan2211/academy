<Step title="The economy's workshops" kind="explain">

Behind almost everything you buy sits a **firm** — an organisation that takes resources and turns them into goods and services people want. A bakery turns flour, ovens and labour into bread. A software company turns engineers, laptops and electricity into apps. Firms are where production actually happens.

To produce anything, a firm combines the **factors of production** — the basic inputs economists sort into four buckets:

- **Land** — natural resources: the soil, minerals, water and physical space a firm uses.
- **Labour** — human effort and skill, from the cashier to the chief engineer.
- **Capital** — the tools, machines, buildings and equipment that help labour produce more.
- **Entrepreneurship** — the person who brings the other three together, takes the risk and organises production.

Each factor earns a reward: land earns **rent**, labour earns **wages**, capital earns **interest**, and the entrepreneur earns **profit**.

</Step>

<Step title="The goal: profit" kind="explain">

Why do firms bother? In a market economy, the answer is **profit** — what is left of revenue after costs are paid.

<Formula tex="\text{Profit} = \text{Total Revenue} - \text{Total Cost}" block />

Total revenue is straightforward: it is the price of each unit times the number of units sold. Total cost is trickier, and getting it right is the heart of how firms decide what to do. The pursuit of profit is the incentive that pushes firms to find the cheapest way to make what customers value most — Adam Smith's famous invisible hand at work.

But there are two very different ways to measure that cost, and they give two very different pictures of how a business is really doing.

</Step>

<Step title="Accounting profit vs economic profit" kind="explain">

An accountant counts only **explicit costs** — the money that actually leaves the firm's bank account: wages, rent, materials, electricity. Subtract those from revenue and you get **accounting profit**.

Economists insist on counting one more thing: **implicit costs**, the value of resources the owner already owns and uses up. The biggest is **opportunity cost** — the income the owner gives up by not doing the next-best thing with their time and money.

<Formula tex="\text{Economic Profit} = \text{Revenue} - \text{Explicit Costs} - \text{Implicit Costs}" block />

Imagine you quit a €60,000-a-year job to run a café. The café brings in €200,000 and pays out €150,000 in explicit costs, so your accounting profit is €50,000. But you gave up a €60,000 salary to be here. Your **economic** profit is €50,000 − €60,000 = **−€10,000**. On paper you made money; in reality you would have been €10,000 better off keeping your old job.

<Callout type="tip">
When economists say a firm earns "zero economic profit," that is not failure — it means the firm is doing exactly as well as its owner's next-best alternative. It is a perfectly healthy, normal outcome.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- A **firm** combines the four **factors of production** — land, labour, capital, entrepreneurship — to make goods and services.
- Firms are driven by **profit**: total revenue minus total cost.
- **Accounting profit** subtracts only explicit (out-of-pocket) costs; **economic profit** also subtracts **implicit** costs like the owner's opportunity cost.
- **Zero economic profit** means the firm matches its next-best alternative — a normal, healthy result, not a loss.

</Step>
