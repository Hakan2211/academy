<Step title="The cost you don't see on the price tag" kind="explain">

Scarcity forces choices, and here is the deep consequence: **the true cost of anything is what you give up to get it.** Economists call this its **opportunity cost** — the value of the next-best option you passed up.

Notice this cost is often invisible. If you spend a free evening watching a film, the price tag says zero. But the real cost is whatever you *would* have done instead — studied, earned money at a shift, slept. That forgone alternative is the opportunity cost, and it's just as real as cash out of your wallet.

This is why economists love the line: **"there's no such thing as a free lunch."** Even a gift you didn't pay for costs you the time and attention you could have spent elsewhere.

</Step>

<Step title="Spend a Saturday" kind="interactive">

Opportunity cost is always about the *single* best alternative — not the whole pile of things you didn't do. Pick how to spend a free day and watch which option becomes its cost.

<OpportunityCost />

When you choose one option, every other option is ruled out — but the opportunity cost is just the most valuable one among them. That's the one you'll quietly miss.

</Step>

<Step title="It's measured in whatever is scarce" kind="explain">

Opportunity cost isn't only about money. It's measured in whatever resource the choice uses up:

- **Time** — an hour spent commuting can't be spent with family.
- **Money** — €1,000 spent on a holiday can't also buy a course.
- **Resources** — farmland used for corn can't grow wheat this season.

A powerful habit follows: when deciding, don't ask "can I afford this?" alone — ask **"what am I giving up?"** A "free" two-hour meeting isn't free if those two hours were your most productive of the week. Thinking in opportunity costs is one of the most useful mental upgrades economics offers.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Opportunity cost** = the value of the **next-best alternative** you give up when you choose.
- It's often invisible — no price tag — but every bit as real as money spent.
- It's the single best option forgone, not the sum of everything you didn't pick.
- It's measured in whatever is scarce: time, money, or resources.
- Smart decisions ask not just "can I afford it?" but **"what am I giving up?"**

</Step>
