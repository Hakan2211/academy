<Step title="The great divergence" kind="explain">

The gap between the world's richest and poorest countries is staggering. A typical person in a wealthy nation produces and earns *thirty or more times* what a typical person in the poorest does. They were not always so far apart — for most of history, everyone was roughly equally poor. The yawning gap we see today opened up over the last two centuries, as some countries caught the engines of growth and others did not. Economists call this the **great divergence**, and explaining it — and closing it — is the work of **development economics**.

Why did growth take off in some places and stall in others? The answer is less about geography or natural resources than you might expect, and more about something harder to see.

</Step>

<Step title="Institutions: the deepest cause" kind="explain">

The engines of growth — capital, human capital, technology — don't fire on their own. People have to *choose* to build factories, get educated, and invent. They make those long-term investments only when they expect to keep the rewards. What guarantees that? **Institutions**: the rules of the game.

Good institutions include **secure property rights** (your business won't be seized), the **rule of law** (contracts are enforced, courts are fair), **low corruption**, **stable government**, and **open markets**. Where these exist, people invest, innovate, and trade — and growth follows. Where they're missing — where a strongman can confiscate your land, where bribes are needed at every step, where the law protects the powerful — even abundant resources and aid fail to produce growth. The investments simply never get made.

This is why two countries with similar geography and resources can end up worlds apart. The classic illustration is a single city split by a border: same climate, same people, same history — but one side, with secure property and the rule of law, prospers, while the other, without them, stays poor.

<Callout type="info" title="Why not just send machines?">
Decades of experience taught development economists a hard lesson: shipping capital and technology to a poor country rarely sparks lasting growth on its own. Without institutions that make investment safe and reward effort, the machines rust and the knowledge goes unused. Institutions come first.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="The poverty trap" kind="explain">

Poor countries face a cruel bind called the **poverty trap**. Growth needs investment — in tools, schools, infrastructure. Investment needs *saving*, which means consuming less than you produce. But a country so poor that its people can barely eat has nothing left to save. With no saving, there's no investment; with no investment, no growth; with no growth, the country stays too poor to save. The cycle feeds on itself.

Weak institutions tighten the trap further: even what little could be invested gets eaten by corruption or scared off by insecurity. Breaking out usually takes some combination of a big external push (aid, debt relief, foreign investment) *and* the institutional reforms that let that push translate into lasting growth rather than evaporating.

</Step>

<Step title="The catch-up effect" kind="explain">

Here's the hopeful side. A poor country that *does* establish decent institutions has a powerful advantage: the **catch-up effect**, or **convergence**. Because it starts far behind, it can grow *faster* than rich countries — for two reasons.

First, **diminishing returns to capital** run in its favour: the first machines and roads in a capital-starved economy yield huge gains, while a rich country, already saturated with capital, gets only modest returns from adding more. Second, a poor country can **borrow technology** rather than invent it — adopting proven ideas, methods, and machines off the shelf, skipping the costly trial and error the leaders had to do.

Together these mean income levels can **converge**: the poor, if they get the conditions right, climb toward the rich. South Korea, Taiwan, and China rode exactly this dynamic, growing for decades at rates the rich world could never match — and converging.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- The **great divergence**: rich and poor nations, once roughly equal, split apart over the last two centuries.
- **Institutions** — secure property, rule of law, low corruption, open markets — are the deepest cause of who grows. They make the engines of growth fire.
- The **poverty trap**: too poor to save → no investment → no growth → still too poor. Weak institutions deepen it.
- The **catch-up effect (convergence)**: poor countries *can* grow faster thanks to diminishing returns to capital and the ability to borrow proven technology.
- Convergence is conditional — it happens only when institutions reward investment; otherwise countries stay trapped.

</Step>
