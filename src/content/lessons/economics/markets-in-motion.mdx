<Step title="Putting the pieces together" kind="explain">

You now have all four moving parts: the downward demand curve, the upward supply curve, the shifters that move each one, and the equilibrium where they cross. This deepdive ties them into a single skill — the one economists use constantly: **predicting how an equilibrium changes when the world changes.**

The world rarely sits still. A drought, a tax, a tech breakthrough, a viral trend — each ripples through prices and quantities. The good news is that *every* such story can be cracked with one disciplined routine. Master it here, and you'll reuse it in every world to come.

</Step>

<Step title="The four-step method" kind="explain">

For any change hitting a market, ask four questions in order:

1. **Which curve shifts — demand, supply, or both?** Decide whether the event changes what *buyers* want at each price (demand) or what *sellers* offer at each price (supply). Remember the acid test: if it isn't the good's *own* price, a curve shifts.
2. **Which direction — right or left?** Right means more at every price (an increase); left means less (a decrease).
3. **Draw the shift** on the diagram and find the **new crossing point**.
4. **Read off the new equilibrium price and quantity**, and compare to the old.

That's it. A single shift always gives a clear answer for *both* price and quantity. For example: a **demand increase** (right) raises price *and* quantity; a **supply decrease** (left) raises price but lowers quantity.

</Step>

<Step title="Single shifts, the four cases" kind="explain">

It's worth memorising the four single-shift outcomes — they cover an enormous amount of economic news:

- **Demand up (right):** price ↑, quantity ↑.
- **Demand down (left):** price ↓, quantity ↓.
- **Supply up (right):** price ↓, quantity ↑.
- **Supply down (left):** price ↑, quantity ↓.

Notice the pattern: with a **demand** shift, price and quantity move *together*; with a **supply** shift, they move in *opposite* directions. That one observation lets you reconstruct any case from scratch.

</Step>

<Step title="When both curves move" kind="explain">

Reality often shifts both curves at once, and that's where the four-step method earns its keep. The rule: **when both curves shift, one of price or quantity is predictable, but the other is ambiguous — it depends on which shift is bigger.**

Take demand up *and* supply up (say, a popular product that also gets cheaper to make). Both shifts raise quantity, so quantity *definitely* rises. But demand-up pushes price *up* while supply-up pushes price *down* — so the price could go either way depending on which shift dominates. You can pin down one variable for sure and must reason about magnitudes for the other.

<Callout type="tip" title="Find the variable both shifts agree on">
When two curves move, identify the variable both shifts push the same way — that one is certain. The variable they push opposite ways is ambiguous without knowing the relative sizes. Naming the certain one first keeps the analysis clean.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="When prices aren't free to move: price controls" kind="explain">

Everything so far assumed prices can adjust freely to clear the market. But sometimes a government *forbids* that adjustment with a **price control** — a legal limit on how high or low a price may go. When it binds, the self-correcting mechanism is switched off, and the surplus or shortage *persists*.

- A **price ceiling** is a legal **maximum** price (think rent control, or capped fuel prices). It only bites when set *below* equilibrium — and then it locks in a permanent **shortage**: buyers want more than sellers will provide at the capped price. Queues, waiting lists, and quality decline follow.
- A **price floor** is a legal **minimum** price (think a minimum wage, or farm price supports). It only bites when set *above* equilibrium — locking in a permanent **surplus**: sellers offer more than buyers want. Unsold output, or in a labour market, unemployment.

A control set on the "harmless" side of equilibrium (a ceiling above, or a floor below the market price) does nothing at all — the market already clears within the allowed range.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- The **four-step method**: (1) which curve shifts, (2) which direction, (3) draw it, (4) read the new equilibrium.
- **Single shifts:** demand moves price and quantity *together*; supply moves them *oppositely*. Memorise the four cases.
- **Both curves shift:** one variable is certain, the other ambiguous (it depends on the relative sizes of the shifts).
- A **price ceiling** (legal maximum) below equilibrium causes a lasting **shortage**; a **price floor** (legal minimum) above equilibrium causes a lasting **surplus**.
- A control on the harmless side of equilibrium has **no effect**.
- Price controls don't suspend supply and demand — they turn a price signal into a quantity imbalance.

</Step>
