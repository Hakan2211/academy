<Step title="One more, or not?" kind="explain">

Most real decisions aren't all-or-nothing. You rarely choose between "no education" and "infinite education," or "starve" and "eat forever." You choose **one more** — one more year of study, one more slice of pizza, one more hour at work.

Economists call these **marginal** choices, and they argue that rational decision-making means weighing the **marginal benefit** (the extra benefit from one more unit) against the **marginal cost** (the extra cost of that unit). The guiding rule is simple:

> Keep doing something as long as its marginal benefit is at least as big as its marginal cost — and stop when they meet.

</Step>

<Step title="Find the sweet spot" kind="interactive">

Add units one at a time. Each extra unit brings a little less benefit and a little more cost. Watch where pushing further starts to hurt.

<MarginalThinking />

The best stopping point isn't where total benefit is highest, and it isn't "as much as possible." It's the last unit where the **marginal** benefit still covers the **marginal** cost. One step past that, you're losing value.

</Step>

<Step title="The trap of sunk costs" kind="explain">

Marginal thinking comes with a warning about its evil twin: the **sunk cost**. A sunk cost is money or effort already spent that you can't get back — and because it's gone no matter what you do next, it should play **no role** in your next decision.

You've paid €50 for a concert ticket, but on the night you feel ill and miserable. "I paid for it, so I have to go" is the sunk-cost fallacy. The €50 is gone either way. The only sensible question is the marginal one: *from here forward*, will going make you better or worse off than staying home? Decide at the margin, and let the past stay in the past.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- Most choices are **marginal** — about one more unit, not all-or-nothing.
- Rational rule: act while **marginal benefit ≥ marginal cost**; stop where they cross.
- The optimum is *not* "maximum total" or "as much as possible" — it's the last worthwhile unit.
- **Sunk costs** (already spent, unrecoverable) should be ignored in forward-looking decisions.

</Step>
