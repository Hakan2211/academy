<Step title="A wage is just a price" kind="explain">

You already know how supply and demand set a price in a market for, say, coffee. The labor market works exactly the same way — only here the "good" being traded is **work**, and its price is the **wage**.

The two sides swap roles, though. In the market for coffee, firms supply and households demand. In the labor market it flips: **households supply labor** (you offer your time and effort) and **firms demand it** (they hire workers to produce things). The wage is the price that brings the two together.

That single insight — *a wage is a price, set by supply and demand* — explains an enormous amount about who earns what, and why.

</Step>

<Step title="Why firms demand labor" kind="explain">

Firms don't want workers for their own sake. They hire a worker only because that worker helps produce something the firm can sell. So the demand for labor is a **derived demand** — derived from the demand for whatever the workers make.

How much is one more worker worth to a firm? Exactly the extra revenue that worker brings in: the **marginal revenue product of labor** — the worker's extra output multiplied by the price it sells for.

<Formula tex="MRP_L = \text{(extra output)} \times \text{(price of output)}" block />

A firm keeps hiring as long as the next worker's MRP is at least the wage, and stops when they meet — pure marginal thinking. Because each extra worker tends to add a little less output than the last, the MRP falls as employment rises. That falling MRP **is** the firm's labor-demand curve: firms hire more workers only when the wage drops.

Labor supply slopes the other way. A higher wage makes work more attractive and tempts more people in, so the quantity of labor supplied rises with the wage.

</Step>

<Step title="Find the equilibrium wage" kind="interactive">

Below, downward-sloping labor demand (firms) meets upward-sloping labor supply (workers). Where they cross is the **equilibrium wage** and the number of workers hired.

Now slide worker productivity. Because labor demand is derived from how much value workers produce, more productive workers shift the whole demand curve — and the wage with it.

<LaborMarket />

Notice the direction: when workers become more productive, demand moves **right**, and *both* the wage and employment rise. This is the deep reason wages climb over time and differ across jobs — they track productivity. It also means a wage isn't handed down by an employer's generosity; it emerges from the meeting of two curves.

</Step>

<Step title="What moves the curves" kind="explain">

Anything that changes how valuable workers are shifts **labor demand**: better technology or training, a higher price for the firm's product, or simply more capital and machinery to work with all raise the marginal revenue product and push demand right. A slump in the product's demand pushes it left.

**Labor supply** shifts too: a bigger or more skilled population, immigration, or work becoming more attractive (better conditions, lower taxes) all push supply right, which — holding demand fixed — tends to lower the wage while raising employment.

Real wages in rich countries have risen over the long run mainly because demand has risen: workers armed with more education, technology and capital simply produce more, so they are worth more.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- The labor market is supply and demand with the roles flipped: **households supply** labor, **firms demand** it, and the **wage** is the price.
- Firms' demand for labor is **derived** from the demand for what workers make; a worker is worth their **marginal revenue product** (extra output times its price).
- Labor demand slopes down (each extra worker adds a bit less); labor supply slopes up (higher wages attract more workers). They cross at the **equilibrium wage**.
- More productive workers shift labor demand **right**, raising both the wage and employment — which is why **wages ultimately track productivity**.

</Step>
