<Step title="No country makes everything" kind="explain">

Look around the room you're in. The phone in your pocket, the coffee in your cup, the clothes on your back — almost none of it was made entirely in your own country. Modern life runs on **international trade**, and yet trade is one of the ideas people find hardest to accept. It *feels* like a contest: if they sell to us, surely we lose.

Economists have spent two centuries showing the opposite. Trade between nations works for the same reason trade between people works — it lets each party do what it does best and swap for the rest. The puzzle is figuring out exactly *what* each country should make. The answer turns out to be surprising, and it rests on a distinction between two kinds of "being good at something."

</Step>

<Step title="Absolute vs comparative advantage" kind="explain">

There are two ways a country can be "good" at making a good.

A country has an **absolute advantage** in a good if it can simply produce *more* of it with the same resources — it is more productive in raw terms. If Portugal can make more wine per worker than England, Portugal has the absolute advantage in wine.

But the idea that actually decides who should make what is **comparative advantage**: a country has it in the good it can produce at the lower **opportunity cost** — the good it gives up the *least* of something else to make. This is the deep insight, first nailed down by David Ricardo in 1817.

Here is the part that breaks people's intuition. A country can have an absolute advantage in *everything* — be more productive at every single good — and **still** benefit from trade. Why? Because it cannot have a comparative advantage in everything. Being relatively better at one thing necessarily means being relatively worse at another. Opportunity cost is a trade-off, and trade-offs always cut both ways.

</Step>

<Step title="Where the gains come from" kind="explain">

The magic isn't that trade lets a country make more with the same resources — it can't. The magic is in **reallocation**. When each country pours its effort into the good it sacrifices the least to produce, the *world* produces more wine and more cloth from the very same labour. That extra output is the **gains from trade**, and the two countries split it by exchanging at a price between their two opportunity costs.

This is why "we should make everything ourselves" is almost always a mistake. A self-sufficient country pins itself somewhere on its own modest frontier. A trading country specialises, then trades to consume a bundle *beyond* what it could ever produce alone. Trade doesn't shift your production frontier — it lets you escape it.

<Callout type="tip" title="The doctor and the gardener">
Even if a surgeon is also the fastest gardener in town, she should still hire a gardener. Every hour she spends weeding is an hour not spent in surgery — a huge opportunity cost. Her comparative advantage is surgery, the gardener's is gardening, and both come out ahead. Nations work exactly the same way.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- No country is self-sufficient; modern life runs on **international trade**.
- **Absolute advantage** = producing more of a good with the same resources (raw productivity).
- **Comparative advantage** = producing a good at a lower **opportunity cost** — and this is what decides who should make what.
- A country can hold an absolute advantage in everything but a comparative advantage in only some things — so trade still pays.
- The gains come from **reallocating** effort to each country's lowest-opportunity-cost good, letting both consume **beyond** their own frontiers.

</Step>
