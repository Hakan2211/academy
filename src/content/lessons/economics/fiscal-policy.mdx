<Step title="The government's two levers" kind="explain">

The last world showed that the economy can settle *below* its potential — a recessionary gap with idle workers and wasted capacity — or *above* it, an inflationary gap that overheats and bids up prices. Self-correction works, but slowly. So who can speed things along?

One answer is the government's budget. **Fiscal policy** is the use of **government spending** and **taxes** to influence aggregate demand. It works directly on two of the four components of AD — C + I + G + NX:

- **Government spending (G)** is part of aggregate demand by definition. When the government builds roads, hires teachers, or buys equipment, it adds to total spending immediately.
- **Taxes** work *indirectly* through consumption (C) and investment (I). Cut income taxes and households keep more of their pay, so they spend more. Cut business taxes and firms have more to invest.

Both levers, then, push the same curve: **aggregate demand**. The art of fiscal policy is choosing which way, and how hard, to push.

</Step>

<Step title="Expansionary vs contractionary" kind="explain">

Fiscal policy comes in two flavours, named for what they do to aggregate demand.

**Expansionary fiscal policy** *increases* AD. The government either spends more or taxes less (or both). It is the medicine for a **recession**: a recessionary gap means output is below potential, so shifting AD right lifts output back toward full employment. The price: bigger budget deficits, since spending rises or revenue falls.

**Contractionary fiscal policy** *decreases* AD. The government spends less or taxes more. It is the medicine for an **overheating** economy: an inflationary gap means demand is running ahead of potential, so pulling AD left cools things and tames inflation.

<Callout type="info" title="A handy mnemonic">
Expansionary = spend up **or** tax down. Contractionary = spend down **or** tax up. The two levers can point the same direction (spend up *and* cut taxes for a strong stimulus) or be mixed and matched.
</Callout>

There's a subtlety worth flagging: a dollar of government spending and a dollar of tax cut don't pack the same punch. Government spending hits AD in full straight away; a tax cut only boosts AD by the part households actually *spend* rather than save. So spending changes tend to move AD by more than equal-sized tax changes — a point that matters when the multiplier from last world amplifies whatever the first injection is.

</Step>

<Step title="Stabilisers that work on autopilot" kind="explain">

Not all fiscal policy needs a vote in parliament. Some of it kicks in automatically as the economy moves — these are **automatic stabilizers**, and they are fiscal policy's quiet workhorses.

When a recession hits and incomes fall, two things happen *without anyone deciding*:

- **Tax revenue drops.** Income and sales taxes are tied to earnings and spending, so they fall as the economy shrinks — leaving households with relatively more to spend, cushioning the fall.
- **Transfer payments rise.** Unemployment benefits and welfare automatically increase as more people lose jobs, putting money back into the hands most likely to spend it.

The reverse happens in a boom: revenue surges and benefit payments shrink, gently restraining an overheating economy. Because they need no new legislation, automatic stabilizers act *instantly* — exactly when discretionary policy is still stuck in committee. They don't eliminate the business cycle, but they dampen it, smoothing the wobbles you met in the business-cycle lesson.

The flip side: in a slump, automatic stabilizers swing the budget toward deficit all on their own. That deficit isn't reckless spending — it's the stabilizers doing their job. Untangling that automatic part from *deliberate* policy choices is something you'll weigh when we look at budgets and debt next.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Fiscal policy** uses **government spending (G)** and **taxes** to shift **aggregate demand**.
- **Expansionary** policy (spend up *or* tax down) shifts AD **right** — the cure for a **recessionary gap**, at the cost of a bigger deficit.
- **Contractionary** policy (spend down *or* tax up) shifts AD **left** — the cure for an **inflationary gap**.
- A dollar of spending moves AD by more than a dollar of tax cut, because households save part of a tax cut; the **multiplier** amplifies whichever injection occurs.
- **Automatic stabilizers** — taxes that fall and benefits that rise in a downturn — act instantly with no new legislation, dampening the business cycle on autopilot.

</Step>
