<Step title="The bank of banks" kind="explain">

Above all the ordinary commercial banks sits one special institution: the **central bank**. In the United States it's the **Federal Reserve** (the "Fed"); in the eurozone it's the **European Central Bank** (ECB); most countries have one. It is not where you keep your checking account — it's the bank *for the banks*, and the issuer of the nation's money.

A central bank has two great jobs. First, it **controls the money supply** — the total amount of money flowing through the economy. Second, it stands behind the banking system as a backstop in a crisis. Crucially, a central bank is usually kept **independent** of day-to-day politics, so that the temptation to print money for short-term gain doesn't override the long-term goal of stable money.

Because the banking system *multiplies* money (the previous lesson), the central bank doesn't have to touch every dollar. It nudges a few key inputs and the whole multiplier machine amplifies the effect across the economy.

</Step>

<Step title="The three levers" kind="explain">

The central bank can't simply order people to hold more or less money. Instead it pulls three levers that make the money supply expand or contract *through* the banking system:

- **Open-market operations** — the central bank buys or sells government bonds. *Buying* bonds pays banks with fresh reserves, which they lend out, so money expands. *Selling* bonds drains reserves back in, contracting money. This is the everyday workhorse tool.
- **The reserve requirement** — the minimum reserve ratio banks must hold. Lower it and each dollar of reserves supports more loans (a bigger multiplier); raise it and lending shrinks.
- **The policy interest rate** — the rate at the heart of the banking system. *Cutting* it makes borrowing cheaper throughout the economy, so households and firms borrow and spend more; *raising* it cools borrowing down.

</Step>

Pick a tool, set it, and watch the money supply respond. Each lever moves the same target — how much money flows through the economy — from a different angle.

Notice the shared logic: every tool either feeds more reserves into the banking system, lets each reserve dollar stretch further, or changes how eager people are to borrow. In each case the multiplier you met last lesson amplifies the central bank's small nudge into a large change in the money supply.

</Step>

<Step title="Lender of last resort" kind="explain">

The central bank's second great job appears only in emergencies — but it may be the most important of all. Recall the danger of fractional-reserve banking: if too many depositors demand their cash at once, even a perfectly healthy bank can run out, because most of its money is tied up in loans. That's a **bank run**, and panic is contagious — a run on one bank can spark runs on others.

This is where the central bank acts as the **lender of last resort**. Because it can create reserves on demand, it can lend freely to a solvent bank caught in a panic, guaranteeing there's always enough cash to meet withdrawals. Once depositors *know* the central bank stands behind the system, the reason to panic largely vanishes — so the backstop often works just by existing.

<Callout type="info" title="Why a modern banking system needs a backstop">
Fractional-reserve banking and the lender of last resort are two halves of one design. Lending out most deposits makes the system productive but fragile; a central bank that can supply reserves in a crisis is what makes that fragility safe to live with. The dramatic interventions of 2008 and 2020 were this role in action.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- The **central bank** (the Fed, the ECB) is the bank *for banks* and the issuer of money, usually kept **independent** of politics.
- Its two great jobs: **control the money supply** and act as a crisis backstop.
- Three levers: **open-market operations** (buy/sell bonds), the **reserve requirement**, and the **policy interest rate** — each expands or contracts money through the banking system's multiplier.
- As **lender of last resort**, it can create reserves to rescue solvent banks in a panic, stopping bank runs.
- These two roles are the safety net that lets a fragile fractional-reserve system function.

</Step>
