<Step title="An apparent bargain" kind="explain">

Policymakers chasing low unemployment with expansionary policy noticed something in the data. In 1958 the economist A.W. Phillips plotted decades of British figures and found a striking pattern: years of **low unemployment** were years of **high wage inflation**, and years of high unemployment had low inflation. Plot inflation against unemployment and the points traced a smooth **downward-sloping curve** — the **Phillips curve**.

The economic logic is intuitive. When unemployment is low, the labour market is tight: firms compete for scarce workers, bid up wages, and pass the higher costs on as higher prices. When unemployment is high, slack labour markets keep wages and prices subdued. Stimulate aggregate demand and you ride *down* the curve — lower unemployment, higher inflation; restrain demand and you climb *up* it — higher unemployment, lower inflation.

This looked like a **menu**. A government could simply *choose* its preferred mix — a little more inflation in exchange for a little less unemployment, or vice versa — and pick the point on the curve it liked best. For a while in the 1960s, that's exactly how policy was run.

</Step>

<Step title="The menu disappears" kind="explain">

In the 1970s the bargain fell apart. Economies suffered **high inflation and high unemployment at the same time** — *stagflation*, the supply-shock nightmare you met two worlds ago. Points were landing far above and to the right of the old curve. The stable menu had vanished.

Economists Milton Friedman and Edmund Phelps had predicted exactly this, and their explanation hinges on **expectations**. The trade-off works only as long as inflation *surprises* people. Stimulate demand and, at first, prices rise faster than workers expected; their real wages quietly fall, firms find labour cheap, and they hire — unemployment drops. But people aren't fooled forever. Once workers *expect* higher inflation, they demand matching wage rises. Real wages climb back, the cost advantage evaporates, firms cut back, and unemployment returns to where it started — now with permanently higher inflation.

In other words, you can buy lower unemployment with inflation only *temporarily*, and only by accelerating inflation faster than people anticipate. Stand still at a high inflation rate and expectations catch up; the short-run curve **shifts upward**, and you're back to the same unemployment at a worse inflation rate.

</Step>

<Step title="The long-run curve is vertical" kind="explain">

This points to a special level of unemployment: the **natural rate**. It's the rate the economy gravitates to when inflation is fully and correctly anticipated — the unemployment that remains from normal job-switching (frictional) and skills mismatch (structural) even in a healthy economy. It is *not* zero.

The crucial result: in the **long run**, after expectations have fully adjusted, unemployment always returns to the natural rate *regardless of the inflation rate*. Plot that and the **long-run Phillips curve is vertical**, standing at the natural rate. There is **no permanent trade-off** between inflation and unemployment. Higher inflation buys you nothing lasting — it just shifts the short-run curve up to a worse position.

Turn the long-run curve back on in the diagram, push unemployment below the natural rate, then raise the expected-inflation slider. Watch the whole short-run curve march upward and the economy drift back to the vertical line: more inflation, same unemployment.

<Callout type="info" title="What this means for policy">
The short-run trade-off is real, so demand policy *can* fight a recession or cool a boom in the near term. But trying to hold unemployment permanently below its natural rate just ratchets inflation higher and higher with no lasting gain. The lasting way to lower unemployment isn't more inflation — it's supply-side reform that lowers the natural rate itself: better job matching, training, and more flexible labour markets.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- The **short-run Phillips curve** slopes **down**: lower unemployment comes with higher inflation, and vice versa — a genuine near-term trade-off.
- It once looked like a stable **menu**, but **stagflation** in the 1970s — high inflation *and* high unemployment together — broke it.
- The trade-off works only while inflation **surprises** people. Once **expectations** adjust, the short-run curve shifts up and unemployment returns to the **natural rate**.
- The **long-run Phillips curve is vertical** at the natural rate: **no permanent trade-off** between inflation and unemployment.
- Demand policy can manage the short run; lasting reductions in unemployment require **supply-side** reforms that lower the natural rate itself.

</Step>
