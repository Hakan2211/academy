<Lesson>

<Step title="Scarcity, drawn as a curve" kind="explain">

How do you picture the trade-offs facing an entire economy? Economists use one of their most famous diagrams: the **production possibilities frontier (PPF)**.

Imagine a country that makes only two things — say pizzas and tablets. With its limited land, labour, and capital, it can make *lots* of pizzas and few tablets, *lots* of tablets and few pizzas, or some balance between. Plot every maximum combination it could produce and you trace a curve: the frontier of what's **possible**.

The PPF turns the abstract idea of scarcity into something you can see and reason about — including efficiency, opportunity cost, and growth, all in one picture.

</Step>

<Step title="Walk the frontier" kind="interactive">

Slide resources between the two goods and watch the trade-off. Then try growing the economy.

<PPFCurve labelA="Pizzas" labelB="Tablets" />

Three regions tell the story: a point **on** the curve uses every resource fully (efficient); a point **inside** wastes resources (idle factories, unemployment); a point **outside** is simply unattainable today — no matter how you organise, you can't get there.

</Step>

<Step title="Why the curve bows outward" kind="explain">

Did you notice the frontier bulges out rather than running in a straight line? That shape is **increasing opportunity cost** made visible.

Resources aren't equally good at everything. The first workers you shift from pizzas to tablets might be natural engineers — you gain lots of tablets, losing few pizzas. But as you keep shifting, you start pulling over people who were brilliant pizza chefs and clumsy at electronics. Each additional tablet now costs *more and more* pizzas. That rising sacrifice — steeper and steeper as you specialise — is exactly what bends the curve.

<Callout type="tip" title="Growth = pushing the whole curve out">
A society beats the trade-off only by getting more resources or better technology. That shifts the entire frontier outward, turning yesterday's "unattainable" point into tomorrow's "easily done." This is what economic growth looks like on the diagram — and the whole point of the Growth world later on.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- The **PPF** shows every maximum combination of two goods an economy can produce.
- **On** the curve = efficient; **inside** = wasteful (idle resources); **outside** = unattainable now.
- It **bows outward** because of **increasing opportunity cost** — resources aren't equally suited to every job.
- The slope at any point is the opportunity cost of one good in terms of the other.
- **Economic growth** shifts the entire frontier outward.

</Step>
