<Step title="Growth that wobbles" kind="explain">

You now have the three great gauges of an economy — **GDP**, **inflation**, and **unemployment**. This deepdive ties them into one moving picture: the **business cycle**, the economy's recurring rhythm of good times and bad.

Over the long run, real GDP trends *upward*: economies grow as they accumulate workers, capital, and better technology. But that climb is anything but smooth. Real GDP wobbles **above and below** its long-run trend in irregular waves — surging in some years, stalling or shrinking in others. Those wobbles are the business cycle, and understanding them is the gateway to everything macroeconomic policy tries to do.

Crucially, the cycle is a deviation *around* a rising trend — not a circle that returns to where it started. Each peak tends to sit higher than the last, because the underlying trend keeps lifting.

</Step>

<Step title="The four phases" kind="explain">

Every cycle moves through the same four landmarks:

- **Expansion** — real GDP is rising. Firms hire, unemployment falls, incomes and spending grow. This is the good part, and historically expansions last longer than the downturns between them.
- **Peak** — the top of the cycle, where GDP stops rising. The economy is running hot; unemployment is low and inflationary pressure often builds.
- **Recession (contraction)** — real GDP falls. A common rule of thumb marks a recession as **two consecutive quarters of falling real GDP**, though official bodies look more broadly at output, jobs, and incomes. Firms cut back, **cyclical unemployment** rises, and spending sags.
- **Trough** — the bottom, where the decline halts and recovery begins. The cycle then turns back into a new expansion.

A severe, prolonged recession is called a **depression** — there's no precise threshold, but the 1930s Great Depression, with output collapsing roughly a quarter and unemployment near 25%, is the defining example.

</Step>

<Step title="Why the gauges move together" kind="explain">

The business cycle is why GDP, unemployment, and inflation tend to dance in a predictable choreography:

- During an **expansion**: real GDP ↑, **unemployment falls** (firms hiring), and as the economy nears its peak, **inflation often rises** (demand pressing against capacity).
- During a **recession**: real GDP ↓, **unemployment rises** (the cyclical kind), and **inflationary pressure usually eases** as demand weakens.

This is the empirical backbone of two relationships you'll meet later. The link between output and joblessness over the cycle is **Okun's law** (faster growth → lower unemployment). The short-run tension between inflation and unemployment is the **Phillips curve**. Both are just the business cycle viewed from a different angle. For now, the headline is simple: when the economy slows, jobs go first and prices cool; when it races, jobs are plentiful and prices heat up.

<Callout type="info" title="The cycle is recurring, not regular">
"Cycle" can mislead. Business cycles are *recurrent* — booms and busts keep coming — but they are **not periodic**. They don't arrive on a fixed clock; one expansion might run a decade, the next just a couple of years. Their irregular timing is exactly what makes them so hard to forecast.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Reading the cycle: leading indicators" kind="explain">

Because the cycle is irregular, forecasters hunt for **leading indicators** — data that tend to turn *before* the overall economy does, offering an early warning. Economists sort indicators by their timing:

- **Leading indicators** turn *ahead* of the cycle: new building permits, stock prices, new orders for machinery, consumer confidence, and the average workweek. A sustained slide in these hints that a slowdown is coming.
- **Coincident indicators** move *with* the cycle, confirming where we are right now: real GDP itself, employment, and industrial production.
- **Lagging indicators** turn *after* the cycle: the unemployment rate (firms are slow to rehire even after recovery starts) and the inflation rate.

No single indicator is reliable — markets famously "predicted nine of the last five recessions." Forecasters watch a *bundle* of them, looking for several to point the same way at once. One closely watched signal is the **yield curve**: when short-term interest rates rise above long-term ones (an "inverted" curve), a recession has historically tended to follow.

</Step>

<Step title="Why we care: smoothing the ride" kind="explain">

If the long-run trend rises regardless, why fight the cycle at all? Because the wobbles inflict real human costs. A recession isn't an abstraction — it's lost jobs, foreclosed homes, idle factories, and young people graduating into a market with nothing to offer them. And a runaway boom carries its own danger: overheating that breeds inflation and the asset bubbles whose bursts trigger the next bust.

This is the entire motivation for **stabilisation policy**. The aim of the central bank and the government is to *smooth* the cycle — to soften recessions and cool overheated booms — nudging the economy to track its trend more closely. They do it with the **monetary and fiscal tools** you'll study in the worlds ahead: adjusting interest rates and the money supply, and varying government spending and taxes. The business cycle, in other words, is the problem all of macroeconomic policy is trying to solve.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- The **business cycle** is the recurring fluctuation of real GDP **above and below** its rising long-run **trend** — recurrent but **not** periodic.
- Four phases: **expansion** (GDP rising), **peak** (top), **recession** (GDP falling, often two quarters), **trough** (bottom); a deep, prolonged recession is a **depression**.
- The gauges move together: expansions bring **GDP up, unemployment down, inflation pressure rising**; recessions reverse all three — the basis of **Okun's law** and the **Phillips curve**.
- **Leading** indicators (permits, stock prices, confidence, the yield curve) turn early; **coincident** (GDP, employment) move with the cycle; **lagging** (unemployment rate, inflation) turn late.
- Cycles impose real costs, which is why **stabilisation policy** — monetary and fiscal tools — tries to smooth them, the central problem of macroeconomics.

</Step>
