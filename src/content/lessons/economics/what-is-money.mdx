<Step title="The trouble with barter" kind="explain">

Imagine a world with no money at all. You bake bread; you want a pair of shoes. To trade, you must find a shoemaker who not only has shoes to spare but also happens to want bread — right now, in roughly the amount you're offering. Economists call this the **double coincidence of wants**, and it is the great problem of barter: every trade needs *both* sides to want *exactly* what the other has.

In a small village you might manage. In a modern economy of millions of goods, the odds of finding your perfect trading partner collapse to almost nothing. You'd spend your whole life searching instead of baking.

Money is the elegant fix. Once everyone accepts one thing in trade, you no longer need a double coincidence. You sell bread for money to *anyone* who wants bread, then spend that money on shoes from *anyone* selling shoes. Money is the universal middleman that lets a vast web of strangers trade.

</Step>

<Step title="The three functions of money" kind="explain">

Anything that serves as money does three jobs at once:

- **Medium of exchange** — it's the thing everyone accepts in trade, which is what kills the double-coincidence problem.
- **Store of value** — it holds purchasing power over time, so you can earn today and spend next month. (Fresh fish would make a terrible money: it rots.)
- **Unit of account** — it's the common yardstick we quote every price, wage, and debt in, so a laptop at $899 and a phone at $599 can be compared at a glance.

These functions are why money has to be durable, divisible, portable, and — above all — widely accepted. Things as varied as gold, silver, cattle, salt, and even cigarettes (in prisoner-of-war camps) have all served as money because they did these jobs well enough for their time.

</Step>

<Step title="Commodity money vs fiat money" kind="explain">

Where does money's value actually come from? There are two answers.

**Commodity money** is made of something valuable in itself. A gold coin is worth something even if you melt it down; salt and silver had real uses. Its supply is limited by what can be mined or produced, which keeps its value relatively stable — but also makes it clumsy to carry and hard to expand when an economy grows.

**Fiat money** is the kind in your wallet today. A banknote is just printed paper with almost no value as an object. It works only because the government declares it **legal tender** and — crucially — because everyone *trusts* that others will accept it tomorrow. The word *fiat* is Latin for "let it be done": it is money because we collectively agree it is.

<Callout type="info" title="Money rests on trust">
Fiat money's great strength is flexibility — a central bank can expand the supply as the economy grows. Its great danger is the same thing: print far too much and the trust evaporates, taking the money's value with it. That link between the quantity of money and its value is the theme of this whole world.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="What counts as money? M1 and M2" kind="explain">

If money is "whatever we use to buy things," how much money is actually out there? Economists draw the line in layers:

- **M1** is the most liquid money — cash in circulation plus the balances in your checking account that you can spend instantly. This is money in the narrowest, spend-it-now sense.
- **M2** is broader. It's everything in M1 *plus* assets that are *almost* money: savings accounts and small time deposits. You can't tap a savings account at a vending machine, but a quick transfer turns it into spendable cash.

The point of the layers is that "money" isn't one sharp thing — it's a spectrum from cash you can spend this second to assets that are one short step away. Central banks track both M1 and M2 to gauge how much spending power is sloshing through the economy, which matters enormously once we get to inflation.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Barter** needs a **double coincidence of wants** — both sides must want exactly what the other has. Money removes that obstacle.
- Money does three jobs: **medium of exchange**, **store of value**, and **unit of account**.
- **Commodity money** (gold, salt) is worth something in itself; **fiat money** (modern banknotes) is worth something only by government decree and shared **trust**.
- **M1** is the narrowest, most spendable money (cash + checking); **M2** adds near-money like savings accounts.
- Because fiat money rests on trust, the *quantity* of it matters — the link we'll trace through banking and inflation.

</Step>
