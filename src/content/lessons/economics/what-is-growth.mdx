<Step title="The most important chart in economics" kind="explain">

For almost all of human history, life barely changed from one century to the next. A peasant in the year 1000 lived about as well as a peasant in the year 1. Then, a few hundred years ago, something extraordinary happened: in a handful of countries, average living standards began to *rise* — and kept rising, decade after decade. That ongoing rise is **economic growth**, and it is the difference between a world of widespread poverty and a world where billions live longer, healthier, more comfortable lives.

Growth is the central question of macroeconomics. Why do some nations grow rich while others stay poor? What turns the engine? And once it's running, just how powerful is it? This world answers those questions — starting with what we actually mean by "growth."

</Step>

<Step title="Real GDP per capita" kind="explain">

When economists say an economy has *grown*, they have a precise measure in mind: **real GDP per capita**. Let's unpack that phrase, because each word is doing work.

- **GDP** — gross domestic product — is the total value of everything an economy produces in a year. A bigger GDP means more goods and services.
- **Real** means we've stripped out inflation, so we're comparing actual *stuff* produced, not just rising prices. (A pile of money that doubles is meaningless if prices also double.)
- **Per capita** means *per person* — total output divided by population. This matters enormously: a country can grow its total GDP simply by adding people, but if output per person isn't rising, the average citizen is no better off.

Real GDP per capita is our best single yardstick for the material standard of living. When it rises, the typical person can afford more — and growth, properly defined, is the **sustained increase in real GDP per capita over time**.

<Callout type="info" title="Output equals income">
Every dollar of output is also someone's income — a wage, a profit, a rent. So "more output per person" and "more income per person" are two sides of the same coin. Growth makes the average person richer because it makes the average person more *productive*.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Why growth compounds" kind="explain">

Here is the feature that makes growth so astonishing: it **compounds**. Growth doesn't add a fixed amount each year — it multiplies. A 3% growth rate this year applies to a base that already includes last year's gains, which already included the year before's, and so on. Each year's growth stands on the shoulders of every year before it.

That's why growth, plotted over time, doesn't rise in a straight line — it curves upward, steeper and steeper. A modest-looking annual percentage, repeated for a lifetime, can multiply a nation's income many times over.

</Step>

<Step title="The rule of 70" kind="explain">

Compounding is hard to feel intuitively, so economists use a handy shortcut: the **rule of 70**. To find roughly how many years it takes for something growing at a steady rate to *double*, divide 70 by the growth rate.

<Formula tex="\text{doubling time} \approx \frac{70}{\text{growth rate (\%)}}" block />

Grow at 1% a year and income doubles every 70 years — once a lifetime. Grow at 7% and it doubles every *10* years — several times in a lifetime. This tiny formula is why economists obsess over fractions of a percentage point: a country growing at 2% versus 4% isn't doing "twice as well," it's doubling its living standard twice as often, pulling steadily and permanently ahead.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Economic growth** is the sustained rise in **real GDP per capita** — output (and income) per person, adjusted for inflation.
- "Per capita" matters: adding people can raise total GDP without making the average person better off.
- Growth **compounds** — it multiplies on an ever-larger base — so plotted over time it curves steeply upward.
- The **rule of 70** (doubling time ≈ 70 ÷ growth rate) shows why small differences in growth rates create huge long-run gaps.
- Because output equals income, growth raises living standards by making people more productive.

</Step>
