<Step title="From the textbook to the control room" kind="explain">

You now have the full stabilisation toolkit. **Fiscal policy** shifts aggregate demand through spending and taxes; **monetary policy** shifts it through interest rates and the money supply; the **Phillips curve** warns you what inflation you'll pay for chasing low unemployment. On a clean diagram, steering the economy looks almost like driving a car: see the gap, push the right lever, watch output return to potential.

Reality is messier. The diagram assumes the driver knows exactly where the car is, that the steering responds instantly, and that the road doesn't move. None of that holds. Real policymakers act on stale data, with tools that bite only after long delays, against an economy that is itself constantly shifting. This deepdive is about that gap — between policy in theory and policy in the storm.

We'll work through three big complications: the **lags** that make timing treacherous, two **case studies** (2008 and COVID) that show the toolkit under fire, and the enduring debate over whether policy should follow fixed **rules** or trust human **discretion**.

</Step>

<Step title="The wave policy is trying to flatten" kind="interactive">

Recall what stabilisation policy is *for*. Real GDP doesn't grow smoothly — it oscillates above and below its rising trend, in the business cycle.

<BusinessCycle />

Every dip below trend is a recessionary gap, every surge above it an inflationary gap. The entire promise of fiscal and monetary policy is to *shrink these wobbles* — to lean against the cycle so output hugs the trend. The trouble, as you'll now see, is that leaning at the wrong moment can make the wave *bigger* instead of smaller.

</Step>

<Step title="The three lags" kind="explain">

The deepest problem in stabilisation policy is timing, and the villain is **lags** — the delays between a problem starting and policy actually working. There are three, and they stack up:

- **The recognition lag.** Economic data arrive late and get revised. A recession is officially confirmed only *months* after it begins, because GDP figures are quarterly and provisional. By the time everyone agrees there's a problem, the problem is already old.
- **The implementation lag.** Acting on the diagnosis takes time too. Fiscal policy is especially slow: spending and tax changes must be debated, voted on, and passed — which can take a year or more, and politics may stall it entirely. Monetary policy is far nimbler here; a central-bank committee can change rates in an afternoon.
- **The impact lag.** Even once policy is in place, it works only gradually. A rate cut takes many months to feed through borrowing, investment, and spending into actual output. Monetary policy's effects are often said to arrive with "long and variable lags."

Add the three together and you might be applying the brakes a year or more after the danger appeared. The risk is **pro-cyclical** policy: a stimulus aimed at a recession that doesn't *land* until the economy has already recovered — at which point it overheats a boom instead of curing a slump, making the cycle worse. Long lags are the single strongest argument for caution, and for relying on the instant action of **automatic stabilizers**.

<Callout type="warn" title="Fighting the last war">
Because of lags, policymakers are always acting on a picture of the economy that is months out of date. It's like steering a ship by looking at where the waves were, not where they are. This is why good stabilisation is so hard even when the *direction* of the right policy is obvious.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="2008: the textbook recession" kind="explain">

The 2008 global financial crisis was, in macro terms, a colossal **negative demand shock**. A housing bubble burst, banks teetered, credit froze, and households and firms slashed spending all at once. Aggregate demand collapsed; output and the price level fell *together* — the signature of a demand shock, exactly as the model predicts.

The response threw the entire toolkit at it, on both fronts:

- **Monetary policy** went first and hard. Central banks slashed policy rates to near **zero** — and then, having hit that floor, invented **quantitative easing (QE)**: buying bonds on a massive scale to push *longer-term* rates down when the short-term rate could fall no further. This is open-market operations taken to an extreme.
- **Fiscal policy** followed with large **stimulus** packages — emergency spending and tax cuts to prop up demand directly, leaning hard against the slump (and ballooning deficits in the process).

The episode taught a hard lesson about the **zero lower bound**: once interest rates hit zero, conventional monetary policy runs out of room, and the burden shifts to fiscal policy and unconventional tools like QE. It also reignited the old debate from the booms-and-busts world — act decisively, or trust self-correction? With a shock that severe, almost everyone agreed: act.

</Step>

<Step title="COVID: a shock unlike the rest" kind="explain">

The 2020 COVID-19 pandemic was stranger. It hit **both** sides at once. Lockdowns shut factories, broke supply chains, and idled workers — an adverse **supply shock**. Simultaneously, fear and closed businesses crushed spending — a **demand shock**. Output cratered with terrifying speed, far faster than in 2008.

The policy response was the largest and quickest in peacetime history, and it leaned almost entirely toward demand support: near-zero rates again and renewed QE from central banks, plus enormous fiscal packages — direct cash payments, expanded unemployment benefits, and loans to keep firms alive. Crucially, this time the response was *fast*: the recognition lag nearly vanished because the cause was visible to everyone in real time.

But the supply side left a sting in the tail. As economies reopened, demand — fattened by all that stimulus and pent-up savings — came roaring back against supply chains still tangled and labour still scarce. The result, in 2021–22, was the highest **inflation** in forty years. It was a live demonstration of the booms-and-busts lesson: when a **supply shock** lurks underneath, pumping up **demand** doesn't just restore output — it bids up prices. Central banks then had to swing hard the *other* way, raising rates sharply to bring inflation back down.

</Step>

Shift aggregate demand right and watch the recessionary gap close — the clean textbook cure. Now picture the lags: by the time your stimulus actually lands, self-correction may have already nudged the economy back toward potential. The very same rightward AD shift that would have *cured* the recession now shoves output *past* potential into an **inflationary gap**. Same policy, wrong timing, opposite result. That single picture is the whole case for humility in stabilisation.

</Step>

<Step title="Rules versus discretion" kind="explain">

All of this feeds the longest-running argument in macroeconomic policy: should policymakers follow fixed **rules**, or exercise case-by-case **discretion**?

- **The case for rules.** Because of lags and the temptation to over-tinker, discretionary fine-tuning often destabilises rather than smooths. A simple, predictable rule — *keep money growing at a steady rate*, or *set interest rates by a fixed formula like the Taylor rule*, or *target 2% inflation* — anchors everyone's expectations. People and markets know what the central bank will do, which (recall the Phillips curve) keeps inflation expectations stable. Rules also remove the political temptation to juice the economy before an election.
- **The case for discretion.** No rule can anticipate every shock. A rigid formula written for normal times would have been useless in the chaos of 2008 or 2020, when the right response was unprecedented (QE, emergency fiscal packages). Skilled, accountable humans can read a novel crisis and respond creatively in ways no pre-written rule could.

Modern practice splits the difference: most central banks operate under **constrained discretion** — a firm, credible *rule* for the long-run goal (typically a **2% inflation target**) that anchors expectations, combined with the *discretion* to deviate temporarily when an extraordinary shock demands it. It's an attempt to win the credibility of rules without the rigidity, blending the classical trust in stable frameworks with the Keynesian readiness to act.

<Callout type="tip" title="The honest summary">
Stabilisation policy is real and useful — but it is a blunt instrument wielded with old data and slow tools against a moving target. It works best against clear demand shocks, struggles against supply shocks, and is most dangerous when applied aggressively and late. Knowing the *limits* of the toolkit is as important as knowing the toolkit.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- Real-world stabilisation is hampered by three **lags** — **recognition**, **implementation**, and **impact** — that can make policy land too late and turn it **pro-cyclical**.
- **2008** was a giant **demand shock**: rates cut to the **zero lower bound**, plus **quantitative easing** and fiscal stimulus — the textbook cure, applied at scale.
- **COVID** hit **supply and demand together**; a fast, huge demand-support response restored output but, with supply constrained, helped unleash the **inflation** of 2021–22 — forcing a sharp tightening.
- The **rules vs discretion** debate pits predictable, expectation-anchoring rules against the flexibility to handle novel crises; modern policy uses **constrained discretion** around a **2% inflation target**.
- The toolkit works best against demand shocks, struggles against supply shocks, and is riskiest when used aggressively and late — knowing its **limits** matters as much as the tools themselves.

</Step>
