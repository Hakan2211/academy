<Step title="When the whole curve moves" kind="explain">

Last lesson we learned that a good's **own price** moves you *along* its demand curve. But plenty of other things can make buyers want more — or less — at *every* price. When that happens, the entire demand curve slides sideways. That's a **shift in demand**, and it's a completely different event from a movement along the curve.

A shift **right** means buyers want more at every price (demand increased). A shift **left** means they want less at every price (demand decreased). The trick to mastering supply and demand is keeping these two ideas crisply apart: own price → move *along*; anything else → *shift* the curve.

</Step>

<Step title="The five demand shifters" kind="explain">

Economists boil the "anything else" down to five usual suspects. A handy mnemonic is **TRIBE**:

- **T — Tastes & preferences.** A viral trend or health scare changes how much people want a good regardless of price. Trendy → shift right; out of favour → shift left.
- **R — Related goods' prices.** A **substitute** is something you'd buy instead (tea for coffee). If coffee's price rises, demand for tea shifts right. A **complement** is something you buy together (printers and ink). If printers get cheaper and you buy more, demand for ink shifts right.
- **I — Income.** For a **normal** good, more income shifts demand right. For an **inferior** good (instant noodles, bus rides), more income shifts demand *left* as people trade up.
- **B — Buyers (the number of them).** A bigger market — population growth, a new region opening up — shifts demand right.
- **E — Expectations.** If buyers expect prices to jump next month, they buy now: demand shifts right today.

If a change isn't the good's *own* price, it works through one of these five — and it shifts the curve.

</Step>

<Step title="Along vs shift, one more time" kind="explain">

Here's the classic trap. Imagine the price of beef falls and people buy more beef. *Movement along* the beef demand curve — beef's own price changed.

Now imagine a TV documentary scares people off beef. Even at the *same* price, they buy less: the whole beef demand curve shifts **left**. Same observable outcome (less beef bought) could come from either, so always ask: *did beef's own price change, or did something else?* That single question tells you whether you're moving along the curve or shifting it.

<Callout type="warn" title="A price change of a RELATED good shifts the curve">
Careful: it's only the good's *own* price that moves you along the curve. A change in the price of a *substitute or complement* is a TRIBE shifter — it moves the *whole* curve. The price of tea shifts the coffee curve; the price of coffee moves you along it.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- A change in the good's **own price** moves you **along** the curve; **anything else** **shifts** the whole curve.
- **Shift right** = more wanted at every price (demand up); **shift left** = less wanted (demand down).
- The five shifters — **TRIBE**: **T**astes, **R**elated-goods' prices (substitutes & complements), **I**ncome (normal vs inferior), **B**uyers' number, **E**xpectations.
- A **substitute's** price rise shifts a good's demand right; a **complement's** price fall shifts it right.
- Use the **four-step** routine: which curve, which way, draw it, read the new equilibrium.

</Step>
