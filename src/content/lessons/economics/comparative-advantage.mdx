<Step title="The most important idea you can compute" kind="explain">

Last lesson we met comparative advantage in words. Now let's nail it with numbers — because comparative advantage is one of the few big economic ideas you can work out on the back of an envelope, and doing so removes all the mystery.

The whole thing rests on **opportunity cost**: the value of the next-best thing you give up. When a country decides to make one more car, the real cost isn't measured in money — it's the wheat (or phones, or anything else) that those same workers could have made instead. A country should specialise in whatever has the *lowest* opportunity cost for it. That, and nothing else, is comparative advantage.

</Step>

<Step title="A worked example" kind="explain">

Suppose two countries each have 100 workers, and we know how much one worker can make in a year:

<Formula tex="\text{Home: } 1\ \text{worker} \to 10\ \text{wheat} \;\;\text{or}\;\; 5\ \text{cloth}" block />

<Formula tex="\text{Foreign: } 1\ \text{worker} \to 6\ \text{wheat} \;\;\text{or}\;\; 4\ \text{cloth}" block />

Home is better at *both* — it has the absolute advantage in wheat and in cloth. Naively, you'd think Home should just make everything. But look at the **opportunity costs**.

For Home, making 10 wheat means giving up 5 cloth, so 1 wheat costs **½ cloth** — and 1 cloth costs **2 wheat**. For Foreign, 6 wheat costs 4 cloth, so 1 wheat costs **⅔ cloth**, and 1 cloth costs **1½ wheat**.

Now compare. Home gives up only ½ cloth per wheat versus Foreign's ⅔ — so **Home's comparative advantage is wheat**. But Foreign gives up only 1½ wheat per cloth versus Home's 2 — so **Foreign's comparative advantage is cloth**. Even though Foreign is worse at everything in absolute terms, it is *relatively* less bad at cloth. Each country should specialise accordingly.

</Step>

Watch what happens as you slide one country up to dominate in raw output: it keeps its absolute advantage, but the *comparative* advantage in one of the two goods stubbornly stays with the other country — until you make their opportunity-cost ratios identical, at which point the gains from trade vanish. **Different opportunity costs are the entire engine of trade.**

</Step>

<Step title="The terms of trade" kind="explain">

Specialising only helps if the countries can then trade at a price that beats making the good themselves. That price — how much cloth swaps for how much wheat — is called the **terms of trade**, and it has to land *between* the two countries' opportunity costs.

In our example, Home values 1 cloth at 2 wheat; Foreign values it at only 1½ wheat. So any trade price between 1½ and 2 wheat per cloth makes *both* better off: Home buys cloth for less than its own 2-wheat cost, and Foreign sells cloth for more than its own 1½-wheat cost. The gains-from-trade pie gets split by exactly where in that band the price settles — which is itself decided by supply and demand on world markets.

<Callout type="warn" title="Don't confuse the slope with the elasticity">
Comparative advantage is about *ratios of opportunity cost*, not who is bigger or richer. A tiny, low-productivity country still has a comparative advantage in *something* — and trading on it is its surest route to a higher standard of living. Telling poor countries to "make everything themselves" keeps them poor.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Comparative advantage** = the good a country can make at the **lowest opportunity cost**.
- Compute it: opportunity cost of good X = how much of the other good a unit of X forces you to give up.
- A country worse at *everything* in absolute terms still has a comparative advantage in **something** — and should specialise there.
- Specialisation only pays if the **terms of trade** land *between* the two countries' opportunity costs.
- Identical opportunity costs ⇒ no comparative advantage ⇒ no gains from trade. **Difference is the engine.**

</Step>
