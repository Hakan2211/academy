<Step title="Why do we buy what we buy?" kind="explain">

When you spend money, you're really buying something less tangible than the object itself: **satisfaction**. A coffee warms you up, a song lifts your mood, a textbook helps you pass. Economists give this satisfaction a name — **utility** — and treat the pursuit of it as the engine behind every purchase.

To reason about it, early economists imagined measuring utility in made-up units called **utils**. You can't really put a number on how much you enjoyed a slice of pizza, but pretending you can turns "I liked it" into something we can graph and compare. The util is a thinking tool, not a real measurement — and it unlocks a surprisingly powerful idea.

</Step>

<Step title="Total vs marginal utility" kind="explain">

Two different questions hide inside "how much do you enjoy pizza?"

- **Total utility** is the *whole* satisfaction from everything you've consumed — the sum of enjoyment across all the slices you ate.
- **Marginal utility** is the satisfaction from *one more* unit — just the next slice, on top of what you already have.

These two move together but not in lockstep. As you eat more pizza, your total utility keeps climbing (more pizza is still more enjoyment overall). But each *additional* slice tends to thrill you a little less than the one before. The first slice when you're starving is heavenly; the fifth is just fine; the eighth might make you queasy. That's marginal utility quietly shrinking, even while the total still rises.

</Step>

<Step title="Watch the two diverge" kind="interactive">

Eat slices one at a time. The bars show the **marginal** utility of each slice; the line traces your running **total** utility.

<UtilityLab />

Notice the pattern: the bars get shorter (marginal utility falls) while the line keeps rising but flattens out (total utility grows by less each time). Eventually a slice adds nothing at all — and if you kept going past that, it might even subtract. The two curves tell different stories, and confusing them is the single most common mistake in thinking about value.

</Step>

<Step title="Why the difference matters" kind="explain">

Keeping total and marginal utility separate is what makes the whole theory of consumer choice work.

Decisions are always about the **next** unit, so they hinge on **marginal** utility, not total. "Should I buy one more coffee?" depends on how much *that coffee* will add — not on how wonderful coffee is in general. A thing can be hugely valuable in total yet barely worth anything at the margin, simply because you already have a lot of it.

That distinction sounds abstract, but it resolves a puzzle that stumped economists for centuries: why is life-giving water nearly free while useless-but-shiny diamonds cost a fortune? The answer is entirely about the margin — and it's the next lesson.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Utility** is the satisfaction we get from consuming goods; economists imagine measuring it in **utils**.
- **Total utility** is the satisfaction from everything consumed; **marginal utility** is the satisfaction from *one more* unit.
- As you consume more of the same thing, total utility rises but each extra unit usually adds less — marginal utility falls.
- Choices are about the *next* unit, so they depend on **marginal**, not total, utility — the key to the diamond–water paradox ahead.

</Step>
