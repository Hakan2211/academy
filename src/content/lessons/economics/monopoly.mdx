<Step title="The other extreme" kind="explain">

If perfect competition is one end of the spectrum, **monopoly** is the other. A monopoly is a market with a **single seller** of a product that has no close substitutes. Where the competitive firm was powerless, the monopolist is a **price maker** — it can choose its price, not merely accept one.

What keeps rivals out? **Barriers to entry**. A monopoly survives only because something stops new firms from competing the profits away. The usual barriers are:

- **Control of a key resource** — owning the only diamond mines, say.
- **Government grants** — patents, copyrights, or an exclusive licence.
- **Natural monopoly** — costs that keep falling over the whole market, so one big firm is cheaper than many (a water network, a power grid).
- **Network effects and high fixed costs** — markets where being first and big is self-reinforcing.

Without a barrier, the monopoly cannot last: profit would lure entrants and the market would drift back toward competition.

</Step>

<Step title="Facing the whole demand curve" kind="explain">

The crucial difference is the demand curve. A competitive firm faced a flat line — it could sell any amount at the market price. A monopolist *is* the entire industry, so it faces the whole market's **downward-sloping** demand curve.

That single fact changes the firm's choices completely. To sell more, the monopolist must **lower its price**. It can pick a high price and sell little, or a low price and sell lots — but it cannot have both. The downward slope is the leash on its power: even a monopolist is limited by what customers are willing to pay.

</Step>

<Step title="Why marginal revenue sits below price" kind="explain">

Here is the subtle, decisive point. When a monopolist wants to sell one more unit, it must drop the price — and (assuming it charges everyone the same) it must drop the price on *every* unit, not just the last one.

So the extra revenue from one more unit is the new price on that unit, *minus* the price cut now suffered on all the units it was already selling. That makes **marginal revenue lower than the price**. In fact, for a straight-line demand curve, marginal revenue falls **twice as fast** as the demand curve.

<Formula tex="MR < P \quad\text{(for a monopolist, because price must fall to sell more)}" block />

This is completely unlike the competitive firm, where price never had to fall and so marginal revenue *equalled* price. The wedge between MR and P is the source of the monopoly's distortion.

</Step>

<Step title="The monopolist's choice" kind="interactive">

The monopolist still maximises profit the same way every firm does — where **marginal revenue equals marginal cost**. But because MR sits below demand, the consequences are striking. Drag the marginal-cost level and watch.

<MonopolyGraph />

Trace the logic. The monopolist finds the quantity where **MR = MC**, then walks *straight up to the demand curve* to read off the price it can charge. That price sits **above marginal cost** — the monopoly markup. And the output, **Qm**, falls **short of the competitive quantity Qc** (where price would equal marginal cost). The firm deliberately holds back production to keep the price high.

</Step>

<Step title="The deadweight loss" kind="explain">

Compare the monopoly to what perfect competition would have delivered. A competitive industry would have produced all the way to where price equals marginal cost — every unit that buyers value more than it costs to make. The monopolist stops earlier.

Between the monopoly quantity and the competitive quantity lies a set of trades that **never happen**: units that buyers would have valued *above* their cost of production, but which the monopolist refuses to make because expanding output would force its price down. Those lost, mutually beneficial trades are the shaded triangle — the **deadweight loss**. It is value that simply vanishes, gained by no one.

<Callout type="warn">
The monopoly markup itself — the higher price on units that *are* sold — is a *transfer* from consumers to the firm, not a loss to society. The genuine inefficiency is the deadweight loss: the trades that never occur because output is restricted. That is the real economic case against unchecked monopoly.
</Callout>

This is why monopoly worries economists: not because the firm earns profit, but because restricted output makes the whole economy smaller than it could be.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- A **monopoly** is a single seller with no close substitutes, protected by **barriers to entry** (key resources, patents, natural monopoly, network effects).
- The monopolist is a **price maker** facing the whole **downward-sloping** demand curve, so it must cut price to sell more.
- That makes **marginal revenue lie below price**; the firm sets **MR = MC**, then charges the higher price off the demand curve.
- It produces **less than the competitive quantity**, creating a **deadweight loss** — mutually beneficial trades that never happen. The markup is a transfer; the restricted output is the true inefficiency.

</Step>
