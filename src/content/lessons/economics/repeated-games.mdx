<Step title="The shadow of the future" kind="explain">

The Prisoner's Dilemma painted a grim picture: rational players betray each other and everyone loses. Yet look around — people cooperate constantly. Businesses honour deals, neighbours help each other, nations sign treaties that hold. If defection is dominant, how does any of this survive?

The escape hatch is **repetition**. The dilemma is bleakproof only when the game is played **once** and never again. But most of life is played over and over with the *same* people: you'll trade with this supplier next month, see this neighbour tomorrow, face this rival every quarter. When the game repeats, today's betrayal invites tomorrow's revenge — and that **shadow of the future** can make cooperation the smart choice after all.

Suddenly your move isn't just about this round's payoff. It's an investment in (or against) the relationship.

</Step>

<Step title="Tit-for-tat: nice, but no pushover" kind="explain">

In the 1980s the political scientist Robert Axelrod ran a famous tournament: he invited experts to submit strategies for a *repeated* Prisoner's Dilemma and pitted them against one another over many rounds. The winner was almost embarrassingly simple — a strategy called **tit-for-tat**:

> **Cooperate on the first move. After that, do whatever your opponent did last round.**

Tit-for-tat won by being four things at once: **nice** (it never defects first), **retaliatory** (it punishes a defection immediately, so it can't be exploited), **forgiving** (it returns to cooperation the moment the opponent does), and **clear** (its rule is obvious, so others learn they can safely cooperate with it). Against another cooperative strategy, two tit-for-tat players cooperate forever and both score handsomely.

</Step>

Over many rounds, *cooperation outscores betrayal* — which is exactly why it survives.

<Step title="Reputation and the threat of punishment" kind="explain">

What actually sustains cooperation in a repeated game? Two linked forces:

- **Reputation.** When you'll meet again, your past behaviour becomes information. A reputation for honesty earns you future deals; a reputation for cheating gets you shunned. Cooperation becomes a valuable asset worth protecting.
- **The credible threat of punishment.** Tit-for-tat and grim trigger both work because defecting triggers a cost down the line. If the future matters enough — many rounds still to come — the long-run cost of being punished outweighs the one-round gain from cheating. So you cooperate.

There's a catch, though. If the players *know* the game has a **fixed final round**, the logic unravels by **backward induction**: in the last round there's no future to protect, so both defect — which makes the second-to-last round effectively the last, so they defect there too, and so on back to the start. Cooperation in repeated games leans on the future being open-ended (or at least uncertain in length).

</Step>

<Step title="Designing for cooperation" kind="explain">

The deepest lesson of repeated games is that cooperation is **engineerable**. We can't change the payoffs of a one-shot dilemma, but we *can* change whether and how the game repeats — and that changes everything. Societies are full of institutions built to do exactly this:

- **Contracts and courts** turn a one-shot deal into a repeated relationship with the legal system — break your word and you'll pay later.
- **Reviews and ratings** (think online marketplaces) give strangers a reputation, so even one-time interactions carry a future.
- **Repeated elections** discipline politicians who'd otherwise have no reason to keep promises.
- **Trade agreements and treaties** create ongoing relationships where defection invites retaliation.

The takeaway runs against the cynical reading of the dilemma: cooperation is fragile, but not doomed. Lengthen the shadow of the future, make reputations visible, and let people meet again — and self-interest itself starts to favour playing nice.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- A **repeated game** is the same game played over and over with the same players — and the **shadow of the future** can rescue cooperation.
- **Tit-for-tat** — cooperate first, then copy the opponent — won Axelrod's tournament by being nice, retaliatory, forgiving, and clear.
- Cooperation is sustained by **reputation** and the **credible threat of punishment** for defecting.
- It relies on an open-ended future: a known final round can unravel cooperation through **backward induction**.
- Institutions — contracts, courts, reviews, elections, treaties — are ways of engineering repetition so self-interest favours cooperation.

</Step>
