<Step title="Shifting the supply curve" kind="explain">

Demand has its shifters; so does supply. When something other than the good's **own price** changes how much sellers want to produce, the **entire supply curve** slides sideways.

But watch the direction convention, because it can feel upside-down. A supply *increase* means sellers offer **more** at every price — the curve moves **right** (and visually downward, since they'll accept a lower price for any given quantity). A supply *decrease* means less at every price — the curve moves **left** (and upward). Right = increase, left = decrease, exactly as with demand.

</Step>

<Step title="The supply shifters" kind="explain">

The usual suspects on the supply side:

- **Input (resource) costs.** Wages, raw materials, energy, rent. Cheaper inputs make production more attractive → supply shifts **right**. Costlier inputs → shifts **left**. This is the big one.
- **Technology.** A better production method lowers cost per unit, so sellers offer more at every price → supply shifts **right**. Technology essentially never goes backwards, so it's a steady rightward force over time.
- **Number of sellers.** More firms entering the market means more total output at every price → supply shifts **right**. Firms exiting → shifts **left**.
- **Expectations.** If sellers expect a higher price soon, they may hold back today's output to sell later, shifting today's supply **left**.
- **Taxes & subsidies.** A per-unit tax acts like a cost rise (shift left); a subsidy acts like a cost cut (shift right). (More on taxes in the Elasticity world.)

If the change isn't the good's *own* price, it runs through one of these — and shifts the curve.

</Step>

The same **four-step** routine applies: (1) demand or supply? (2) which direction? (3) draw the shift; (4) read the new price and quantity. A supply *increase* (right) pushes price *down* and quantity *up*; a supply *decrease* (left) pushes price *up* and quantity *down*.

</Step>

<Step title="The trap, supply edition" kind="explain">

Same trap as demand, mirror image. If the price of oil rises and oil firms pump more, that's a movement *along* the oil supply curve — oil's own price changed.

But if a new drilling technology slashes extraction costs and firms pump more even at the *same* price, the whole supply curve shifts **right**. Both produce "more oil pumped," yet they're different events.

<Callout type="tip" title="The acid test">
For any market story, ask one question: *did the good's OWN price change first?* If yes → movement along the curve. If the trigger was anything else (costs, tech, sellers, expectations, taxes) → the curve shifts. This single question untangles almost every supply-and-demand puzzle.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- A change in the good's **own price** moves you **along** the supply curve; **anything else** **shifts** it.
- **Right = increase** (more at every price); **left = decrease** (less at every price).
- Supply shifters: **input costs**, **technology**, the **number of sellers**, **expectations**, and **taxes/subsidies**.
- A supply **increase** lowers price and raises quantity; a supply **decrease** does the reverse.
- The acid test: *did the good's own price change first?* No → the curve shifts.

</Step>
