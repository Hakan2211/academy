<Step title="The benchmark market" kind="explain">

We have built up the firm's costs; now we ask the bigger question: *what kind of market does the firm sell into?* The answer shapes everything — the price it can charge, the profit it can keep, and whether new rivals can join the party.

Economists start with one extreme: **perfect competition**. It almost never exists in pure form, but it is the benchmark against which every other market is measured. A perfectly competitive market has four features:

- **Many small buyers and sellers** — so many that no single one matters.
- **An identical product** — every firm sells exactly the same thing, so buyers do not care whose they buy.
- **Free entry and exit** — firms can join or leave with no barriers.
- **Full information** — everyone knows the going price.

Roughly competitive markets do exist: wheat, currencies, a stock with thousands of traders. None is perfect, but each is close enough that the model predicts their behaviour well.

</Step>

<Step title="Why the firm is a price taker" kind="explain">

Here is the surprising consequence of those four features. A single firm in perfect competition has **no power to set its own price**. It is a **price taker**: it must accept whatever price the market has settled on.

Why? Suppose a single wheat farmer tries to charge even a cent more than the market price. Buyers simply walk to the thousands of identical farmers selling at the going rate — the lone farmer sells nothing. And charging *less* would be pointless: the farmer can already sell every bushel at the market price, so why give money away?

So the demand curve facing *one firm* is **horizontal** at the market price — perfectly elastic. This is different from the *market* demand curve, which slopes down as usual. The whole market faces downward-sloping demand; the tiny individual firm, a flat line. Each firm is too small to budge the price, so it takes the price as given and only decides *how much to produce*.

</Step>

At the far left — perfect competition — there are very many firms, the demand curve each one faces is flat, and price is pinned right down to marginal cost, leaving no markup. As market power rises toward monopoly, firms grow fewer, the demand curve the firm faces tilts steep, and a markup over cost opens up. Perfect competition is simply the corner where firms have the *least* power.

</Step>

<Step title="The profit-maximising rule" kind="explain">

Even a price taker still chooses its output, and the rule is the one we already know: produce up to the point where **marginal cost equals the price**.

<Formula tex="P = MC" block />

For a competitive firm the price *is* its marginal revenue — selling one more unit always brings in exactly the market price, since the firm can sell as much as it likes at that price without moving it. So the familiar profit-max condition MR = MC becomes simply P = MC. The firm rides up its marginal-cost curve until the cost of the next unit just catches the price.

</Step>

<Step title="The long-run squeeze to zero profit" kind="explain">

Now the magic of **free entry and exit** does its work over time.

Suppose competitive firms are earning a juicy economic profit. Because there are no barriers, new firms rush in. More firms means more supply, which pushes the market price *down*. New firms keep entering until the price falls to the level where firms earn **zero economic profit** — just enough to cover all costs, including a normal return on the owners' capital.

The reverse happens if firms are making losses: some exit, supply shrinks, price rises back up. Either way, the long-run resting point is **zero economic profit**, with each firm producing at the lowest point of its average-total-cost curve.

<Callout type="info">
Zero *economic* profit is not zero *accounting* profit. Economic profit already subtracts opportunity cost — what the owners could have earned elsewhere. A firm at zero economic profit is doing perfectly fine; it is simply earning exactly the normal return, no more.
</Callout>

This relentless squeeze is why perfect competition is so admired by economists: price ends up equal to marginal cost *and* to minimum average cost, so consumers get the good at the lowest sustainable price and no resources are wasted.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Perfect competition** has many small firms, an identical product, free entry/exit and full information — the benchmark market.
- Each firm is a **price taker** facing a **horizontal (perfectly elastic) demand curve**; only the whole market's demand slopes down.
- Firms maximise profit where **P = MC**, since for a price taker price equals marginal revenue.
- **Free entry and exit** drive long-run **economic profit to zero**, with firms producing at minimum average total cost — the efficient outcome.

</Step>
