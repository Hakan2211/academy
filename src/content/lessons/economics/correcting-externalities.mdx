<Step title="Folding the spillover back into the price" kind="explain">

We left the last lesson with a clear diagnosis: a negative externality makes the market over-produce because firms ignore the costs they push onto others. The cure follows directly — make the firm *feel* that cost. If polluting is no longer free, the firm will pollute less, all on its own.

The economist Arthur Pigou proposed the cleanest version: a tax on each polluting unit equal to the harm it causes. We call it a **Pigouvian tax**. It does not ban anything or set a quota. It simply changes the price the firm faces so that the firm's *private* cost finally matches the *social* cost — and then lets the market do the rest.

</Step>

<Step title="The right tax closes the gap exactly" kind="interactive">

A per-unit tax lifts the firm's supply curve upward — every unit now costs the firm more by the amount of the tax. Slide the tax and watch the supply curve climb toward the dashed social-cost line.

<PigouvianTax />

The magic is in the *size*. Set the tax too low and the firm still under-pays for the harm, so it over-produces. Set it too high and you over-correct — now the market makes too little, and a new welfare loss opens on the other side. But set the tax **equal to the external cost**, and the supply curve lands exactly on the social-cost curve. The market quantity slides down to the social optimum, and the welfare loss disappears. The externality has been **internalised**: the price now tells the whole truth.

</Step>

<Step title="Taxes, subsidies, and the budget twist" kind="explain">

The same logic runs in reverse for **positive** externalities. If a vaccination or a year of schooling benefits society beyond the buyer, a **Pigouvian subsidy** — a payment per unit equal to the external benefit — lowers the price the buyer faces, nudges quantity up to the optimum, and captures the spillover. Tax the bad, subsidise the good.

A subtle bonus: a pollution tax doesn't just shrink output, it raises **revenue** — money a government can use to cut other taxes or repair the very damage the pollution caused. The tax does useful work even on the units that *do* still get produced, because the firm is at last paying for the harm.

<Callout type="tip" title="Tax the externality, not the activity">
A good Pigouvian tax targets the *spillover itself* — tons of carbon, not 'factories'; tailpipe emissions, not 'driving'. That way firms keep every cheap way to cut harm (cleaner fuel, scrubbers, off-peak shifts) and only pay for the damage that remains. Banning the activity outright throws away those cheaper adjustments.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Cap-and-trade and the Coase surprise" kind="explain">

Taxes set the *price* of polluting and let quantity adjust. **Cap-and-trade** flips that: the government sets the total *quantity* of pollution allowed — the cap — issues that many permits, and lets firms buy and sell them. A firm that can cut emissions cheaply does so and sells its spare permits; a firm for whom cutting is costly buys permits instead. The cap is hit at the lowest total cost, and the permit price emerges from the market.

Then there is the contrarian insight. Ronald Coase argued that if **property rights are clear** and bargaining is cheap, the people involved can sometimes solve an externality *themselves*, with no government at all. If a factory's smoke bothers a nearby laundry, and the right to the air is well defined, the two parties can simply strike a deal — whoever values the clean air more pays the other to get it. The remarkable claim of the **Coase theorem** is that the efficient outcome emerges regardless of *who* holds the right; the right only decides who pays whom.

<Callout type="warn" title="Coase has a catch">
The Coase theorem works only when bargaining is cheap and the parties are few. With a million people downwind of a power plant — strangers who can never all sit at one table — the transaction costs are enormous and private bargaining collapses. That is precisely when a tax, a subsidy, or a cap does the job the market cannot.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- A **Pigouvian tax** equal to the external cost makes the firm's private cost match the **social cost**, pushing output to the optimum — the externality is **internalised**.
- Set it too low and the market over-produces; too high and it under-produces; **equal to the harm** closes the gap exactly.
- **Positive** externalities call for a **Pigouvian subsidy** — pay the external benefit to lift quantity up.
- **Cap-and-trade** fixes the quantity of pollution and lets a permit market set the price, hitting the cap at least cost.
- The **Coase theorem**: with clear property rights and cheap bargaining, parties can fix externalities privately — but high transaction costs (many strangers) break it, leaving room for policy.

</Step>
