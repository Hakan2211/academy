<Step title="What knocks the economy off course" kind="explain">

You've built the master diagram — AD, SRAS, LRAS — and learned to spot recessionary and inflationary gaps. This deepdive puts the diagram in motion. Booms and busts aren't random; they happen when something **shocks** one of the curves. And the single most useful skill in macro is reading *which* curve a shock hits, because demand shocks and supply shocks produce very different — sometimes opposite — symptoms.

Recall the business cycle from the *Measuring the Economy* world: real GDP wobbling above and below its rising trend. Now we can explain *why* it wobbles. An expansion is AD or AS pushing output above potential; a recession is a shock pulling it below. The AD/AS model is the engine under the hood of the business cycle.

</Step>

<Step title="See the cycle the shocks produce" kind="interactive">

Before we dissect the shocks, recall the pattern they generate. Real GDP oscillates around its rising long-run trend: the climbs are expansions, the falls recessions, the turning points peaks and troughs.

<BusinessCycle />

Everything in this lesson is an explanation of *this* wave. Each dip below trend is a recessionary gap opened by some shock; each surge above is an inflationary gap. The whole of stabilisation policy is the attempt to flatten these wobbles — to keep output hugging the trend.

</Step>

<Step title="Demand shocks: output and prices move together" kind="explain">

A **demand shock** is a sudden shift in aggregate demand. A wave of pessimism, a stock-market crash, a credit crunch, or austerity pulls AD **left**; a confidence boom, a tax cut, or a surge in exports pushes it **right**.

The tell-tale sign of a demand shock is that **output and the price level move in the same direction**:

- A **negative** demand shock (AD left) drags both real GDP *and* the price level **down**. Output falls below potential — a recessionary gap — and inflation cools. This is the classic recession: a slump *and* falling prices together. The 2008 financial crisis was largely a giant negative demand shock.
- A **positive** demand shock (AD right) pushes both real GDP *and* the price level **up**. Output rises above potential — an inflationary gap — and prices heat up. This is a demand-pull boom.

Because output and inflation move together, demand shocks pose policymakers a *comfortable* trade-off: fighting the recession (boosting demand) also lifts prices back up; cooling inflation (restraining demand) also reins in an overheating boom. One lever fixes both symptoms.

</Step>

<Step title="Supply shocks: the nasty case" kind="explain">

A **supply shock** hits the SRAS curve instead — typically by changing the cost of production economy-wide. The textbook example is an **oil-price spike**: when energy suddenly costs far more, producing almost everything becomes dearer, so firms supply less at every price level and SRAS shifts **left**.

Now the symptoms are *opposite*: real GDP **falls** while the price level **rises** at the same time. That toxic combination — stagnant output (and rising unemployment) together with inflation — is **stagflation**. It shattered the old belief that inflation and unemployment always trade off against each other, because here they got worse *together*.

The 1970s are the defining episode: OPEC oil embargoes sent crude prices soaring, and Western economies suffered high inflation and high unemployment simultaneously for years. Supply shocks aren't only adverse — a *favourable* one (a productivity breakthrough, cheaper energy, the tech boom of the late 1990s) shifts SRAS **right**, giving the happy combination of growing output *and* falling prices.

<Callout type="warn" title="Why stagflation is a trap">
A supply shock gives policymakers no clean move. Boost demand to fight the recession, and you make the inflation worse. Restrain demand to fight inflation, and you deepen the recession. There is no single lever that fixes both — which is exactly why the stagflation of the 1970s was so agonising and why central banks dread cost shocks.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Demand shock vs supply shock, side by side" kind="interactive">

Toggle between the two shocks on the same diagram. Watch which curve moves and, crucially, which way the **price level** goes relative to output.

<SupplyShock />

The demand shock moves AD: output and the price level fall **together** (a recession, no inflation). The supply shock moves SRAS: output falls while the price level **rises** — stagflation. Same fall in GDP, but the price level points in opposite directions. That price-level signal is how economists diagnose, in real time, whether a downturn is coming from the demand side or the supply side — and therefore what, if anything, to do about it.

</Step>

<Step title="Should we wait, or act?" kind="explain">

Recall the model's promise of **self-correction**: a recessionary gap should, in theory, cure itself as idle workers and capacity push wages and costs down, shifting SRAS right until output returns to potential. So why not just wait?

This is one of the great debates in macroeconomics:

- **The classical / market view** trusts self-correction. Prices and wages are flexible enough that gaps close fairly quickly on their own; government attempts to "help" mostly arrive late, misfire, and add instability. Best to let markets adjust.
- **The Keynesian view** counters that wages are **sticky downward** — people fiercely resist pay cuts — so a recessionary gap can persist for *years*, with mass unemployment the whole time. As Keynes put it, "in the long run we are all dead." Waiting is not costless; active policy can speed the return to potential and spare real suffering.

Modern macro takes from both: self-correction is real but *slow and uneven*, so there's a genuine role for stabilisation policy — while respecting that policy has lags and limits, and that it works far better against demand shocks than against the cruel arithmetic of a supply shock. Resolving *how* to act — with government spending and taxes, or with interest rates and the money supply — is precisely the toolkit of the next world.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- Booms and busts happen when a **shock** shifts a curve; the AD/AS model is the engine behind the **business cycle**.
- **Demand shocks** move AD, so output and the price level move **together**: a negative one brings recession *and* falling prices; a positive one brings a boom *and* rising prices — a comfortable one-lever fix.
- **Supply shocks** move SRAS, so output and the price level move **oppositely**: an adverse one (e.g. an oil spike) brings falling output *and* rising prices — **stagflation**, the 1970s nightmare with no clean policy fix.
- A favourable supply shock (productivity, cheaper energy) gives growth *and* lower prices.
- **Self-correction** works but is **slow** (sticky-downward wages): the classical view trusts it; the Keynesian view favours active policy. Modern macro blends both — setting up the policy world ahead.

</Step>
