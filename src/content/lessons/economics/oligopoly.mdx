<Step title="A market of a few" kind="explain">

Some of the biggest industries in the world are run by just a handful of firms: airlines, mobile networks, soft drinks, smartphone makers, oil producers. This structure is **oligopoly** — a market dominated by a **few large firms**.

What lets a few firms dominate is the same thing that creates monopoly: **barriers to entry**. Usually they are enormous fixed costs and economies of scale — building a phone network or a chip factory costs billions, so only a few firms can reach the scale needed to compete. The products may be near-identical (oil, steel) or differentiated (smartphones, cars), but either way the market belongs to a small club.

</Step>

<Step title="The defining feature: interdependence" kind="explain">

The thing that makes oligopoly fascinating — and hard to analyse — is **interdependence**. With only a few firms, each one's decisions visibly affect the others, and *everyone knows it*.

When a perfectly competitive farmer cuts output, no one notices. When one of three airlines on a route cuts its fares, its two rivals notice *immediately* and must decide how to respond. Each firm's best move depends on what it expects the others to do — and the others are reasoning the very same way about it.

This is **strategic** behaviour: choices made in anticipation of rivals' reactions. It is exactly the situation that **game theory** was built to analyse, which is why oligopoly is the bridge from ordinary supply-and-demand into the strategic world we will explore at the summit of this island.

</Step>

<Step title="The temptation to collude" kind="explain">

Because the firms are so few, a tantalising possibility arises: instead of competing, why not **cooperate** and act like a single monopoly?

If the firms could agree to keep prices high and split the market, they would jointly earn monopoly-level profits — far more than they make fighting each other. A formal agreement to do this is a **cartel** (OPEC is the most famous example). Even without a written deal, firms may fall into **tacit collusion** — quietly matching each other's high prices without ever meeting.

Collusion is great for the firms and bad for consumers, which is exactly why it is **illegal** in most countries. But there is a deeper reason cartels keep collapsing even when they form, and it has nothing to do with the law.

</Step>

Walk through the tension. **Both high** is best *jointly* — the collusive outcome, fat profits all round. But look what happens if you undercut while your rival stays high: you grab the market and earn *even more*. Both firms see that temptation, so both are pulled toward **Price Low**. And once both undercut, they crash into a **price war** with thin profits — the worse outcome they were both trying to avoid. Pricing low is each firm's *dominant strategy*, so that low–low corner is where the market tends to settle.

</Step>

<Step title="Why cartels crack" kind="explain">

That little game captures why collusion is so unstable. Every member of a cartel has a private **incentive to cheat**: shave the price a little, sell a lot more, and pocket extra profit while everyone else holds the line. But since *every* member faces the same temptation, the agreement is constantly under strain.

The result, again and again, is the **price war** — firms undercut one another until prices tumble toward competitive levels and profits evaporate. Consumers love a price war; the firms dread it. Real oligopolies spend enormous effort trying to escape this trap without breaking the law: matching rivals' prices, signalling through public announcements, following a **price leader**, or competing on advertising and features instead of price.

<Callout type="info">
This is a real-world version of the **prisoner's dilemma**: each player, acting in its own rational self-interest, drives everyone to a worse outcome than if they had all cooperated. We will meet the dilemma formally — and ask how repetition and reputation can rescue cooperation — in the game-theory world.
</Callout>

Oligopoly thus lives in permanent tension: powerful enough to raise prices like a monopoly *if* the firms can hold together, but constantly pulled apart by each firm's incentive to compete. Where it lands — closer to monopoly or closer to competition — depends on how well the few firms can manage their interdependence.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- An **oligopoly** is a market of a **few large firms**, kept exclusive by big barriers to entry (high fixed costs, economies of scale).
- Its defining feature is **interdependence**: each firm's best move depends on rivals' reactions, making behaviour **strategic** — the bridge to **game theory**.
- Firms are tempted to **collude** (a **cartel** or tacit price-matching) to earn monopoly profits, which is why collusion is illegal.
- But each firm has an **incentive to cheat** by undercutting, so cartels are unstable and often collapse into **price wars** — a real-world **prisoner's dilemma**.

</Step>
