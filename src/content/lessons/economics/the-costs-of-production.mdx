<Step title="Fixed and variable costs" kind="explain">

Once we know how inputs become output, we can attach a price tag to production. A firm's costs split neatly into two kinds:

- **Fixed costs** don't change with how much you produce. Rent on the factory, the loan repayment on machines, insurance — you pay these whether you make zero units or a million.
- **Variable costs** rise and fall with output. Raw materials, electricity for the machines, the wages of production workers — make more, spend more.

Add them together and you get **total cost**:

<Formula tex="\text{Total Cost} = \text{Fixed Cost} + \text{Variable Cost}" block />

In the short run, fixed costs are unavoidable. That single fact — that some costs must be paid no matter what — turns out to drive some of the most important decisions a firm makes.

</Step>

<Step title="Averages and the margin" kind="explain">

Total cost is useful, but firms care more about cost *per unit*. Three measures matter:

- **Average variable cost (AVC)** — variable cost divided by quantity.
- **Average total cost (ATC)** — total cost divided by quantity; this is the cost per unit you must beat with your price to make a profit.
- **Marginal cost (MC)** — the extra cost of producing *one more* unit. This is the key decision variable, the cost-side twin of marginal product.

The gap between ATC and AVC is the **average fixed cost** — the fixed cost spread across the units made. As output grows, that fixed cost is shared over more and more units, so average fixed cost keeps falling and ATC drifts down toward AVC.

</Step>

Drag the output level and watch the three curves move together. This single chart is the workhorse of firm analysis.

<CostCurves />

Notice three things. First, **AVC and ATC are U-shaped**: they fall at first (fixed cost spreading out, early gains), then rise as diminishing returns make extra units costly. Second, the **MC curve cuts both AVC and ATC at their lowest points**. Third — and this is the reason for that crossing — when marginal cost is *below* an average, it pulls the average *down*; when MC is *above* the average, it drags the average *up*. The average can only be flat (at its minimum) exactly where MC equals it.

</Step>

<Step title="Why MC crosses at the minimum" kind="explain">

That crossing is not a coincidence; it is just arithmetic. Think of your exam average. If your next exam scores *below* your current average, your average falls. If the next exam scores *above* it, your average rises. Your average stops falling and starts rising precisely when one more exam scores exactly your average.

Marginal cost is that "next exam" for cost. While each additional unit costs less than the average so far, it drags the average down. Once an extra unit costs more than the average, it pulls the average up. So average cost bottoms out exactly where marginal cost passes through it.

<Callout type="tip">
This MC-cuts-average-at-its-minimum rule is worth memorising. It reappears the moment we ask where a firm maximises profit and at what price it should shut down.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- Costs split into **fixed** (unchanged by output) and **variable** (rise with output); together they make **total cost**.
- **AVC** and **ATC** are cost per unit; the gap between them is **average fixed cost**, which shrinks as output spreads fixed cost over more units.
- **Marginal cost** is the cost of one more unit — the key decision variable.
- AVC and ATC are **U-shaped**, and **MC cuts each at its minimum point** — because a marginal value below the average pulls it down, and above it pushes it up.

</Step>
