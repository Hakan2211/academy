<Step title="The one problem behind it all" kind="explain">

People think economics is about money, the stock market, or dry graphs. Underneath, it is about something far more human: **economics is the study of how people make choices under scarcity.**

Here is the problem in a sentence. **Our wants are essentially unlimited, but the resources to satisfy them are limited.** You want more time, more money, more comfort, a better phone, a longer holiday — and so does everyone else. But there are only so many hours, workers, machines, and materials to go around. That gap between boundless wants and finite means is called **scarcity**, and it never goes away. Even a billionaire faces it: they cannot buy back a wasted afternoon.

Because we can't have everything, we must **choose** — and economics is the science of those choices.

</Step>

<Step title="What the world has to work with" kind="explain">

The "limited resources" economists mean are the ingredients every economy uses to produce things, traditionally grouped as the four **factors of production**:

- **Land** — natural resources: soil, water, oil, a physical location.
- **Labour** — human effort, physical and mental.
- **Capital** — the tools, machines, and buildings we make in order to produce *more* (a tractor, a laptop, a factory).
- **Entrepreneurship** — the spark that combines the other three, takes risks, and organises production.

There is only so much of each at any moment. That is why a country can't simultaneously build every hospital, school, road, and rocket it might want — the workers and materials used for one can't also be used for another.

</Step>

<Step title="Feel the pinch" kind="interactive">

Scarcity isn't an abstraction — you meet it every time your wants outrun your budget. Try to buy everything you want with the money you have.

<ScarcityChoice />

There's no "wrong" pick. The point is that the limit is real: choosing some things means giving up others. That unavoidable trade-off is the seed from which all of economics grows.

</Step>

<Step title="Two altitudes: micro and macro" kind="explain">

Economists study choices at two scales.

**Microeconomics** zooms in on individual decision-makers — a shopper, a firm, a single market. Why did coffee get more expensive? Should this company hire another worker? How do buyers and sellers settle on a price?

**Macroeconomics** zooms out to the whole economy at once — growth, inflation, unemployment, recessions, the things that make national news. Why is the cost of living rising? Why do economies boom and then slump?

Same core idea — choice under scarcity — viewed through a microscope or a telescope. This island starts micro and zooms out to macro as you climb.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Economics** is the study of how people make **choices under scarcity**.
- **Scarcity** = unlimited wants meeting limited resources; it's universal and permanent.
- Production draws on four limited resources — **land, labour, capital, entrepreneurship**.
- Because we can't have it all, every choice involves a trade-off.
- **Microeconomics** studies individual choices and markets; **macroeconomics** studies the whole economy.

</Step>
