<Step title="What a bank actually does" kind="explain">

We tend to picture a bank as a giant safe where money sits still. It is almost the opposite. A bank is a **middleman for money in motion**: it gathers up the savings of people who have spare cash and channels them to people and firms who need to borrow.

On one side are **depositors** — you, putting your paycheck into a checking or savings account. On the other are **borrowers** — a family buying a house, a business buying equipment, a student paying tuition. The bank stands in the middle, taking deposits and turning most of them into **loans**.

This matters because savers and borrowers rarely know each other and want very different things. You want your money safe and available any time; the borrower wants a large sum for years. The bank bridges that gap: it pools thousands of small, short-term deposits to fund big, long-term loans — a service called **maturity transformation**.

</Step>

<Step title="How a bank makes money: the spread" kind="explain">

A bank earns its living on the gap between two interest rates.

It pays depositors a *low* rate of **interest** to attract their savings — say 2%. It charges borrowers a *higher* rate on loans — say 7%. The difference, the **spread**, is the bank's revenue. Out of it the bank covers its costs, absorbs the losses from loans that aren't repaid, and keeps the rest as profit.

<Formula tex="\text{spread} = \text{interest charged on loans} - \text{interest paid on deposits}" block />

Interest itself is simply the **price of borrowing money** — the rent you pay for using someone else's funds for a while. A higher interest rate means borrowing is more expensive, so people borrow less; a lower rate makes borrowing cheap and tempting. (That lever turns out to be one of the most powerful tools in all of economics, as a later world on monetary policy will show.)

</Step>

<Step title="Why credit matters" kind="explain">

**Credit** — borrowing now and repaying later — is one of the quiet engines of a modern economy. It lets people and firms act *before* they've saved up the full amount.

A young couple doesn't have to spend thirty years saving the price of a house before they can live in one; a mortgage lets them buy now and pay over time. A startup with a brilliant idea but no cash can borrow to hire and build, generating value (and repayments) it could never have reached otherwise. Credit moves resources to where they'll be most productive *today*, instead of leaving them idle until savings slowly accumulate.

<Callout type="tip" title="Credit is a tool, not free money">
Every loan is a promise to repay *more* later. Used well, credit funds homes, education, and productive businesses. Overused — borrowing for things that don't pay off, or more than income can support — it becomes a trap, because the interest compounds whether or not the borrowing was wise.
</Callout>

But here's the twist that the rest of this world builds on: because a bank lends out most of what it takes in, your deposit doesn't just sit there — it gets lent to someone else, who deposits it, and it gets lent again. Banks don't just *move* money. They help **create** it.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- A bank is a **middleman**, channelling **depositors'** savings to **borrowers** as **loans**.
- It performs **maturity transformation**: pooling small, short-term deposits to fund large, long-term loans.
- Banks earn the **spread** — the gap between interest charged on loans and interest paid on deposits.
- **Interest** is the price of borrowing; **credit** lets people and firms act before they've saved, moving resources to where they're productive now.
- Because banks lend out most deposits, they don't just move money — they help **create** it, the topic of the next lesson.

</Step>
