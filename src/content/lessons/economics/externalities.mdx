<Step title="When your choice spills onto others" kind="explain">

So far, markets have looked almost magical: buyers and sellers pursue their own interests, prices adjust, and — as if guided by an invisible hand — the outcome serves everyone. But that happy result quietly assumes one thing: that the only people affected by a trade are the buyer and the seller.

Often that assumption breaks. A factory makes steel and sells it; the buyer and the factory both gain. But the smoke drifting over the town harms people who never set foot in the market. That spilled-over harm is an **externality** — a cost (or benefit) of a transaction that lands on a third party, outside the price.

When externalities are present, the market still clears — but it clears at the **wrong** quantity. This is our first **market failure**: a case where free markets, left alone, do not produce the best outcome for society.

</Step>

<Step title="Private cost versus social cost" kind="explain">

The key is to separate two things the market usually treats as one.

- **Private cost** is what the producer personally pays — wages, materials, energy. This is what the supply curve reflects.
- **Social cost** is the private cost *plus* the spillover — the harm to bystanders (the smog, the polluted river). This is the cost society as a whole bears.

With a **negative externality**, social cost sits *above* private cost. Because firms decide based only on the costs they personally face, they happily produce every unit where their price covers their **private** cost — sailing right past the point where the unit's **social** cost exceeds its value. The result: the market **over-produces**.

The mirror image is a **positive externality**, where a transaction also helps third parties. A vaccination protects you *and* everyone you might have infected; education makes you more productive *and* a better-informed neighbour. Here the **social benefit** exceeds the private benefit, and the market **under-produces** — people buy only enough to satisfy *their own* gain, ignoring the bonus to everyone else.

</Step>

Notice the two marked quantities pull apart. With pollution, the market quantity overshoots the socially optimal one; with vaccination, it falls short. The grey triangle is the **welfare loss** — the value society throws away by producing the wrong amount. Shrink the spillover to zero and the gap closes: when there is no third-party effect, the free market is efficient after all.

</Step>

<Step title="Why the gap is real money" kind="explain">

It is tempting to dismiss the welfare loss as a textbook abstraction, but it is genuine. Every unit the steel mill makes between the social optimum and the market quantity is a unit whose true cost — including the damage to lungs and rivers downstream — is *greater* than what anyone gains from it. Society would be richer if those units were never made.

<Callout type="info" title="Externalities are everywhere">
Traffic congestion (your car slows everyone else's), antibiotic overuse (each prescription erodes everyone's future protection), a neighbour's beautiful garden (a gift to the whole street), and carbon emissions (the textbook case, on a planetary scale) are all externalities. The market price simply never asked the bystanders.
</Callout>

The diagnosis points straight at the cure. If the problem is that decision-makers ignore costs (or benefits) that fall on others, then the fix is to make them *feel* those costs and benefits — to fold the spillover back into the price. That is exactly what the next lesson does.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- An **externality** is a cost or benefit of a transaction that falls on a **third party**, outside the price — the first market failure.
- **Negative externality** (pollution): social cost > private cost, so the market **over-produces**.
- **Positive externality** (vaccination, education): social benefit > private benefit, so the market **under-produces**.
- The gap between the market quantity and the **socially optimal** quantity is a real **welfare loss** — value society wastes.
- The cure is to fold the spillover back into the price, which the next lesson tackles.

</Step>
