<Step title="You are a tiny economy" kind="explain">

Everything in this world — utility, the budget constraint, the equimarginal rule, the biases — comes together in the most familiar economy of all: your own life. Every day you allocate scarce resources (money, time, attention) across competing wants, and the same logic that governs markets governs your Tuesday afternoon.

This deep dive takes the toolkit and turns it on you. We'll see how the equimarginal rule quietly applies to *every* choice you make, and then meet three traps — the sunk-cost fallacy, present bias, and mental accounting — where real human behaviour drifts away from what the rule recommends. Spotting them is one of the most practical payoffs economics offers.

</Step>

<Step title="One rule to rule them all" kind="explain">

Recall consumer equilibrium: spend so the **last dollar on each good buys the same extra utility** — equalise marginal utility per dollar.

<Formula tex="\frac{MU_1}{P_1} = \frac{MU_2}{P_2} = \cdots = \frac{MU_n}{P_n}" block />

The astonishing thing is how *general* this is. Swap "dollar" for "hour" and it tells you how to split study time across subjects: put the next hour wherever it lifts your grade most, until every subject's marginal payoff per hour is equal. Swap it for "attention" and it governs what you read. Any scarce resource, any set of competing uses — the optimum is always **equalise the marginal return per unit**. It's the single deepest idea in the economics of choice.

</Step>

<Step title="Allocate to the optimum" kind="interactive">

See the rule bite. Split a fixed budget between two goods and watch the marginal utility *per dollar* of each. Your job: make the bars match.

<EquimarginalRule />

When one good towers over the other, you're leaving utility on the table — moving a dollar toward the taller bar buys more satisfaction. As you shift, diminishing marginal utility pulls the tall bar down and pushes the short one up, until they meet. That balance point is the most satisfaction this budget can buy. Now hold onto that ideal, because the next three habits all sabotage it.

</Step>

<Step title="Trap 1: the sunk-cost fallacy" kind="explain">

A **sunk cost** is money, time, or effort already spent and impossible to recover. The rational rule is brutal: since it's gone no matter what you do next, it should carry **zero weight** in your decision. Only marginal costs and benefits *from here forward* matter.

Yet we cling to sunk costs constantly. You force down a terrible meal because you "paid for it." You sit through a dreadful film because of the ticket. You pour more money into a failing project to "not waste" what's already spent — and waste even more. The fix is to ask one marginal question: *ignoring what's already gone, does the next step make me better off?* The €50 ticket is gone either way; the only live question is whether going *now* beats staying home.

</Step>

<Step title="Trap 2: present bias" kind="explain">

The equimarginal rule compares utility across goods, but real choices also stretch across *time* — and here we're wildly inconsistent. **Present bias** is our tendency to overweight rewards we can have *right now* and steeply discount those that arrive later, even when waiting clearly wins.

It's why we snooze the alarm, skip the workout, splurge instead of save, and procrastinate on work that's due next week. Calmly, in advance, we plan to behave well; in the moment, the immediate temptation balloons and the future shrinks to a rumour. Economists model this as **hyperbolic discounting** — the future gets discounted hardest right at the present moment. The practical defence is to make good behaviour the *present* easy option: automate savings, lay out gym clothes the night before, delete the app. You're not fighting your future self; you're outmanoeuvring your present one.

</Step>

<Step title="Trap 3: mental accounting" kind="explain">

The final trap breaks a rule economists call **fungibility**: a dollar is a dollar, no matter where it came from. **Mental accounting** is our habit of filing money into separate mental jars — "salary," "tax refund," "winnings," "vacation fund" — and treating each jar by different rules.

So we blow a $500 lottery win on a luxury we'd never buy with $500 of salary. We leave money earning nothing in a "savings" jar while paying high interest on a credit card, because the debt lives in a *different* jar. We splurge on holiday because it's "vacation money." A perfectly rational chooser would pool every dollar and aim it where its marginal utility per dollar is highest — exactly the equimarginal rule. Mental accounting quietly stops us, walling off money that *should* flow to wherever it does the most good.

</Step>

<Step title="Putting it together" kind="explain">

Notice the thread running through all three traps: each one breaks the equimarginal logic in a different way. The **sunk-cost fallacy** drags irrelevant past costs into a forward-looking margin. **Present bias** distorts the marginal utility of *time*, inflating now and shrinking later. **Mental accounting** blocks dollars from flowing to their highest marginal use.

Behavioural economics doesn't junk the rational model — it shows where and *why* we predictably stray from it, which is exactly what makes the model useful. Once you can name these traps, you can catch yourself mid-mistake: *that's a sunk cost, ignore it; that's present bias, automate around it; that's a mental jar, the money's still just money.* The economics of you is the most useful economics there is.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- Your daily life is a tiny economy: scarce money, time, and attention allocated across competing wants.
- The **equimarginal rule** generalises to *any* scarce resource — equalise the marginal return per unit (dollar, hour, unit of attention).
- **Sunk-cost fallacy**: letting unrecoverable past spending sway a forward-looking choice; ignore it and decide at the margin.
- **Present bias**: overweighting immediate rewards (hyperbolic discounting); beat it by making good behaviour the easy *present* option.
- **Mental accounting**: filing money into jars and breaking fungibility, so dollars don't flow to their highest-value use.
- Naming these traps lets you catch yourself straying from your own equimarginal optimum.

</Step>
