<Step title="The spectrum, end to end" kind="explain">

We have now met all four market structures. Before we ask what to *do* about market power, let us see them as a single continuum — because in the real world the boundaries blur, and a market can drift along the spectrum over time.

At one end is **perfect competition**: countless tiny price takers, identical products, free entry, zero long-run economic profit. At the other is **monopoly**: a single price maker behind barriers, restricting output and charging a fat markup. In between sit **monopolistic competition** (many firms, differentiated products, modest power) and **oligopoly** (a few interdependent firms with real but contested power).

The single thread running through all four is **market power** — the ability of a firm to set its price *above* marginal cost. Perfect competitors have none; monopolists have the most; the in-between structures have some. The more market power, the higher the markup and the larger the deadweight loss.

</Step>

<Step title="Reading the spectrum" kind="interactive">

Slide across the whole range and watch the three things that define a firm's power move together: the **number of firms**, the **markup over cost**, and the **slope of the demand curve the firm faces**.

<CompetitionSpectrum />

As you move from competition toward monopoly, three trends march in lockstep. The number of firms **falls**. The demand curve the firm faces **rotates from flat to steep** — from price taker to price maker. And the **markup over marginal cost grows**, opening the gap between price and cost that signals lost efficiency. This is the map. Antitrust policy is the toolkit for keeping markets from sliding too far up it.

</Step>

<Step title="What's actually wrong with market power" kind="explain">

It is worth being precise about the *harm*, because the goal of policy is to fix the harm, not to punish bigness.

Recall the monopoly diagram. The high price on units that *are* sold is a **transfer** — money moving from consumers to the firm. That may offend our sense of fairness, but it does not make the economy smaller; one party's loss is another's gain.

The genuine economic damage is the **deadweight loss**: the mutually beneficial trades that *never happen* because the firm restricts output to prop up its price. Those are units buyers valued above their cost of production, now simply not made. On top of that, unchallenged market power can breed **slack**: with no competitive pressure, a protected firm may let costs creep up, innovate slowly, or offer shoddy service. Competition is the discipline that keeps firms lean and inventive; remove it and that discipline goes too.

</Step>

<Step title="The policy toolkit" kind="explain">

Governments have three broad responses to excessive market power.

- **Antitrust (competition) law.** Rules that *promote competition* by attacking the conduct and structures that destroy it: banning price-fixing cartels, reviewing mergers that would concentrate a market, and prohibiting abuse of a dominant position. The aim is to keep markets contestable.
- **Regulation.** Where competition is impossible or undesirable — a **natural monopoly** like a water network or power grid — the answer is not to break the firm up but to *regulate* it: cap its prices near average cost, oversee service quality, and let it keep the efficiency of a single network without the monopoly markup.
- **Public ownership.** In some countries, the state simply *owns* the natural monopoly (the rail track, the postal service) and runs it in the public interest. Less common today, but still part of the toolkit.

The art is matching the tool to the problem: break up or block what should be competitive, regulate what cannot be.

</Step>

<Step title="A casebook" kind="interactive">

Theory becomes concrete when you face real situations. Here are four classic market-power problems. For each, identify the structure at issue, then see the policy response a competition authority would reach for.

<AntitrustCases />

Notice the pattern. A **merger** that would create a tight oligopoly is met with **merger review** — blocked, or cleared only with divestitures. A secret **cartel** fixing prices is the cardinal sin, met with heavy fines and even criminal charges. A **natural monopoly** utility is *not* broken up — it is **regulated**, because one network is genuinely cheapest. And a dominant **platform** abusing its position is met with **conduct remedies** that target the behaviour, not the size. Different structures, different cures.

</Step>

<Step title="The hard part: not anti-bigness" kind="explain">

Here is the subtlety that makes antitrust genuinely difficult. **Big is not the same as bad.** A firm can become dominant by being *better* — lower prices, a superior product, real economies of scale. Punishing that would harm the very consumers policy is meant to protect, and would blunt the incentive to compete hard in the first place.

So good competition policy distinguishes between **market power earned by serving customers well** and **market power used to harm them** — by colluding, by foreclosing rivals, by buying up nascent competitors. The first should be left alone (or even celebrated); the second is what the law targets. The test is not size but **consumer harm**.

<Callout type="warn">
This is also why **government can fail**, just as markets can. Regulators may be captured by the industry they oversee, set prices wrongly, or block efficient mergers out of caution. The choice is rarely "perfect market versus perfect government" but "imperfect market versus imperfect remedy" — which is why economists weigh the costs of intervention against the costs of doing nothing.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Why some monopoly is tolerated on purpose" kind="explain">

Finally, a twist: society sometimes *grants* monopolies deliberately. A **patent** hands an inventor a temporary monopoly over a new drug or device; a **copyright** does the same for a book or song.

Why create the very market power we usually fight? Because of the **incentive to innovate**. Inventing is expensive and risky; copying is cheap. Without temporary protection, inventors could not recoup their costs and would invent far less. So we accept a *temporary* deadweight loss (the patent monopoly's high price) in exchange for the *long-run* benefit of new ideas — and the protection expires, after which competition floods in and prices fall.

This captures the whole spirit of market-structure policy. The goal is never competition or monopoly for its own sake. It is to get the **most value for society** — balancing the efficiency of competition, the gains from scale, and the incentive to innovate, intervening where market power does genuine harm and standing back where it does not.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- The four structures form a **spectrum of market power** — the ability to price above marginal cost — from none (perfect competition) to most (monopoly), with monopolistic competition and oligopoly in between.
- The real harm of market power is the **deadweight loss** from restricted output (and the **slack** of unchallenged firms), not the price markup, which is a transfer.
- The policy toolkit is **antitrust** (ban cartels, review mergers, stop abuse), **regulation** (for natural monopolies), and sometimes **public ownership** — matching the tool to the problem.
- Good policy is **not anti-bigness**: it distinguishes power *earned by serving customers* from power *used to harm them*, judging by **consumer harm**.
- Society even **grants temporary monopolies** (patents, copyrights) to reward innovation — proof that the goal is total value for society, not competition for its own sake.

</Step>
