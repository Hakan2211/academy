<Step title="A science of human choice" kind="explain">

Economics is a **social science**: it studies people, but it does so with the scientist's toolkit — observe, theorise, model, and test against data. You can't usually run a clean laboratory experiment on a whole economy, so economists lean heavily on **models**: deliberately simplified pictures of reality that strip away detail to reveal how the essential pieces fit together.

That simplification is a feature, not a flaw. A subway map isn't an accurate drawing of a city — it ignores streets, scale, and distance — and that's exactly why it's useful for the one thing it's built to do. Economic models work the same way: the PPF assumed only two goods, and that "unrealistic" assumption is what let it show opportunity cost so clearly.

</Step>

<Step title="Holding everything else still" kind="explain">

To study one cause at a time, economists use the assumption **ceteris paribus** — Latin for "all else equal."

When we say "a higher price reduces the quantity people buy," we mean *holding everything else constant* — incomes, tastes, the prices of other goods. In the real world many things move at once, so this assumption is how we isolate a single relationship before reassembling the full picture. It's the economist's version of a controlled experiment, done on paper.

</Step>

<Step title="A model of the whole economy" kind="interactive">

Here's a classic model that captures an entire economy in one loop: the **circular-flow diagram**. It strips the economy down to two players — households and firms — trading in two kinds of markets.

<CircularFlow />

Follow the loops. Households own the resources (labour, land, capital) and sell them to firms in **resource markets**, earning income. Firms use those resources to make goods and sell them back to households in **goods markets**. Money flows one way; goods and resources flow the other. The deep insight: **every dollar of spending is also a dollar of someone's income** — the economy is one connected circle, not a collection of separate pockets.

</Step>

<Step title="Positive vs normative" kind="explain">

Economists carefully separate two kinds of claims:

- **Positive statements** describe *what is* — they can be tested against evidence. "A minimum-wage rise reduces teen employment" is positive: data can confirm or refute it.
- **Normative statements** prescribe *what ought to be* — they rest on values, not facts alone. "The government *should* raise the minimum wage" is normative: it depends on what you think is fair.

Good analysis keeps them distinct. The positive question (*what will this policy actually do?*) is for evidence; the normative question (*is that outcome desirable?*) is for society's values. Mixing them up is how arguments go in circles.

</Step>

<Step title="Why economists disagree" kind="explain">

If economics is a science, why do economists argue on the news? Two honest reasons:

1. **Different positive views.** The economy is fiendishly complex, data is noisy, and you can't rerun history. Reasonable experts can read the same evidence and draw different conclusions about cause and effect.
2. **Different normative values.** Even when they agree on *what a policy will do*, they may disagree on whether that trade-off — say, a bit more growth for a bit more inequality — is *worth it*. That's a values question no dataset can settle.

In fact, economists agree far more than the headlines suggest. The disputes you hear about are usually the genuinely hard, unsettled cases — which is exactly where the interesting questions live.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- Economics is a **social science** that uses simplified **models** to reveal how an economy works.
- **Ceteris paribus** ("all else equal") isolates one cause at a time — a paper experiment.
- The **circular-flow model** shows households and firms linked by goods and resource markets: every dollar spent is a dollar earned.
- **Positive** statements describe what *is* (testable); **normative** statements say what *ought* to be (value-based).
- Economists disagree mainly over hard positive questions and differing values — but agree more than headlines imply.
- **The big ideas so far:** scarcity forces choice · choices have an opportunity cost · think at the margin · people respond to incentives · trade creates gains.

</Step>
