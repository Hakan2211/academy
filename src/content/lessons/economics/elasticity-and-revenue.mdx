<Lesson>

<Step title="Should you raise the price, or cut it?" kind="explain">

Here is the question that keeps business owners up at night: *if I change my price, what happens to my total revenue?* It seems like raising the price should always bring in more money — and cutting it, less. But that intuition is wrong, and elasticity tells you exactly when.

**Total revenue** is simply price times quantity sold:

<Formula tex="TR = P \times Q" block />

When you change the price, *both* factors move — but in opposite directions. Raise the price and each sale earns more, yet you make fewer sales. Which effect wins? That depends entirely on elasticity. This is the **total-revenue test**.

</Step>

<Step title="The total-revenue test" kind="explain">

The logic falls straight out of elasticity:

- **Elastic demand (|E| > 1):** quantity reacts *more* than price. Cut the price and the flood of new buyers more than makes up for charging each one less — **revenue rises**. Raise the price and revenue falls. Price and revenue move in *opposite* directions.
- **Inelastic demand (|E| < 1):** quantity reacts *less* than price. Cut the price and the trickle of extra buyers cannot offset charging everyone less — **revenue falls**. Raise the price and revenue rises. Price and revenue move in the *same* direction.
- **Unit elastic (|E| = 1):** the two effects exactly cancel — revenue is at its **maximum** and a small price change barely moves it.

So the revenue-maximising price sits precisely where demand turns from elastic to inelastic: the unit-elastic point.

</Step>

<Step title="Why this rules pricing" kind="explain">

This is why the answer to "should I raise my price?" is always *"it depends — where are you on the curve?"*

A concert promoter with a near-sold-out, one-of-a-kind show faces inelastic demand: fans have no substitute, so raising the price pulls in more revenue. A roadside stall selling the same strawberries as ten neighbours faces elastic demand: raise the price and customers stroll three metres to a rival, so cutting the price actually earns more. The same move — a price hike — is brilliant in one case and ruinous in the other.

<Callout type="tip" title="The test in one line">
On the elastic part of demand, price and revenue move in *opposite* directions; on the inelastic part, they move *together*. Revenue peaks where the two regions meet.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Total revenue = price × quantity**; a price change moves both, in opposite directions.
- The **total-revenue test**: on **elastic** demand, price and revenue move in *opposite* directions; on **inelastic** demand, they move *together*.
- Revenue is **maximised at unit elasticity** — the peak of the revenue hill.
- This explains pricing strategy: cut prices when demand is elastic, raise them when it is inelastic.

</Step>
