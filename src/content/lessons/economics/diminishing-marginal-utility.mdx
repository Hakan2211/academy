<Step title="The first is the best" kind="explain">

There's a rhythm to enjoyment that almost everyone recognises. The first bite of dessert is bliss; the first sip of water after a run is glorious; the first hour of a video game flies by. The fifth bite, sip, or hour? Pleasant, maybe — but a shadow of the first.

Economists turned this everyday feeling into a law: the **law of diminishing marginal utility**. It says that as you consume more units of the *same* good in a given period, the extra satisfaction from each additional unit tends to **fall**. Not your total enjoyment — that can keep growing — but the *bonus* each new unit delivers.

</Step>

<Step title="Why it happens" kind="explain">

Why should the second slice satisfy less than the first? Because your most urgent want gets met first.

When you're hungry, the first slice attacks the sharpest need — basic hunger — and the relief is huge. By the second slice that need is mostly handled, so the slice now serves a milder want. By the fifth, you're satisfying a very faint craving indeed, and the extra utility is small. We naturally spend the first units of anything on what matters most to us, leaving the later units to chase ever-less-pressing desires.

This is one of the most reliable patterns in all of economics. It quietly shapes how much we'll pay, when we stop buying, and — most famously — why the price of a thing has almost nothing to do with how essential it is.

</Step>

<Step title="The diamond–water paradox" kind="interactive">

Here is the puzzle that troubled Adam Smith himself. Water keeps you alive; diamonds are pretty rocks. Yet water is nearly free and diamonds cost a fortune. How can the *more useful* thing be *cheaper*?

Toggle between the two goods and watch their marginal utility.

<DiamondWater />

The resolution is pure diminishing marginal utility. Water is abundant, so we consume it far out along the curve — to the point where one *more* litre is barely worth anything. That tiny **marginal** utility is what sets its price. Diamonds are scarce, so we own only the first few units, where marginal utility is still sky-high. Price tracks the value of the **last** unit, not the **total** value — and that flips the paradox on its head.

</Step>

<Step title="Total value vs marginal value" kind="explain">

Say it once more, because it's the whole point: a good's **total** value to your life and its **marginal** value can be wildly different.

Water's total value is almost infinite — without it you die. But because there's so much of it, its *marginal* value (and therefore its price) is close to zero. Diamonds have almost no total value to survival, yet their scarcity keeps their *marginal* value enormous. Markets price the margin, not the total. The thing you'd die without can be free, simply because you're not short of it.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- The **law of diminishing marginal utility**: each additional unit of the same good usually adds *less* extra satisfaction than the one before.
- It happens because we meet our most urgent wants first; later units chase weaker desires.
- The **diamond–water paradox**: water is essential but cheap, diamonds are frivolous but dear.
- Resolution: price tracks **marginal** utility, not total. Abundance → low marginal utility → low price; scarcity → high marginal utility → high price.

</Step>
