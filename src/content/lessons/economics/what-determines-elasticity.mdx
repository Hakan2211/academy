<Step title="What sets a good's elasticity?" kind="explain">

Why is demand for insulin almost perfectly rigid while demand for one brand of soda is wildly sensitive? Elasticity is not random — a handful of features predict how much wiggle room buyers have. The more easily a buyer can say *no*, *later*, or *something else instead*, the more elastic demand becomes.

Four factors do most of the work: the **availability of substitutes**, whether the good is a **necessity or a luxury**, the **time horizon**, and the **share of the budget** the good eats up.

</Step>

<Step title="The four big factors" kind="explain">

**Substitutes.** This is the strongest force. If close alternatives exist, buyers flee the moment your price rises — demand is elastic. A specific brand of soda has many rivals (very elastic); salt has almost none (inelastic). Narrowing the definition adds substitutes: demand for *all* fizzy drinks is far less elastic than demand for *one brand*.

**Necessity versus luxury.** You buy a necessity whatever the price — demand is inelastic. A luxury is easy to skip or delay when it gets pricey — elastic. Heart medication is a necessity; a second holiday is a luxury.

**Time horizon.** Demand is more elastic in the long run, because buyers need time to adjust. When petrol spikes, you cannot change cars overnight (inelastic this week), but over years you switch to a smaller car or move closer to work (elastic eventually).

**Share of the budget.** A good that swallows a big chunk of income gets scrutinised hard — demand is elastic. A trivially cheap good (salt, a box of matches) is bought without a second thought, so demand is inelastic even though substitutes exist.

</Step>

The two extremes anchor your intuition. Insulin sits near *perfectly inelastic* (a vertical line — a life-saving necessity with no substitute). One farmer's wheat sits near *perfectly elastic* (a horizontal line — identical to every other farmer's, so a penny too high and buyers vanish).

<Step title="Putting the factors together" kind="explain">

Real goods blend these forces, and they can pull in opposite directions. Table salt is a necessity (inelastic) with few substitutes (inelastic) *and* a tiny budget share (inelastic) — every factor points the same way, so demand is extremely inelastic. A restaurant meal at one particular chain is a luxury (elastic), with countless substitutes (elastic), often a noticeable expense (elastic) — so demand is very elastic.

When you meet a new good, run the checklist: *How easily can buyers substitute? Must they have it? Can they wait? Does it dent the budget?* The answers tell you, before any data, roughly how elastic demand will be.

<Callout type="info" title="Addiction tightens demand">
Goods people feel they cannot do without — cigarettes, caffeine — behave like necessities with no acceptable substitute, making demand strikingly inelastic. That is exactly why they are such reliable targets for taxes, as the next lessons show.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- Elasticity is driven by four factors: **substitutes, necessity vs luxury, time horizon, and budget share**.
- **Substitutes** matter most — more (and closer) substitutes mean more elastic demand.
- **Necessities** are inelastic; **luxuries** are elastic.
- Demand is **more elastic in the long run**, as buyers find ways to adjust.
- A **bigger budget share** makes buyers more price-sensitive (more elastic).
- The extremes: insulin ≈ perfectly inelastic (vertical); one farmer's identical wheat ≈ perfectly elastic (horizontal).

</Step>
