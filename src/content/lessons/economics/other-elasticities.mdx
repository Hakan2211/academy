<Step title="Elasticity beyond a good's own price" kind="explain">

So far elasticity has measured how quantity responds to a good's *own* price. But quantity demanded reacts to other things too — your income, and the prices of *related* goods. Each gets its own elasticity, and the *sign* of the answer (not just its size) carries the meaning.

Three more elasticities round out the toolkit: **income elasticity**, **cross-price elasticity**, and the **price elasticity of supply**. Together they let economists classify goods and predict how whole markets shift.

</Step>

<Step title="Income elasticity: normal vs inferior goods" kind="explain">

**Income elasticity** measures how quantity demanded responds to a change in income:

<Formula tex="E_I = \frac{\%\,\Delta Q}{\%\,\Delta\,\text{Income}}" block />

The sign tells you what kind of good it is:

- **Normal goods** have *positive* income elasticity — get richer and you buy more (restaurant meals, holidays, new cars). Luxuries are the strongest case, with income elasticity above 1: a 10% raise lifts your spending on them by *more* than 10%.
- **Inferior goods** have *negative* income elasticity — get richer and you buy *less*. As income rises people switch away from instant noodles, bus travel, and second-hand clothes toward pricier alternatives.

A single good can be normal at low incomes and inferior at high ones, which is why these labels describe a good *for a given group of buyers*, not a permanent property.

</Step>

<Step title="Cross-price elasticity: substitutes vs complements" kind="explain">

**Cross-price elasticity** measures how the quantity of good A responds to a change in the *price of good B*. Again the sign is the whole story:

- **Substitutes** have *positive* cross-price elasticity. When tea gets pricier, people buy more coffee — the price of one and the quantity of the other move *together*. Goods that replace each other.
- **Complements** have *negative* cross-price elasticity. When printers get pricier, people buy fewer ink cartridges — the price of one and the quantity of the other move in *opposite* directions. Goods consumed together.
- A cross-price elasticity near **zero** means the goods are basically unrelated (the price of milk and the demand for socks).

This is how a firm gauges its rivals: a strongly positive cross-price elasticity means another product is a close substitute — a direct competitor whose price changes you ignore at your peril.

</Step>

<Step title="And the supply side responds too" kind="interactive">

Demand is not the only side with elasticity. The **price elasticity of supply** measures how strongly quantity *supplied* responds to price. It is high when producers can ramp output up or down easily (a factory with spare capacity, or any good with lots of time to adjust) and low when they cannot (beachfront land, a vintage wine already bottled, a crop mid-season).

Use the gallery again, reading each shape as a *supply* curve this time: a flat curve is elastic supply (easy to expand), a steep one is inelastic supply (output is nearly fixed).

<ElasticityTypes />

The same flat-means-elastic, steep-means-inelastic logic that governed demand governs supply — and, as the next lesson shows, the *relative* elasticity of the two sides decides who really pays a tax.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Income elasticity**: positive = **normal** good (luxuries > 1), negative = **inferior** good (bought less as income rises).
- **Cross-price elasticity**: positive = **substitutes** (replace each other), negative = **complements** (used together), near zero = unrelated.
- The **sign** of these elasticities, not just the size, carries the meaning.
- **Price elasticity of supply** measures how easily producers adjust quantity — high with spare capacity and time, low when output is fixed.
- Relative demand-vs-supply elasticity sets up the next question: who really pays a tax?

</Step>
