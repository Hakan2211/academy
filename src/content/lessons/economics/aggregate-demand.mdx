<Step title="From one market to the whole economy" kind="explain">

So far you've drawn supply and demand for a *single* good — coffee, say, or labour. Now we zoom all the way out. **Macroeconomics** asks about the whole economy at once: not the price of one thing, but the average **price level** of everything, and not the output of one firm, but the total **real GDP** of the nation.

To analyse that, economists draw a new diagram that looks deceptively similar. On the vertical axis is the **price level** (an index of average prices). On the horizontal axis is **real GDP** — total output. The first curve we add is **aggregate demand (AD)**: the total quantity of goods and services that households, firms, the government, and foreigners want to buy at each price level.

Aggregate demand is just the four spending components of GDP added up: **C + I + G + NX** (consumption, investment, government, and net exports). Like an ordinary demand curve, AD slopes **downward** — but, surprisingly, for completely different reasons.

</Step>

<Step title="Why aggregate demand slopes down" kind="explain">

A single good's demand slopes down mostly because buyers substitute *toward cheaper alternatives*. But the AD curve covers *everything at once*, so "switch to a substitute" can't be the reason — there's nothing outside the economy to switch to. Instead, three distinct effects make total spending rise as the overall price level falls:

- **The wealth effect.** When the price level falls, the money and savings people hold buy more. Feeling richer, households spend more. (A higher price level erodes that wealth and spending falls.)
- **The interest-rate effect.** A lower price level means people need less cash for everyday transactions, so they save more. More saving pushes interest rates **down**, which makes borrowing cheaper — so firms invest more and households buy more homes and cars.
- **The exchange-rate effect.** Lower interest rates also make domestic assets less attractive, so the currency tends to weaken. A weaker currency makes exports cheaper abroad and imports dearer at home, so **net exports rise**.

All three point the same way: a lower price level → more total spending. That's the downward slope of AD.

</Step>

<Step title="What shifts the whole curve" kind="explain">

A change in the price level moves us *along* AD. But anything that changes spending **at a given price level** shifts the entire curve. Since AD is C + I + G + NX, anything boosting one of those four components shifts AD **right** (more demand); anything cutting them shifts it **left**:

- **Consumption (C):** consumer confidence, wealth (a stock-market or housing boom), taxes, expectations of the future.
- **Investment (I):** business confidence, interest rates, expected profits, the cost of capital.
- **Government (G):** a deliberate change in government spending — a key policy lever you'll meet next world.
- **Net exports (NX):** foreign incomes, exchange rates, the relative growth of trading partners.

A wave of optimism, a tax cut, a spending programme, a booming export market — each pushes AD outward. Pessimism, austerity, or a recession abroad pulls it back.

<Callout type="tip" title="Move along vs shift">
Same rule as ordinary demand. A change in the **price level** is a movement *along* AD. A change in anything else — confidence, taxes, spending, exchange rates — *shifts* the whole AD curve. Keep the two straight and macro reasoning stays simple.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Aggregate demand (AD)** is total spending in the economy at each price level: **C + I + G + NX**, plotted against real GDP.
- AD slopes **down** for three macro reasons: the **wealth**, **interest-rate**, and **exchange-rate** effects — *not* substitution between goods.
- A change in the **price level** is a movement *along* AD.
- Anything that changes C, I, G, or NX at a given price level **shifts** AD: confidence, wealth, taxes, interest rates, government spending, exchange rates, foreign incomes.
- A rightward AD shift raises both the price level and real GDP; a leftward shift lowers both — the signature of a demand-driven swing.

</Step>
