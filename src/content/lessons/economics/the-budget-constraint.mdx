<Step title="Wanting it all, affording some" kind="explain">

Diminishing marginal utility tells us how much we'd *enjoy* extra units. But enjoyment isn't the only thing that decides what we buy — money does too. You might love both great coffee and good books, yet your wallet forces a trade-off: every dollar spent on one is a dollar not spent on the other.

That limit is the **budget constraint**. Given your income and the prices of things, it marks the boundary between what you *can* afford and what you can only dream about. Choice lives entirely inside that boundary — and the best choice usually sits right on its edge.

</Step>

<Step title="Drawing the budget line" kind="explain">

Put two goods on a graph — coffees across, books up. If your income is **M** and the prices are **Pₓ** (coffee) and **Pᵧ** (book), then any bundle you can *just* afford satisfies:

<Formula tex="P_x \cdot x + P_y \cdot y = M" block />

That equation draws a straight **budget line**. Its two ends are the extremes: spend everything on coffee (M ÷ Pₓ coffees, no books) or everything on books (M ÷ Pᵧ books, no coffee). Every point on the line spends your whole budget; every point below it leaves money unspent; every point above it is unaffordable.

The line's slope is **−Pₓ/Pᵧ** — the market's trade-off. If coffee costs half as much as a book, you must give up half a book for each extra coffee. More income slides the whole line outward; a price change pivots one end.

</Step>

<Step title="Explore what you can afford" kind="interactive">

Adjust your income and the two prices to reshape the line, then slide a point along it to feel the trade-off.

<BudgetConstraint />

Watch how a higher income pushes the line *out* (more of everything) while a price rise pivots its end *in* (less of that good). The green **best bundle** marks the choice that squeezes the most satisfaction out of this budget — and it's never at a corner. The sweet spot mixes both goods. Why? That's the equimarginal rule.

</Step>

<Step title="The equimarginal rule" kind="explain">

Of all the affordable bundles, which gives the most utility? The answer is a beautifully simple rule. Keep shifting a dollar from one good to another as long as it buys *more* extra utility there. You stop only when the **last dollar spent on each good buys the same extra utility** — when marginal utility *per dollar* is equal across goods:

<Formula tex="\frac{MU_x}{P_x} = \frac{MU_y}{P_y}" block />

This is **consumer equilibrium**, and it's just marginal thinking applied to spending. If coffee gave more utility per dollar than books, you'd buy more coffee — which (by diminishing marginal utility) lowers coffee's MU and raises books', until the two ratios meet. At that point no reshuffle can make you better off. You've spent optimally.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- The **budget constraint** limits choice to what your income and prices allow.
- The **budget line** (Pₓ·x + Pᵧ·y = M) shows every bundle that spends income fully; its slope −Pₓ/Pᵧ is the market trade-off.
- More income shifts the line out; a price change pivots one end.
- **Consumer equilibrium** maximises utility where **MUₓ/Pₓ = MUᵧ/Pᵧ** — the **equimarginal rule**: equalise marginal utility per dollar across goods.

</Step>
