<Step title="The four factors of production" kind="explain">

Labor isn't the only thing firms hire. To produce anything, an economy combines **four factors of production**, and each earns its own kind of income:

- **Land** (and natural resources) earns **rent**.
- **Labor** (human effort) earns **wages**.
- **Capital** (machines, tools, buildings) earns **interest**.
- **Entrepreneurship** (organizing the other three and bearing risk) earns **profit**.

We've met the labor market. The good news is that the markets for land and capital work the **same way** — they are *factor markets*, and the income each factor earns is a price set by supply and demand. The demand for every factor is **derived**: a firm wants land, machines or workers only for what they help produce.

</Step>

<Step title="The same diagram, three times" kind="interactive">

Here is the labor market again — but read the axes more generally. The vertical axis is the **price of a factor**, the horizontal axis is the **quantity** of it hired, and the demand curve is that factor's **marginal revenue product**: how much extra revenue one more unit brings in.

Slide productivity. As a factor becomes more productive, demand shifts right and its price rises — exactly as it did for labor.

<LaborMarket />

Relabel the picture and it becomes any factor market. Make the price **rent** and the quantity **acres of land**. Or make the price the **interest rate** and the quantity **machines**. The mechanics never change: a factor's price is set where the firms' demand for it meets its supply, and that demand is driven by how much value the factor produces.

</Step>

<Step title="Rent and interest" kind="explain">

**Land** has a peculiar feature: its total quantity is roughly **fixed** — you can't manufacture more acres of a city center. So supply is nearly vertical, and the **rent** is determined almost entirely by demand. When demand for a location soars (a booming city), its rent soars, because more land simply can't appear. This is why prime real estate commands such high rents: scarcity meets strong, derived demand.

**Capital** — the machines, tools and buildings used to produce things — earns **interest**, the price paid to use funds or equipment over time. A firm borrows or invests in capital only if the extra output it generates is worth at least the interest cost — marginal thinking again. The interest rate is essentially the price of capital, set in financial markets by the supply of savings and the demand for investment funds.

</Step>

<Step title="Why entrepreneurs earn profit" kind="explain">

The fourth factor is different. An **entrepreneur** assembles land, labor and capital into a working business, decides what to make and how, and **bears the risk** that it all fails. There is no guaranteed wage or rent for this; the entrepreneur is the residual claimant — they get whatever is **left over** after every other factor has been paid. That residual is **profit** (or, if the venture flops, a loss).

Profit is the reward for risk-taking and for spotting opportunities others missed, and it's the engine of a market economy: the chance of profit is what lures people into starting firms and trying new ideas. Notice the pattern across all four factors — each input is paid according to what it contributes, and the prices of land, labor, capital and enterprise are how a market economy decides *how* to produce and *who* gets the rewards.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- Production uses four factors, each earning its own income: **land → rent, labor → wages, capital → interest, entrepreneurship → profit**.
- Factor markets all work like the labor market: a factor's price is set by supply and demand, and demand is **derived** from the factor's marginal revenue product.
- **Land** is roughly fixed in supply, so rent is driven mainly by demand; **capital** earns interest, the price of using funds or equipment over time.
- **Entrepreneurs** organize the other factors and bear risk; their reward is **profit**, the residual left after everyone else is paid — the incentive that drives a market economy.

</Step>
