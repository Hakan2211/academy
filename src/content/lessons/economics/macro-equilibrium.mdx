<Step title="Where AD meets AS" kind="explain">

You now have both halves of the diagram: a downward **aggregate demand** curve and an upward **short-run aggregate supply** curve, with a vertical **long-run aggregate supply** line standing at potential output. Put AD and SRAS on the same axes and they cross at one point. That crossing is the **short-run macroeconomic equilibrium** — the combination of **price level** and **real GDP** at which total spending exactly equals total production.

Just like a single market, the economy is pushed toward this crossing. If output ran *above* it, goods would pile up unsold and producers would cut back; if output ran *below* it, shelves would empty and producers would ramp up. Spending and production settle where AD = SRAS, fixing the economy's price level and real GDP for now.

But there's a deeper question this diagram answers that a single market never could: is the economy producing *the right amount*? That's where the vertical LRAS earns its keep.

</Step>

<Step title="Three places the economy can land" kind="explain">

The short-run equilibrium (AD ∩ SRAS) need not sit on the LRAS. Comparing **equilibrium real GDP** to **potential output** gives three possibilities — and this comparison is the single most useful judgement in macro:

- **At potential.** Equilibrium GDP lands right on the LRAS. The economy is at full employment, with no gap. This is the healthy resting point everything tends toward in the long run.
- **A recessionary gap.** Equilibrium GDP falls **short** of potential (to the left of LRAS). Factories sit idle, workers are jobless beyond the normal frictional level, and the economy is producing less than it could. This is the macro face of a recession.
- **An inflationary gap.** Equilibrium GDP runs **above** potential (to the right of LRAS). The economy is overheating: resources are stretched past their normal limits, and that pressure bids the price level up. It can't last.

The distance between equilibrium GDP and potential is the **output gap**. Closing it — without overshooting — is the central goal of stabilisation policy.

</Step>

<Step title="Why the gaps don't last forever" kind="explain">

Here's the elegant part. The same sticky costs that gave SRAS its slope eventually unstick, and that's what (in theory) closes the gaps on its own — the economy's **self-correction**.

In a **recessionary gap**, high unemployment and idle capacity put downward pressure on wages and input prices. As those costs fall, SRAS gradually shifts **right**, dragging equilibrium GDP back up to potential at a lower price level. In an **inflationary gap**, scarce labour and overworked capital bid wages and costs **up**, shifting SRAS **left** and pulling output back down to potential at a higher price level. Either way, the economy drifts back to the LRAS.

The catch is *time*. As the economist John Maynard Keynes warned, the adjustment can be painfully slow — wages are especially sticky *downward*, so a recessionary gap may linger for years, with real people unemployed the whole while. That delay is the entire reason governments and central banks intervene rather than simply waiting, the debate we return to in the deepdive.

<Callout type="warn" title="Short run is not long run">
Where the economy *is* (short-run equilibrium, AD ∩ SRAS) and where it *belongs* (potential output, on LRAS) are two different points. They coincide only at full employment. The gap between them — recessionary or inflationary — is exactly the problem macro policy exists to fix.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Short-run macroeconomic equilibrium** is where **AD crosses SRAS**, setting the **price level** and **real GDP**.
- Comparing equilibrium GDP to **potential output** (the LRAS) gives the **output gap**.
- **Recessionary gap:** equilibrium GDP **below** potential — idle resources, high unemployment.
- **Inflationary gap:** equilibrium GDP **above** potential — overheating, rising price level.
- Gaps **self-correct** as sticky costs adjust: SRAS shifts right out of a recession, left out of a boom, returning output to potential — but **slowly**, which is why policy may intervene.

</Step>
