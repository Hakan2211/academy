<Step title="The case for growth — and the doubts" kind="explain">

We've spent this world celebrating growth, and for good reason. Sustained growth in real GDP per capita is the closest thing economics has to a miracle: it lifted billions out of poverty, doubled human life expectancy, slashed child mortality, and freed most people from backbreaking labour and chronic hunger. No anti-poverty program in history has done a fraction as much as plain economic growth.

And yet a nagging question persists, asked by economists, philosophers, and ordinary people alike: **does more growth actually make us better off?** Beyond a certain point, does piling on GDP per capita keep improving human lives — or are we measuring the wrong thing entirely? This capstone steps back from the cheerleading to examine growth's limits: what it buys us, what it misses, and whether it can continue without wrecking the planet that sustains it.

</Step>

<Step title="Does growth equal happiness?" kind="explain">

Start with the most direct test: do richer countries report being happier? The honest answer is *up to a point*.

For poor countries, the link is strong and unmistakable. Escaping poverty — gaining clean water, reliable food, basic health care, schooling, electricity — transforms lives and dramatically raises reported well-being. Here, growth and happiness march together, and there is no serious debate.

But once a country reaches a comfortable level — somewhere around the income of a typical developed nation — the link weakens. Reported life satisfaction keeps rising with income, but ever more slowly. This is the familiar shape of **diminishing marginal utility**, now applied to whole societies: each extra thousand dollars of income buys less additional happiness than the last. A related puzzle, the **Easterlin paradox**, notes that within a rich country people's average reported happiness has often barely budged across decades, even as real incomes doubled. Part of the explanation is that, past a point, well-being depends less on *absolute* income and more on *relative* income, health, relationships, security, and a sense of purpose — things GDP doesn't capture.

<Step title="GDP's blind spots" kind="explain">

GDP was never designed to measure well-being. Its inventor, Simon Kuznets, warned as much in the 1930s. GDP is a tally of *market production* — and that narrow focus leaves out much of what makes life good or bad.

- **Leisure and free time.** A country that works its people to exhaustion can post a higher GDP than a relaxed one, even if the relaxed country is plainly better off. GDP rewards more hours, not better lives.
- **The environment.** GDP counts the timber from a clear-cut forest but not the lost forest; it counts the output of a polluting factory but subtracts nothing for the dirty air. Environmental destruction can *raise* measured GDP even as it makes us poorer in every real sense.
- **Inequality.** GDP per capita is an *average*, and averages hide distribution. A country can grow richer on paper while most of its people see no gain, because the new income flows to a few. Two countries with the same GDP per capita can have wildly different lived experiences.
- **Unpaid and household work.** Care for children and the elderly, cooking, cleaning, volunteering — vast amounts of valuable work happen outside markets and so go entirely uncounted. (Oddly, GDP rises if you pay a stranger to do what you used to do for free.)
- **The "bads" counted as goods.** Spending on prisons, cleaning up oil spills, treating preventable illness, and rebuilding after disasters all add to GDP, though we'd be better off needing none of it.

Because of these blind spots, economists have built alternative measures: the **Human Development Index (HDI)**, which blends income with health and education; the **Genuine Progress Indicator**, which subtracts environmental and social costs; and various national well-being and happiness indices. None is perfect, and GDP remains useful and widely used — but the lesson is to read it as a *production gauge*, not a *welfare verdict*.

<Callout type="warn" title="A map is not the territory">
GDP is a powerful map of market activity, but it is not the territory of human well-being. Treating "grow GDP" as the only goal can quietly steer a society toward overwork, inequality, and ecological damage — all while the headline number looks great.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Can growth be sustainable?" kind="explain">

The sharpest challenge to endless growth is physical: we live on a finite planet. If growth means consuming ever more energy, minerals, and land, and dumping ever more carbon and waste, it eventually collides with hard limits — climate change above all. The pessimist's conclusion is that growth must stop, an idea associated with **degrowth**.

Mainstream economics offers a more hopeful answer: **decoupling** growth from resource use. Because growth is ultimately about *productivity* and *ideas* — doing more with the same or fewer inputs — output can in principle rise while resource use and emissions fall. We already see partial decoupling: many rich economies have grown while *cutting* energy use per dollar of GDP and, increasingly, total emissions. The vision of **green growth** is to push this further — renewable energy, electrification, efficiency, recycling, and innovation that lets living standards keep rising on a shrinking environmental footprint.

Whether green growth can move fast enough is a genuine open question, and a fiercely debated one. But notice the deep point: the very engine that makes growth so powerful — **better ideas, with no obvious limit** — is also the only thing that could let growth continue on a finite planet. Technology is both the source of the problem and our best hope for solving it.

</Step>

<Step title="The future of growth" kind="explain">

So where does this leave us? Not with a tidy verdict, but with a more mature view of a force we began by celebrating.

Growth is indispensable for the world's poor, where it remains the surest path out of misery — here the moral case for growth is overwhelming. For the already-rich, the picture is subtler: more GDP buys less happiness, the gains often flow unevenly, and the environmental bill is coming due. The frontier question for the 21st century isn't simply *how to grow faster*, but **how to grow well** — growth that raises real well-being, is shared broadly, and lives within the planet's means. Getting the measure right (looking past GDP), getting the institutions right (so growth is inclusive), and getting the technology right (so it's green) are the great economic projects of our era.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- Growth is a genuine miracle for the **poor**, transforming lives — but for the **rich**, extra GDP brings diminishing gains in reported well-being (the **Easterlin paradox**).
- **GDP measures market production, not well-being**: it omits leisure, the environment, inequality, and unpaid work, and counts costly "bads" (clean-ups, prisons) as gains.
- Alternative measures — **HDI**, the Genuine Progress Indicator, happiness indices — try to capture what GDP misses.
- Sustainability requires **decoupling** growth from resource use; **green growth** aims to keep raising living standards on a shrinking footprint.
- Technology — ideas without obvious limits — is both the source of the environmental problem and our best hope for solving it.
- The frontier question is not just how to grow faster, but **how to grow well**: inclusively, sustainably, and toward real well-being.

</Step>
