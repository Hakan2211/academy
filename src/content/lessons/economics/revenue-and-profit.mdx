<Step title="Revenue and marginal revenue" kind="explain">

We have the cost side; now the money coming in. **Total revenue** is simply price times quantity sold:

<Formula tex="\text{Total Revenue} = \text{Price} \times \text{Quantity}" block />

Just as costs have a margin, so does revenue. **Marginal revenue** is the extra revenue from selling *one more* unit. For a small firm in a competitive market — a single wheat farmer, say — the price is set by the market and the firm can sell as much as it likes at that price. So each extra unit brings in exactly the price, and **marginal revenue equals the price**. (A firm big enough to move the price, like a monopoly, faces a falling marginal revenue — a story for the next world.)

</Step>

<Step title="Produce where MR = MC" kind="interactive">

How much should the firm make? Compare the revenue and cost of *each extra unit*. As long as one more unit brings in more than it costs — marginal revenue above marginal cost — making it adds to profit. Once an extra unit costs more than it earns, it subtracts from profit. So profit is largest where the two margins meet:

<Formula tex="MR = MC" block />

Set the price and output below and watch the profit rectangle.

<ProfitMax />

The vertical "MR = MC" line marks the profit-maximising output. Produce less and you are leaving profitable units on the table; produce more and the extra units lose money. The shaded rectangle — the gap between total revenue and total cost — is widest exactly at that quantity.

</Step>

<Step title="Profit, loss, and the short run" kind="explain">

Producing at MR = MC maximises profit — but it does not guarantee a profit. If the market price is low, the best the firm can do may still be a loss. The relevant comparison is price against **average total cost**: if price is above ATC the firm profits, if below it loses money on each unit.

That raises an uncomfortable question. If a firm is losing money, why not just stop? Because in the short run the **fixed costs are owed either way**. Closing the doors does not make the rent disappear. So a loss-making firm has to ask a subtler question: does producing lose *less* money than shutting down?

</Step>

<Step title="The shutdown rule" kind="interactive">

The answer compares price to **average variable cost**. As long as price covers AVC, every unit sold contributes something toward those unavoidable fixed costs — so producing beats sitting idle, even at a loss. But once price falls below the *minimum* of AVC, each unit fails to cover even its own variable cost, and the firm loses less by halting output.

<ShutdownRule />

That minimum point of the AVC curve is the **shutdown price**. Above it, produce. Below it, shut down — you will still owe the fixed cost, but you stop bleeding extra money on variable costs.

<Callout type="warn">
Shutting down is a short-run decision: output stops but the firm still exists and pays its fixed costs. Exiting the industry for good — escaping fixed costs entirely — is a separate long-run decision based on average *total* cost.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Total revenue** = price × quantity; **marginal revenue** is the revenue from one more unit, and for a competitive firm it equals the price.
- Profit is maximised where **MR = MC** — produce every unit that earns more than it costs, and no more.
- MR = MC maximises profit but may still be a loss; compare price to **ATC** to see whether the firm profits.
- **Shutdown rule:** produce when price ≥ minimum **AVC**; shut down when price < AVC, because then each unit fails to cover even its variable cost.

</Step>
