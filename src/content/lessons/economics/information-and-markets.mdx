<Step title="When one side knows more" kind="explain">

Markets rest on a quiet assumption we rarely state: that buyers and sellers know what they're trading. You know the apple is fresh; the grocer knows it too. But for many goods one side knows far more than the other — the used-car seller knows the car's history, you don't; the job applicant knows their own work ethic, the employer doesn't; you know your own health, the insurer doesn't.

This is **asymmetric information**, and it's a third kind of market failure. When information is lopsided, good trades that *should* happen don't — and sometimes the whole market unravels.

</Step>

<Step title="Adverse selection: the market for lemons" kind="explain">

The classic case is the used-car market, analysed by George Akerlof in a paper so influential it won a Nobel Prize. Some used cars are good (call them **peaches**), some are bad (**lemons**). The seller knows which is which; the buyer cannot tell them apart on the lot.

So what will a buyer pay? Not the price of a peach — that would be a sucker's bet, since the car might be a lemon. The rational move is to offer only the **average** value of cars on the lot. But that average price is *below* what a peach is genuinely worth, so peach owners pull their cars off the market. Now the lot is more lemon-heavy, the average drops further, and even more good cars exit. Quality spirals downward. This self-reinforcing exit of the good side of the market is **adverse selection**.

</Step>

Each round, the price drags down the average, the underpaid peaches leave, and the price falls again — until the good cars are gone and mostly lemons remain. A market that could have served *everyone* — buyers who'd love a fairly priced peach, sellers who'd love to sell one — has mostly collapsed, purely because one side couldn't verify quality.

</Step>

<Step title="Moral hazard and the cures" kind="explain">

Asymmetric information has a second face. **Adverse selection** is about hidden information *before* a deal — who shows up. **Moral hazard** is about hidden *action after* a deal — how people behave once they're protected. With full fire insurance, you might grow careless about leaving the stove on; once a bank is bailed out, it might take wilder risks. The insurer or rescuer can't watch your every move, so the protected party behaves worse than they otherwise would.

The good news is that real markets fight back with clever institutions:

- **Signalling** — the informed side proves its quality at a cost only the genuine can bear: a used-car *warranty*, a diploma, a brand's reputation.
- **Screening** — the uninformed side designs choices that sort people: insurers offer a low-premium-high-deductible plan that only the low-risk will pick.
- **Reputation and reviews** — repeat business and ratings give sellers a reason to be honest.
- **Regulation** — mandatory disclosures, inspections, lemon laws, and required insurance (which kills adverse selection by forcing *everyone* into the pool).

<Callout type="tip" title="Why a warranty is information, not just a perk">
A seller only offers a strong warranty if the car is genuinely reliable — a lemon would trigger costly claims. So the warranty isn't merely a nice extra; it's a credible *signal* that lets the good cars stay in the market. The deductible on an insurance plan plays the same trick in reverse, screening the careful from the reckless.
</Callout>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Asymmetric information** — one side of a trade knows more than the other — is a market failure: good trades fail to happen.
- **Adverse selection** is hidden information *before* the deal. In the **market for lemons**, buyers pay only the average value, good cars exit, and quality spirals down.
- **Moral hazard** is hidden *action after* the deal — being protected (insurance, a bailout) makes people behave more carelessly.
- Markets fight back with **signalling** (warranties, diplomas), **screening** (deductibles), **reputation/reviews**, and **regulation** (disclosure, mandatory insurance).

</Step>
