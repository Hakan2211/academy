<Step title="The world stitched together" kind="explain">

For most of human history, you consumed what your village could make. Today the average shopping cart is a small atlas: bananas from Ecuador, a phone designed in California with chips from Taiwan and assembly in China, a shirt sewn in Bangladesh from cotton grown in India. This dense weaving-together of national economies — through trade, investment, migration, and information — is **globalization**.

It is comparative advantage operating at planetary scale. Each country, region, even town gravitates toward what it does at the lowest opportunity cost, and the pieces are knitted into **global supply chains** that span continents before a product reaches you. The result has been one of the great economic accelerations in history. It has also produced a backlash fierce enough to reshape elections. Both reactions, it turns out, are responding to real things.

</Step>

<Step title="Trade deals and the rules of the game" kind="explain">

Globalization didn't just happen — it was *built*, deliberately, through agreements that lowered barriers. After the devastation of two world wars and a depression that protectionism helped deepen, countries chose to bind themselves to rules of open trade.

- The **GATT**, and later the **World Trade Organization (WTO)**, set up a global rulebook and a referee for disputes, ratcheting tariffs down across decades.
- **Regional blocs** went further among neighbours — the **European Union** built a single market with free movement of goods, capital, and people; **NAFTA** (now USMCA) linked North America.
- **Containerization** — the humble steel shipping box, standardized in the 1950s — and the internet slashed the cost of moving goods and coordinating across oceans, making far-flung supply chains practical.

Each step lowered the cost of distance, letting firms slice production into stages and place each stage wherever it's cheapest. That's how a single product comes to have a dozen passports.

</Step>

The stages that capture the most of the final price — design, software, advanced chips — sit in high-skill, high-wage countries. **Assembly**, the stage everyone pictures when they say "made in China," captures one of the *smallest* slices. The phrase "made in" hides where the real value is created — and that mismatch is at the heart of the globalization debate.

</Step>

<Step title="The case for globalization" kind="explain">

The benefits are large and, for the most part, well documented.

- **Lower prices and more variety** for consumers everywhere. The same comparative-advantage logic that lifts world output puts cheaper food, electronics, and clothing within reach of ordinary families.
- **The fastest poverty reduction in history.** As countries plugged into world trade — China after 1978, then India, Vietnam, and others — hundreds of millions of people climbed out of extreme poverty. Export-led growth is, empirically, the most reliable escalator a poor country has ever found.
- **Spread of technology and ideas.** Trade carries not just goods but know-how, management practices, and standards across borders.
- **Peace through interdependence.** Countries woven together by commerce have, historically, found it costlier to go to war with one another — though this is a tendency, not a law.

</Step>

<Step title="Its discontents" kind="explain">

But globalization creates losers as surely as winners, and ignoring them is both unjust and politically explosive.

- **Displaced workers.** When a factory's work moves abroad, the displaced rarely glide into new jobs. Whole towns built around a single industry can hollow out, and the gains-from-trade theory's promise that "winners could compensate losers" is hollow if the compensation never actually happens. The economic pain is real even when the *country* comes out ahead.
- **A race to the bottom?** Critics fear that footloose capital pressures countries to slash wages, weaken unions, and gut environmental rules to stay competitive. The evidence here is mixed — many developing countries' standards have *risen* with growth — but the fear isn't baseless.
- **Sweatshops and exploitation.** Early-stage manufacturing jobs are often grim by rich-country standards. Defenders note they're usually *better* than the rural poverty they replace, and a rung on the development ladder; that's true and cold comfort at once.
- **Fragility.** Hyper-efficient global chains optimized for cost turn out to be brittle. A pandemic, a blocked canal, or a single chip-factory disruption can ripple through the whole world — the trade-off for all that efficiency is lost resilience.
- **Cultural and sovereignty worries.** Some resent global brands flattening local culture, or international bodies and treaties constraining national choices.

</Step>

<Step title="So what should we make of it?" kind="explain">

The mainstream economic verdict is nuanced, and worth stating carefully: globalization **expands the total economic pie**, but it **redistributes** within and between countries — and the distribution is where the politics lives.

The standard implication isn't "stop trading" — that would throw away enormous, widely shared gains and hit the poorest hardest. It's that the **winners should genuinely compensate the losers**: through retraining, wage insurance, regional investment, and a real safety net for displaced workers. Economists have long argued this; societies have mostly failed to do it, which is a large part of why the backlash arrived.

The recent turn toward **reshoring** and "friend-shoring" — pulling critical production closer to home or toward allies — is partly a response to the fragility lesson and partly to the political one. It buys resilience and votes; it also raises costs and surrenders some gains from trade. As with almost everything in economics, there is no free option — only a choice among trade-offs, to be weighed with eyes open.

<Callout type="tip" title="Hold both truths at once">
The mark of economic literacy here is refusing the two easy stories. Globalization is *not* a pure miracle that lifts all boats, and it is *not* a pure scam that only helps elites. It is a powerful engine that grows the whole while reshuffling the parts — and whether it feels like a blessing or a betrayal depends heavily on which part you happen to be standing in.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Globalization** is comparative advantage at planetary scale, knitting economies together via **trade, investment, and global supply chains**.
- It was *built* through **trade deals** (GATT/WTO, the EU, NAFTA) and enabling tech (**containerization**, the internet) that slashed the cost of distance.
- A product like a phone is designed, sourced, assembled, and sold across many countries — and the **"made in" label hides where the value is really created**.
- **Benefits:** lower prices and variety, the fastest poverty reduction in history, technology spread, interdependence.
- **Discontents:** displaced workers and hollowed-out towns, race-to-the-bottom fears, sweatshop conditions, fragile chains, sovereignty worries.
- The mainstream verdict: globalization **grows the pie but redistributes it** — so the case for trade comes paired with a case for **compensating the losers**, the step that's mostly been neglected.

</Step>
