<Step title="If trade is so good, why block it?" kind="explain">

The last two lessons made trade sound like a free lunch — both sides gain, world output rises. So why does almost every country on Earth restrict imports with **tariffs**, **quotas**, and other barriers?

The honest answer: trade makes a country *richer in total*, but it doesn't make *everyone* richer. The gains are spread thinly across millions of consumers, while the losses pile up on a few visible, organised industries that suddenly face foreign competition. Those industries lobby hard for protection, and politicians often oblige. Understanding who really wins and loses from a tariff is one of the most useful things economics can teach you.

</Step>

<Step title="The tools of protection" kind="explain">

There are several ways to wall off a domestic market:

- A **tariff** is a tax on imported goods. It raises the price foreigners' products sell for inside the country, nudging buyers toward domestic alternatives — and handing the government revenue.
- A **quota** is a hard cap on the *quantity* that may be imported. It pushes the domestic price up just like a tariff, but the extra money goes to whoever holds the import licences rather than to the treasury.
- Other barriers hide in plain sight: subsidies to domestic firms, mountains of paperwork, fussy safety or labelling rules used as disguised protection.

All of them do the same fundamental thing: they raise the price inside the country above the world price, which protects domestic producers at the expense of domestic consumers.

</Step>

Walk through what happens. The internal price climbs toward the no-trade level. Domestic production rises (good for those firms), consumption falls (bad for buyers), and **imports shrink**. The green rectangle is **government tariff revenue**. But notice the two grey triangles — the **deadweight loss**. These are mutually beneficial trades the tariff destroys outright: consumers who'd have happily bought at the world price now don't, and resources get pulled into high-cost domestic production. Nobody captures that lost surplus. It simply evaporates.

</Step>

<Step title="Winners, losers, and the arguments" kind="explain">

Add it up. A tariff transfers money *from* consumers *to* domestic producers and the government — but the consumer loss is bigger than those two gains combined. The difference is the deadweight loss, so the country as a whole is poorer. Trade barriers don't grow the pie; they re-slice it while shrinking it.

So why do them anyway? The serious arguments:

- **Infant industries** — a new sector might need temporary shelter to grow up and compete. (Risk: "temporary" protection rarely ends.)
- **National security** — you may not want to depend on a rival for food, weapons, or critical chips.
- **Cushioning the losers** — sudden import surges can devastate whole towns, and the displaced rarely get retrained fast.

And the weak ones, which sound appealing but don't survive scrutiny: "protecting jobs" (it saves some jobs at enormous cost per job, while destroying others in exporting and downstream industries) and "cheap foreign labour is unfair" (low wages reflect low productivity — that's *why* trade with them is mutually beneficial).

<Callout type="info" title="Retaliation makes it worse">
Tariffs invite **retaliation**. When one country protects, its trading partners often hit back with tariffs of their own — and a trade war can leave every country poorer than before, the modern echo of the 1930s Smoot-Hawley spiral that deepened the Great Depression.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Tariffs** (import taxes), **quotas** (quantity caps), and hidden barriers all **raise the domestic price** above the world price.
- A tariff **transfers** surplus from consumers to domestic producers and the government — but consumers lose **more** than those parties gain.
- The shortfall is **deadweight loss**: mutually beneficial trades the barrier destroys, captured by no one.
- Protection helps a visible few at the spread-out expense of many; the nation as a whole is **poorer**.
- Genuine cases (infant industries, national security, cushioning losers) exist, but "saving jobs" and "unfair cheap labour" mostly don't survive scrutiny — and **retaliation** can trigger costly trade wars.

</Step>
