<Lesson>

<Step title="When the number lies" kind="explain">

Suppose a country's GDP doubles in ten years. Are people twice as well off? Not necessarily — and spotting why is one of the most important skills in macroeconomics.

GDP is prices times quantities added up across the whole economy. So GDP can rise for two completely different reasons:

1. The country genuinely produces **more stuff** — more cars, more haircuts, more software.
2. **Prices simply went up** while output barely changed.

The first is real progress. The second is just inflation wearing a disguise. A measure that can't tell them apart is dangerous, so economists split GDP in two.

</Step>

<Step title="Nominal vs real" kind="explain">

- **Nominal GDP** values output at the prices of the **current** year. Because it mixes changing quantities with changing prices, it overstates growth whenever prices rise.
- **Real GDP** values output at the prices of a fixed **base year**. By holding prices constant, it strips inflation out, so any change reflects a genuine change in the *quantity* of goods and services.

Here is the intuition in one example. If a baker makes the exact same 1,000 loaves this year as last year, but the price rises from 2 dollars to 3 dollars, nominal "bread GDP" jumps from 2,000 to 3,000 — a 50% leap. Yet not a single extra loaf was baked. Real GDP, valuing both years at the base-year price of 2 dollars, stays flat at 2,000. It correctly reports **zero** real growth.

When news reports say the economy "grew 2.5% last year," they almost always mean **real** GDP. That is the number that tracks living standards.

</Step>

<Step title="The GDP deflator" kind="explain">

How do we convert between the two? With a price index called the **GDP deflator**, set to 100 in the base year:

<Formula tex="\text{GDP deflator} = \frac{\text{Nominal GDP}}{\text{Real GDP}} \times 100" block />

The deflator measures the average price level of everything in GDP relative to the base year. A deflator of 130 means prices are 30% higher than in the base year. Rearranging the formula lets you recover real GDP from the nominal figure:

<Formula tex="\text{Real GDP} = \frac{\text{Nominal GDP}}{\text{GDP deflator}} \times 100" block />

So the deflator is the bridge. Inflate output at this year's prices to get nominal GDP; divide back out by the deflator to get real GDP. The two only agree in the base year, where the deflator is exactly 100.

</Step>

Push real growth to zero and inflation high: the nominal line still climbs steeply, but the real line lies flat — *all* of that apparent growth is rising prices. <Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Nominal GDP** uses current-year prices, so it mixes price changes with quantity changes and overstates growth during inflation.
- **Real GDP** uses fixed base-year prices, so its changes reflect genuine changes in output.
- The **GDP deflator** = (nominal ÷ real) × 100 measures the price level relative to the base year (where it equals 100).
- **Real GDP** = (nominal ÷ deflator) × 100 strips inflation back out.
- Roughly, **nominal growth ≈ real growth + inflation**, so real growth ≈ nominal growth − inflation.
- Living standards track **real** GDP, not nominal — a doubling number can be all prices and no extra stuff.

</Step>
