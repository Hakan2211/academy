<Step title="The price of money itself" kind="explain">

When you cross a border, your money suddenly buys different amounts of things. That's because every currency has a *price* measured in other currencies — its **exchange rate**. One euro might cost you 1.10 dollars today and 1.05 tomorrow. Those wobbles ripple through the entire global economy: they set how expensive your country's exports look abroad, how cheap imported goods are at home, and how much a foreign holiday costs.

The good news is that you already know how exchange rates are set. A currency is just another good traded in a market, so its price is fixed by the same force as everything else: **supply and demand**.

</Step>

<Step title="What moves a currency" kind="explain">

Think of the market for, say, Japanese yen. Who *demands* yen?

- Anyone abroad who wants to buy **Japanese exports** must first get yen to pay for them.
- **Tourists** heading to Japan need yen to spend.
- **Investors** who want to park money in Japanese banks or bonds buy yen — especially when Japan's **interest rates** are attractive.

And who *supplies* yen to the market? Japanese residents who want foreign goods, foreign travel, or foreign investments — they sell yen to get the other currency.

When demand for a currency rises or its supply falls, its price climbs: economists call that an **appreciation** (the currency gets "stronger"). When demand falls or supply rises, the price drops — a **depreciation** ("weaker"). Same supply-and-demand logic as a market for apples, just with money as the good.

</Step>

Trigger an **export boom** or **higher interest rates** and demand jumps right — the currency *appreciates*. Trigger a **tourism slump** and it *depreciates*. Now here's the counter-intuitive payoff to notice: when the currency gets **stronger**, the "our exports cost" index goes *up* (foreigners must pay more for our goods) while "imports cost us" goes *down*. A strong currency is a double-edged sword — wonderful for importers and tourists, brutal for exporters.

</Step>

<Step title="Purchasing-power parity" kind="explain">

If exchange rates bounce around daily, is there any *anchor* — a level they should drift toward over the long run? One famous idea says yes: **purchasing-power parity (PPP)**.

PPP claims that, eventually, a currency should trade at the rate that makes a basket of goods cost the same in both countries. If a basket costs 100 dollars in the US and 800 pesos in Mexico, PPP says the rate should head toward 8 pesos per dollar. The logic is **arbitrage**: if the same good were much cheaper in one country, traders would buy there and sell elsewhere until prices (and the exchange rate) lined up.

The playful test of this is *The Economist's* **Big Mac Index** — comparing the price of a Big Mac around the world to spot currencies that look over- or under-valued. PPP is a rough long-run guide, not a daily predictor: in the short run, interest rates, speculation, and confidence swamp it. But over years, currencies do tend to drift toward their purchasing-power level.

<Callout type="info" title="Strong isn't the same as good">
People hear "strong currency" and assume it's always a win. Not so. A strong currency makes imports and holidays cheap, but it can hollow out a country's exporters by pricing them out of foreign markets. A *weaker* currency can be exactly what a struggling, export-driven economy needs. There's no universally "right" exchange rate — only trade-offs.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- An **exchange rate** is the price of one currency in terms of another, set by **supply and demand** in the foreign-exchange market.
- Demand for a currency comes from buyers of its **exports**, **tourists**, and **investors** chasing higher **interest rates**.
- Rising demand (or falling supply) ⇒ **appreciation** (stronger); the reverse ⇒ **depreciation** (weaker).
- A **stronger** currency makes exports pricier abroad but imports cheaper at home; a **weaker** one does the opposite.
- **Purchasing-power parity (PPP)** is a long-run anchor — currencies drift toward equalising the price of a common basket — but short-run rates are driven by rates, speculation, and confidence.

</Step>
