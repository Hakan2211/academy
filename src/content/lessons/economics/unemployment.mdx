<Step title="Who counts as unemployed?" kind="explain">

"Unemployed" has a surprisingly strict economic definition. To be counted, a person must be **without a job, available to work, and actively looking** for one. Miss any of those, and you're not unemployed in the statistical sense.

That has a sharp consequence. The economy's adults split into three groups:

- **Employed** — anyone with a paid job, even part-time.
- **Unemployed** — jobless but actively searching.
- **Not in the labour force** — everyone else: retirees, full-time students, stay-at-home parents, and the **discouraged workers** who have given up looking.

The **labour force** is just the first two combined — the people either working or trying to. Everyone in the third group sits *outside* it entirely.

</Step>

<Step title="The unemployment rate" kind="explain">

The headline number is the **unemployment rate**: the share of the *labour force* that is unemployed.

<Formula tex="\text{Unemployment rate} = \frac{\text{unemployed}}{\text{labour force}} \times 100" block />

The denominator is the key subtlety. It's the labour force, **not** the whole population, so people who aren't looking never enter the calculation. This produces a famously counter-intuitive effect: if discouraged workers stop searching, they leave the labour force, and the unemployment rate can *fall* even though nobody got a job.

That's why economists pair the unemployment rate with the **labour-force participation rate** — the share of the working-age population that is *in* the labour force. A falling unemployment rate alongside falling participation can be a warning sign, not good news.

</Step>

<Step title="Three kinds of unemployment" kind="explain">

Not all unemployment has the same cause or cure. Economists name three types:

- **Frictional** unemployment is the short-term joblessness of people *between* jobs or searching for their first one. It reflects the time it takes to match workers to openings. A little of it is normal and even healthy — it means people are finding better fits.
- **Structural** unemployment comes from a *mismatch* between the skills (or location) workers have and the jobs available. When automation replaces a task, or an industry moves away, displaced workers can't simply walk into the new jobs — they need retraining or relocation. It tends to be longer-lasting.
- **Cyclical** unemployment rises and falls with the **business cycle**. In a recession, weak demand across the whole economy means firms everywhere cut jobs; in a boom, it shrinks toward zero. This is the type policymakers most try to fight.

Frictional and structural unemployment never fully disappear — together they make up the economy's **natural rate of unemployment**, the level that persists even in good times. "Full employment" doesn't mean zero unemployment; it means cyclical unemployment is roughly zero and only the natural rate remains.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- To be **unemployed** you must be jobless, available, and **actively looking**; others (retirees, students, discouraged workers) are **not in the labour force**.
- The **labour force** = employed + unemployed; the **unemployment rate** = unemployed ÷ labour force × 100.
- Because the denominator excludes non-searchers, the rate can **fall when discouraged workers quit looking** — so watch the **participation rate** too.
- **Frictional** = between jobs (normal); **structural** = skills/location mismatch (longer-lasting); **cyclical** = driven by recessions.
- Frictional + structural make up the **natural rate**; "full employment" means roughly zero **cyclical** unemployment, not zero unemployment.

</Step>
