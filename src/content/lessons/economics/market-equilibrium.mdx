<Step title="Where the two curves meet" kind="explain">

We've drawn demand sloping down and supply sloping up. Put them on the same axes and they cross at exactly one point. That crossing is the **market equilibrium** — the one price at which the **quantity demanded equals the quantity supplied**.

At that price, called the **equilibrium price** (or market-clearing price), the market is in balance: every buyer willing to pay it finds a seller, and every seller willing to accept it finds a buyer. Nothing is left over and nothing runs short. The quantity traded there is the **equilibrium quantity**.

What's remarkable is that no one *chooses* this price. No committee sets it. It emerges from countless buyers and sellers each acting in their own interest — Adam Smith's famous "invisible hand." But how does the market actually *find* it?

</Step>

<Step title="What happens off equilibrium" kind="explain">

The equilibrium is special because every *other* price creates pressure that pushes back toward it.

**Price too high → a surplus.** When the price sits above equilibrium, sellers eagerly offer lots (high quantity supplied) but buyers shy away (low quantity demanded). The excess — goods piling up unsold — is a **surplus**. Sellers slash prices to clear their shelves, and the price slides *down* toward equilibrium.

**Price too low → a shortage.** When the price sits below equilibrium, buyers clamour for lots (high quantity demanded) but sellers offer little (low quantity supplied). The gap — buyers who can't find the good — is a **shortage**. Frustrated buyers bid the price *up*, and it climbs toward equilibrium.

Either way, the disequilibrium contains the seed of its own cure. Surpluses push prices down; shortages push prices up; both stop only when quantity supplied equals quantity demanded. The market is **self-correcting**.

</Step>

<Step title="Prices are signals" kind="explain">

The equilibrium price does more than balance one market — it carries information. A high price *shouts* to producers "make more of this!" and gently warns buyers "use less." A low price does the reverse. Prices coordinate millions of strangers who never meet, channelling resources toward what people value most.

<Callout type="info" title="The market as a giant message board">
When a frost destroys a coffee harvest, no one has to announce a global coffee shortage. The price simply rises — and that single number tells growers everywhere to plant more, tells roasters to economise, and tells drinkers to perhaps switch to tea. The price *is* the message, and it travels instantly.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Equilibrium** is where the demand and supply curves cross: **quantity demanded = quantity supplied**.
- The **equilibrium price** clears the market; the matching quantity is the **equilibrium quantity**.
- A price **above** equilibrium creates a **surplus** (excess supply), pushing the price **down**.
- A price **below** equilibrium creates a **shortage** (excess demand), pushing the price **up**.
- The market is **self-correcting**: surpluses and shortages generate the very pressure that erases them.
- Prices act as **signals**, coordinating buyers and sellers without any central planner.

</Step>
