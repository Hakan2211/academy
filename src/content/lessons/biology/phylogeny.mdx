<Step title="Drawing the family tree of life" kind="explain">

If all species descend from common ancestors through repeated splitting, then the history of life has the shape of a **branching tree**. Reconstructing that tree — working out who is related to whom, and how closely — is called building a **phylogeny**.

It's like a family tree, but for whole species, stretching back billions of years to a single root.

</Step>

<Step title="Reading relatedness" kind="interactive">

Click through the branches. Each fork is a point where one ancestral lineage split into two.

<TreeOfLife />

The rule for reading it: the more **recently** two species share a branch point, the more **closely related** they are. Humans and chimps branch off very recently; humans and mushrooms parted ways billions of years ago.

</Step>

<Step title="How the tree is built" kind="explain">

How do biologists know where the branches go? By looking for **shared features** that signal common ancestry:

- **Anatomy** — shared body plans (like the homologous limb bones).
- **DNA** — the gold standard. The more DNA two species share, the more recently they diverged. Genetic differences even act as a **molecular clock**, estimating *when* lineages split.

Modern trees combine all of this. And they revealed surprises — for instance, that whales are close cousins of hippos, and that birds are living dinosaurs.

<Callout type="info" title="Classification follows the tree">
The naming system you met earlier (domains, kingdoms, genus, species) is meant to mirror this tree — grouping species by shared ancestry, not just by how they look.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- The history of life is a **branching tree**; reconstructing it is **phylogeny**.
- **Recent** branch points mean **close** relationships.
- Trees are built from shared **anatomy** and especially shared **DNA** (a molecular clock dates the splits).
- Classification is meant to **mirror** this tree of common ancestry.

</Step>
