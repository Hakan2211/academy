<Step title="What a nerve impulse really is" kind="explain">

We've said neurons carry "electrical impulses". But a nerve impulse is **not** electricity flowing through a wire. It's something stranger and more beautiful: a wave of **charged particles** moving across the cell membrane, sweeping down the axon. This wave is called an **action potential**, and it's the basis of every thought, sensation, and movement.

To understand it, we return to the membrane and its ion pumps from an earlier world.

</Step>

<Step title="A charged-up membrane at rest" kind="interactive">

Even when "doing nothing", a neuron is primed. The sodium–potassium pump keeps more sodium (Na⁺) outside and more potassium (K⁺) inside, leaving the inside of the membrane slightly **negative** — about **−70 mV**. This is the **resting potential**: a tiny battery, ready to fire.

<Neuron />

That stored voltage difference is the energy the impulse will release — like a row of dominoes standing ready to fall.

</Step>

<Step title="The spike, phase by phase" kind="interactive">

When a stimulus is strong enough, channels in the membrane fly open and the voltage **spikes**. Trace it.

<ActionPotential />

- **Depolarisation:** Na⁺ channels open and sodium floods *in* — the voltage shoots up past 0 to about +40 mV.
- **Repolarisation:** Na⁺ channels close, K⁺ channels open, and potassium flows *out* — the voltage drops back down.
- **Recovery:** a brief overshoot and reset by the pump, ready to fire again.

The whole spike lasts only a couple of **thousandths of a second**.

</Step>

<Step title="All or nothing" kind="explain">

Here's a key feature: an action potential is **all-or-nothing**. If a stimulus reaches the **threshold** (about −55 mV), a full spike fires. If it doesn't, nothing happens at all. There's no such thing as a half-spike.

So how does your nervous system signal a *strong* stimulus versus a weak one? Not by a bigger spike — every spike is identical — but by **firing more often**. A bright light or a heavy weight produces a *higher frequency* of impulses, not larger ones. The brain reads intensity from the **rate** of firing.

</Step>

<Step title="Racing down the axon" kind="explain">

An action potential at one spot triggers the same thing in the patch of membrane next door — so the spike **propagates** down the axon, regenerating itself at full strength the whole way. That's why a signal can travel a metre to your toe without fading.

The **myelin sheath** speeds this up dramatically: the impulse effectively **jumps** between gaps in the myelin, rather than crawling along every point — making myelinated nerves up to 100 times faster.

<Callout type="info" title="Why it never weakens">
Unlike a current in a wire, which fades with distance, an action potential rebuilds itself at every step. Each spike is a fresh, full-sized event — so the message arrives as strong at the end as at the start.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="The bigger picture" kind="explain">

Step back and marvel at the system. Every move you make, every word you read, every feeling you have, is built from these identical tiny spikes — billions of neurons each firing all-or-nothing pulses, their patterns and timing encoding everything.

It ties the whole world together: the **membrane** and **ion pumps** from cell biology, **diffusion** down gradients, **protein** channels, and **chemical** synapses where impulses pass on (and where drugs act). The firing neuron is biology's masterpiece — the physical basis of the mind.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- A nerve impulse is an **action potential** — a wave of ions crossing the membrane, not current in a wire.
- At rest the membrane is charged to **−70 mV** (the Na⁺/K⁺ pump). A spike: **Na⁺ in (depolarise) → K⁺ out (repolarise) → reset**.
- It's **all-or-nothing** above a **threshold**; stronger stimuli mean **more frequent** firing, not bigger spikes.
- The spike **regenerates** down the axon (so it never fades) and **myelin** makes it jump faster.
- It unifies membranes, ion gradients, channels, and synapses — the physical basis of mind.

</Step>
