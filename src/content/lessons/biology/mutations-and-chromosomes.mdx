<Lesson>

<Step title="Changes to the code" kind="explain">

DNA is copied with astonishing accuracy — but not perfectly. Every so often a change slips in. A **mutation** is any change to the DNA sequence, and it can happen at two scales: a tiny change to a single gene, or a large change to a whole chromosome.

Mutations sound alarming, but most are harmless. And crucially, they're also the ultimate source of all the **variation** that evolution works on.

</Step>

<Step title="Gene mutations" kind="explain">

The smallest mutations change just one or a few bases in a gene:

- **Substitution** — one base swapped for another.
- **Insertion or deletion** — a base added or removed, which can shift the whole reading frame.

The effect ranges widely. Thanks to the genetic code's redundancy, many substitutions change **nothing**. Some change one amino acid (as in sickle-cell anaemia). A few, in the wrong place, disable a protein entirely.

</Step>

<Step title="Chromosome mutations" kind="interactive">

Larger mutations affect whole **chromosomes** — usually when they fail to separate properly during meiosis (**non-disjunction**), leaving a gamete with too many or too few. Toggle to see one.

<Karyotype />

An extra copy of chromosome 21 gives **47** chromosomes instead of 46 — this is **Down syndrome (trisomy 21)**. Because a whole chromosome carries hundreds of genes, these mutations have wide-ranging effects.

</Step>

<Step title="Causes — and the upside" kind="explain">

Mutations happen spontaneously during DNA copying, but some factors raise the rate — **mutagens** like UV light, tobacco smoke, and certain chemicals (which is why they can cause cancer).

And mutations aren't only bad:

- **Most** are neutral — no effect at all.
- **Some** are harmful.
- A **rare few** are beneficial, giving an advantage.

Those rare beneficial mutations, accumulated over time, are the raw material of **evolution** — the subject of the very next world.

<Callout type="tip" title="Variation's source">
Without mutation there would be no new alleles, no variety for natural selection to act on, and no evolution. Life's diversity ultimately traces back to copying errors.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- A **mutation** is any change to DNA — from a single base (**gene mutation**) to a whole **chromosome**.
- Gene mutations: substitution, insertion, deletion — effects range from none to serious.
- Chromosome mutations (e.g. **trisomy 21**) usually come from **non-disjunction** in meiosis.
- **Mutagens** (UV, smoke) raise the rate; most mutations are neutral, but rare beneficial ones drive **evolution**.

</Step>
