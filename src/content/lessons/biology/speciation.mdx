<Lesson>

<Step title="Where do new species come from?" kind="explain">

Natural selection changes a population over time. But how does one species become **two**? How did a single ancestor give rise to the millions of species alive today?

First, what *is* a species? The usual definition: a group whose members can **breed together** and produce fertile offspring. So making a new species means producing a group that can **no longer interbreed** with the original. This splitting is called **speciation**.

</Step>

<Step title="Split, diverge, separate" kind="interactive">

The classic route is geographic. Step through it.

<Speciation />

1. One population breeds freely.
2. A **barrier** — a river, mountain, or ocean — divides it.
3. The two halves face different conditions and **diverge** through natural selection and mutation.
4. Eventually they're so different they **can't interbreed** even if reunited — two species.

</Step>

<Step title="Why the barrier matters" kind="explain">

The barrier's job is to stop the two groups **mixing their genes**. While they're separate:

- Different **selection pressures** push them in different directions (different food, predators, climate).
- **Mutations** arise independently in each group.
- Over many generations the differences pile up — in body, behaviour, even mating signals.

Once they can no longer produce fertile offspring together, the split is permanent. **Darwin's finches** on the Galápagos are a textbook case: one ancestral finch, isolated on different islands, became many species with beaks suited to different foods.

<Callout type="tip" title="It's slow, but it's been seen">
Full speciation usually takes thousands of generations. But we've watched the early stages happen — in finches, in fish, even in fruit flies in the lab.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- A **species** is a group that can interbreed to produce fertile offspring.
- **Speciation** = one species splitting into two that can no longer interbreed.
- The classic route: a **barrier** isolates two groups, which **diverge** under different pressures until reproductively separate.
- Real examples: **Darwin's finches**, canyon squirrels.

</Step>
