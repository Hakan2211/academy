<Step title="One idea that unifies all of biology" kind="explain">

Physics has a handful of grand unifying laws. Biology has one that towers above the rest: **all life on Earth is related**. Every organism alive today — every bacterium, mushroom, oak, and human — descends from a single ancestral population that lived nearly **four billion** years ago.

That's not a poetic flourish. It's the single most powerful organising idea in the life sciences, and the evidence for it is overwhelming. This deep-dive unpacks how we know, and what the great tree really looks like.

</Step>

<Step title="The smoking gun: a shared code" kind="explain">

Here's the clincher. Every living thing — without exception — stores its instructions in **DNA**, written in the same four chemical "letters". And nearly every organism reads that code using the **same dictionary**: the triplet `GGA` means the amino acid glycine in a bacterium, a banana, and a blue whale alike.

There is no chemical reason it *had* to be this way — countless other codes would work. The only sensible explanation for one universal code is that everything inherited it from a **common ancestor**. Life shares a code because life shares a family.

</Step>

<Step title="Reading the tree" kind="interactive">

A family tree of life is called a **phylogeny**. Click through the branches — each split marks a point where one ancestral lineage divided into two.

The closer two tips branch off from a shared node, the more recently they shared an ancestor — and the more DNA they have in common. The tree isn't a guess; it's reconstructed by comparing the genes of living things letter by letter.

</Step>

<Step title="How we reconstruct it" kind="explain">

Before DNA, biologists built trees from **anatomy** — a bat's wing, a whale's flipper, and your arm share the same bones in the same arrangement, betraying a common ancestor. Powerful, but it can mislead: a shark and a dolphin look alike yet aren't close kin.

Today we read the **molecules** directly. The more letters two species share in a slowly-changing gene, the more recently they diverged. Differences accumulate like ticks of a clock, so genetic distance even estimates *when* lineages split. Anatomy, fossils, and DNA now tell one consistent story.

</Step>

<Step title="LUCA, at the root" kind="explain">

Trace every branch back far enough and they meet at a single point: **LUCA**, the **Last Universal Common Ancestor**.

LUCA wasn't the first living thing, and it wasn't "simple" — it was already a sophisticated cell with DNA, the universal genetic code, ribosomes to build proteins, and ATP for energy. We can reconstruct features of an organism nobody ever saw, simply because every one of its descendants still carries them.

<Callout type="info" title="A living fingerprint">
The fact that *your* cells use the same code, the same ribosomes, and the same energy molecule as a pond bacterium is LUCA's fingerprint, pressed into all four billion years of its descendants.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Three domains, not five kingdoms" kind="explain">

For a long time life was split into a handful of "kingdoms" sorted by looks. Then, in 1977, **Carl Woese** did something radical: instead of comparing how organisms *looked*, he compared a gene they *all* share (the RNA of the ribosome).

The results redrew the tree. The deepest division in life isn't plants-versus-animals — it's between three **domains**: **Bacteria**, **Archaea**, and **Eukarya**. The whole visible living world is a few twigs on the Eukarya branch. Most of life's true diversity is microbial and invisible.

</Step>

<Step title="A bush, not a ladder" kind="explain">

The most common misconception about the tree is to read it as a **ladder** climbing toward us — as if evolution were aiming at humans all along.

It isn't. The tree is a **bush**. Every tip alive today — the bacterium and the human — has been evolving for exactly the same four billion years. No living species is "more evolved" than another; they're just adapted to different ways of life. A human is not the goal of the tree. We're one twig among millions.

<Callout type="tip" title="Spot the error">
"Humans evolved from chimps" is wrong in the same way. Chimps are our *cousins*, not our ancestors — we share a common ancestor that was neither human nor chimp.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- All life is related — descended from a single ancestral population, **LUCA**.
- The decisive evidence is the **universal genetic code**, backed by shared anatomy, fossils, and DNA comparisons.
- A **phylogeny** maps relatedness; recent branch-points mean recent common ancestors.
- The deepest split is the **three domains** (Bacteria, Archaea, Eukarya), most of it microbial.
- Evolution is a **bush, not a ladder** — no species is the "goal", and all living things are equally ancient.

</Step>
