<Step title="The control centre" kind="explain">

If a cell is a factory, the **nucleus** is head office. It holds the complete set of instructions for building and running the cell — the **DNA** — and it directs everything the cell does.

Nearly every cell in your body carries the same full library of DNA in its nucleus. What makes a muscle cell different from a brain cell is *which* instructions it reads.

</Step>

<Step title="Find the nucleus" kind="interactive">

Inside it you'd find the DNA, wound up tight, plus a dense spot called the **nucleolus**.

</Step>

<Step title="How instructions get out" kind="explain">

There's a problem: the DNA is far too precious to leave the nucleus, yet the proteins it codes for are built *outside*, on ribosomes in the cytoplasm. The solution is elegant:

- The DNA stays safely inside, behind the **nuclear membrane**.
- A working **copy** of a single gene is made as a messenger molecule (**mRNA**).
- That copy slips out through tiny **nuclear pores** to the ribosomes, which read it and build the protein.
- The **nucleolus** is the factory that builds those ribosomes in the first place.

<Callout type="info" title="One library, many readers">
The master DNA never leaves. Only disposable copies go out — so the original blueprint can never be lost or damaged on the factory floor.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- The **nucleus** stores the cell's **DNA** and controls its activities.
- The DNA stays inside; **mRNA copies** of genes leave through **nuclear pores** to the ribosomes.
- The **nucleolus** inside the nucleus builds ribosomes.
- Cells differ not by their DNA but by which parts of it they use.

</Step>
