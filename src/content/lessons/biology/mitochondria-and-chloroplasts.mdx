<Lesson>

<Step title="The power stations of life" kind="explain">

Two organelles handle the living world's energy, and between them they keep almost everything alive:

- **Chloroplasts** (in plants) capture sunlight and store its energy in **sugar**.
- **Mitochondria** (in plants *and* animals) release that stored energy from sugar to power the cell.

One banks the energy of the Sun; the other spends it.

</Step>

<Step title="Spot them in a plant cell" kind="interactive">

A plant cell has both. Click the green **chloroplasts** and the red **mitochondria**.

<CellViewer initial="plant" highlight="chloroplast" />

Animal cells have mitochondria too — but no chloroplasts, which is why animals must *eat* to get their energy rather than making it from light.

</Step>

<Step title="Two complementary machines" kind="explain">

These organelles are beautifully matched opposites:

- The **chloroplast** runs **photosynthesis**: light + water + carbon dioxide → glucose + oxygen.
- The **mitochondrion** runs **respiration**: glucose + oxygen → carbon dioxide + water + usable energy.

Each is the other's reaction run backwards. And both share a clever trick: their inner membranes are **folded** into huge surface areas, packing in more of the machinery that does the work.

<Callout type="tip" title="A telling clue">
Both organelles have their own little loop of DNA and build their own proteins — almost as if they were once free-living cells. That's a mystery we'll solve in this world's deep-dive.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Chloroplasts** capture sunlight to make sugar (**photosynthesis**) — plants only.
- **Mitochondria** release energy from sugar (**respiration**) — plants and animals.
- They run opposite reactions, and both fold their inner membranes to maximise working area.
- Animals lack chloroplasts, so they must eat to get energy.

</Step>
