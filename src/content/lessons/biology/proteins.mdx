<Step title="The workhorses of the cell" kind="explain">

If carbohydrates and lipids are fuel and storage, **proteins** are the *machinery*. They do more different jobs than any other molecule in your body — and they're built from just **20** kinds of building block.

Proteins contain carbon, hydrogen, oxygen, and crucially **nitrogen** (and sometimes sulfur), which sets them apart from carbohydrates and lipids.

</Step>

The monomer is the **amino acid**. There are 20 of them, and a cell strings them together in a specific **order** to make a chain (a polypeptide). That chain then folds into a precise 3-D shape.

</Step>

<Step title="One family, endless jobs" kind="explain">

Because the 20 amino acids can be arranged in any order and length, proteins come in astronomical variety — and that variety lets them do almost everything:

- **Enzymes** — speed up the cell's reactions (the next world's star).
- **Structure** — keratin in hair and nails, collagen in skin and bone.
- **Transport** — haemoglobin carries oxygen in your blood.
- **Defence** — antibodies fight infection.
- **Hormones** — insulin controls blood sugar.

What decides a protein's job is its **shape**, and its shape comes from its amino-acid order — the idea we'll dig into in this world's deep-dive.

<Callout type="info" title="The order is everything">
Change a single amino acid in the chain and you can change the whole protein. In sickle-cell anaemia, swapping just one amino acid in haemoglobin reshapes the entire red blood cell.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Proteins** (C, H, O, N) are the cell's workhorses.
- Monomer = **amino acid**; there are **20**, strung in a specific **order** into a chain.
- That sequence folds into a shape, and the shape sets the job: **enzymes, structure, transport, defence, hormones**.

</Step>
