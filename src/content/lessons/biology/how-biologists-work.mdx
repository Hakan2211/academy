<Step title="More than collecting facts" kind="explain">

Biology isn't a pile of facts to memorise — it's a *method* for finding them out. It usually runs like this:

1. **Observe** something curious ("the plants on the windowsill grow taller").
2. Ask a **question** ("is it the light?").
3. Propose a **hypothesis** — a testable idea ("more light makes plants grow taller").
4. Make a **prediction** and run an **experiment** to test it.
5. Look at the data and **refine** the idea.

The heart of it all is step 4: a *fair test*.

</Step>

<Step title="Anatomy of a fair test" kind="interactive">

To test whether light affects growth, you grow several plants under different amounts of light and measure how tall they get. Tap each role to see how a fair test is built.

<VariableLab />

The trick is ruthless fairness: change **one** thing, measure **one** thing, and hold **everything else** identical. If you also gave the brightest plant extra water, you could never tell whether light or water did it.

</Step>

<Step title="Controls, repeats, and honesty" kind="explain">

Two more habits separate good biology from wishful thinking:

- A **control** — a plant given "normal" conditions to compare against. Without it you have nothing to measure the effect *against*.
- **Repeats** — many plants at each light level, not one. One tall plant could be a fluke; a clear trend across dozens is convincing.

<Callout type="tip" title="Why repeats matter">
Living things vary. Any single result might be luck. Repeating the test and averaging cancels out the noise and reveals the real pattern — and lets others check your work.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- Biology is a method: observe → question → **hypothesis** → experiment → refine.
- A **fair test** changes one variable (independent), measures one (dependent), and keeps the rest (controlled) the same.
- A **control group** gives you something to compare against.
- **Repeats** average out natural variation and make results trustworthy.

</Step>
