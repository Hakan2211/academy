<Step title="Why common names fail" kind="explain">

A "robin" in Britain is a small orange-breasted bird. A "robin" in America is a completely different, much larger thing. A "bug" might mean an insect, a germ, or a software glitch.

Common names are local, inconsistent, and change from language to language. Science needs names that mean **exactly one thing**, everywhere on Earth. The solution, from Carl Linnaeus in the 1700s, was a clever two-word system.

</Step>

<Step title="Two words, one species" kind="explain">

Every species gets a **binomial** — a two-part Latin name:

- The first word is the **genus** (the close-relatives group), always capitalised.
- The second is the **species** (the exact kind), always lower-case.

So humans are *Homo sapiens*, the domestic dog is *Canis familiaris*, and the lion is *Panthera leo*. Written in *italics*, it's understood by a biologist in any country regardless of their language.

<Callout type="info" title="Reading a name">
*Panthera leo* (lion) and *Panthera tigris* (tiger) share a genus — that first word tells you instantly they are close cousins.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Identify with a key" kind="interactive">

How do you put a name to an unknown creature? You use a **dichotomous key** — a chain of yes/no questions that splits the possibilities in half each time until one is left.

Each answer is a fork in the road. A well-built key always offers exactly two choices and narrows down to a single, certain identification.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- Common names are ambiguous; science uses a universal **binomial** system.
- A name has two parts: **Genus species** (e.g. *Homo sapiens*) — capitalised genus, lower-case species, in italics.
- Sharing a genus signals close relationship.
- A **dichotomous key** identifies an unknown organism through a chain of yes/no questions.

</Step>
