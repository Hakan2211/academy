<Lesson>

<Step title="Why are cells so small?" kind="explain">

Here's a puzzle. If cells are such successful little factories, why don't they just grow huge? Why is almost every cell microscopic, and big organisms built from *trillions* of tiny ones rather than a few giant ones?

The answer is geometry. Everything a cell needs — oxygen, food, waste removal — has to cross its **surface**. Everything it has to *supply* fills its **volume**. And as a cell grows, those two don't keep pace.

</Step>

<Step title="The shrinking ratio" kind="interactive">

Drag the side length and watch the numbers. Surface area grows with the **square** of size; volume grows with the **cube**.

<SurfaceVolume />

The result is the **surface-area-to-volume ratio** — and it *falls* as the cell gets bigger. A big cell has a huge interior but, relatively, far too little surface to feed it. The centre would suffocate.

</Step>

<Step title="Nature's fix: more surface" kind="explain">

Life has two ways around the problem:

1. **Stay small and divide** — most cells do exactly this.
2. **Fold the surface** — where exchange must happen fast, evolution crams in surface area:
   - **Alveoli** give your lungs the area of a tennis court.
   - **Villi and microvilli** carpet your gut lining.
   - **Root hairs** vastly extend a plant's water-absorbing surface.
   - Flat, thin shapes (like a red blood cell) shorten the distance to the middle.

<Callout type="info" title="A rule across all of biology">
Wherever fast exchange is needed — lungs, gut, gills, roots, capillaries — you'll find a structure that maximises surface area. It's one of biology's most repeated design tricks.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- A cell's **surface** does all its exchange; its **volume** is what must be served.
- As size grows, volume outpaces surface, so the **surface-area-to-volume ratio falls** — big cells can't supply their centres.
- Life responds by staying small and dividing, or by **folding surfaces** (alveoli, villi, root hairs) to maximise exchange.

</Step>
