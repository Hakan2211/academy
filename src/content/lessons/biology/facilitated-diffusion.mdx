<Lesson>

<Step title="When molecules can't get through" kind="explain">

Oxygen slips across the membrane easily. But the cell needs plenty of things the oily core flat-out blocks — glucose, amino acids, and charged ions like sodium and potassium. They're either too big or too electrically charged to dissolve through the fat.

The cell's answer: build **doors**. Special proteins in the membrane let these molecules through — a process called **facilitated diffusion**.

</Step>

<Step title="Channels and carriers" kind="interactive">

There are two kinds of protein door. Click the **channel protein** and the **carrier protein** in the membrane.

<FluidMosaic />

- A **channel protein** is a water-filled tunnel — ions and water slip straight through.
- A **carrier protein** grabs a specific molecule (like glucose), changes shape, and releases it on the other side.

Each protein is picky: a glucose carrier only carries glucose.

</Step>

<Step title="Still passive, still downhill" kind="explain">

Here's the crucial point: facilitated diffusion is still **diffusion**. The molecules move **down** their concentration gradient, from crowded to sparse, and the cell spends **no energy**. The protein just provides a route through a barrier that would otherwise stop them.

There's a twist, though: because it relies on a limited number of proteins, the rate **levels off** once every protein is busy — unlike plain diffusion, which just keeps speeding up with a steeper gradient.

<Callout type="tip" title="Passive vs active">
"Facilitated" means *helped*, not *forced*. The protein is a doorway, not a pump — the molecule still has to be heading downhill on its own.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Facilitated diffusion** moves molecules that can't cross the oily core (glucose, ions) using membrane **proteins**.
- **Channel proteins** = open tunnels; **carrier proteins** = shape-changing shuttles. Each is specific.
- It's still **passive** (no energy) and **downhill**, but its rate **plateaus** when the proteins are saturated.

</Step>
