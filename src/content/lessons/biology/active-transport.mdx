<Lesson>

<Step title="Going uphill" kind="explain">

Diffusion always runs *downhill* — from crowded to sparse. But cells often need the opposite. A root hair must pull in minerals even though the soil has **less** of them than the root. Your gut absorbs the last traces of glucose even when blood already has more.

To move a substance **against** its gradient — from low to high concentration — a cell must do work. This is **active transport**, and like all work, it costs energy.

</Step>

<Step title="The pump at work" kind="interactive">

A carrier protein acts as a **pump**: it grabs a molecule on the scarce side and forces it across to the crowded side, burning **ATP** for each push. Toggle the ATP supply and watch what happens.

<ActiveTransport />

Switch the ATP off and the pump simply stalls. No energy, no uphill movement — the defining feature of active transport.

</Step>

<Step title="Powered by respiration" kind="explain">

The energy comes from **ATP**, the cell's energy currency, which is made by **respiration** in the mitochondria. This links straight back to the cell's structure:

- Cells that do lots of active transport — root hair cells, gut lining cells, kidney cells — are **packed with mitochondria** to supply the ATP.
- It's the only transport method that can move things against the gradient, so it's how cells **accumulate** what they need and **expel** what they don't.

<Callout type="info" title="Spot the difference">
Diffusion, osmosis, and facilitated diffusion are all **passive** — downhill, no energy. **Active transport** is the odd one out: uphill, and it always costs ATP.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Active transport** moves substances **against** their concentration gradient (low → high).
- It requires **energy as ATP**, supplied by respiration — switch the energy off and it stops.
- Carrier **pump** proteins do the work; cells that rely on it are rich in **mitochondria**.
- It's the only method that lets a cell accumulate or expel substances against the natural flow.

</Step>
