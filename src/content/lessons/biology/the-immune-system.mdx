<Step title="The body's defence force" kind="explain">

Pathogens are everywhere, constantly trying to get in. That you're not ill most of the time is down to your **immune system** — a layered defence force that keeps invaders out, and hunts down any that break in.

It works in **three lines of defence**, from simple physical barriers to a highly targeted attack.

</Step>

<Step title="Three lines of defence" kind="interactive">

Click each defence to see how it works.

<ImmuneSystem />

- **First line — barriers:** skin, mucus, and stomach acid keep most microbes out.
- **Second line — phagocytes:** white blood cells that engulf *any* invader that gets in.
- **Third line — lymphocytes:** white blood cells that mount a *targeted* attack and remember the enemy.

</Step>

<Step title="The specific attack" kind="explain">

The clever part is the third line. Every pathogen carries unique molecules on its surface called **antigens**. The **lymphocytes** recognise a specific antigen and respond in two ways:

- They produce **antibodies** — Y-shaped proteins that lock onto that exact pathogen, clumping invaders together and marking them for destruction.
- They make **memory cells** that linger for years, so if the *same* pathogen ever returns, the response is almost instant.

This memory is the basis of **immunity** — and the reason you usually catch diseases like chickenpox only once. It's also exactly what vaccines exploit, as the next lesson shows.

<Callout type="info" title="Antibodies are specific">
Each antibody fits one antigen, like a key fits one lock (the same lock-and-key idea as enzymes). An antibody against measles does nothing against flu — which is why immunity to one disease doesn't protect against another.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- The **immune system** defends in **three lines**: barriers (skin, mucus, acid), **phagocytes** (engulf any invader), **lymphocytes** (targeted attack).
- Lymphocytes recognise a pathogen's **antigens** and make **antibodies** (specific) plus **memory cells**.
- **Memory cells** give long-term **immunity** — and are the basis of vaccination.

</Step>
