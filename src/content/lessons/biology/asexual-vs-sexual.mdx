<Lesson>

<Step title="Two strategies for the future" kind="explain">

No organism lives forever, so every species must make a new generation. There are two fundamentally different ways to do it — and the choice shapes everything about how a species lives and evolves.

**Asexual** reproduction needs just one parent and makes identical copies. **Sexual** reproduction needs two parents and makes varied offspring.

</Step>

<Step title="Clones or combinations?" kind="interactive">

Toggle between the two and weigh up their advantages.

<ReproductionCompare />

- **Asexual** → genetically identical **clones**. Fast and simple — one parent, no mate needed.
- **Sexual** → offspring with a **mix** of both parents' genes. Slower, but every child is unique.

</Step>

<Step title="The great trade-off" kind="explain">

Each strategy wins in different conditions:

- **Asexual** is brilliant when conditions are **stable** — reproduce fast, colonise quickly. But because the offspring are identical, a single new disease or a change in the environment can wipe them **all** out.
- **Sexual** is slower and costlier, but its **variation** is a hedge against change. If the environment shifts or a disease strikes, some varied offspring are likely to survive — and that variation is the fuel for **evolution**.

Many organisms hedge their bets by using **both**: a strawberry plant sends out runners (asexual) *and* makes flowers and seeds (sexual).

<Callout type="info" title="Why sex is worth the cost">
Sexual reproduction is expensive — you need a mate and pass on only half your genes. Its payoff is **variation**, the raw material natural selection needs. That's why most complex life reproduces sexually despite the cost.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Asexual** reproduction: one parent, identical **clones**, fast — but no variation (vulnerable to change).
- **Sexual** reproduction: two parents, **varied** offspring, slower and costlier — but adaptable.
- Variation from sexual reproduction is the **raw material of evolution**.
- Many organisms use **both** strategies.

</Step>
