<Step title="When traits break the simple rules" kind="explain">

Mendel's neat dominant-recessive rules explain a lot — but real inheritance is often richer. Many traits don't sort into two tidy categories. This lesson covers the most important "exceptions" that go **beyond Mendel**.

</Step>

<Step title="Codominance & incomplete dominance" kind="explain">

Sometimes neither allele fully masks the other:

- **Incomplete dominance** — the two blend. A red flower (RR) crossed with white (WW) can give **pink** offspring (RW), a mix of both.
- **Codominance** — both alleles show fully, side by side. In human **blood groups**, the A and B alleles are codominant, so someone with both is blood type **AB** — expressing A *and* B, not a blend.

So a heterozygote isn't always identical to a dominant homozygote — sometimes it's visibly different.

</Step>

<Step title="Sex linkage" kind="interactive">

Some genes sit on the **sex chromosomes** (X and Y), which makes their inheritance depend on sex. Because males have only one X (XY), a single recessive allele on it shows up — they have no second X to mask it.

<PunnettSquare dom="X" rec="x" trait="X-linked condition" domPheno="unaffected" recPheno="affected" parent1={["X","x"]} parent2={["X","x"]} />

This is why conditions like **red-green colour blindness** and **haemophilia** are far more common in males: a carrier mother can pass an affected X to her sons.

</Step>

<Step title="Polygenic traits" kind="explain">

Finally, many traits aren't controlled by one gene at all. **Height, skin colour,** and **eye colour** are **polygenic** — shaped by *many* genes adding up, plus the environment.

That's why these traits form a smooth **range** across a population (lots of medium-height people, few very tall or very short) rather than a few distinct categories. Mendel's peas were unusually simple; most of *you* is polygenic.

<Callout type="info" title="The bigger picture">
Mendel's rules are the foundation, not the whole story. Codominance, sex linkage, and polygenic inheritance show how a few simple principles combine into the rich variety of real traits.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Incomplete dominance** blends alleles (red + white → pink); **codominance** shows both (blood type AB).
- **Sex-linked** genes (on X/Y) affect males more — males have only one X, so recessive X alleles aren't masked (colour blindness, haemophilia).
- **Polygenic** traits (height, skin colour) come from many genes + environment, giving a continuous range.

</Step>
