<Step title="Sugars and starches" kind="explain">

The first family of life's molecules is the **carbohydrates** — the sugars and starches. Made of just **carbon, hydrogen, and oxygen**, they are the body's go-to **fuel** and a handy way to **store** energy.

When you eat bread, pasta, or fruit, you're eating carbohydrates, and your cells will break them down to power respiration.

</Step>

<Step title="From single sugars to long chains" kind="interactive">

Keep the toggle on "Carbohydrate" and look at how it's built.

<BiomoleculeViewer focus="carbohydrate" />

A single sugar unit (a **monosaccharide**) like **glucose** is the monomer. Link many together and you get a **polysaccharide** — a long storage or structural chain.

</Step>

<Step title="One building block, many jobs" kind="explain">

The same glucose units, chained differently, do completely different jobs:

- **Glucose** — the small, soluble sugar cells actually respire for energy.
- **Starch** — how *plants* store glucose, coiled into compact grains.
- **Glycogen** — how *animals* (you) store glucose, in the liver and muscles.
- **Cellulose** — chains so strong they build the **cell walls** of plants — the most abundant molecule in the living world.

<Callout type="info" title="Storage vs structure">
Storage carbohydrates (starch, glycogen) are easy to break down for quick energy. Structural ones (cellulose) are tough and rigid — which is exactly why we can't digest the cellulose in vegetables.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Carbohydrates** (C, H, O) are sugars and starches — fuel and energy storage.
- Monomer = a single sugar (**monosaccharide**, e.g. glucose); polymer = a **polysaccharide**.
- Same glucose, different linkages: **starch** (plant store), **glycogen** (animal store), **cellulose** (plant cell walls).

</Step>
