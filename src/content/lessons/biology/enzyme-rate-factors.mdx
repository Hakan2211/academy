<Step title="Enzymes have a sweet spot" kind="explain">

An enzyme isn't equally happy in all conditions. Because it's a folded protein, its activity depends on keeping that delicate shape intact — and a few conditions can speed it up, slow it down, or wreck it entirely.

The main factors are **temperature**, **pH**, and **substrate concentration**. Let's see how each one bends the rate.

</Step>

<Step title="Temperature and pH" kind="interactive">

Drag the value and trace the curve. Switch between temperature and pH.

<EnzymeRate />

- **Temperature:** the rate climbs as it warms — until it hits the **optimum** (about 40°C in humans). Push past it and the enzyme **denatures**: its shape unravels and the rate crashes.
- **pH:** each enzyme has an optimum pH where it works best. Too acidic or too alkaline disrupts the active site.

</Step>

<Step title="Why the curves look that way" kind="explain">

- **Below the optimum:** more heat gives the molecules more energy, so substrate and enzyme collide more often — faster rate.
- **Above the optimum:** heat breaks the weak bonds holding the enzyme's shape. The active site deforms, the substrate no longer fits, and the enzyme is **denatured** — usually for good.
- **Substrate concentration:** more substrate means a faster rate — until every active site is busy. Then the rate **plateaus**; adding more substrate can't help.

<Callout type="tip" title="Optimum, not maximum">
Hotter is faster only up to a point. Past the optimum, "more" becomes "broken". This is why a high fever is dangerous and why food kept cold (slowing enzymes) lasts longer.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- Enzyme rate depends on **temperature, pH,** and **substrate concentration**.
- Rate rises to an **optimum**, then **denaturation** (from heat or wrong pH) destroys the shape and the rate crashes.
- Substrate concentration raises the rate until all active sites are busy, then it **plateaus**.
- Each enzyme has its own optimum conditions.
