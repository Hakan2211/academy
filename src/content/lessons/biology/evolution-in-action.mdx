<Step title="Evolution you can watch" kind="explain">

A common objection to evolution is "you can't see it happen." But you can — when generations are short enough. Bacteria divide every twenty minutes, so a population goes through more generations in a week than humans have in all of history. In fast-breeding life, evolution unfolds before our eyes.

The most important real-time example isn't in a jungle — it's in our hospitals: the rise of **antibiotic resistance**.

</Step>

<Step title="Antibiotic resistance, generation by generation" kind="interactive">

It's natural selection, exactly as before — only the "predator" is a drug. Run it.

<NaturalSelection preset="bacteria" />

A few bacteria, by chance mutation, are **resistant**. Add the antibiotic and it kills the rest — leaving the resistant ones to multiply. Within a few generations the whole population is resistant. The drug didn't *create* resistance; it **selected** for the rare resistant individuals already there.

</Step>

<Step title="Why this is dangerous" kind="explain">

This is one of the biggest threats in modern medicine. Every time antibiotics are used — especially when overused or not finished — they apply a selection pressure that favours resistant bacteria:

- Resistant bacteria survive and spread.
- Infections become harder, then impossible, to treat with that drug.
- "Superbugs" like MRSA resist multiple antibiotics at once.

It also shows evolution's logic perfectly: the variation (resistance) existed **before** the antibiotic. Selection didn't make bacteria want to resist — it simply removed the ones that couldn't.

<Callout type="tip" title="Slowing it down">
This is why doctors urge you to finish a course of antibiotics and not demand them for viral colds (antibiotics don't even work on viruses). Less unnecessary selection pressure = slower spread of resistance.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="More evolution we've witnessed" kind="explain">

Antibiotic resistance isn't alone. We've directly observed:

- **Pesticide resistance** in insects and **herbicide resistance** in weeds — the same story, different chemical.
- **Peppered moths** shifting colour with industrial pollution and back again as air cleaned up.
- **Darwin's finches** changing beak size measurably during droughts.
- Every **flu vaccine** being remade yearly because the virus evolves.

Evolution isn't only ancient history written in rocks — it's an ongoing process we measure and must plan around.

</Step>

<Step title="Clearing up the myths" kind="explain">

Evolution is widely misunderstood. Three corrections worth locking in:

- **"Individuals evolve."** No — *populations* evolve. A single beetle can't change its colour; the *proportion* of colours in the population shifts across generations.
- **"Evolution has a goal / aims at humans."** No — it has no foresight and no target. It just preserves whatever survives in the moment. Humans are one twig, not the summit.
- **"It's just a theory" (meaning a guess).** In science, a **theory** is a well-tested explanation backed by huge evidence — like the theory of gravity. Evolution is about as solid as science gets.

</Step>

<Step title="The grand picture" kind="explain">

Step all the way back. A few simple rules — variation, inheritance, selection, and vast amounts of time — are enough to transform single cells into the entire pageant of life: forests, whales, fungi, and the brain reading this sentence.

That's the profound power of evolution. No designer, no plan — just descent with modification, filtered by survival, repeated for nearly four billion years. It is the single idea that ties all of biology together.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- Evolution is **observable** in fast-breeding life — most importantly **antibiotic resistance**.
- The drug **selects** pre-existing resistant variants; it doesn't create resistance — a major medical threat.
- Other real cases: pesticide/herbicide resistance, peppered moths, finches, the yearly flu vaccine.
- **Myths to drop:** populations (not individuals) evolve; evolution has **no goal**; a scientific "theory" is well-tested, not a guess.
- A few rules + deep time = all of life's diversity.

</Step>
