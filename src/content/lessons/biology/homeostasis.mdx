<Step title="Keeping the inside steady" kind="explain">

Your cells are fussy. They work only within a narrow range of temperature, water, and chemical conditions. Yet the outside world is anything but steady. So your body works constantly to keep its **internal environment** stable — a process called **homeostasis**.

Blood glucose, body temperature, water, and pH are all held within tight limits, no matter what you eat, do, or where you go.

</Step>

<Step title="Negative feedback" kind="interactive">

The trick behind homeostasis is **negative feedback**: whenever a level strays from its **set point**, the body triggers changes that push it **back**. Poke the blood glucose and watch it self-correct.

<Homeostasis />

- Glucose rises (after a meal) → the pancreas releases **insulin** → cells take up glucose → it falls.
- Glucose falls (during exercise) → the pancreas releases **glucagon** → the liver frees glucose → it rises.

Each correction opposes the change — that's what "negative" feedback means.

</Step>

<Step title="The general recipe" kind="explain">

Every homeostatic system has the same parts:

1. A **receptor** detects the change from the set point.
2. A **coordinator** (brain or gland) processes it.
3. An **effector** makes the correction.
4. The change is reversed — bringing the level back.

It runs everywhere: controlling **temperature** (sweating and shivering, run by the hypothalamus), **water balance** (the kidneys), and **blood glucose** (the pancreas). When homeostasis fails — for example, when the body can't control blood sugar — the result is disease, such as **diabetes**.

<Callout type="info" title="Diabetes">
In type 1 diabetes the pancreas can't make insulin, so blood glucose isn't controlled and can climb dangerously high. Treatment replaces the missing insulin — a direct application of understanding this feedback loop.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Homeostasis** keeps the body's internal environment stable (glucose, temperature, water, pH).
- It works by **negative feedback**: any drift from the **set point** triggers an opposing correction.
- The recipe: **receptor → coordinator → effector → reverse the change.**
- Examples: insulin/glucagon (glucose), sweating/shivering (temperature), the kidneys (water). Failure causes disease like **diabetes**.

</Step>
