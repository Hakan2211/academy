<Step title="A war fought inside you" kind="explain">

When a pathogen breaks through your barriers, an extraordinary, coordinated battle begins. Billions of cells communicate, multiply, and attack — usually winning so quietly you never even notice. This deep-dive opens up that battle: the two arms of the immune system, how they work together, and why it gives lifelong protection.

</Step>

<Step title="Two arms: innate and adaptive" kind="explain">

Your immune system has two cooperating parts:

- The **innate** immune system — fast, general, and always ready. It includes the barriers and the **phagocytes** that engulf *any* invader. It doesn't care which pathogen it is — it attacks them all the same way, within minutes to hours.
- The **adaptive** immune system — slower but **specific** and with a **memory**. The **lymphocytes** learn to recognise one particular pathogen and attack it precisely, over days.

Innate buys time; adaptive wins the war and remembers it.

</Step>

<Step title="Phagocytes: eat first, ask later" kind="interactive">

The front-line troops are the **phagocytes**. Watch them work alongside antibodies.

<ImmuneResponse />

A phagocyte engulfs a pathogen whole (**phagocytosis** — the endocytosis you met earlier) and digests it inside. It's fast and indiscriminate — vital for holding the line while the slower, specific response gears up.

</Step>

<Step title="Lymphocytes and antibodies: the targeted strike" kind="explain">

Meanwhile the **lymphocytes** mount the precision attack, in two teams:

- **B-lymphocytes** make **antibodies** — proteins tailored to one pathogen's **antigens**. Antibodies clump pathogens together, neutralise their toxins, and flag them for phagocytes.
- **T-lymphocytes** hunt down and kill the body's *own* cells that have been infected (like virus factories), and coordinate the whole response.

The catch is speed: it takes days to find the right lymphocyte and multiply it. That delay is why you feel ill for a few days before recovering — the adaptive response is still ramping up.

</Step>

<Step title="Memory: the lasting payoff" kind="interactive">

After the battle is won, most of the fighters die — but some lymphocytes survive as **memory cells**, primed for years. That's what turns a hard-won victory into lasting **immunity**.

<Vaccination />

If the *same* pathogen ever returns, memory cells trigger a **secondary response** so fast and large the pathogen is crushed before you notice. This is why you catch chickenpox once, and exactly what **vaccines** create — immunity without the illness.

</Step>

<Step title="When the system misfires" kind="explain">

So powerful a system can also go wrong, which reveals how it works:

- **Allergies** — the immune system overreacts to something harmless (pollen, peanuts), causing the symptoms of hay fever or, dangerously, anaphylaxis.
- **Autoimmune disease** — it mistakenly attacks the body's *own* cells (type 1 diabetes, rheumatoid arthritis).
- **Immunodeficiency** — it's weakened, as when **HIV** destroys the very T-lymphocytes that coordinate the response, leaving the body open to infections it would normally shrug off.

Each disorder is the immune system doing too much, too little, or aiming at the wrong target.

</Step>

<Step title="The grand design" kind="explain">

Step back and admire it. With no central commander, billions of cells run a layered defence: barriers, then fast general troops, then a slow precise strike that learns its enemy and never forgets. It draws on ideas from across all of biology — **endocytosis**, **proteins** shaped to fit (antibodies, like enzymes), **cell signalling**, **gene** rearrangement to make millions of antibody types, and **natural selection** of the best-fitting lymphocytes inside your own body.

It's one of the most sophisticated systems known — and it's running, silently, inside you right now.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- The immune system has two arms: **innate** (fast, general — barriers and **phagocytes**) and **adaptive** (slow, **specific**, with **memory** — lymphocytes).
- **B-lymphocytes** make **antibodies** against antigens; **T-lymphocytes** kill infected cells and coordinate.
- **Memory cells** give lasting **immunity** — a fast, huge **secondary response** — exploited by **vaccines**.
- Misfires cause **allergies**, **autoimmune disease**, and **immunodeficiency** (e.g. HIV).

</Step>
