<Lesson>

<Step title="The whole journey, end to end" kind="explain">

We've met the steps one at a time. Now let's run the complete pipeline — the path from a stretch of DNA to a finished, working protein. It's the central process of molecular biology, and following it whole reveals just how tightly the pieces fit together.

The route is short to state and profound in consequence: **DNA → mRNA → protein**. Biologists call it the **central dogma**.

</Step>

<Step title="Step 1 — the gene in the DNA" kind="interactive">

It begins with a **gene**: a stretch of the double helix whose base sequence spells out one protein.

<DNAHelix />

The order of the bases *is* the information. Change the order and you change the protein it codes for — that's the whole point of the code.

</Step>

<Step title="Step 2 — transcription" kind="interactive">

Inside the nucleus, the gene is copied into a portable **mRNA** message.

<Transcription />

RNA polymerase reads the DNA and writes the complementary mRNA (with U for T). That message then slips out of the nucleus to the ribosomes.

</Step>

<Step title="Step 3 — translation" kind="interactive">

At the ribosome, the message is decoded codon by codon, and the protein is built.

<Translation />

Each codon calls in a tRNA carrying one amino acid. The chain grows in the exact order the gene specified, then folds into its working shape. The journey from gene to protein is complete.

</Step>

<Step title="One direction, one logic" kind="explain">

Step back and see the elegance:

- The **sequence of bases** in DNA determines
- the **sequence of codons** in mRNA, which determines
- the **sequence of amino acids** in the protein, which determines
- the protein's **folded shape**, which determines
- what the protein **does** — and so, ultimately, the organism's traits.

Each link sets the next, in one direction. This is how a one-dimensional string of chemical letters builds the three-dimensional machinery of a living thing.

</Step>

<Step title="When one letter changes" kind="explain">

Because each step depends on the one before, a single change to the DNA — a **mutation** — can ripple all the way through:

- Change one base → possibly change one codon → possibly change one amino acid → possibly change the protein's shape → possibly change how it works.
- In **sickle-cell anaemia**, exactly this happens: a single base change swaps one amino acid in haemoglobin, deforming red blood cells.

But thanks to the code's redundancy, many mutations change *nothing* — the new codon still codes for the same amino acid. This is why some mutations are harmless, some are harmful, and a rare few are beneficial — the raw material for evolution.

<Callout type="tip" title="Genotype to phenotype">
Biologists call the DNA you carry your **genotype**, and the traits it produces your **phenotype**. The gene-to-protein pipeline is the bridge between them — how the code becomes the creature.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- The **central dogma**: DNA → mRNA → protein, in one direction.
- **Transcription** (in the nucleus) copies a gene to mRNA; **translation** (at the ribosome) builds the protein from it.
- Base sequence → codon sequence → amino-acid sequence → protein shape → protein function → trait.
- A **mutation** can ripple through this chain — harmful, harmless, or (rarely) helpful — linking **genotype** to **phenotype**.

</Step>
