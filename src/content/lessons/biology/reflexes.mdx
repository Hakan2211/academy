<Lesson>

<Step title="Faster than thought" kind="explain">

Touch something hot and your hand snaps back **before** you even feel the pain. Dust flies at your eye and you blink without deciding to. These are **reflexes** — automatic, lightning-fast responses that protect you from harm.

The trick to their speed is that they **don't wait for the brain** to decide. The signal takes a shortcut.

</Step>

<Step title="The reflex arc" kind="interactive">

Watch the signal travel the shortcut — through the **spinal cord**, not the brain.

<ReflexArc />

The pathway is the **reflex arc**: receptor → **sensory neuron** → a **relay neuron** in the spinal cord → **motor neuron** → effector (muscle). Because it skips the brain, the response happens in a fraction of a second.

</Step>

<Step title="Automatic and protective" kind="explain">

Reflexes share three features:

- **Fast** — the short spinal route saves precious time.
- **Automatic** — you can't choose to stop a true reflex; no conscious thought is involved.
- **Protective** — most reflexes prevent damage (pulling from heat, blinking, the knee-jerk that keeps you upright).

Your brain is still informed — that's how you *feel* the pain a moment later — but the protective action has already happened by then. Evolution favoured creatures that reacted to danger before stopping to think.

<Callout type="tip" title="Reflex vs voluntary">
Picking up a cup is **voluntary** — your brain plans it. Yanking your hand from a flame is a **reflex** — your spinal cord handles it automatically, and the brain only finds out afterwards.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Reflexes** are fast, automatic, protective responses.
- They follow a **reflex arc**: receptor → sensory neuron → **relay neuron (spinal cord)** → motor neuron → effector.
- They're fast because they **bypass the brain**; the brain is informed afterwards (so you feel pain a moment later).

</Step>
