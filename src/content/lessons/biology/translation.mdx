<Lesson>

<Step title="Building the protein" kind="explain">

The mRNA has carried the gene's message out to the cytoplasm. Now comes the payoff: turning that string of codons into an actual **protein**. This second half of the process is **translation**, and it happens on a molecular machine called the **ribosome**.

If transcription was *copying* the recipe, translation is *cooking* — reading the recipe and assembling the dish, one amino acid at a time.

</Step>

<Step title="The ribosome reads, the tRNA delivers" kind="interactive">

Watch the ribosome work its way along the mRNA, three bases at a time.

<Translation />

For each **codon**, a matching **transfer RNA (tRNA)** arrives carrying the correct **amino acid**. The amino acid is added to the growing chain, the ribosome shifts to the next codon, and the protein lengthens — until a **STOP** codon ends it.

</Step>

<Step title="From start to stop" kind="explain">

The whole thing reads like a sentence with punctuation:

1. The ribosome finds the **START** codon (AUG) and begins.
2. It reads each codon, and the matching **tRNA** brings the right amino acid.
3. Amino acids link into a chain in the exact order the mRNA dictates.
4. At a **STOP** codon, the finished chain is **released**.

That chain then folds into its 3-D shape and becomes a working protein. The amino-acid order — and so the protein's identity — was set all the way back in the DNA.

<Callout type="tip" title="The whole chain of command">
DNA → (transcription) → mRNA → (translation) → protein. This one-way flow of information is called the **central dogma** of biology — and the deep-dive follows it end to end.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Translation** builds a protein from the mRNA message, on the **ribosome**.
- The ribosome reads **codons**; a matching **tRNA** delivers each **amino acid**.
- The chain grows from the **START** codon until a **STOP** codon releases it.
- Full flow (the **central dogma**): DNA → mRNA → protein.

</Step>
