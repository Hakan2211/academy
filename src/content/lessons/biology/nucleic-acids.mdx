<Lesson>

<Step title="The information molecules" kind="explain">

The last family is the most famous: the **nucleic acids**, **DNA** and **RNA**. They don't build structures or store energy — they store **information**: the complete instructions for building every protein in an organism.

They add one more element to the mix: **phosphorus**, alongside carbon, hydrogen, oxygen, and nitrogen.

</Step>

<Step title="Nucleotides, linked into a strand" kind="interactive">

Switch the toggle to "Nucleic acid".

<BiomoleculeViewer focus="nucleic" />

The monomer is the **nucleotide**, and it has three parts: a **phosphate**, a **sugar**, and a **base**. Chain millions of nucleotides together and you get a strand of DNA.

</Step>

<Step title="A four-letter code" kind="explain">

The clever part is the **base**. There are only four kinds (in DNA: A, T, C, and G), and their **order** along the strand is a code — a four-letter alphabet spelling out the instructions for life.

- **DNA** is the master archive: two strands twisted into the famous **double helix**, kept safe in the nucleus.
- **RNA** is a single-stranded working copy used to carry instructions out to the ribosomes.

How that code is read and turned into proteins is the entire story of the **DNA & the Code of Life** world ahead.

<Callout type="info" title="From molecule to message">
The same four bases, in different orders, can spell the instructions for a bacterium, a redwood, or you. Nucleic acids are where biology becomes information.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Nucleic acids** (C, H, O, N, P) — **DNA** and **RNA** — store genetic **information**.
- Monomer = the **nucleotide**: phosphate + sugar + **base**.
- Four bases, in order, form a code. **DNA** is the double-helix master copy; **RNA** is the single-stranded working copy.

</Step>
