<Lesson>

<Step title="The wonder drugs" kind="explain">

Before the 1940s, a simple infected cut could kill you. Then came **antibiotics** — drugs that kill **bacteria** (or stop them growing) without harming your own cells. They work by targeting features bacteria have but you don't, like the bacterial **cell wall**. Penicillin and the antibiotics that followed transformed medicine and saved countless lives.

But there's a catch, and it's evolution.

</Step>

<Step title="Resistance evolves" kind="interactive">

Antibiotics are a powerful selection pressure — and bacteria evolve fast. Run the simulation.

<NaturalSelection preset="bacteria" />

A few bacteria, by chance mutation, are **resistant** to the drug. The antibiotic kills the rest, leaving the resistant ones to multiply. Within a few generations, the whole population resists the drug. This is **natural selection** happening in days, right inside a patient.

</Step>

<Step title="A growing crisis" kind="explain">

The drug doesn't *create* resistance — the resistant bacteria were already there. The antibiotic just **selects** for them. And because bacteria swap resistance genes on **plasmids**, resistance spreads even between species.

The result is a serious global threat: **antibiotic-resistant bacteria** like **MRSA**, which shrug off our best drugs. To slow it down, everyone has a part to play:

- **Only use antibiotics when needed** — never for viral colds and flu (they don't work, and add selection pressure).
- **Finish the full course**, so no surviving bacteria are left to breed resistance.
- **Develop new antibiotics** — but bacteria keep catching up, so this is a constant race.

<Callout type="tip" title="It's evolution, not malice">
Resistance isn't bacteria "fighting back" — it's pure natural selection. Understanding it as evolution is what tells us how to slow it: reduce the selection pressure by using antibiotics carefully.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Antibiotics** kill bacteria by targeting features they have but we don't (e.g. the cell wall) — they do **not** work on viruses.
- **Resistance evolves** by natural selection: the drug selects pre-existing resistant bacteria; **plasmids** spread the genes.
- **Resistant superbugs** (e.g. MRSA) are a major threat.
- Slow it by **using antibiotics only when needed** and **finishing the course**.

</Step>
