<Step title="The monk with the pea plants" kind="explain">

Long before anyone knew what a gene was, a monk named **Gregor Mendel** worked out the rules of inheritance by breeding thousands of **pea plants** in a monastery garden. By carefully counting tall and short plants, purple and white flowers, he uncovered the hidden logic of heredity.

His key insight: traits are passed on as discrete **units** (we now call them **genes**), and each comes in alternative versions called **alleles**.

</Step>

<Step title="Dominant and recessive" kind="explain">

Each plant carries **two** alleles for a trait — one from each parent. Mendel found that some alleles are **dominant** and others **recessive**:

- A **dominant** allele (written as a capital, e.g. **B**) shows up whenever it's present.
- A **recessive** allele (lower-case, **b**) only shows when there's no dominant allele to mask it.

So **BB** and **Bb** both look the same (dominant trait), while only **bb** shows the recessive trait. The alleles you carry are your **genotype**; the trait you show is your **phenotype**.

</Step>

<Step title="The famous 3:1 ratio" kind="interactive">

Mendel crossed two plants that each carried one of each allele (**Bb × Bb**) and counted the offspring. Set both parents to **Bb** and look at the result.

<PunnettSquare parent1={["B","b"]} parent2={["B","b"]} />

Three out of four show the dominant trait, one shows the recessive — the classic **3:1 ratio**. The recessive trait reappears in offspring even when neither parent shows it.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Mendel** discovered inheritance works through discrete units (**genes**) with alternative versions (**alleles**).
- You carry **two alleles** per trait; **dominant** (capital) masks **recessive** (lower-case).
- **Genotype** = the alleles; **phenotype** = the trait shown. BB and Bb look alike; only bb shows the recessive trait.
- A **Bb × Bb** cross gives the classic **3:1** ratio.

</Step>
