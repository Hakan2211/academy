<Lesson>

<Step title="Getting the message out" kind="explain">

The DNA blueprint is precious and stays locked in the **nucleus**. But proteins are built outside it, on ribosomes in the cytoplasm. So the cell faces the nucleus's classic problem: how do you use the instructions without risking the original?

The answer is to make a **working copy** of just the one gene you need. That copy is **messenger RNA (mRNA)**, and making it is called **transcription** — the first half of turning a gene into a protein.

</Step>

<Step title="Copying a gene into RNA" kind="interactive">

An enzyme, **RNA polymerase**, unzips a small stretch of DNA and reads one strand, building a complementary mRNA copy as it goes.

<Transcription />

It follows the base-pairing rule, with one change: **RNA uses U (uracil) instead of T**. So where the DNA template has an A, the mRNA gets a U.

</Step>

<Step title="DNA vs RNA" kind="explain">

The mRNA copy differs from DNA in a few important ways — all suited to its job as a short-lived, mobile messenger:

- It's **single-stranded**, not a double helix.
- It uses the base **U** in place of T.
- It's **short** — a copy of one gene, not the whole genome.
- It can **leave the nucleus** through the nuclear pores to reach the ribosomes.

Once made, the mRNA carries the gene's message out to where proteins are built — setting up the second half of the process, **translation**.

<Callout type="info" title="Why a copy?">
By sending out a disposable RNA copy, the cell keeps its master DNA safe in the nucleus — and can make many copies of a gene at once when it needs lots of a protein.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Transcription** makes a **mRNA** working copy of one gene, inside the nucleus.
- **RNA polymerase** reads a DNA strand and builds complementary mRNA, using **U instead of T**.
- mRNA is **single-stranded, short,** and can **leave the nucleus** to reach the ribosomes.
- It keeps the master DNA safe while carrying the gene's message out.

</Step>
