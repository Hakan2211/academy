<Step title="Same DNA, different cells" kind="explain">

Here's a puzzle. Every cell in your body — skin, muscle, neuron, liver — carries the **exact same** DNA, the same complete set of genes. So why do they look and behave so differently?

The answer is that no cell uses *all* its genes. Each cell type switches **on** the genes it needs and keeps the rest **off**. This selective control is **gene regulation**, and it's how one genome builds hundreds of different cell types.

</Step>

<Step title="Switching genes on and off" kind="interactive">

A few "housekeeping" genes (like those for respiration) run in every cell. But the genes that *define* a cell — keratin in skin, myosin in muscle, ion channels in a neuron — are switched on only where they're needed.

</Step>

<Step title="How and why it's controlled" kind="explain">

Cells control genes mainly at the **transcription** step — deciding whether a gene gets copied into mRNA at all. Regulatory proteins bind near a gene and act like switches, turning transcription on or off in response to the cell's needs and signals.

This control is everywhere in biology:

- **Development** — it's how one fertilised egg becomes a body of specialised cells (a later world's story).
- **Response** — bacteria switch on sugar-digesting genes only when that sugar is present, saving energy.
- **Disease** — cancers often involve genes being wrongly switched on or off.

<Callout type="info" title="The genome is a library, not a script">
Think of the genome as a vast library every cell owns in full. What makes a cell unique isn't which books it has — it's which ones it chooses to *read*.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- Every cell shares the **same DNA**, but uses only **some** of its genes.
- **Gene regulation** switches genes on and off — mainly by controlling **transcription**.
- It's how one genome makes many cell types, lets cells respond to signals, and (when it goes wrong) contributes to disease.
- The genome is a library; each cell differs by which genes it **reads**.

</Step>
