<Lesson>

<Step title="One system, beautifully integrated" kind="explain">

We've met the parts — the pump, the pipes, the blood. This deep-dive puts them together as one **cardiovascular system** and looks more closely at how the heart actually beats, how it's controlled, and what goes wrong when it fails.

It's a system that has to work flawlessly, without rest, for your entire life — and it does, roughly three billion beats.

</Step>

<Step title="Tracing a full circuit" kind="interactive">

Follow one drop of blood all the way around. Remember: through the heart **twice**.

<HeartPump />

**Body** → vena cava → **right atrium** → right ventricle → pulmonary artery → **lungs** (picks up O₂, drops CO₂) → pulmonary vein → **left atrium** → left ventricle → aorta → **body** (drops O₂, picks up CO₂) → and round again. Two loops, one continuous flow.

</Step>

<Step title="The cardiac cycle" kind="explain">

A single heartbeat is a precise sequence — the **cardiac cycle** — with valves opening and closing to keep blood moving one way:

1. The **atria** contract, squeezing blood down into the ventricles.
2. The **ventricles** contract, forcing blood out to the lungs and body. The valves between atria and ventricles snap shut (the "lub" sound).
3. The heart **relaxes** and refills; the valves to the arteries close (the "dub" sound).

Those one-way **valves** are essential — they're why blood can't flow backwards, and a faulty valve causes a heart "murmur".

</Step>

<Step title="Keeping the blood healthy" kind="interactive">

The blood flowing through it all is itself finely organised. Revisit what it carries and the vessels it runs in.

<BloodViewer />

Every component matters: lose red cells and you can't carry oxygen (anaemia); lose platelets and you can't clot; damage the vessels and the whole delivery network suffers.

</Step>

<Step title="Control: matching supply to demand" kind="explain">

Your needs change constantly — resting, sprinting, frightened — and the system adjusts:

- **Heart rate** speeds up (driven by nerves and the hormone **adrenaline**) to pump more blood during exercise or stress.
- **Vessels widen or narrow** to send more blood where it's needed — to muscles during exercise, to the skin to lose heat.

This is the cardiovascular system doing **homeostasis** — keeping every cell supplied no matter what you're doing. It's why your pulse races when you run and calms when you rest.

</Step>

<Step title="When it goes wrong" kind="explain">

Because it's so vital, cardiovascular disease is a leading cause of death — and much of it is preventable:

- **Atherosclerosis** — fatty deposits narrow the arteries.
- **Heart attack** — a coronary artery (feeding the heart muscle itself) gets blocked, starving part of the heart of oxygen.
- **Stroke** — the same blockage problem, but in the brain.

The risk factors are familiar: a diet high in saturated fat, smoking, high blood pressure, and lack of exercise. Looking after the cardiovascular system is one of the most powerful things you can do for your health — a direct, personal payoff from understanding the biology.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- The **cardiovascular system** = heart + vessels + blood, working as one integrated transport network.
- The **cardiac cycle**: atria contract → ventricles contract → heart relaxes, with one-way **valves** preventing backflow.
- The system is **controlled** (heart rate, vessel width) to match supply to demand — a form of **homeostasis**.
- **Cardiovascular disease** (atherosclerosis, heart attack, stroke) is a major, largely preventable cause of death.

</Step>
