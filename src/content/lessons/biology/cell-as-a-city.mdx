<Lesson>

<Step title="A city that runs itself" kind="explain">

We've met the organelles one by one. Now let's see them work as a **whole** — because a living cell isn't a parts list, it's a functioning city, with a government, power plants, factories, a postal service, walls, and waste disposal, all coordinated.

And buried in that city is one of the strangest stories in biology: two of its power plants were once **independent living creatures** that moved in and never left.

</Step>

<Step title="Tour the city" kind="interactive">

Run through the map once more, this time thinking of each organelle as part of a city.

<CellViewer />

- **Nucleus** = city hall (keeps the master plans, gives orders)
- **Ribosomes / rough ER** = factories (build proteins)
- **Golgi** = post office (packages and ships)
- **Mitochondria** = power plants
- **Membrane** = the border and customs
- **Cytoskeleton** = roads and scaffolding

</Step>

<Step title="A suspicious power plant" kind="explain">

Look closely at the mitochondria and you find clues that don't fit the rest of the cell. A mitochondrion has:

- its **own** loop of DNA — separate from the nucleus,
- its **own** ribosomes, of the *bacterial* type,
- a **double** membrane, as if it had been wrapped in a second one,
- and it **divides on its own**, by splitting in two like a bacterium.

Why would a mere organelle behave so much like an independent cell? Because it once *was* one.

</Step>

<Step title="The endosymbiosis story" kind="interactive">

This is the theory of **endosymbiosis**, championed by biologist Lynn Margulis. Step through how it happened.

<Endosymbiosis />

Around two billion years ago, a host cell engulfed a free-living bacterium — and instead of digesting it, struck a deal. The bacterium supplied energy; the host supplied shelter and food. Over time, neither could live without the other.

</Step>

<Step title="The evidence stacks up" kind="explain">

Endosymbiosis sounds wild, but the evidence is overwhelming — and it's exactly the list of "suspicious" features:

- Mitochondria and chloroplasts have **circular DNA**, just like bacteria.
- Their **ribosomes** resemble bacterial ribosomes, not the host's.
- They're wrapped in **two membranes** — the inner one theirs, the outer one from being swallowed.
- They **reproduce by binary fission**, independently of the cell.
- Their DNA sequences place them firmly among the bacteria on the tree of life.

<Callout type="info" title="Two separate mergers">
Mitochondria came from an aerobic bacterium — every eukaryote has them. Chloroplasts came **later**, from a photosynthetic cyanobacterium swallowed by one lineage. That lineage became the ancestor of all plants and algae.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Why it mattered so much" kind="explain">

This wasn't a minor upgrade — it may be *the* reason complex life exists. A bacterium powers itself across its outer membrane, which limits how big and gene-rich it can get.

By packing in many mitochondria, the eukaryotic cell gained an enormous **energy surplus**. That surplus paid for bigger genomes, more proteins, and ultimately the complexity that led to plants, animals, and you. Complex life is, in a real sense, **powered by ancient captured bacteria**.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- The cell works as an integrated **city** of cooperating organelles.
- **Mitochondria** (and **chloroplasts**) were once free-living bacteria, taken in by a host cell — the theory of **endosymbiosis**.
- The evidence: their own circular DNA, bacterial ribosomes, double membranes, and independent division.
- This merger gave eukaryotes an **energy surplus** that made complex, multicellular life possible.

</Step>
