<Lesson>

<Step title="Training the immune system" kind="explain">

The immune system's memory has one drawback: you usually have to **catch** a disease — and survive it — to become immune. For dangerous diseases, that's a terrible price.

**Vaccination** is the brilliant workaround: it trains your immune system to recognise a pathogen **without** the danger of the real disease. Vaccines have saved more lives than almost any other medical advance, and wiped out smallpox entirely.

</Step>

<Step title="How a vaccine works" kind="interactive">

A vaccine contains a **harmless version** of the pathogen — dead, weakened, or just its antigens. Your immune system responds as if it were the real thing, making antibodies and, crucially, **memory cells** — but you don't get ill. Compare what happens later with and without it.

<Vaccination />

The payoff comes on the *second* exposure: thanks to the memory cells, the real pathogen triggers a **fast, massive** response and is destroyed before it can make you sick.

</Step>

<Step title="Primary and secondary responses" kind="explain">

The graph shows the key idea — two very different responses:

- **Primary response** (first exposure / the vaccine): slow and small. It takes days to ramp up, which is long enough to feel ill from a real infection.
- **Secondary response** (later exposure): fast and huge, because memory cells are already primed. The pathogen is wiped out before symptoms appear — you are **immune**.

Vaccinate enough of a population and you also get **herd immunity** (from the last lesson), protecting those who can't be vaccinated. This is why vaccination programmes target whole communities, not just individuals.

<Callout type="tip" title="Why some need boosters">
For some diseases, memory cells fade over years, so a **booster** top-up re-primes the immune system. For others (like measles), immunity lasts a lifetime.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Vaccination** trains immunity using a **harmless version** of a pathogen — no real disease needed.
- It produces antibodies and, crucially, **memory cells**.
- The later **secondary response** is fast and large (vs the slow, small **primary response**) — so you don't get ill.
- Wide vaccination gives **herd immunity**; some diseases need **boosters**.

</Step>
