<Step title="Too big to use" kind="explain">

Here's the problem with food: the molecules in it — starch, protein, fat — are **too large** to pass through your gut wall into your blood. Before your body can use a meal, it has to break those big molecules down into small, soluble ones.

That's **digestion**: a journey through a long muscular tube where food is physically broken up and chemically dismantled by **enzymes**.

</Step>

The big molecules are broken into small ones — starch into glucose, protein into amino acids, fat into fatty acids — each by its own kind of **enzyme**, made in the mouth, stomach, pancreas, and small intestine.

</Step>

<Step title="Break down, then absorb" kind="explain">

Two things have to happen, in order:

1. **Digestion** — enzymes break large insoluble molecules into small soluble ones.
   - **Carbohydrases** (like amylase) → break starch into glucose.
   - **Proteases** → break protein into amino acids.
   - **Lipases** → break fats into fatty acids and glycerol (helped by **bile**, which emulsifies fat into tiny droplets).
2. **Absorption** — in the **small intestine**, those small molecules pass into the blood through millions of tiny finger-like **villi**, which give a huge surface area.

Finally, the **large intestine** absorbs the water, leaving solid waste.

<Callout type="info" title="Built for absorption">
The small intestine is several metres long and lined with villi (themselves covered in microvilli). All that folding creates an enormous surface area — exactly the surface-area trick you met earlier — to absorb nutrients fast.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Digestion** breaks large insoluble food molecules into small soluble ones, using **enzymes**.
- Carbohydrases → glucose; proteases → amino acids; lipases (+ **bile**) → fatty acids.
- **Absorption** happens in the **small intestine** through **villi** (huge surface area).
- The **large intestine** absorbs water, leaving solid waste.
