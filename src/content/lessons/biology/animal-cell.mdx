<Step title="A factory in miniature" kind="explain">

A single one of your cells is busier than a city. Inside that microscopic bag of jelly, thousands of chemical reactions run every second — building proteins, releasing energy, exporting products, recycling waste.

How does it stay organised? With **organelles** — specialised compartments, each doing one job. Let's take the tour.

</Step>

Find the **nucleus** (the control centre), the sausage-shaped **mitochondria** (power), the **rough ER** dotted with ribosomes (protein-building), and the **Golgi** (packaging).

</Step>

<Step title="Division of labour" kind="explain">

The genius of the cell is **division of labour**. No single part does everything; each organelle specialises, and together they keep the cell alive:

- **Nucleus** — stores DNA, gives the orders.
- **Ribosomes & rough ER** — build proteins.
- **Golgi apparatus** — packages and ships them.
- **Mitochondria** — release energy by respiration.
- **Cell membrane** — controls what comes in and out.

<Callout type="info" title="Cells with extra demand">
A muscle cell, working hard, is packed with mitochondria. A cell that secretes lots of protein has tons of rough ER. The mix of organelles reveals the cell's job.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- An animal cell is an organised **factory** of specialised **organelles**.
- Key parts: **nucleus** (control), **mitochondria** (energy), **ribosomes/rough ER** (build proteins), **Golgi** (package/ship), **membrane** (border control).
- **Division of labour** keeps it all running — and a cell's organelle mix reflects its job.

</Step>
