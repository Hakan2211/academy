<Lesson>

<Step title="Why DNA must be copied" kind="explain">

Every time a cell divides, both new cells need a **complete, identical** copy of the DNA. You started as one cell and now have trillions, each carrying the same genome. That means your DNA has been copied an astronomical number of times — with remarkable accuracy.

The double-helix structure makes it possible. Because the two strands are complementary, each one carries all the information needed to rebuild its partner.

</Step>

<Step title="Unzip and copy" kind="interactive">

Replication works by unzipping the helix and building a new partner for each old strand.

<DNAReplication />

1. The two strands **separate**, like a zip opening.
2. Free **nucleotides** float in and pair against each exposed strand by the base-pairing rule (A–T, C–G).
3. The result is **two** complete double helices, each identical to the original.

</Step>

<Step title="Semi-conservative copying" kind="explain">

Notice the clever detail: each new helix is made of **one old strand and one new one**. The original is never thrown away — it's split and reused as the template.

Biologists call this **semi-conservative** replication ("half-kept"). It has a built-in advantage: by always copying from a proven original, errors are kept rare. Special enzymes also **proofread** the new strand, fixing most mistakes — so the copy is faithful to about one error in a billion bases.

<Callout type="tip" title="Mistakes matter">
The few errors that slip through are **mutations** — changes to the DNA. Most are harmless, but they're also the raw material of variation and evolution, as a later world explores.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- DNA is **copied** before a cell divides, so each new cell gets a full set.
- The helix **unzips**, and free nucleotides pair against each strand (A–T, C–G).
- Copying is **semi-conservative**: each new helix keeps one old strand + one new one.
- Proofreading makes it extremely accurate; rare errors are **mutations**.

</Step>
