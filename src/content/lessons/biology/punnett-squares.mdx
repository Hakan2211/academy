<Step title="Predicting the odds" kind="explain">

Once you know the parents' alleles, you can **predict** the likely offspring — and the tool for it is the **Punnett square**, a simple grid invented by Reginald Punnett.

It works because of meiosis: each parent passes on just **one** of its two alleles, at random, to each gamete. The square lays out every possible combination of parent gametes.

</Step>

<Step title="Building a Punnett square" kind="interactive">

Put one parent's alleles along the top, the other's down the side. Each box combines one allele from each — a possible offspring. Click the alleles to change the parents and watch the predictions update.

<PunnettSquare parent1={["B","b"]} parent2={["B","b"]} />

Try **Bb × bb**: now half the offspring show the recessive trait (a 1:1 ratio). Try **BB × bb**: every child is **Bb** — all show the dominant trait, but all carry the recessive allele.

</Step>

<Step title="It's all probability" kind="explain">

A crucial caution: a Punnett square gives **probabilities**, not guarantees. A 3:1 ratio means each offspring has a **3-in-4 chance** of the dominant trait — like flipping a coin, not filling a quota.

- With four offspring you might get 4 purple and 0 white — chance is lumpy in small numbers.
- Over **hundreds** of offspring, the real ratio settles close to the predicted 3:1.

This is exactly why Mendel counted *thousands* of plants — only large numbers reveal the underlying ratio clearly.

<Callout type="tip" title="Each birth is independent">
Having one recessive child doesn't make the next one more or less likely to be recessive. Every offspring is a fresh roll of the dice.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- A **Punnett square** predicts offspring by combining one allele from each parent in a grid.
- It works because each parent passes **one random allele** per gamete (from meiosis).
- It gives **probabilities**, not certainties — ratios only emerge clearly over **large numbers**.
- Each offspring is an **independent** event.

</Step>
