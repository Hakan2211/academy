<Step title="The body's delivery network" kind="explain">

Once food is absorbed and oxygen breathed in, they have to reach **every** one of your trillions of cells. That's the job of the **circulatory system**: a network of vessels with a tireless pump at its centre — the **heart**.

Your heart beats around 100,000 times a day, pushing blood on a continuous loop that delivers oxygen and nutrients and carries waste away.

</Step>

<Step title="A double pump" kind="interactive">

The heart isn't one pump but **two**, side by side. Watch it beat and follow the blood.

<HeartPump />

- The **right** side (blue) receives **deoxygenated** blood from the body and pumps it to the **lungs**.
- The **left** side (red) receives **oxygenated** blood from the lungs and pumps it to the **body**.

Blood therefore passes through the heart **twice** per full circuit — this is **double circulation**.

</Step>

<Step title="Why double is better" kind="explain">

Why bother with two loops? Because it lets blood be pumped at **high pressure** to the body without damaging the delicate lungs:

- The **pulmonary** circuit (heart → lungs → heart) runs at gentle pressure, suited to the fragile alveoli.
- The **systemic** circuit (heart → body → heart) runs at high pressure, to force blood all the way to your toes and brain.

The heart's left side is more muscular than the right, precisely because it has the bigger job of pushing blood around the whole body.

<Callout type="info" title="The heart feeds itself">
The heart muscle has its own blood supply — the coronary arteries. If one gets blocked, that part of the muscle is starved of oxygen: a heart attack.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- The **heart** pumps blood through the **circulatory system** to every cell.
- It's a **double pump**: the **right** side sends blood to the **lungs**, the **left** side to the **body**.
- **Double circulation** = blood passes through the heart twice per loop (pulmonary + systemic).
- The left side is more muscular because it pumps to the whole body at high pressure.

</Step>
