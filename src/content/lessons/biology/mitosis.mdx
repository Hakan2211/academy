<Step title="Making an exact copy" kind="explain">

When your body grows or heals a wound, it needs **more** cells — and every one must be a perfect copy of the original, with a full, identical set of chromosomes. The process that achieves this is **mitosis**: one cell dividing into two genetically identical daughter cells.

Mitosis is the engine of **growth, repair,** and (in many organisms) **asexual reproduction**.

</Step>

<Step title="The stages of mitosis" kind="interactive">

Step through the process. Watch the copied chromosomes condense, line up, and split.

<Mitosis />

The sequence is **PMAT**: **P**rophase (condense), **M**etaphase (line up), **A**naphase (split apart), **T**elophase (two nuclei) — followed by **cytokinesis**, where the cell pinches in two.

</Step>

<Step title="Why the copies are identical" kind="explain">

The key is what happened *before* mitosis. During interphase, the DNA was copied, so each chromosome became two identical **sister chromatids** joined together.

In mitosis, those sisters are pulled **apart** — one into each new cell. Because the sisters were exact copies, each daughter cell ends up with a complete, identical set of chromosomes (in humans, 46).

<Callout type="tip" title="Same number, every time">
Mitosis keeps the chromosome number constant. A human body cell has 46 chromosomes; after mitosis, both daughter cells still have 46. Nothing is gained or lost.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Mitosis** makes two **genetically identical** daughter cells — for growth, repair, and asexual reproduction.
- Stages: **prophase → metaphase → anaphase → telophase**, then **cytokinesis**.
- Copied **sister chromatids** are pulled apart, one set into each cell.
- The chromosome number stays the **same** (46 → 46 in humans).

</Step>
