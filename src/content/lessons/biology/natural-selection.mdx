<Step title="Darwin's big idea" kind="explain">

In 1859, Charles Darwin published an idea so simple it's almost obvious in hindsight — yet it explains the entire diversity of life. It's called **natural selection**, and it follows from a few plain facts:

1. Organisms produce **more offspring** than can survive.
2. Those offspring **vary**.
3. Some variations **survive and reproduce** better than others.
4. Those helpful variations are **inherited** by the next generation.

Put together, they have an inevitable result.

</Step>

No one designed this. The environment simply lets some variants leave more offspring — and over generations the population **shifts**. That's natural selection.

</Step>

<Step title="The logic, step by step" kind="explain">

Follow the chain of cause and effect:

- More beetles are born than can survive (**overproduction**).
- They vary in colour (**variation**).
- On dark soil, pale beetles get eaten; dark ones survive (**selection pressure**).
- Survivors breed and pass on the "dark" allele (**inheritance**).
- Next generation has more dark beetles. Repeat for thousands of generations → the population is transformed.

"Fittest" doesn't mean strongest — it means **best suited to the environment**. A change in the environment (the background) instantly changes which variant is fittest.

<Callout type="tip" title="Selection has no goal">
Natural selection isn't trying to make beetles "better" or aiming at anything. It just preserves whatever happens to survive and reproduce in the current conditions. Change the conditions and the "right" answer changes.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Natural selection**: overproduction + variation + a selection pressure + inheritance → the population changes over generations.
- The **best-suited** variants survive and reproduce more, passing on their helpful alleles.
- "**Fittest**" means best adapted to the *current* environment — not strongest.
- Selection has **no goal**; it simply preserves what survives.

</Step>
