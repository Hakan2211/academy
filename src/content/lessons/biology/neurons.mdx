<Step title="The cell that carries a signal" kind="explain">

The messages of the nervous system travel along special cells called **neurons** (nerve cells). They're shaped like nothing else in the body — some are over a **metre** long, stretching from your spinal cord all the way to your toe — built entirely to carry signals fast and far.

</Step>

<Step title="Anatomy of a neuron" kind="interactive">

<Neuron />

Signals come in through the branched **dendrites**, are summed in the **cell body**, then shoot down the long **axon** to the **synapse**, where they pass to the next cell. The fatty **myelin sheath** insulates the axon to speed the impulse up.

</Step>

<Step title="Crossing the gap" kind="explain">

A neuron's impulse is electrical, but neurons don't quite touch. Between one neuron and the next is a tiny gap — the **synapse**. So how does the signal cross?

It switches from electrical to **chemical**. When the impulse reaches the end of the axon, it triggers the release of chemical messengers called **neurotransmitters**, which diffuse across the gap and set off a new impulse in the next neuron.

This chemical step is more than a detail — it's where many **drugs and poisons** act (and where your brain does much of its computing). Caffeine, nicotine, antidepressants, and nerve agents all work by altering what happens at the synapse.

<Callout type="info" title="Why myelin matters">
The myelin sheath lets the impulse effectively jump along the axon, hugely speeding it up. In multiple sclerosis, myelin is damaged — and signals slow or fail, causing weakness and numbness.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Neurons** carry nerve impulses; they can be very long and are built for fast signalling.
- Parts: **dendrites** (receive), **cell body** (nucleus), **axon** (carries impulse), **myelin** (insulates/speeds), **synapse** (passes on).
- The impulse is **electrical** along the axon but crosses the **synapse** as **chemical neurotransmitters**.
- The synapse is where many drugs and poisons act.

</Step>
