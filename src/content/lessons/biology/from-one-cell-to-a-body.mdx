<Step title="The deepest puzzle in development" kind="explain">

Here is one of biology's most astonishing facts. You began as a **single cell**. That cell divided into the ~37 trillion cells of your body — and yet they are wildly different: nerve cells, muscle cells, blood cells, skin cells.

But every one of those cells carries the **same DNA**. So how does one cell, copying its identical genome over and over, build a brain, a heart, and a hand? This deep-dive tackles that question — **differentiation** — and the remarkable cells at its heart.

</Step>

<Step title="The journey, revisited" kind="interactive">

Step through development once more, this time watching for the moment cells start to *differ*.

<Development />

Early on, the cells are all the same — a plain ball of identical cells. Then, around the embryo stage, they begin to **specialise** into different types. That transformation is the real magic of development.

</Step>

<Step title="Same genes, different cells" kind="explain">

The answer is **gene regulation** — exactly the idea from the DNA world. Every cell has the full genome, but each switches on a **different set of genes**:

- A muscle cell switches on the genes for muscle proteins (myosin) and stays silent on the rest.
- A nerve cell switches on the genes for ion channels and neurotransmitters.

This switching-on of a specific gene set, turning an unspecialised cell into a particular type, is **differentiation**. It's controlled by signals between cells — neighbours telling each other what to become, based on their position in the embryo.

</Step>

<Step title="Stem cells: the unspecialised ones" kind="explain">

At the start, the cells haven't chosen yet — they're **stem cells**, with the power to become *any* type. As development proceeds, they commit to their fates one by one.

There are two important kinds:

- **Embryonic stem cells** — found in the early embryo, able to become **any** cell type in the body (the ultimate flexibility).
- **Adult stem cells** — kept in tissues (like bone marrow) throughout life, able to become a **limited** range of types, to repair and replace cells.

This is why adult stem cells in your bone marrow can keep churning out new blood cells your whole life.

</Step>

<Step title="The promise — and the debate" kind="explain">

Because stem cells can become other cell types, they hold huge **medical promise**. If we can direct them, we might:

- grow replacement tissue for damaged organs,
- repair a spinal injury or a failing heart,
- treat diseases like diabetes (growing new insulin cells) or Parkinson's.

Embryonic stem cells are the most powerful, but using them raises **ethical questions** because they come from embryos. This drove a major breakthrough: scientists learned to reprogram ordinary adult cells *back* into stem-like cells — sidestepping the ethical problem and winning a Nobel Prize. It's one of the most active frontiers in biology and medicine.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- One cell (the zygote) builds a whole body of trillions of different cells — all sharing the **same DNA**.
- **Differentiation** turns unspecialised cells into specific types by **gene regulation** (switching on different gene sets), guided by signals from neighbouring cells.
- **Stem cells** are unspecialised: **embryonic** (can become any type) and **adult** (a limited range, for repair).
- Stem cells hold great **medical promise** (growing replacement tissue) alongside ethical debate — a leading frontier of biology.

</Step>
