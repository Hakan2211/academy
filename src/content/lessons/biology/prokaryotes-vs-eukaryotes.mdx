<Lesson>

<Step title="Two ways to build a cell" kind="explain">

All cells share the basics — a membrane, cytoplasm, DNA, and ribosomes. But there are exactly **two fundamental designs**, and the difference comes down to a single feature: **does the cell keep its DNA in a nucleus?**

- **Prokaryotes** (bacteria and archaea) do *not*. Their DNA floats loose.
- **Eukaryotes** (animals, plants, fungi, protists) *do*. Their DNA is sealed inside a nucleus, and they pack in many other organelles too.

</Step>

The headline: prokaryotes are small, simple, and ancient; eukaryotes are large, compartmentalised, and came later. Every cell in your body is a eukaryote.

</Step>

<Step title="Why the nucleus matters" kind="explain">

Wrapping the DNA in its own membrane was a turning point. It let eukaryotic cells grow far **bigger** and divide their work among specialised compartments — like a workshop separating into distinct rooms.

That compartmentalisation made complex, multicellular life possible. A bacterium does everything in one open space; your cells run dozens of separate chemical "factories" at once.

<Callout type="tip" title="Don't confuse 'simple' with 'unsuccessful'">
Prokaryotes are anything but failures — they outnumber every other cell on Earth and thrive everywhere from boiling vents to your gut. Simple is a winning strategy.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- There are **two** cell designs, divided by one feature: the **nucleus**.
- **Prokaryotes** (bacteria, archaea): small, no nucleus, DNA in a loop, no membrane-bound organelles.
- **Eukaryotes** (animals, plants, fungi, protists): large, nucleus-bound DNA, many organelles.
- The nucleus and compartments let eukaryotes grow big and complex — but prokaryotes remain wildly successful.

</Step>
