<Step title="Making food from light" kind="explain">

Almost all life on Earth ultimately runs on sunlight — and the machine that captures it is the green plant. In **photosynthesis**, plants take three cheap, abundant ingredients — **light, water, and carbon dioxide** — and build **glucose**, releasing **oxygen** as a by-product.

This single process feeds nearly the entire living world and fills the air with the oxygen you breathe. It happens in the **chloroplasts**, using the green pigment **chlorophyll** to absorb the light.

</Step>

The word equation is:

<Formula tex="\text{carbon dioxide} + \text{water} \xrightarrow{\text{light}} \text{glucose} + \text{oxygen}" block />

More light speeds it up — but only so far.

</Step>

<Step title="Limiting factors" kind="explain">

Photosynthesis needs several things at once, and the one in **shortest supply** caps the rate — it's the **limiting factor**:

- **Light intensity** — no light, no reaction; more light, faster (up to a point).
- **Carbon dioxide concentration** — often the limiting factor on a bright day.
- **Temperature** — the reactions are enzyme-controlled, so they slow in the cold and stop if too hot (denaturation).

Raise the limiting factor and the rate climbs until something *else* becomes limiting. Greenhouse growers exploit this, adding light, warmth, and CO₂ to push photosynthesis — and crop yields — higher.

<Callout type="info" title="Where your food comes from">
The glucose a plant makes becomes starch, fruit, and the body of the plant — and the energy in every meal you eat traces back to photosynthesis.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Photosynthesis**: carbon dioxide + water → glucose + oxygen, powered by **light** in the **chloroplasts**.
- It feeds the living world and produces atmospheric oxygen.
- Its rate is set by the **limiting factor** — usually **light, CO₂,** or **temperature** — whichever is in shortest supply.

</Step>
