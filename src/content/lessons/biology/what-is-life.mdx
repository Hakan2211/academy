<Lesson>

<Step title="Alive or not?" kind="explain">

A candle flame **moves**, it **grows**, it **consumes** fuel, it gives off **waste** gases, and it can even **spread** to a new wick. By a quick glance it ticks a lot of boxes — yet nothing about a flame is alive.

So what *is*? Biology is the study of living things, which means it needs a sharp answer to a deceptively hard question: what separates a living thing from a candle, a crystal, or a river?

Biologists settled on a checklist. To count as alive, something has to do **seven** things — all of them, all the time.

</Step>

<Step title="The seven signs of life" kind="interactive">

Tap each letter to see what it means. Together they spell a phrase biology students never forget: **MRS GREN**.

<MrsGren />

A flame manages a few of these, but never all seven — it can't sense its surroundings, and it certainly can't reproduce true copies of itself. A living thing does every one.

</Step>

<Step title="The blurry edge" kind="explain">

The checklist is powerful, but nature loves to sit on the fence.

- A **virus** carries genes and reproduces — but only by hijacking a living cell. On its own it does nothing at all. Most biologists say it isn't alive.
- A dry **seed** can sit for decades doing almost nothing, then spring to life with water.
- A **mule** is clearly alive but can't reproduce.

<Callout type="tip" title="The real lesson">
"Alive" isn't a magic spark — it's a *bundle of processes*. Something is living when it actively does all seven, using energy to stay organised against a universe that tends toward disorder.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

Living things are defined not by one feature but by a whole set of processes:

- **M**ovement, **R**espiration, **S**ensitivity, **G**rowth, **R**eproduction, **E**xcretion, **N**utrition.
- A candle or a car mimics a few — only living things do all seven.
- The edges are fuzzy (viruses, seeds), which is exactly why biology needs a clear, shared definition to build on.

</Step>
