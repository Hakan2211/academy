<Step title="The cell's living skin" kind="explain">

Every cell is wrapped in a membrane just two molecules thick — a film so thin that 10,000 of them stacked up would barely reach the width of a hair. Yet this flimsy-sounding layer is one of the cell's most important structures. It decides **what gets in and what stays out**.

Get this border wrong and the cell dies. So how does something so thin do something so vital? The secret is what it's made of.

</Step>

<Step title="The fluid mosaic" kind="interactive">

The membrane is a double layer of fat molecules — **phospholipids** — dotted with proteins, like tiles in a drifting mosaic. Click each part to explore it.

<FluidMosaic />

Each phospholipid has a water-loving **head** and two water-hating **tails**. In water they line up automatically, tails tucked inward, forming a self-sealing sheet. It's "fluid" because the molecules constantly drift past one another.

</Step>

<Step title="A selective barrier" kind="explain">

That oily core is the key to control. It lets some things slip straight through and blocks others completely:

- **Pass freely:** small, oily (non-polar) molecules like oxygen and carbon dioxide.
- **Blocked by the oily core:** charged ions and large molecules like glucose — they need a **protein** doorway.

This selectivity is why we call the membrane **partially permeable**. The rest of this world is the story of the different ways things cross it.

<Callout type="info" title="Why 'partially permeable' matters">
A fully open border couldn't keep a cell organised; a sealed one would starve it. "Partially permeable" is the sweet spot — and the proteins decide the exceptions.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- The membrane is a **phospholipid bilayer** — heads out, tails in — studded with proteins: the **fluid mosaic**.
- It is **partially permeable**: small non-polar molecules pass freely; ions and large molecules need protein help.
- This selective control of what crosses is the membrane's central job.
