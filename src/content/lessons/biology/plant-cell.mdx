<Step title="The same kit, plus extras" kind="explain">

A plant cell has everything an animal cell has — nucleus, mitochondria, ribosomes, Golgi, membrane. Then it adds **three features of its own** that suit a life rooted in one spot, making food from sunlight.

Those extras are what let plants stand tall, store water, and feed the rest of the living world.

</Step>

<Step title="Explore a plant cell" kind="interactive">

Notice the thick **cell wall** around the outside, the big central **vacuole** pushing everything to the edges, and the green **chloroplasts**.

</Step>

<Step title="Three plant-only parts" kind="explain">

- **Cell wall** — a tough layer of cellulose outside the membrane. It gives the cell a fixed shape and the strength to hold a plant upright.
- **Vacuole** — a large sac of cell sap. Filled with water, it presses outward and keeps the cell firm (**turgid**). When plants wilt, their vacuoles have lost water.
- **Chloroplasts** — green organelles packed with chlorophyll that capture sunlight to make sugar in **photosynthesis**.

<Callout type="tip" title="Why plants are green">
Chloroplasts are full of the pigment **chlorophyll**, which absorbs red and blue light but reflects green — so that's the colour that reaches your eye.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- Plant cells have all the organelles animal cells do, **plus three extras**.
- **Cell wall** (cellulose) — shape and support.
- **Vacuole** — water store that keeps the cell firm (turgid).
- **Chloroplasts** — capture sunlight for photosynthesis, and make plants green.

</Step>
