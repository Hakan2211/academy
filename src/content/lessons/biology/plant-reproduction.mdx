<Step title="Sex, for plants" kind="explain">

Plants reproduce sexually too — and the **flower** is their reproductive organ. It makes the gametes, brings them together, and produces the seeds that become the next generation. Bright petals and sweet scents aren't decoration; they're tools for getting pollen from one flower to another.

</Step>

A flower has **male** parts — the **stamens** (anther + filament), which make pollen — and **female** parts — the **carpel** (stigma, style, and **ovary** containing ovules). The **petals** attract pollinators; the **sepals** protected the bud.

</Step>

<Step title="Pollination, fertilisation, and seeds" kind="explain">

Plant reproduction happens in a clear sequence:

1. **Pollination** — pollen is carried from an anther to a stigma, usually by **insects** (drawn by petals and nectar) or by **wind**.
2. **Fertilisation** — the pollen grows a tube down the style to an ovule, and the male gamete fuses with the female gamete.
3. **Seeds and fruit** — each fertilised ovule becomes a **seed**, and the ovary swells into a **fruit** around them.
4. **Dispersal** — fruits and seeds are spread away from the parent (by animals eating fruit, by wind, or by bursting pods) so the seedlings don't compete with the parent.

<Callout type="info" title="Why move the pollen?">
Carrying pollen between *different* flowers (cross-pollination) mixes genes from two plants — creating variation, just like sexual reproduction in animals. That's the whole point of the flower's elaborate machinery.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- The **flower** is the plant's reproductive organ: **stamens** (male, make pollen) and **carpel** (female, with the **ovary**).
- **Pollination** moves pollen (by insects or wind) → **fertilisation** → **seeds** (from ovules) and **fruit** (from the ovary).
- **Seed dispersal** spreads offspring away from the parent.
- Cross-pollination creates **variation**, the purpose of all the flower's machinery.

</Step>
