<Step title="A living planet, overflowing" kind="explain">

Life on Earth comes in a staggering range — from bacteria too small to see, to fungi that stretch for kilometres underground, to whales the size of buildings. Biologists have named about **1.5 million** species and estimate there are around **8.7 million** in total. Most have never been described.

Faced with that flood, the first job of biology is to **organise**. We sort all of life into a few great groups that share a deep ancestry — and the very top level is the **domain**.

</Step>

<Step title="The three domains" kind="interactive">

Every living thing belongs to one of three domains. Click a branch to explore it — the highlighted path traces it back to the common ancestor of all life.

<TreeOfLife />

Notice the surprise: the two domains of tiny, nucleus-free cells — **Bacteria** and **Archaea** — are as different from each other as either is from *you*. All the visible life you know (plants, animals, fungi) is crammed into a corner of the third domain, **Eukarya**.

</Step>

<Step title="Kingdoms within Eukarya" kind="explain">

Eukaryotes — cells *with* a nucleus — split further into **kingdoms**:

- **Animals** — multicellular, eat other organisms, usually move.
- **Plants** — multicellular, make food from sunlight.
- **Fungi** — absorb their food; the great decomposers.
- **Protists** — a catch-all of mostly single-celled eukaryotes like amoebas and algae.

<Callout type="info" title="The nested boxes">
Each level sits inside the last: domain → kingdom → phylum → class → order → family → genus → species. It's one giant set of nested boxes, narrowing from "all life" down to a single kind of organism.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- Life is enormously diverse — millions of species, most still unnamed.
- We organise it into a nested hierarchy, topped by **three domains**: Bacteria, Archaea, and Eukarya.
- Eukarya contains the familiar **kingdoms**: animals, plants, fungi, and protists.
- Appearances deceive — true grouping follows shared ancestry, not surface looks.

</Step>
