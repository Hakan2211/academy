<Step title="Too big to fit through a door" kind="explain">

Channels and pumps handle single molecules. But sometimes a cell needs to move something **enormous** — a whole protein, a clump of nutrients, even an entire bacterium. None of those will fit through a protein doorway.

The solution is to wrap the cargo in a bubble of membrane and move the whole package. This is **bulk transport**, and it comes in two directions: out (**exocytosis**) and in (**endocytosis**).

</Step>

<Step title="In and out by vesicle" kind="interactive">

Toggle between the two modes and watch a membrane bubble — a **vesicle** — do the work.

<BulkTransport />

- **Exocytosis:** a vesicle inside the cell travels to the membrane, fuses with it, and dumps its contents **outside**.
- **Endocytosis:** the membrane folds inward around outside material and pinches off a vesicle **inside**.

Both need energy (ATP), because reshaping the membrane is work.

</Step>

<Step title="Where your body uses it" kind="explain">

Bulk transport runs constantly inside you:

- **Exocytosis** secretes things: nerve cells release neurotransmitters, gland cells release **insulin** and digestive enzymes.
- **Endocytosis** takes things in: your **white blood cells** engulf and destroy invading bacteria whole — a special case called **phagocytosis** ("cell eating").

<Callout type="tip" title="Cell eating">
When a white blood cell swallows a bacterium, it's doing endocytosis on a grand scale. The captured microbe ends up trapped in a vesicle, where the cell digests it.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Bulk transport** moves cargo too large for protein doorways, by wrapping it in a membrane **vesicle**. It needs ATP.
- **Exocytosis** = releasing material **out** (secreting insulin, enzymes, neurotransmitters).
- **Endocytosis** = taking material **in** (white blood cells engulfing bacteria = phagocytosis).

</Step>
