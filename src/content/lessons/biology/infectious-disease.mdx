<Step title="Catching a disease" kind="explain">

A **pathogen** is any microbe that causes disease — a harmful bacterium, virus, fungus, or protist. A disease that can spread from one person to another is **infectious** (or **communicable**), and understanding *how* it spreads is the key to stopping it.

Pathogens make you ill in two main ways: by **damaging your cells** directly, or by releasing **toxins** (poisons) that upset how your body works.

</Step>

<Step title="How pathogens travel" kind="explain">

Pathogens spread by several routes, and each suggests a way to block it:

- **Through the air** — in droplets when you cough or sneeze (flu, colds, COVID). → cover your mouth, ventilate rooms.
- **In water or food** — contaminated supplies (cholera, food poisoning). → clean water, cook food, wash hands.
- **By direct contact** — touching skin or surfaces (athlete's foot). → hygiene, clean surfaces.
- **By vectors** — animals that carry pathogens, like mosquitoes for malaria. → nets, repellents, controlling the vector.

</Step>

With nobody protected, the disease tears through everyone. But vaccinate enough people and infected individuals run out of neighbours to infect — the outbreak fizzles out. This is **herd immunity**: protecting even the unvaccinated by leaving the pathogen nowhere to spread.

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- A **pathogen** causes disease; an **infectious** disease spreads between people.
- Pathogens harm you by damaging cells or releasing **toxins**.
- Spread routes: **air** (droplets), **water/food**, **contact**, and **vectors** (e.g. mosquitoes) — each blockable by hygiene, clean water, or vaccination.
- **Herd immunity**: enough immune people stop the spread, protecting even the unvaccinated.

</Step>
