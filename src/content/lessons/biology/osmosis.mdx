<Step title="The special case of water" kind="explain">

Water is so important to cells that its movement gets its own name: **osmosis**.

Osmosis is the diffusion of **water** across a partially permeable membrane, from a **dilute** solution (lots of water) to a **concentrated** one (little water). The water moves to dilute the side that has more dissolved stuff — evening out the concentration, just like diffusion, but it's only the water that crosses.

</Step>

<Step title="Swell, hold, or shrink" kind="interactive">

A cell's fate depends on the solution around it. Drag the outside solute concentration and watch water flow — and switch between an animal and a plant cell.

<OsmosisLab />

- **Pure water outside (hypotonic):** water rushes in — the cell swells.
- **Salty outside (hypertonic):** water leaves — the cell shrinks.
- **Equal (isotonic):** no net movement.

</Step>

<Step title="Why animal and plant cells differ" kind="explain">

Put an animal cell in pure water and it keeps swelling until it **bursts** — it has nothing to stop it. That's why your cells are bathed in carefully balanced (isotonic) blood and tissue fluid.

A plant cell, though, has a tough **cell wall**. Water still floods in, but the wall pushes back, so the cell becomes firm and **turgid** instead of bursting. This turgor pressure is what holds plants upright — and why a thirsty plant **wilts** when its cells lose water.

<Callout type="info" title="Turgor in action">
A crisp, fresh lettuce leaf is full of turgid cells. Leave it out and it goes limp as the cells lose water and become flaccid — osmosis you can see on your plate.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Osmosis** = diffusion of **water** across a partially permeable membrane, from dilute to concentrated solution.
- **Hypotonic** (dilute outside) → water in → swell; **hypertonic** (concentrated outside) → water out → shrink; **isotonic** → no net change.
- Animal cells can **burst**; plant cells become firm (**turgid**) because their **cell wall** resists, giving plants their support.

</Step>
