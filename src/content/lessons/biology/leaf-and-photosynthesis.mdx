<Step title="A solar panel made of cells" kind="explain">

A leaf has one overriding job: to **catch sunlight** and use it to make food. Almost every feature of a leaf is an adaptation for this — for photosynthesis (which you met back in the energy world) and for the gas exchange it needs.

A leaf is broad and flat to catch maximum light, thin so gases diffuse quickly, and packed with the green organelles that do the work.

</Step>

<Step title="Inside a leaf" kind="interactive">

<LeafStructure />

Light passes through the clear upper layers to the **palisade cells**, crammed with chloroplasts. Below, the **spongy layer** has air spaces for gas exchange, and **stomata** on the underside let CO₂ in. **Veins** deliver water and carry the sugar away.

</Step>

<Step title="Every feature, an adaptation" kind="explain">

Match each structure to its purpose and the leaf makes complete sense:

- **Broad and flat** — a large surface to absorb light and exchange gases.
- **Thin** — short diffusion distance for CO₂ and O₂.
- **Palisade cells at the top, full of chloroplasts** — catch the most light.
- **Air spaces in the spongy layer** — let CO₂ reach every cell.
- **Stomata** — adjustable pores that let CO₂ in (and control water loss).
- **Veins (xylem and phloem)** — bring water, take sugar away.

It all serves the reaction: **carbon dioxide + water → glucose + oxygen**, powered by light in the chloroplasts.

<Callout type="info" title="A delicate balance">
Stomata must open to let CO₂ in — but open stomata also lose water. The plant constantly trades photosynthesis against water loss, opening in the light and closing at night.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- A leaf is built to **catch light** and make food by photosynthesis.
- Adaptations: broad and flat (light + gas exchange), thin (fast diffusion), **palisade cells** with chloroplasts at the top, air spaces, **stomata** (CO₂ in, water out), and **veins**.
- Stomata balance letting CO₂ in against losing water.

</Step>
