<Lesson>

<Step title="A different kind of division" kind="explain">

Mitosis makes identical copies — perfect for growth, but useless for sexual reproduction. If a sperm and an egg each had the full 46 chromosomes, their offspring would have 92, doubling every generation.

So gametes (sex cells) are made by a special division, **meiosis**, that **halves** the chromosome number and **shuffles** the genes. The result is sex cells that are all different from each other and from the parent.

</Step>

<Step title="Two divisions, four cells" kind="interactive">

Step through it. Notice there are **two** divisions, not one.

<Meiosis />

- **Meiosis I** separates the **homologous pairs** (halving the number).
- **Meiosis II** separates the **sister chromatids** (like mitosis).
- The result: **four** cells, each with **half** the chromosomes (23 in humans) — gametes.

</Step>

<Step title="Where variation comes from" kind="explain">

Meiosis doesn't just halve — it **mixes**, which is why no two siblings (except identical twins) are alike. Two shuffling tricks do it:

- **Crossing over** — homologous chromosomes swap matching segments in prophase I, creating new gene combinations.
- **Independent assortment** — which parent's chromosome goes to which pole is random, multiplying the combinations.

Add fertilisation — a random sperm meeting a random egg — and the variety is essentially limitless.

<Callout type="info" title="Mitosis vs meiosis">
**Mitosis:** 1 division → 2 identical cells, full chromosome number — for growth.
**Meiosis:** 2 divisions → 4 varied cells, half the chromosomes — for gametes.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Meiosis** makes **gametes**: **two divisions** → **four** cells, each with **half** the chromosomes (haploid).
- It halves the number so fertilisation can restore it without doubling each generation.
- It creates **variation** through **crossing over** and **independent assortment**.
- Contrast with mitosis: 1 division, 2 identical full-number cells.

</Step>
