<Lesson>

<Step title="A hidden world" kind="explain">

In 1665, Robert Hooke pointed an early microscope at a sliver of cork and saw it was divided into tiny boxes. They reminded him of the little rooms monks lived in, so he called them **cells**. Soon after, Antonie van Leeuwenhoek aimed a sharper lens at pond water and was astonished to find it *teeming* with tiny moving creatures.

Two centuries of looking led to one of the most important ideas in all of science — that these little boxes aren't just *in* living things. They **are** living things, all the way down.

</Step>

<Step title="Seeing is believing" kind="interactive">

Every leaf, every muscle, every drop of blood resolves — under enough magnification — into cells. Turn up the power and watch a green smear become a community of cells.

<MicroscopeZoom />

No matter where you look in the living world, you find the same thing: cells, and only cells.

</Step>

<Step title="The three big ideas" kind="explain">

Those observations crystallised into **cell theory**, the foundation of biology. It has three parts:

1. **All living things are made of one or more cells.**
2. **The cell is the basic unit of life** — the smallest thing that is truly alive.
3. **All cells come from pre-existing cells** — they don't appear from nothing; they divide.

<Callout type="info" title="Why the third part matters">
The idea that cells only come from other cells killed off the old belief in "spontaneous generation" — that maggots or microbes simply appear in rotting food. Life comes from life.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- The **microscope** revealed that all living things are built from **cells**.
- **Cell theory** has three parts: all life is cellular, the cell is the basic unit of life, and cells come only from pre-existing cells.
- This idea replaced the myth of spontaneous generation — **life comes from life**.
