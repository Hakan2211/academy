<Lesson>

<Step title="Why we breathe" kind="explain">

Every cell respires, and respiration needs **oxygen** and produces **carbon dioxide**. So your body has a constant two-way trade with the air: take oxygen in, get carbon dioxide out. The organs for this swap are the **lungs**, and the swap itself is **gas exchange**.

Breathing moves air in and out; gas exchange is the actual transfer of gases between that air and your blood.

</Step>

<Step title="The swap at the alveoli" kind="interactive">

Deep in the lungs, air reaches millions of tiny sacs called **alveoli**, each wrapped in capillaries. Watch the gases diffuse.

<GasExchange />

**Oxygen** diffuses from the air in the alveolus into the blood; **carbon dioxide** diffuses the other way, out of the blood to be breathed out. Both move by simple **diffusion**, down their concentration gradients.

</Step>

<Step title="Built for fast diffusion" kind="explain">

The lungs are a masterpiece of the surface-area principle. To swap gases fast enough to keep you alive, the alveoli are designed to maximise diffusion:

- **Huge surface area** — millions of alveoli give the area of a tennis court.
- **Thin walls** — one cell thick, so gases cross almost instantly.
- **Moist surface** — gases dissolve before diffusing.
- **Rich blood supply** — capillaries constantly carry gases away, keeping the gradient steep.
- **Good ventilation** — breathing refreshes the air, keeping oxygen high and CO₂ low.

Every feature is there to make diffusion as rapid as possible.

<Callout type="info" title="Breathing keeps the gradient steep">
Why keep breathing? Each breath brings in fresh, oxygen-rich air and removes CO₂-laden air, maintaining the steep concentration gradients that drive fast diffusion at the alveoli.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Gas exchange** swaps oxygen (in) for carbon dioxide (out) between air and blood, by **diffusion**.
- It happens in the **alveoli** — tiny air sacs wrapped in capillaries.
- Alveoli maximise diffusion: **large surface area, thin moist walls, rich blood supply,** good ventilation.
- **Breathing** keeps the gradients steep by refreshing the air.

</Step>
