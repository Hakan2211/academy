<Step title="The life of a cell" kind="explain">

You grew from a single cell into trillions, and you replace billions of cells every day — skin, blood, gut lining. All of that comes from cells **dividing**. But a cell can't just split whenever; it follows an orderly sequence called the **cell cycle**.

Most of a cell's life is spent not dividing at all, but growing and preparing. Division itself is brief.

</Step>

<Step title="Around the cycle" kind="interactive">

<CellCycle />

- **Interphase** (G1 → S → G2) is the long part: the cell grows, does its job, and crucially **copies its DNA** during S phase.
- **M phase** is the short finale, where the cell actually divides (mitosis).

</Step>

<Step title="Checkpoints and control" kind="explain">

A cell must not divide with damaged or half-copied DNA, so the cycle has **checkpoints** — quality controls that halt the cycle until everything is ready:

- Is the cell big enough and is the DNA undamaged? (before S)
- Was the DNA copied completely and correctly? (before M)
- Are the chromosomes properly lined up? (during M)

When these controls **fail**, cells can divide uncontrollably — which is what happens in **cancer**. A tumour is, at its core, the cell cycle running without its brakes.

<Callout type="info" title="Why the order matters">
DNA is copied in S phase *before* M phase for a reason: each new cell needs a full set. Copy first, then divide — never the other way around.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- The **cell cycle** is the orderly sequence a cell follows to grow and divide.
- **Interphase** (G1, S, G2) is the long growth-and-copy stage; **DNA is replicated in S phase**.
- **M phase** is the brief division (mitosis).
- **Checkpoints** stop a faulty cell dividing; when they fail, the result can be **cancer**.

</Step>
