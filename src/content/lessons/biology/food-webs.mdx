<Step title="Who eats whom" kind="explain">

A **food chain** shows the path of energy through an ecosystem — for example: **grass → rabbit → fox**. The arrows point in the direction the **energy flows**, from the thing eaten to the thing that eats it.

Each link has a name: **producers** (make their own food), **primary consumers** (herbivores), **secondary** and **tertiary consumers** (carnivores), and **decomposers** that break down everything when it dies.

</Step>

<Step title="From chains to webs" kind="interactive">

In reality, most animals eat more than one thing and are eaten by more than one predator. Linking all the food chains together gives a **food web**. Click a species to see its connections.

<FoodWeb />

Now try **removing** a species. Watch how the effect ripples — every predator that depended on it is suddenly short of food, which affects *their* predators in turn.

</Step>

<Step title="Everything is connected" kind="explain">

The food web reveals the central lesson of ecology: ecosystems are deeply **interconnected**, and a change to one species ripples through the whole web.

- Remove a **predator** and its prey may boom, then overgraze their food.
- Remove a **producer** or key prey and everything above it suffers.
- Add or remove just one species and the balance can tip in surprising ways.

Don't forget the **decomposers** — the bacteria and fungi that break down dead organisms and waste. They don't appear in the simple web, but they're essential: they return nutrients to the soil, which we'll follow in the next lesson.

<Callout type="tip" title="Keystone species">
Some species have an outsized effect — remove them and the whole web reshapes. Sea otters, for instance, keep sea urchins in check; without otters, urchins destroy entire kelp forests. They're called **keystone species**.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- A **food chain** traces energy flow (arrows point from eaten → eater): producers → consumers.
- A **food web** links many chains — most species eat and are eaten by several others.
- Ecosystems are **interconnected**: removing one species ripples through the web (especially **keystone species**).
- **Decomposers** recycle nutrients from the dead back into the soil.

</Step>
