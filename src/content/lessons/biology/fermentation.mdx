<Lesson>

<Step title="Respiration without oxygen" kind="explain">

Sometimes a cell needs energy but can't get enough oxygen — a sprinter's legs mid-race, a yeast cell deep in dough. Life has a backup: **anaerobic respiration**, releasing energy from glucose **without** oxygen.

The trade-off is steep. Without oxygen, glucose is only **partly** broken down, so far less energy is released — and a leftover by-product builds up.

</Step>

<Step title="Two anaerobic routes" kind="interactive">

Switch between the muscle and yeast modes and compare them with aerobic respiration.

<CellularRespiration />

- **In your muscles:** glucose → **lactic acid**. Just ~2 ATP per glucose, and the lactic acid causes the burning ache of hard exercise.
- **In yeast (fermentation):** glucose → **ethanol + carbon dioxide**. The same low energy yield — but humanity has used it for thousands of years.

</Step>

<Step title="Far less energy — and an oxygen debt" kind="explain">

Look at the ATP bars: aerobic respiration yields about **38 ATP** per glucose, anaerobic only about **2**. Oxygen makes respiration nearly twenty times more efficient.

When you sprint, your muscles go anaerobic and lactic acid piles up. Afterwards you keep breathing hard, gulping the extra oxygen needed to break that lactic acid down — your **oxygen debt**.

Yeast's version, **fermentation**, is the engine of the food and drink industry:

- **Ethanol** ferments beer and wine.
- The **carbon dioxide** makes bread dough rise.

<Callout type="info" title="An ancient partnership">
Brewing and baking are anaerobic respiration put to work. Every loaf of bread and glass of wine depends on yeast respiring without oxygen.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Anaerobic respiration** releases energy from glucose **without oxygen** — far less of it (~2 ATP vs ~38).
- In **muscles**: glucose → lactic acid (the burn; repaid as an **oxygen debt**).
- In **yeast (fermentation)**: glucose → ethanol + carbon dioxide — the basis of brewing and baking.

</Step>
