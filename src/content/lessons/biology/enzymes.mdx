<Lesson>

<Step title="Life's reactions are too slow" kind="explain">

The chemical reactions that keep you alive — digesting food, copying DNA, releasing energy — would, left alone, take years. Your body runs them in **fractions of a second**. The difference is **enzymes**.

An enzyme is a **biological catalyst**: a protein that speeds up a reaction enormously without being used up itself. Without enzymes, the chemistry of life simply couldn't happen fast enough to live.

</Step>

<Step title="Lock and key" kind="interactive">

Each enzyme has a precisely shaped pocket — the **active site** — and only one kind of molecule, the **substrate**, fits it. Watch a reaction happen.

<EnzymeLab />

The substrate slots into the active site like a key into a lock, the enzyme catalyses the reaction, the products no longer fit and leave — and the enzyme is **unchanged**, ready to go again.

</Step>

<Step title="Specific and reusable" kind="explain">

Two features make enzymes powerful:

- **Specificity** — the active site's shape means each enzyme works on just one substrate. Your gut has different enzymes for starch, protein, and fat.
- **Reusability** — because the enzyme isn't consumed, a single molecule can catalyse thousands of reactions per second.

Enzyme names usually end in **-ase** and hint at the substrate: *amylase* breaks down starch (amylose), *protease* breaks down protein, *lipase* breaks down lipids.

<Callout type="info" title="Build or break">
Enzymes work both ways: some **break** big molecules into small ones (digestion), others **join** small molecules into big ones (building starch or proteins). Either way, the active site does the work.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Enzymes** are protein **catalysts** — they speed reactions up hugely and aren't used up.
- The substrate fits the enzyme's **active site** like a key in a lock (specificity).
- One enzyme works on **one substrate**; names often end in **-ase**.
- Enzymes both break molecules apart and build them up.

</Step>
