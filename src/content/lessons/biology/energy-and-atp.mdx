<Step title="The cell's energy currency" kind="explain">

Everything a cell does — building molecules, pumping ions, contracting muscle, sending nerve signals — costs energy. But cells can't run directly off the energy locked in food. They need it in a usable, bite-sized form, on demand.

That form is **ATP** (adenosine triphosphate). Think of it as the cell's universal **energy currency**: a small molecule that delivers a packet of energy exactly where and when it's needed.

</Step>

<Step title="A rechargeable battery" kind="interactive">

ATP works like a rechargeable battery. Watch it cycle.

<ATPCycle />

ATP has three phosphate groups. Snap off the **third** one and energy is released for the cell to use — leaving **ADP** (two phosphates). Add the phosphate back, using energy from respiration, and you've recharged it to ATP.

</Step>

<Step title="Spent and recharged, constantly" kind="explain">

The beauty of ATP is that it's used over and over:

- **Releasing energy:** ATP → ADP + phosphate (+ energy for the cell).
- **Storing energy:** ADP + phosphate (+ energy from respiration) → ATP.

You'd be amazed how fast this turns over — your body recycles roughly its **own body weight in ATP every day**, each molecule being recharged thousands of times. ATP isn't a long-term store (that's fat and starch); it's the immediate cash the cell spends second to second.

<Callout type="info" title="Why a middle-man?">
Food stores a lot of energy, but in a form too big to use directly. ATP breaks that energy into small, standard units — so the same currency powers every different job in the cell.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **ATP** is the cell's universal **energy currency** — usable energy on demand.
- It's a **rechargeable battery**: ATP → ADP + phosphate releases energy; recharging stores it.
- The energy to recharge ATP comes from **respiration**.
- ATP is for immediate use, not long-term storage — and it turns over astonishingly fast.

</Step>
