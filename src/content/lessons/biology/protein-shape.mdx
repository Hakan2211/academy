<Step title="Why shape is the whole story" kind="explain">

A protein is a chain of amino acids — but a chain alone does nothing. The magic happens when that chain **folds** into a precise three-dimensional shape. The shape is what lets a protein grip a specific molecule, catalyse one exact reaction, or carry oxygen.

Get the shape right and the protein works. Lose the shape and it's useless — no matter that every atom is still there. This deep-dive is about how shape is built, and how easily it's destroyed.

</Step>

<Step title="From sequence to structure" kind="interactive">

It starts with the **sequence** — the order of amino acids in the chain.

<BiomoleculeViewer focus="protein" />

That order isn't random: it's dictated by a gene. And remarkably, the sequence alone usually determines how the chain will fold — the protein folds itself into the one shape its sequence specifies.

</Step>

<Step title="Levels of folding" kind="explain">

Biologists describe protein structure in levels:

- **Primary** — the sequence of amino acids (the order in the chain).
- **Secondary** — the chain coils into **helices** or folds into **sheets**, held by weak bonds.
- **Tertiary** — the whole thing folds into a unique 3-D blob, the working shape.
- **Quaternary** — several folded chains join (haemoglobin is four chains together).

Each level is held together by **weak bonds** between parts of the chain. That's the protein's strength — and its weakness.

</Step>

<Step title="The active site" kind="explain">

For an enzyme, folding creates a specially shaped pocket called the **active site**. Only the right molecule fits into it, like a key into a lock. That precise fit is why enzymes are so specific — each one catalyses just one reaction.

And it's entirely a product of shape. The active site exists only because the chain folded exactly the way it did. Disturb the fold, and the pocket is gone.

</Step>

<Step title="Denaturation: losing the shape" kind="interactive">

Because the fold is held by *weak* bonds, it's fragile. Too much **heat** or the wrong **pH** breaks those bonds, and the protein unravels — it **denatures**. Drag the temperature and watch it happen.

<ProteinFold />

Once denatured, the active site is destroyed and the protein stops working. This is usually **permanent**: a cooked egg white never turns clear and runny again. It's also why a high fever is dangerous, and why every enzyme has a temperature and pH at which it works best.

</Step>

<Step title="When folding goes wrong" kind="explain">

Folding matters so much that errors cause disease:

- **Sickle-cell anaemia** comes from a *single* wrong amino acid in haemoglobin, which makes it clump and deform red blood cells.
- **Prion diseases** (like CJD) are caused by a protein folded into the wrong shape, which forces others to misfold too.

The lesson runs through all of biology: in proteins, **structure is function**. The sequence sets the shape, the shape does the job, and a tiny change to either can change everything.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- A protein's **shape**, not just its atoms, determines what it does — **structure is function**.
- The amino-acid **sequence** dictates folding through levels (primary → quaternary), held by **weak bonds**.
- Folding creates the **active site** that makes enzymes specific.
- Heat or wrong pH breaks those bonds: the protein **denatures** (usually permanently) and stops working.
- Tiny folding errors cause diseases like sickle-cell anaemia and prion disease.

</Step>
