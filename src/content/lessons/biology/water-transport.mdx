<Lesson>

<Step title="From soil to sky" kind="explain">

A plant takes up water through its **roots** and loses it from its **leaves**, far above. In between, water travels up through a network of tubes called the **xylem**. In a tall tree that's a journey of tens of metres — straight up, with no pump.

The loss of water vapour from the leaves is called **transpiration**, and surprisingly, it's what pulls all that water up.

</Step>

<Step title="The transpiration stream" kind="interactive">

Toggle the conditions and watch how fast water rises.

<Transpiration />

Water evaporates from the leaf cells and escapes through the **stomata**. As each water molecule leaves, it tugs the next one up behind it — a continuous **transpiration stream** flowing from root to leaf.

</Step>

<Step title="What changes the rate" kind="explain">

Anything that speeds up evaporation from the leaves speeds up the whole stream:

- **Light** — opens the stomata, so more water escapes.
- **Warmth** — evaporation is faster when it's hot.
- **Wind** — blows away humid air, keeping the gradient steep.
- **Humidity** — *slows* it down; moist air means less evaporation.

This is why plants wilt on a hot, windy day — they lose water faster than the roots can replace it. The same water also brings up dissolved **minerals** from the soil and keeps the leaf cells turgid.

<Callout type="tip" title="Root hairs">
Roots absorb water through **root hair cells** — long, thin extensions that hugely increase the surface area for absorption (the surface-area trick again). The water enters by osmosis.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- Water moves up from roots to leaves through the **xylem** — the **transpiration stream**.
- **Transpiration** (water loss from the leaves via stomata) is what pulls the water up.
- Rate increases with **light, warmth,** and **wind**, and decreases with **humidity**.
- Roots absorb water by **osmosis** through **root hair cells** (huge surface area).

</Step>
