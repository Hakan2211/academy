<Step title="Spreading out for free" kind="explain">

Open a bottle of perfume in the corner of a room and, minutes later, you smell it across the room. Nobody pushed the scent molecules — they spread on their own.

This is **diffusion**: the net movement of particles from where they are **crowded** (high concentration) to where they are **sparse** (low concentration). It costs the cell no energy at all, and it's how oxygen, carbon dioxide, and many nutrients move in and out of cells.

</Step>

<Step title="Watch it happen" kind="interactive">

The particles each move at random — but because they start crowded on the left, more of them happen to wander right than left. The result is a steady **net flow** down the gradient until they're evenly spread.

<DiffusionLab />

Notice the endpoint: even at equilibrium the particles keep moving. There's just no longer a *net* direction, because both sides are equally full.

</Step>

<Step title="What makes diffusion faster" kind="explain">

Cells often need to speed diffusion up. Four things control the rate:

- **Steeper concentration gradient** — a bigger difference drives faster net movement.
- **Higher temperature** — particles move faster.
- **Larger surface area** — more room to cross at once.
- **Shorter distance** — a thinner barrier is quicker to cross.

<Callout type="tip" title="Bodies are built for this">
Your lungs and gut maximise all four: a huge surface area, a wall just one cell thick, a rich blood supply to keep the gradient steep, and a warm body temperature.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Diffusion** is the net movement of particles from high to low concentration — driven by random motion, needing no energy.
- It reaches **equilibrium** when evenly spread (particles still move, but with no net direction).
- Rate increases with a **steeper gradient, higher temperature, larger surface area,** and **shorter distance**.

</Step>
