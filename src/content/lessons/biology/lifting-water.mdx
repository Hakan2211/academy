<Lesson>

<Step title="An impossible-seeming feat" kind="explain">

The tallest trees on Earth lift water more than **100 metres** into the air — from their roots to their highest leaves. They do it with **no heart, no pump, and no moving parts**, around the clock, for thousands of years.

This should be impossible. A suction pump can only lift water about 10 metres before the water column breaks. So how does a redwood beat that ten times over? The answer is one of the most elegant mechanisms in all of biology.

</Step>

<Step title="It's pulled, not pushed" kind="interactive">

The secret is that the water isn't **pushed** up from below — it's **pulled** up from above. And the puller is transpiration.

<Transpiration />

As water evaporates from the leaves, it creates a pull — a **tension** — that is transmitted all the way down a continuous column of water to the roots. The tree doesn't push the water up; the drying leaves drag it up.

</Step>

<Step title="Cohesion: water holds itself together" kind="explain">

For a *pull* to work, the entire column of water — root to leaf — must hold together as one unbroken thread, like a rope. And it does, because of **cohesion**: water molecules cling to one another through **hydrogen bonds** (remember water's polarity, from the molecules world).

So when a molecule evaporates at the top, it tugs the one below, which tugs the one below that, all the way down. The water in the narrow xylem tubes behaves like a continuous, unbreakable chain. This is the **cohesion–tension** theory.

</Step>

<Step title="Why the tubes are so narrow" kind="explain">

Water also clings to the *walls* of the xylem tubes — a property called **adhesion**. In the extremely narrow xylem vessels, adhesion and cohesion together let water resist the pull of gravity and even climb, the way water creeps up a thin glass tube (**capillary action**).

The xylem is perfectly built for this: long, narrow, hollow, reinforced tubes of dead cells — essentially a bundle of microscopic drinking straws running the height of the tree, strong enough not to collapse under the tension.

<Callout type="info" title="Water under tension">
The water in a tall tree is under enormous negative pressure — it's literally being stretched. If you nicked the xylem, air would rush *in*, not water out. The whole column is held in suspense by the cohesion of water.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="The whole chain, end to end" kind="explain">

Trace the complete journey and every earlier idea reappears:

1. Water enters the **roots** by **osmosis** (through root hair cells — huge surface area).
2. It rises through the **xylem** as one continuous, cohesive column.
3. It's pulled up by **tension** created when water **evaporates** from the leaves (transpiration).
4. It escapes through the **stomata** as vapour.

Osmosis, diffusion, surface area, water's polarity and hydrogen bonds — they all come together to defy gravity, powered by nothing but the Sun drying the leaves.

</Step>

<Step title="The cost and the bargain" kind="explain">

There's a price. To photosynthesise, a leaf must open its stomata to let CO₂ in — but open stomata leak water. A large tree can lose **hundreds of litres** of water a day this way.

So the plant strikes a constant bargain: open the stomata enough to feed photosynthesis, but not so much that it dries out. In a drought it closes them and sacrifices growth to survive. The towering height of a tree, and the river of water flowing silently up inside it, is the visible result of that bargain — a pump with no moving parts, run entirely by physics and the Sun.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- Trees lift water 100+ m with **no pump** — water is **pulled** up, not pushed.
- **Transpiration** at the leaves creates **tension**; **cohesion** (water's hydrogen bonds) keeps the column unbroken — the **cohesion–tension** mechanism.
- **Adhesion** to the narrow xylem walls helps water resist gravity (capillary action).
- The full chain: osmosis into roots → cohesive column up the xylem → evaporation pulls it → out through stomata.
- The cost is huge **water loss**, so the plant constantly balances photosynthesis against drying out.

</Step>
