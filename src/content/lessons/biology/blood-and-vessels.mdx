<Step title="A liquid organ" kind="explain">

Blood is more than red liquid — it's a tissue with several specialised parts, each doing a different job, all suspended in a pale fluid. And it travels through three distinct kinds of **vessel**, each built for its role.

Together, the blood and the vessels form the transport system that keeps every cell supplied.

</Step>

<Step title="What blood carries — and the pipes" kind="interactive">

Blood is mostly **plasma** (a watery carrier) plus three cell types: **red cells** (oxygen), **white cells** (defence), and **platelets** (clotting). The vessels are **arteries** (away from the heart), **veins** (back to it), and **capillaries** (where exchange happens).

</Step>

<Step title="Form fits function" kind="explain">

Every part is shaped for its job — a recurring theme in biology:

- **Red blood cells** have no nucleus and a biconcave disc shape — all to pack in more haemoglobin and carry more oxygen.
- **Arteries** have thick, elastic, muscular walls to withstand the high pressure of blood leaving the heart.
- **Veins** have thinner walls and **valves** to keep low-pressure blood flowing back the right way.
- **Capillaries** are just one cell thick, so oxygen, glucose, and waste can diffuse straight in and out at the cells.

<Callout type="tip" title="Where the real work happens">
Arteries and veins are just plumbing. The actual exchange of materials with your cells happens only in the **capillaries** — which is why their walls are as thin as possible.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Blood** = **plasma** + **red cells** (oxygen), **white cells** (defence), **platelets** (clotting).
- Three vessels: **arteries** (away from heart, thick walls), **veins** (back to heart, valves), **capillaries** (exchange, one cell thick).
- **Form fits function** throughout — e.g. red cells shaped to carry maximum oxygen.

</Step>
