<Step title="Life across the orders of magnitude" kind="explain">

Biology studies things smaller than light can resolve *and* things the size of a planet — and everything in between. From the **2-nanometre** width of a DNA molecule to the **12,700-kilometre** sweep of the living biosphere is a span of more than **ten powers of ten**.

To think clearly across that range, biologists zoom in steps of about ten at a time. Let's take the elevator.

</Step>

<Step title="Ride the elevator" kind="interactive">

Drag the slider to zoom out, one rung at a time. Each step is roughly **10×** bigger than the last.

<BioScale />

A handful of landmarks worth feeling in your bones: a bacterium is about ten times smaller than one of your cells; your cells are about ten times smaller than the width of a hair; and you are unimaginably larger than the molecules you're built from.

</Step>

<Step title="Levels of organisation" kind="explain">

Life isn't just *small* and *big* — it's **nested**. Each level is built from the one below and cooperates to form the one above:

**molecule → organelle → cell → tissue → organ → organ system → organism → population → community → ecosystem → biosphere**

A muscle cell is built from molecules; muscle tissue from cells; the heart from tissues; the circulatory system from organs; *you* from systems — and you are one member of a population in an ecosystem.

<Callout type="tip" title="Why it matters">
Biology happens at every level. A drug acts on a molecule; a disease attacks an organ; conservation protects an ecosystem. Knowing which level you're working at keeps your thinking sharp.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- Life spans more than **ten orders of magnitude**, from a DNA molecule to the whole biosphere.
- Zooming in steps of ~10× makes that vast range thinkable.
- Living matter is organised into nested **levels**: molecule → cell → tissue → organ → organism → ecosystem → biosphere.
- Each level emerges from cooperation among the parts below it.

</Step>
