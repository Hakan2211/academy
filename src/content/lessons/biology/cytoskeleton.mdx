<Step title="Not just a bag of jelly" kind="explain">

It's tempting to picture a cell as a floppy bag with organelles sloshing around inside. It isn't. Running through the cytoplasm is a hidden framework of protein fibres — the **cytoskeleton** — that gives the cell its shape, holds organelles in place, and even acts as a transport network.

Without it, your cells would be shapeless blobs unable to move, divide, or organise themselves.

</Step>

<Step title="The internal scaffold" kind="interactive">

There are three kinds of fibre. Watch a motor protein haul a cargo vesicle along a microtubule "highway", and tap each filament to learn its role.

<Cytoskeleton />

The fibres aren't permanent — the cell constantly builds and dismantles them, rearranging its own skeleton on the fly.

</Step>

<Step title="Three fibres, three jobs" kind="explain">

- **Microfilaments** (actin) — thin and flexible. They let cells crawl, change shape, and pinch in two during division.
- **Intermediate filaments** — tough, rope-like fibres that resist mechanical stress and anchor the cell's shape.
- **Microtubules** — stiff hollow tubes that serve as **roads** for transport and pull chromosomes apart when the cell divides.

<Callout type="info" title="The cell's railways">
Motor proteins literally *walk* along microtubules, one step at a time, carrying vesicles of cargo to where they're needed. It's a molecular delivery service running inside every cell.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- The **cytoskeleton** is a network of protein fibres that shapes and organises the cell.
- **Microfilaments** — movement and changing shape.
- **Intermediate filaments** — strength and structure.
- **Microtubules** — transport highways and chromosome separation.
- It's dynamic — constantly rebuilt as the cell's needs change.

</Step>
