<Lesson>

<Step title="The biggest shared, the smallest common" kind="explain">

Two numbers, two classic questions:

- The **Highest Common Factor (HCF)** — the largest number that divides *both*. The most you can share equally.
- The **Lowest Common Multiple (LCM)** — the smallest number that's a multiple of *both*. The first time their cycles line up again.

Prime factorisation answers both in one move.

</Step>

<Step title="Share the prime factors" kind="interactive">

Lay out both numbers' prime factors. The factors they **share** build the HCF; **all** the factors together build the LCM.

<HcfLcm />

</Step>

<Step title="The rule, in primes" kind="explain">

Write each number in prime powers, then:

- **HCF** — multiply the shared primes, each to its **lowest** power.
- **LCM** — multiply every prime that appears, each to its **highest** power.

For 12 = 2² × 3 and 18 = 2 × 3²:

<Formula tex="\text{HCF} = 2^1 \times 3^1 = 6 \qquad \text{LCM} = 2^2 \times 3^2 = 36" block />

</Step>

<Step title="A handy shortcut" kind="explain">

The shared factors get counted in *both* the HCF and the LCM, which leads to a neat identity:

<Formula tex="\text{HCF}(a,b) \times \text{LCM}(a,b) = a \times b" block />

So if you know one, you can find the other: LCM(12, 18) = (12 × 18) ÷ 6 = 216 ÷ 6 = 36. LCMs show up whenever two repeating events must coincide — buses leaving every 12 and 18 minutes next meet after 36.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **HCF** = product of shared primes to their **lowest** powers (largest shared divisor).
- **LCM** = product of all primes to their **highest** powers (first common multiple).
- Identity: **HCF × LCM = a × b**.
- LCM solves "when do two cycles next align?"; HCF solves "what's the biggest equal share?".

</Step>
