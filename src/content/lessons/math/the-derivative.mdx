<Step title="A formula for the slope everywhere" kind="explain">

The tangent slope changes from point to point along a curve. The **derivative** is a new function, written **f′(x)**, that gives the slope at *every* point in one go. Differentiating means finding this slope-function from the original.

</Step>

<Step title="Slide along and read the slope" kind="interactive">

For f(x) = ½x², watch the tangent slope as you move the point — it's always exactly x.

<DerivativeExplorer />

</Step>

<Step title="The definition" kind="explain">

The derivative is the limit of the secant slope as the gap closes — the formal version of the last lesson:

<Formula tex="f'(x) = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{f(x+h) - f(x)}{h}" block />

Apply it to f(x) = x²: f(x+h) = x² + 2xh + h², so the difference quotient is (2xh + h²)/h = 2x + h. As h → 0, that becomes **2x**. So the derivative of x² is **2x** — a clean rule born from a limit.

</Step>

<Step title="What the derivative tells you" kind="explain">

The sign and size of f′(x) describe the curve's behaviour:

- **f′(x) > 0** → the function is **increasing** (sloping up).
- **f′(x) < 0** → **decreasing** (sloping down).
- **f′(x) = 0** → a flat spot: a **maximum, minimum, or turning point**.

That last fact is gold for optimisation: to find where something is largest or smallest (maximum profit, minimum cost, shortest path), find where its derivative is zero.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- The **derivative f′(x)** gives the curve's **slope at every point**.
- Definition: f′(x) = limₕ→₀ [f(x+h) − f(x)] / h.
- Worked from the limit, the derivative of **x² is 2x**.
- **f′ > 0** rising, **f′ < 0** falling, **f′ = 0** turning point — the key to optimisation.

</Step>
