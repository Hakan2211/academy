<Step title="Filling space" kind="explain">

**Volume** measures the space a solid occupies, counted in **cubic units** (cm³, m³). A **prism** is a solid with the same cross-section all the way along — a box, a tube, a Toblerone. For every prism, one rule does the job.

</Step>

</Step>

<Step title="Base area × length" kind="explain">

A prism is its **cross-section, stacked up** its length. So:

<Formula tex="\text{Volume} = \text{cross-section area} \times \text{length}" block />

- **Cuboid:** the cross-section is a rectangle, so V = l × w × h.
- **Cylinder:** the cross-section is a circle, so V = πr² × h.

For a triangular prism, it's (½ base × height) × length — find the cross-section's area, then multiply by how long it is.

</Step>

<Step title="Why cubic units" kind="explain">

Volume multiplies three lengths, so the units are **cubed**: cm × cm × cm = cm³. This is also why volume conversions cube their factor (1 m³ = 1 000 000 cm³, from the conversions lesson). A 2 cm cube holds 2 × 2 × 2 = 8 one-centimetre cubes — you can literally count them.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Volume** = space inside, in **cubic units**.
- A **prism** has a constant cross-section: **V = cross-section area × length**.
- Cuboid **l·w·h**; cylinder **πr²h**; triangular prism (½bh)×length.
- Three lengths multiplied → cubic units (and cubed conversion factors).

</Step>
