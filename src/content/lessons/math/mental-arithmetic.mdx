<Step title="Calculate like a mathematician, not a machine" kind="explain">

Strong mental arithmetic isn't about being a human calculator. It's about **choosing the easy path** — rearranging a sum so the numbers become friendly. A few reliable strategies cover almost everything.

</Step>

<Step title="Multiplying and dividing by 10, 100, 1000" kind="explain">

This is the single most useful shortcut, and place value explains it. Multiplying by 10 slides every digit one column to the **left**; dividing by 10 slides them **right**.

<Formula tex="46 \times 100 = 4600 \qquad 4600 \div 10 = 460" block />

"Adding a zero" is just a side effect of the digits moving — which is why it fails for decimals: 4.6 × 10 = 46, no zero in sight.

</Step>

<Step title="Watch the digits slide" kind="interactive">

Press **× 10** and **÷ 10** and keep your eye on the columns, not the decimal point.

<PlaceValue />

</Step>

<Step title="Partition, compensate, double and halve" kind="explain">

Three more strategies that turn hard sums into easy ones:

- **Partition** — break a number into parts: 34 + 28 = (30 + 20) + (4 + 8) = 50 + 12 = 62.
- **Compensate** — round, then fix it: 47 + 9 = 47 + 10 − 1 = 56.
- **Double & halve** — for multiplication: 16 × 5 = 8 × 10 = 80.

</Step>

<Step title="Round and adjust on the line" kind="interactive">

Compensation is just a friendly jump and a small correction. Set the start, then make the change a round number — you can always nudge afterwards.

<NumberLine min={0} max={20} jumps />

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **× / ÷ by 10, 100, 1000**: slide the digits left or right (don't just 'add a zero').
- **Partition** a number into easy parts and add the parts.
- **Compensate**: round to something friendly, then correct.
- **Double and halve** to simplify a multiplication.

</Step>
