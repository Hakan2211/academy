<Lesson>

<Step title="Running expansion backwards" kind="explain">

Expanding gave us (x + a)(x + b) = x² + (a + b)x + ab. **Factorising** reverses it: starting from x² + bx + c, find the two brackets. The clue is right there in that pattern — you need two numbers that **add to the middle coefficient** and **multiply to the constant**.

</Step>

<Step title="Hunt for the pair" kind="interactive">

List the factor pairs of the constant, and find the one whose sum is the middle coefficient.

<FactoriseQuadratic />

</Step>

<Step title="The method" kind="explain">

For x² + 5x + 6: find two numbers that multiply to 6 and add to 5 → that's **2 and 3**. So:

<Formula tex="x^2 + 5x + 6 = (x + 2)(x + 3)" block />

Signs follow naturally: for x² − x − 6 you need a product of −6 and a sum of −1 → −3 and +2 → (x − 3)(x + 2). A **negative product** means the numbers have opposite signs; a **positive product with negative sum** means both are negative.

</Step>

<Step title="Always verify" kind="explain">

Factorising has a built-in check — expand your answer back and you should land on the original:

<Formula tex="(x - 3)(x + 2) = x^2 + 2x - 3x - 6 = x^2 - x - 6 \checkmark" block />

(When the x² has a coefficient bigger than 1, like 2x² + 7x + 3, the hunt is a little longer, but the principle is the same.)

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- Factorising x² + bx + c reverses expansion: find two numbers that **add to b, multiply to c**.
- **Signs:** negative product → opposite signs; positive product + negative sum → both negative.
- **Check** by expanding back to the original.
- It's the doorway to *solving* quadratics, next.

</Step>
