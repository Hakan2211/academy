<Step title="What would a power of ½ mean?" kind="explain">

The index laws have led us to zero and negative powers. Pushing further: what is 9^(½)? The laws decide it for us. Whatever it is, multiplying it by itself must obey the add rule:

<Formula tex="9^{1/2} \times 9^{1/2} = 9^{1/2 + 1/2} = 9^1 = 9" block />

A number that gives 9 when multiplied by itself is **√9 = 3**. So a power of ½ *is* a square root.

</Step>

<Step title="The general rule" kind="explain">

The denominator of a fractional index is the **root**; the numerator is the ordinary **power**:

<Formula tex="a^{1/n} = \sqrt[n]{a}, \qquad a^{m/n} = \left(\sqrt[n]{a}\right)^{m}" block />

So 8^(1/3) = ∛8 = 2, and 8^(2/3) = (∛8)² = 2² = 4. Take the root first (it keeps the numbers small), then raise to the power.

</Step>

<Step title="It all stays consistent" kind="explain">

Every index rule now works for *any* rational power. Combine them freely:

<Formula tex="16^{-1/2} = \frac{1}{16^{1/2}} = \frac{1}{4}" block />

Negative *and* fractional: reciprocal of the square root. This single, unified system — integer, zero, negative, fractional — is one of algebra's quiet triumphs.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- A fractional index is a **root**: a^(1/n) = ⁿ√a.
- In general a^(m/n) = (ⁿ√a)ᵐ — **root by the denominator, power by the numerator**.
- Take the **root first** to keep numbers manageable.
- The index laws now hold for every rational power, including negative-fractional ones.

</Step>
