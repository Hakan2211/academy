<Step title="Counting equally likely outcomes" kind="explain">

When every outcome is **equally likely** — a fair die, a shuffled deck, a balanced spinner — probability is just counting:

<Formula tex="P(\text{event}) = \frac{\text{number of favourable outcomes}}{\text{total number of outcomes}}" block />

The probability of rolling a 4 on a die is 1 favourable out of 6 total = 1/6.

</Step>

<Step title="Theoretical versus experimental" kind="explain">

- **Theoretical** probability is what the maths predicts: P(heads) = ½ for a fair coin.
- **Experimental** (or relative frequency) is what you actually observe: flip 100 times, get 53 heads → 0.53.

The two close in as the number of trials grows — the **law of large numbers**. Ten flips might give 7 heads; ten thousand will hug 50% tightly.

</Step>

<Step title="Expected frequency" kind="explain">

Multiply a probability by the number of trials to predict **how often** something should happen:

<Formula tex="\text{expected} = P \times \text{number of trials}" block />

Roll a die 60 times and you'd expect a six about 1/6 × 60 = **10** times. Not guaranteed — but the best prediction, and what casinos and insurers bank on.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- For equally likely outcomes: **P = favourable ÷ total**.
- **Theoretical** = predicted; **experimental** = observed (they converge — law of large numbers).
- **Expected frequency = P × number of trials.**

</Step>
