<Step title="Equal multipliers" kind="explain">

A **geometric sequence** *multiplies* by a fixed **common ratio** r each step: 2, 6, 18, 54, … (×3). Where arithmetic sequences add and grow steadily, geometric sequences multiply — and when r > 1 they grow **explosively**.

</Step>

<Step title="Growth and decay" kind="interactive">

Slide the ratio. Above 1 the bars rocket up; between 0 and 1 they melt toward zero.

<GeometricSequence />

</Step>

<Step title="The nth-term rule" kind="explain">

Starting from a and multiplying by r each time:

<Formula tex="\text{nth term} = a \cdot r^{\,n-1}" block />

For 2, 6, 18, …: a = 2, r = 3, so the 5th term is 2 × 3⁴ = 162. Notice the **power** — geometric sequences are exponential, the same maths as compound interest and population growth from the Ratio world.

</Step>

<Step title="The chessboard legend" kind="explain">

A courtier asked his king for one grain of rice on the first chessboard square, two on the second, four on the third — doubling each square (r = 2). It sounds modest. But by the 64th square:

<Formula tex="2^{63} \approx 9.2 \times 10^{18} \text{ grains}" block />

— more rice than has ever been grown on Earth. That's the deceptive power of geometric growth: slow to start, then overwhelming. (If 0 < r < 1, the same maths runs in reverse: each term a fraction of the last, fading toward zero — **decay**.)

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Geometric sequence**: constant common ratio r (multiply each step).
- **nth term = a · rⁿ⁻¹** — a power, so growth is **exponential**.
- r > 1 explodes; 0 &lt; r &lt; 1 decays toward zero.
- Geometric growth is slow then staggering — the chessboard/rice legend.

</Step>
