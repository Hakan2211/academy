<Lesson>

<Step title="Double one, double the other" kind="explain">

Two quantities are in **direct proportion** when they grow in lockstep: double one and the other doubles; halve one and the other halves. Buy 3× the petrol, pay 3× the cost. The ratio between them never changes.

</Step>

<Step title="The straight line through the origin" kind="interactive">

Direct proportion has an unmistakable graph — a straight line that passes through (0, 0). Its slope is the **constant of proportionality**. Drag the slope to fit the data.

</Step>

<Step title="The constant of proportionality" kind="explain">

If y is directly proportional to x (written y ∝ x), then

<Formula tex="y = kx, \qquad k = \frac{y}{x} \text{ (always the same)}" block />

The constant **k** is the value of y when x = 1 — the "per one" rate. Finding k from one known pair lets you predict any other: if 4 kg costs £6, then k = 1.5 £/kg, so 10 kg costs 10 × 1.5 = £15.

</Step>

<Step title="Spotting direct proportion" kind="explain">

It's directly proportional only if **y ÷ x is constant** *and* the line goes through the origin (0 of one means 0 of the other). A taxi that charges a £3 flag fee plus £2 per mile is **not** directly proportional — at 0 miles the cost is £3, not £0. The line misses the origin.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Direct proportion**: quantities scale together; their ratio is constant.
- Graph = a **straight line through the origin**; slope = constant **k**.
- **y = kx**, where k is the "per one" rate (y when x = 1).
- A fixed starting fee breaks proportion — the line then misses (0, 0).

</Step>
