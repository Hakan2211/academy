<Step title="More than one thing at once" kind="explain">

What's the chance of rolling two dice and getting a total of 7? Or a head *and* a six? These are **combined events**. The key is to count **all** the possible outcomes systematically — and a **sample space** diagram does exactly that.

</Step>

<Step title="The two-dice sample space" kind="interactive">

</Step>

<Step title="Listing every outcome" kind="explain">

For two dice there are 6 × 6 = **36** equally likely outcomes. A sample space grid shows them all, so you just count the ones you want:

<Formula tex="P(\text{sum} = 7) = \frac{6}{36} = \frac{1}{6}" block />

(The six ways: 1-6, 2-5, 3-4, 4-3, 5-2, 6-1.) Listing beats guessing — many people wrongly think every total is equally likely, but 7 is far more common than 2.

</Step>

<Step title="The AND and OR rules" kind="explain">

For **independent** events (one doesn't affect the other):

<Formula tex="P(A \text{ and } B) = P(A) \times P(B)" block />

So P(head and a six) = ½ × 1/6 = 1/12. For **mutually exclusive** events (can't both happen):

<Formula tex="P(A \text{ or } B) = P(A) + P(B)" block />

A handy rule of thumb: "**and** → multiply, **or** → add."

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Combined events** involve more than one outcome — list them in a **sample space**.
- Two dice = **36** equally likely outcomes; count the favourable ones.
- **AND** (independent) → **multiply**; **OR** (mutually exclusive) → **add**.
- Systematic listing avoids the "all totals equal" trap.

</Step>
