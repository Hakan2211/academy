<Lesson>

<Step title="You can only add matching pieces" kind="explain">

¼ + ¼ = 2/4 = ½ — easy, because the pieces are the same size. But ½ + ⅓ is not 2/5. Halves and thirds are *different-sized pieces*; you can't just add the counts. First you must re-cut both into pieces of the **same size**.

</Step>

<Step title="Find a common denominator, then combine" kind="interactive">

Re-slice both fractions to a common denominator, and now the pieces line up and add.

<FractionAdd />

</Step>

<Step title="The method" kind="explain">

1. Find a **common denominator** (the LCM of the two).
2. Rewrite each fraction over it.
3. **Add or subtract the numerators**; keep the denominator.
4. Simplify.

<Formula tex="\frac{1}{2} + \frac{1}{3} = \frac{3}{6} + \frac{2}{6} = \frac{5}{6}" block />

The denominator never gets added — it just labels the piece size, which doesn't change.

</Step>

<Step title="Mixed numbers" kind="explain">

For mixed numbers, deal with the whole parts and the fraction parts separately, or turn everything into improper fractions first:

<Formula tex="2\tfrac{1}{2} + 1\tfrac{1}{3} = \frac{5}{2} + \frac{4}{3} = \frac{15}{6} + \frac{8}{6} = \frac{23}{6} = 3\tfrac{5}{6}" block />

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- Add/subtract fractions **only over a common denominator** (their LCM).
- Combine the **numerators**; the denominator stays.
- **Never** add or subtract the denominators.
- Simplify the result, and convert mixed numbers if it helps.

</Step>
