<Step title="Numbers with a shape" kind="explain">

Some sequences are best understood as **pictures**. **Figurate numbers** count the dots you can arrange into a shape — triangles, squares, cubes — and their geometry hands you their formula for free. The ancient Greeks studied them, and they reveal lovely hidden links.

</Step>

<Step title="Draw the pattern" kind="interactive">

</Step>

<Step title="Triangular and square numbers" kind="explain">

- **Triangular numbers** 1, 3, 6, 10, 15 stack rows of 1, 2, 3, … Two copies form a rectangle, so Tₙ = n(n+1)/2.
- **Square numbers** 1, 4, 9, 16 fill an n × n grid: Sₙ = n².
- **Cube numbers** 1, 8, 27, 64 stack n layers of n²: Cₙ = n³.

These are the same square and cube powers from the Powers world, now seen as *shapes*.

</Step>

<Step title="Hidden connections" kind="explain">

Figurate numbers hide beautiful relationships. Add two consecutive triangular numbers and you always get a perfect square:

<Formula tex="T_{n-1} + T_n = n^2, \qquad \text{e.g. } 6 + 10 = 16 = 4^2" block />

The two triangles slot together into a square — provable just by sliding the dot pictures together. Geometry proving arithmetic, with no algebra at all.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Figurate numbers** count dots arranged into shapes; the shape gives the formula.
- **Triangular** Tₙ = n(n+1)/2, **square** n², **cube** n³.
- Consecutive triangular numbers sum to a **square** (Tₙ₋₁ + Tₙ = n²).
- Geometry can prove number facts directly.

</Step>
