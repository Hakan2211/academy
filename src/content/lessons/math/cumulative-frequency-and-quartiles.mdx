<Step title="Slicing data into quarters" kind="explain">

The median splits sorted data in half. The **quartiles** split it into quarters:

- **Lower quartile (Q1)** — a quarter of the way through.
- **Median (Q2)** — the middle.
- **Upper quartile (Q3)** — three-quarters through.

The middle 50% sits between Q1 and Q3 — its width, the **interquartile range (IQR = Q3 − Q1)**, is a spread measure that ignores extremes.

</Step>

<Step title="The box plot" kind="interactive">

The five-number summary — min, Q1, median, Q3, max — drawn as a box and whiskers.

<BoxPlot />

</Step>

<Step title="Cumulative frequency" kind="explain">

To find quartiles from grouped data, build a **cumulative frequency** — a running total of how many values fall *below* each point. Plotting it gives an S-shaped curve, and reading across from the ¼, ½ and ¾ heights gives Q1, the median and Q3.

The IQR is prized because, unlike the range, **one freak value can't distort it** — it describes the solid middle of the data.

</Step>

<Step title="Comparing distributions" kind="explain">

Box plots shine for **comparison**. Draw two on the same scale and you instantly see which dataset is higher (median), which is more spread (box width / IQR), and whether either is **skewed** (median off-centre in its box). It's the statistician's quick visual verdict.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Quartiles** Q1, median, Q3 split sorted data into quarters.
- **IQR = Q3 − Q1** — spread of the middle 50%, resistant to outliers.
- **Cumulative frequency** (running totals) locates quartiles from grouped data.
- **Box plots** make distributions easy to compare (centre, spread, skew).

</Step>
