<Lesson>

<Step title="Patterns in a line" kind="explain">

A **sequence** is an ordered list of numbers — its **terms** — that follow a rule: 2, 5, 8, 11, … or 1, 2, 4, 8, … Spotting the rule lets you continue the list forever and predict any term. Pattern-finding is one of the most fundamental acts in mathematics.

</Step>

<Step title="Find the rule" kind="interactive">

The simplest rules are **term-to-term**: what you do to get from one term to the next. Toggle between adding and multiplying.

<SequenceExplorer />

</Step>

<Step title="Term-to-term rules" kind="explain">

To find a term-to-term rule, look at how consecutive terms relate:

- **Constant difference** (add the same each time) → an **arithmetic** sequence: 3, 7, 11, 15 (+4).
- **Constant ratio** (multiply by the same each time) → a **geometric** sequence: 2, 6, 18, 54 (×3).

If neither is constant, look deeper — perhaps the *differences themselves* form a pattern.

</Step>

<Step title="Beyond the obvious" kind="explain">

Not all sequences are arithmetic or geometric. Some famous ones follow other rules entirely:

- **Square numbers:** 1, 4, 9, 16, 25 (differences grow: +3, +5, +7…).
- **Fibonacci:** 1, 1, 2, 3, 5, 8 (each term is the sum of the previous two).

The limitation of term-to-term rules: to find the 100th term you'd have to compute all 99 before it. The next lesson fixes that with a **position-to-term** formula.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- A **sequence** is an ordered list of **terms** following a rule.
- **Term-to-term** rules: constant difference (**arithmetic**) or constant ratio (**geometric**).
- If neither is constant, look at the **pattern of differences**.
- Term-to-term rules are slow for far-off terms — motivating a position formula next.

</Step>
