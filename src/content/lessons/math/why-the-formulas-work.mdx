Mensuration formulas can feel like things to memorise. They're not — each one comes from a vivid idea you can *see*. Understand the pictures and you'll never need to recall a formula again, because you can rebuild it. Here are the most beautiful derivations on the island.

<Step title="Unrolling a circle into a triangle" kind="explain">

Slice a circle into many thin wedges and lay them out alternately up and down: they form a near-perfect **parallelogram** (a rectangle in the limit). Its height is the radius **r**, and its base is **half the circumference**, πr. So:

<Formula tex="A = \text{base} \times \text{height} = \pi r \times r = \pi r^2" block />

The circle's area formula is just a rectangle's area in disguise — the finer the slices, the better the fit, an idea that becomes **calculus** in the frontier worlds.

</Step>

<Step title="Scaling: the square–cube law" kind="explain">

The most powerful idea in this whole world: when you scale a shape by a factor **k**, its measurements scale differently by dimension:

<Formula tex="\text{lengths} \times k, \qquad \text{areas} \times k^2, \qquad \text{volumes} \times k^3" block />

Double a model's size and its surface area quadruples while its volume grows **eight-fold**. This isn't just maths — it's *why* there are no giant insects (volume/weight outgrows leg strength), why ice cubes melt faster when crushed (more surface per volume), and why small animals lose heat quickly. The square–cube law shapes the living world.

<Callout type="info" title="Geometry reaching into calculus">
"Slice into infinitely many infinitely thin pieces and add them up" is the thread running through the circle, the cone, and the sphere. Formalised, it becomes integration — the calculus you'll meet at the summit of this island.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- Every formula has a **picture**: a circle unrolls into a triangle → A = πr².
- Cones/pyramids are **⅓** of their prism; the sphere is **⅔** of its cylinder.
- The **square–cube law**: scale by k → lengths ×k, areas ×k², volumes ×k³.
- "Slice and add" is geometry reaching toward **calculus**.

</Step>
