<Step title="Two points, two questions" kind="explain">

Given two points on the plane, two natural questions arise: where's the **point exactly between** them, and **how far apart** are they? Coordinates turn both into quick calculations — no ruler required.

</Step>

<Step title="Midpoint: average the coordinates" kind="explain">

The midpoint sits halfway in both directions, so you simply **average** the x's and the y's:

<Formula tex="M = \left( \frac{x_1 + x_2}{2}, \; \frac{y_1 + y_2}{2} \right)" block />

For (−2, −1) and (3, 3): M = ((−2+3)/2, (−1+3)/2) = (½, 1). It's just the centre of mass of two points.

</Step>

<Step title="Distance: Pythagoras in disguise" kind="explain">

The horizontal gap Δx and vertical gap Δy form the legs of a **right triangle**; the straight-line distance is its hypotenuse:

<Formula tex="d = \sqrt{(\Delta x)^2 + (\Delta y)^2}" block />

For those same points: Δx = 5, Δy = 4, so d = √(25 + 16) = √41 ≈ 6.4. This is Pythagoras' theorem (which gets its own World) quietly powering coordinate geometry.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Midpoint** = average the coordinates: ((x₁+x₂)/2, (y₁+y₂)/2).
- **Distance** = √(Δx² + Δy²) — Pythagoras on the horizontal and vertical gaps.
- Coordinates make both purely numerical.

</Step>
