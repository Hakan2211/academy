<Step title="Turning a formula around" kind="explain">

The formula v = u + at gives v from u, a and t. But what if you know v and want **a**? You **rearrange** it — also called "changing the subject." A formula is a balanced statement, and you can do anything to it as long as you do the *same thing to both sides*.

</Step>

<Step title="Step through a rearrangement" kind="interactive">

Undo the operations around your target variable, one inverse at a time, always to both sides.

<RearrangeFormula />

</Step>

<Step title="Inverse operations, in reverse order" kind="explain">

To free a variable, peel away what's attached to it using **inverse** operations (+ undoes −, × undoes ÷, square undoes √):

<Formula tex="v = u + at \;\Rightarrow\; v - u = at \;\Rightarrow\; a = \frac{v - u}{t}" block />

Subtract u, then divide by t — undoing the formula from the outside in, like unwrapping a parcel.

</Step>

<Step title="The same skill as solving equations" kind="explain">

Rearranging a formula and solving an equation are the *identical* process — keeping a balance while isolating a letter. The only difference is that here the other letters stay as letters instead of being numbers. Watch for variables trapped inside roots or on both sides — those need an extra step (square both sides, or gather the variable first), which you'll meet in the Equations world.

<Callout type="tip" title="Whatever you do to one side, do to the other">
That single principle is the heart of all equation work. The "=" is a balance; keep it level and you can never go wrong.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Rearranging** = changing which letter is the subject.
- Apply **inverse operations** to **both sides**, peeling from the outside in.
- It's the **same process** as solving an equation, with letters instead of numbers.
- Variables under roots or on both sides need an extra step.

</Step>
