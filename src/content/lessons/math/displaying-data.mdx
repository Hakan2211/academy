<Step title="A good chart tells the story" kind="explain">

Raw numbers hide their patterns; the right **chart** reveals them at a glance. Different data and different questions call for different displays — the skill is choosing the one that shows what matters without distorting it.

</Step>

<Step title="Bars versus pies" kind="interactive">

The same data, two ways: bars to compare amounts, a pie to show shares of the whole.

</Step>

<Step title="The common charts" kind="explain">

- **Bar chart** — compares the size of separate categories (best for most comparisons).
- **Pie chart** — shows each category's **share** of a whole (slice angle = proportion × 360°).
- **Pictogram** — like a bar chart but with symbols (each symbol = a set number).
- **Line graph** — shows change **over time** (a trend).

Pick by purpose: comparing groups → bars; parts of a whole → pie; change over time → line.

</Step>

<Step title="Reading and drawing carefully" kind="explain">

A chart needs a **title**, **labelled axes**, and a sensible **scale** starting (usually) at zero. A pie chart's slices must total 100%. Small differences are hard to judge in a pie but clear in a bar chart — and, as the deep dive will show, a carelessly (or deliberately) chosen scale can mislead badly.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- The right **chart** makes patterns visible.
- **Bar** = compare categories; **pie** = shares of a whole; **line** = change over time; **pictogram** = symbol-bars.
- Always include a **title, labelled axes, sensible scale**.
- Choose the display by the **question** you're answering.

</Step>
