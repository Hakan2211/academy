<Step title="Two sides of the same coin" kind="explain">

Take two whole numbers that multiply to give a third, like 3 × 4 = 12.

- 12 is a **multiple** of 3 and of 4 (it's in their times tables).
- 3 and 4 are **factors** of 12 (they divide into it exactly, no remainder).

Multiples stretch *upward* without end — 3, 6, 9, 12, … Factors are the finite set of numbers that fit *into* a number perfectly.

</Step>

<Step title="Factors come in pairs" kind="explain">

Factors always arrive in pairs, because every division has a partner: since 2 × 6 = 12, finding the factor 2 hands you 6 for free. That's why you only ever need to test up to the square root — beyond it you're just rediscovering pairs you already found.

</Step>

<Step title="The number with the fewest factors" kind="explain">

Some numbers make many rectangles (12 makes three: 1×12, 2×6, 3×4). Others make only one — just the thin 1 × n line. Those special numbers, with exactly **two** factors (1 and themselves), are the **primes**, and they're the subject of the next lesson.

<Callout type="tip" title="One is the loneliest number">
1 has only a single factor (itself), so it is **not** prime. Primes need exactly two distinct factors. This isn't a technicality — it keeps prime factorisation unique, as you'll see.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- A **multiple** of n is in its times table (infinitely many); a **factor** divides n exactly (finitely many).
- Factors come in **pairs**, so you only test up to √n.
- A number's factor pairs are the rectangles you can make from its dots.
- A number with **exactly two** factors is **prime**; 1 is not prime.

</Step>
