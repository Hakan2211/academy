<Step title="The pointed and the round" kind="explain">

Prisms keep the same cross-section throughout. **Pyramids** and **cones** taper to a point, and **spheres** curve in every direction. Their volume formulas all carry a telltale fraction — and there's a beautiful reason why.

</Step>

<Step title="The one-third family" kind="explain">

A cone or pyramid that *just fits* inside a cylinder or prism holds exactly **one third** of it:

<Formula tex="\text{cone} = \tfrac13 \pi r^2 h, \qquad \text{pyramid} = \tfrac13 \times \text{base area} \times h" block />

You can verify it by pouring: it takes exactly **three** cone-fulls of water to fill the matching cylinder. The taper costs you two-thirds of the space.

</Step>

<Step title="The sphere" kind="explain">

The sphere has its own elegant formula:

<Formula tex="V = \tfrac{4}{3}\pi r^3" block />

Archimedes was so proud of discovering that a sphere is exactly **two-thirds** of its enclosing cylinder that he had the diagram carved on his tombstone. Note the **r³** — volume always involves three lengths, so doubling the radius makes a ball **eight times** bigger.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Cone** ⅓πr²h and **pyramid** ⅓ × base × h — exactly **one third** of the enclosing prism/cylinder.
- **Sphere** = **4⁄3 πr³** — two thirds of its enclosing cylinder (Archimedes).
- Volume scales with **r³**: double the radius → eight times the volume.

</Step>
