<Lesson>

<Step title="What statistics is for" kind="explain">

**Statistics** is the science of collecting, displaying and interpreting **data** — turning piles of numbers into understanding. It's how we test medicines, run elections, and check whether the climate is warming. Step one is knowing what kind of data you have.

</Step>

<Step title="Kinds of data" kind="explain">

- **Qualitative** (categorical): words or labels — colours, names, yes/no.
- **Quantitative** (numerical): numbers, split into **discrete** (counted, whole — number of pets) and **continuous** (measured, any value — height, time).

The type decides which charts and averages make sense — you can find the mean height of a class, but not the mean of their eye colours.

</Step>

<Step title="Why we sample" kind="interactive">

You usually can't measure everyone (the **population**), so you study a **sample** and infer. A bigger, fair sample gives a better estimate.

<SamplingViz />

</Step>

<Step title="Fair samples" kind="explain">

A sample must **represent** the population, or conclusions are worthless. Two pitfalls:

- **Bias** — surveying only gym-goers about exercise overstates fitness.
- **Too small** — five people can't speak for a city.

A **random sample**, where everyone has an equal chance of selection, guards against bias. Getting the sample right is the difference between a poll that predicts an election and one that embarrasses itself.

<Callout type="warn" title="Garbage in, garbage out">
No clever maths can rescue conclusions from a biased sample. How the data is collected matters more than how it's crunched.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Statistics** collects, displays and interprets **data**.
- **Qualitative** (categories) vs **quantitative** (discrete = counted, continuous = measured).
- We study a **sample** to infer about a **population**.
- Samples must be **representative** — avoid bias and tiny sizes; random sampling helps.

</Step>
