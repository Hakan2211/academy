<Step title="π ties it all together" kind="explain">

Every circle's measurements run on **π ≈ 3.14159**, the ratio of circumference to diameter. From the radius alone you can find both the distance around and the area inside.

<Formula tex="C = 2\pi r = \pi d, \qquad A = \pi r^2" block />

</Step>

<Step title="Circumference: distance around" kind="explain">

Since π = C ÷ d, rearranging gives **C = πd = 2πr**. A wheel of radius 30 cm has circumference 2π × 30 ≈ 188 cm — so it rolls 1.88 m per turn. Circumference is a *length*, measured in cm or m.

</Step>

<Step title="Area: space inside" kind="explain">

The area is **πr²** — note the radius is **squared**, so area grows fast. Double the radius and the circumference doubles, but the area **quadruples** (it scales by the square, just like the area rule from the conversions lesson).

For **sectors and arcs**, take the right fraction of the whole: a 90° sector is ¼ of the circle, so ¼ of πr² for its area and ¼ of 2πr for its arc.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **C = 2πr = πd** (circumference, a length).
- **A = πr²** (area, square units — r is squared).
- Double r → circumference ×2 but area ×4.
- **Sectors/arcs** are a fraction (angle/360) of the whole circle.

</Step>
