<Step title="Beyond the right triangle" kind="explain">

Right-triangle trig only handles angles between 0° and 90°. The **unit circle** — a circle of radius 1 — frees trig to work for *any* angle. A radius drawn at angle θ has its tip at exactly **(cos θ, sin θ)**. Trig becomes a circle's geometry.

</Step>

<Step title="Walk around the circle" kind="interactive">

The horizontal coordinate of the tip is cos θ; the vertical is sin θ.

<UnitCircle />

</Step>

<Step title="Exact values worth knowing" kind="explain">

A few special angles give clean, exact values (no decimals) that come up constantly:

<Formula tex="\sin 30° = \tfrac12, \quad \cos 60° = \tfrac12, \quad \sin 45° = \cos 45° = \tfrac{\sqrt2}{2}, \quad \tan 60° = \sqrt3" block />

These come from two special triangles: half an equilateral triangle (giving 30° and 60°) and half a square (giving 45°). Worth memorising — they appear in exam questions and elegant results everywhere.

</Step>

<Step title="Why the circle matters" kind="explain">

On the unit circle you can see *why* sin and cos **repeat every 360°** (you're back where you started) and why they swing between −1 and +1. Angles past 90° get negative coordinates, extending sin and cos smoothly into a **wave** — exactly the sine curve from the function gallery. This is the bridge from triangles to the trigonometry of waves, oscillations, and circular motion.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- The **unit circle** extends trig to **any** angle: tip = (cos θ, sin θ).
- **Exact values** (30°, 45°, 60°) come from half-equilateral and half-square triangles.
- sin and cos **repeat every 360°** and stay between −1 and 1.
- The circle turns trig ratios into the **sine wave** — the link to oscillations.

</Step>
