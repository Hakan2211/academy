<Step title="Identical versus same-shape" kind="explain">

Two words with precise meanings:

- **Congruent** — exactly the same shape *and* size. You could pick one up and place it perfectly on the other (allowing flips and turns).
- **Similar** — the same shape but possibly a different size: one is a scaled copy of the other.

Every congruent pair is also similar (scale factor 1), but not vice versa.

</Step>

<Step title="When are triangles congruent?" kind="explain">

You don't need to check all six measurements (3 sides, 3 angles). Any **one** of these conditions guarantees two triangles are congruent:

- **SSS** — three sides match.
- **SAS** — two sides and the angle between them.
- **ASA** — two angles and the side between them.
- **RHS** — right angle, hypotenuse, and one side.

These are the proof tools of geometry — establish one and the triangles are identical.

</Step>

<Step title="Similar triangles and proportions" kind="explain">

**Similar** triangles have equal angles and sides in the same ratio. That single fact is enormously useful: it lets you find unknown lengths by **proportion**.

<Formula tex="\frac{\text{side in big}}{\text{matching side in small}} = \text{scale factor (same for all sides)}" block />

It's how you measure a tree's height from its shadow, or a building from a photo — set up similar triangles and solve a proportion. (Areas scale by the factor², as always.)

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Congruent** = same shape and size; **similar** = same shape, scaled.
- Triangle congruence: **SSS, SAS, ASA, RHS** — one is enough.
- **Similar** triangles: equal angles, sides in a fixed ratio — solve unknowns by **proportion**.
- Areas of similar shapes scale by the factor **squared**.

</Step>
