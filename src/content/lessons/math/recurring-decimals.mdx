<Step title="Why ⅓ never finishes" kind="explain">

Type 1 ÷ 3 into a calculator and it answers 0.3333… forever. Yet ⅓ is a perfectly ordinary, *exact* number. So what's going on with its decimal?

The answer reveals something deep: a fraction's decimal must **either stop or repeat** — it can never be random — and the reason is a beautifully simple counting argument.

</Step>

<Step title="Watch the remainders loop" kind="interactive">

Do the long division and track the **remainders**. The moment a remainder repeats, the digits must repeat too — the division has entered a loop it can never escape.

<RecurringDecimal />

</Step>

<Step title="The pigeonhole reason" kind="explain">

When you divide by *d*, the only possible remainders are 0, 1, 2, …, *d* − 1 — just **d** options. So within at most *d* steps, either you hit remainder 0 (the decimal **terminates**) or a remainder **repeats** — and once it does, every following digit repeats in the same cycle.

This is the **pigeonhole principle**: with more steps than possible remainders, a repeat is unavoidable. A fraction *cannot* produce an endless non-repeating decimal — which is exactly why irrational numbers like π are *not* fractions.

</Step>

<Step title="Turning a recurring decimal back into a fraction" kind="explain">

The loop also lets us prove surprising equalities. Let x = 0.7777…, then multiply by 10 to shift one full cycle and subtract:

<Formula tex="10x = 7.777\ldots,\quad 10x - x = 7 \;\Rightarrow\; 9x = 7 \;\Rightarrow\; x = \tfrac{7}{9}" block />

The same trick delivers the famous result. Let x = 0.999…:

<Formula tex="10x - x = 9 \;\Rightarrow\; 9x = 9 \;\Rightarrow\; x = 1" block />

<Callout type="info" title="0.999… really does equal 1">
This isn't a rounding fudge — it's an exact equality. 0.999… and 1 are two decimal names for the very same point on the number line, just as ½ and 0.5 are.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Which fractions terminate?" kind="explain">

A fraction in simplest form terminates exactly when its denominator's only prime factors are **2 and 5** — the primes of base ten. So 7/40 terminates (40 = 2³ × 5) but 1/6 recurs (6 = 2 × 3, and that stubborn 3 forces the loop). Prime factorisation strikes again.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- Every fraction's decimal **terminates or recurs** — never endless-and-random.
- The reason is the **pigeonhole principle**: only *d* possible remainders, so one must repeat.
- The **10ⁿ-subtraction trick** converts any recurring decimal back to a fraction (and proves 0.999… = 1).
- A fraction terminates iff its denominator's primes are only **2 and 5**.

</Step>
