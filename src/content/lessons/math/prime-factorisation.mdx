<Step title="Every number's prime fingerprint" kind="explain">

Primes are the building blocks of all whole numbers. **Prime factorisation** breaks a number down into the primes that multiply to make it:

<Formula tex="60 = 2 \times 2 \times 3 \times 5 = 2^2 \times 3 \times 5" block />

This list of primes is like a fingerprint — and remarkably, **every** number above 1 has exactly one.

</Step>

<Step title="Build a factor tree" kind="interactive">

Split a number into any two factors, then keep splitting the composite parts until only primes remain at the leaves.

<FactorTree />

</Step>

<Step title="It doesn't matter how you split" kind="explain">

Start 60 as 6 × 10 or as 4 × 15 — the branches look different, but the **leaves are always the same**: two 2s, a 3 and a 5. This is the **Fundamental Theorem of Arithmetic**: every whole number above 1 has a *unique* prime factorisation (ignoring order).

We collect repeats using **powers**: 2 × 2 × 2 becomes 2³. So 360 = 2³ × 3² × 5.

</Step>

<Step title="Why it's so useful" kind="explain">

Once you know a number's primes, a lot becomes easy:

- the number of factors,
- the highest common factor and lowest common multiple of two numbers (next lesson),
- whether a number is a perfect square (every prime power is even).

Prime factorisation turns hard questions about a number into easy questions about its building blocks.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Prime factorisation** writes a number as a product of primes.
- The **Fundamental Theorem of Arithmetic**: that factorisation is **unique**.
- Repeated primes are gathered into **powers** (2×2×2 = 2³).
- Knowing the primes makes factors, HCF, LCM and square-checks easy.

</Step>
