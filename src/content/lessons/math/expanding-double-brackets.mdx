<Lesson>

<Step title="Two brackets, every pair" kind="explain">

Multiplying two brackets, (x + 3)(x + 2), means every term in the first must multiply every term in the second — four products in all. Many people use **FOIL**: **F**irsts, **O**uters, **I**nners, **L**asts. But the deeper idea is simply "each times each."

</Step>

<Step title="See the four products" kind="interactive">

A rectangle split into a 2×2 grid: the four regions are exactly the four products, and their total area is the expansion.

<DoubleBrackets />

</Step>

<Step title="The result is a quadratic" kind="explain">

<Formula tex="(x + 3)(x + 2) = x^2 + 2x + 3x + 6 = x^2 + 5x + 6" block />

The two x-terms (2x and 3x) combine into the **middle term**, and the constants multiply into the last. This shape — x² + (sum)x + (product) — is a **quadratic**, the star of this whole world. Notice: the middle coefficient is a + b, and the constant is a × b. Remember that; it's the key to factorising.

</Step>

<Step title="Squaring a bracket" kind="explain">

A common case is a bracket squared — and it is **not** just squaring each term:

<Formula tex="(x + 4)^2 = (x+4)(x+4) = x^2 + 8x + 16" block />

The middle term 8x is the trap people miss. Always write the bracket out twice rather than "squaring through."

<Callout type="warn" title="(x + 4)² ≠ x² + 16">
Squaring a sum is never the sum of squares. The cross terms — here 2·(x)(4) = 8x — are real and matter.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- Expand double brackets by multiplying **each term by each** (FOIL = the four products).
- The result is a **quadratic** x² + (a+b)x + ab.
- The two x-terms combine into the **middle** term.
- **(x + a)² has a middle term** — write it out twice, never square through.

</Step>
