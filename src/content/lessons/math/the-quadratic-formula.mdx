<Step title="One formula to solve them all" kind="explain">

Complete the square on the *general* quadratic ax² + bx + c = 0 — keeping a, b, c as letters — and out drops a formula that solves **every** quadratic in one step:

<Formula tex="x = \frac{-b \pm \sqrt{b^2 - 4ac}}{2a}" block />

It's worth memorising. Identify a, b, c, substitute, and turn the handle.

</Step>

<Step title="The discriminant decides everything" kind="explain">

The part under the root, **b² − 4ac**, is called the **discriminant**, and its sign alone tells you how many solutions exist — before you finish the sum:

- **b² − 4ac > 0** → two real roots (the parabola crosses the x-axis twice).
- **b² − 4ac = 0** → one repeated root (it just touches the axis).
- **b² − 4ac < 0** → no real roots (it never reaches the axis; you'd need the *imaginary* numbers hinted at in World 1).

</Step>

<Step title="A worked example" kind="explain">

Solve 2x² − 7x + 3 = 0, so a = 2, b = −7, c = 3:

<Formula tex="x = \frac{7 \pm \sqrt{49 - 24}}{4} = \frac{7 \pm 5}{4} = 3 \;\text{ or }\; \tfrac{1}{2}" block />

The formula never fails. Factorising is faster *when it works*; the formula is the reliable fallback that always works.

<Callout type="tip" title="Watch the signs of b">
With b = −7, the −b in the formula becomes +7. Putting b into the formula with its sign is the number-one source of errors — use brackets.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- The **quadratic formula** x = (−b ± √(b²−4ac)) / 2a solves every quadratic.
- The **discriminant b² − 4ac** counts the roots: positive → 2, zero → 1, negative → 0 (real).
- Substitute a, b, c **with their signs** (use brackets).
- Factorise when easy; reach for the formula when not.

</Step>
