<Step title="Two unknowns need two equations" kind="explain">

One equation with two unknowns — like x + y = 10 — has *many* solutions (1 and 9, 4 and 6, …). To pin down both, you need a **second** equation true at the same time. A pair like this is called **simultaneous equations**, and the solution is the (x, y) that satisfies **both at once**.

</Step>

<Step title="Where the lines cross" kind="interactive">

Each equation is a straight line. The single point where they intersect is the simultaneous solution.

<SimultaneousGraph />

</Step>

<Step title="Solving by elimination" kind="explain">

Graphing shows the answer; **elimination** finds it exactly. Line up the equations so adding or subtracting cancels one variable:

<Formula tex="\begin{aligned} 3x + y &= 11 \\ x + y &= 5 \end{aligned} \;\xrightarrow{\text{subtract}}\; 2x = 6 \;\Rightarrow\; x = 3" block />

Then substitute back: x + y = 5 gives 3 + y = 5, so y = 2. Solution **(3, 2)** — exactly where the lines meet.

</Step>

<Step title="Solving by substitution" kind="explain">

The alternative: rearrange one equation for a variable, then substitute into the other. From y = 5 − x:

<Formula tex="3x + (5 - x) = 11 \;\to\; 2x + 5 = 11 \;\to\; x = 3,\; y = 2" block />

Two methods, same answer. Elimination shines when coefficients line up; substitution shines when one variable is already (or easily made) the subject.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Simultaneous equations** share a solution true for both — graphically, their **intersection**.
- **Elimination**: add/subtract to cancel a variable, then back-substitute.
- **Substitution**: make one variable the subject, then sub into the other.
- Two unknowns need two independent equations to pin down.

</Step>
