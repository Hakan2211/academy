<Step title="An equation is a claim of balance" kind="explain">

An **expression** (like 2x + 3) just describes a quantity. An **equation** puts an equals sign between two expressions and *claims they're equal*: 2x + 3 = 11. The equals sign is a balance — both sides weigh the same.

**Solving** means finding the value of the unknown that makes the claim true.

</Step>

<Step title="Find the balancing value" kind="interactive">

Slide x. The beam only levels at the one value that makes both sides equal — that's the solution.

<BalanceScale />

</Step>

<Step title="The golden rule" kind="explain">

Because the two sides are equal, you may do **anything to the equation, as long as you do it to both sides**. Add, subtract, multiply, divide — the balance stays level:

<Formula tex="2x + 3 = 11 \;\xrightarrow{\;-3\;}\; 2x = 8 \;\xrightarrow{\;\div 2\;}\; x = 4" block />

Each move keeps the scales balanced while stripping away everything around x.

</Step>

<Step title="Always check" kind="explain">

A solution is checkable — substitute it back and see if the balance truly holds:

<Formula tex="2(4) + 3 = 8 + 3 = 11 \checkmark" block />

This is one of the joys of equations: you never have to wonder whether you're right. Plug it in and the equation confirms it for you.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- An **equation** claims two expressions are equal; **solving** finds the unknown that makes it true.
- The equals sign is a **balance** — level only at the solution.
- **Golden rule:** do the same operation to **both sides**.
- **Check** by substituting back — the equation verifies itself.

</Step>
