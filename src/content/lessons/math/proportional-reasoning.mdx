<Lesson>

<Step title="One idea, a thousand uses" kind="explain">

Proportion is quietly everywhere: recipes, exchange rates, scaling drawings, mixing paint, comparing prices, converting units, reading maps. Master one tool — the **unitary method** — and all of them become the same problem.

</Step>

<Step title="The unitary method" kind="interactive">

The move is always the same: **find the value of ONE**, then scale to as many as you need.

<UnitaryMethod />

</Step>

<Step title="Best buy: compare per unit" kind="explain">

Which is better value — 6 eggs for £1.50 or 10 eggs for £2.30? Don't compare totals; compare the **unit price**:

<Formula tex="\tfrac{150}{6} = 25\text{p each} \qquad \tfrac{230}{10} = 23\text{p each}" block />

The 10-pack wins. Reducing everything to "per one" makes unlike quantities instantly comparable — the same idea behind price labels showing "£/100 g."

</Step>

<Step title="Scaling a recipe" kind="explain">

A recipe for 4 serves 600 g flour; you're cooking for 6. Find one serving (600 ÷ 4 = 150 g), then scale (× 6 = 900 g). Or notice the **scale factor** directly: 6 ÷ 4 = 1.5, so multiply every ingredient by 1.5. Two routes, same answer — proportional reasoning gives you both.

</Step>

<Step title="When proportion does NOT apply" kind="explain">

The sharpest skill is knowing when *not* to use it. These are **not** proportional:

- A baby grows 25 cm in its first year — it will not grow 250 cm in ten.
- One song takes 3 minutes to download; with a fixed connection fee, ten songs aren't simply 30 minutes of setup.
- Two taps fill a bath in 8 minutes — one tap takes 16, not 4 (that's inverse, but the *relationship* must be checked, not assumed).

Always ask: *if I double the input, does the output truly double?* If not, it isn't direct proportion.

<Callout type="info" title="The deepest pattern in this world">
Repeated proportional change — multiplying again and again — is no longer proportional at all. It becomes **exponential**: compound interest, population growth, radioactive decay. You'll meet that beast in Functions & Graphs.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- The **unitary method** — find one, then scale — solves nearly every proportion problem.
- **Best buys**: compare the **unit price**, not the total.
- **Recipes/conversions**: multiply by the scale factor (new ÷ old).
- Always test "**does doubling the input double the output?**" — and remember repeated multiplying becomes **exponential**, not proportional.

</Step>
