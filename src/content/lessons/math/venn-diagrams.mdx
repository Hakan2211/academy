<Step title="Sorting into overlapping groups" kind="explain">

A **Venn diagram** sorts things into overlapping circles, so you can see how groups relate. Where circles overlap are the items in **both** groups. They make "and / or / not" questions visual — and they come with a compact **set notation**.

</Step>

<Step title="The set symbols" kind="explain">

- **A ∩ B** — the **intersection**: in A **and** B (the overlap).
- **A ∪ B** — the **union**: in A **or** B (everything in either circle).
- **A′** — the **complement**: **not** in A (everything outside it).
- **n(A)** — the **number** of items in A.

The diagram and the notation say the same thing two ways — the picture builds intuition, the symbols make it precise.

</Step>

<Step title="Venn diagrams find probabilities" kind="explain">

Fill a Venn with counts and probability falls right out. With 25 people, if 20 like tea or coffee, then:

<Formula tex="P(\text{tea or coffee}) = \frac{n(A \cup B)}{\text{total}} = \frac{20}{25} = 0.8" block />

A key fact the diagram makes obvious — the overlap is counted once, not twice:

<Formula tex="n(A \cup B) = n(A) + n(B) - n(A \cap B)" block />

(adding both circles double-counts the middle, so subtract it back).

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- A **Venn diagram** shows overlapping groups; the overlap is "in both."
- **∩** = and (intersection), **∪** = or (union), **A′** = not A (complement).
- **n(A ∪ B) = n(A) + n(B) − n(A ∩ B)** (don't double-count the overlap).
- Counts in a Venn give probabilities directly.

</Step>
