<Step title="Gradient tells you the relationship" kind="explain">

Two lines' gradients reveal how they relate. **Parallel** lines never meet — and that's exactly because they have the **same gradient** (same steepness, just shifted). **Perpendicular** lines cross at a right angle, and their gradients follow a more surprising rule.

</Step>

<Step title="See the right angle hold" kind="interactive">

Change line 1's gradient; line 2 adjusts to stay perpendicular. Watch their gradients' product.

<PerpendicularLines />

</Step>

<Step title="Parallel: equal gradients" kind="explain">

y = 3x + 1 and y = 3x − 4 are parallel — both climb at gradient 3, so they keep a constant gap and never cross:

<Formula tex="\text{parallel} \iff m_1 = m_2" block />

The intercepts differ; only the gradient must match.

</Step>

<Step title="Perpendicular: negative reciprocals" kind="explain">

For a right angle, the gradients must be **negative reciprocals** — flip the fraction and change the sign:

<Formula tex="m_1 \times m_2 = -1, \qquad \text{so } m_2 = -\frac{1}{m_1}" block />

If one line has gradient 2, a perpendicular one has gradient −½. (A horizontal line, m = 0, is perpendicular to a vertical line, whose gradient is undefined — the one special case.)

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Parallel** lines have **equal gradients** (m₁ = m₂); only intercepts differ.
- **Perpendicular** lines have **negative-reciprocal** gradients (m₁ × m₂ = −1).
- Flip the fraction, change the sign, to get a perpendicular gradient.
- Horizontal ⟂ vertical is the lone special case.

</Step>
