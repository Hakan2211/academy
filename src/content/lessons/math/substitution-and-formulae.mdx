<Step title="Formulae are reusable recipes" kind="explain">

A **formula** is an equation that states a rule connecting quantities — like the area of a rectangle, A = l × w, or the cost of a taxi, C = 3 + 2m. **Substitution** is the act of replacing each letter with a number and working out the result.

</Step>

<Step title="Turn the handle" kind="interactive">

Feed numbers into the formula v = u + at and watch the value come out — following the order of operations.

</Step>

<Step title="Substitute carefully" kind="explain">

Two habits prevent almost all mistakes:

- Restore the hidden operations: 3x means 3 × x, and x² means x × x.
- Obey **BODMAS** after substituting.

For y = 3x² when x = 4:

<Formula tex="y = 3 \times 4^2 = 3 \times 16 = 48" block />

Not (3 × 4)² = 144 — the index applies to the 4 only. And with negatives, use brackets: if x = −2, then x² = (−2)² = +4.

</Step>

<Step title="Formulae are everywhere" kind="explain">

Science and daily life run on them: speed = distance ÷ time, the area of a circle A = πr², converting temperature F = 1.8C + 32, or compound interest. Learn to substitute fluently and every one of these becomes a quick plug-in calculation. (Turning a formula around to find a *different* letter is the next lesson.)

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- A **formula** is a reusable rule; **substitution** plugs numbers in for the letters.
- Restore hidden operations (3x = 3·x, x² = x·x) and obey **BODMAS**.
- Use **brackets** for negative substitutions: (−2)² = 4.
- Fluent substitution powers every applied calculation.

</Step>
