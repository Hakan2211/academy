<Step title="Bars for continuous data" kind="explain">

A **histogram** looks like a bar chart but is different in two key ways: it's for **continuous** grouped data, and its bars **touch** (because the classes are adjacent intervals, with no gaps). It shows the *shape* of a distribution — where data piles up and how it tails off.

</Step>

</Step>

<Step title="The crucial idea: area = frequency" kind="explain">

In a true histogram, it's the **area** of each bar — not its height — that represents frequency. When all classes are the same width, height works fine. But with **unequal** class widths you must use **frequency density** on the vertical axis:

<Formula tex="\text{frequency density} = \frac{\text{frequency}}{\text{class width}}" block />

A wide class with a tall raw count shouldn't dominate just because it's wide — frequency density keeps every bar's *area* honest.

</Step>

<Step title="What the shape tells you" kind="explain">

The silhouette of a histogram is informative at a glance:

- **Symmetric / bell-shaped** — values cluster around a central mean (the normal distribution).
- **Skewed** — a long tail one side (incomes skew right: most modest, a few enormous).
- **Bimodal** — two peaks, often two mixed groups (e.g. heights of a mixed-sex sample).

Reading shape is how statisticians size up data before any calculation.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- A **histogram** shows **continuous** grouped data; bars **touch**.
- **Area = frequency**; with unequal widths, plot **frequency density = frequency ÷ width**.
- The **shape** reveals symmetry, skew, or multiple peaks.

</Step>
