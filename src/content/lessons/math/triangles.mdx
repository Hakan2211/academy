<Step title="The 180° rule" kind="explain">

The three interior angles of **any** triangle — no matter its shape — always add to exactly **180°**. It's the single most useful fact in geometry: know two angles and the third is forced.

</Step>

<Step title="Why 180°?" kind="explain">

Draw a line through the top vertex parallel to the base. The two base angles reappear at the top as **alternate angles** (from the last lesson!), sitting either side of the apex angle. Together the three angles lie along a **straight line** — which is 180°. The parallel-line rules quietly prove the triangle rule.

</Step>

<Step title="Naming triangles" kind="explain">

By sides:

- **Equilateral** — all sides equal (and all angles 60°).
- **Isosceles** — two sides equal (and the two base angles equal).
- **Scalene** — all different.

By angle: **acute** (all &lt; 90°), **right** (one 90°), or **obtuse** (one &gt; 90°). The **exterior angle** of a triangle equals the sum of the two opposite interior angles — another handy shortcut.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- The interior angles of any triangle sum to **180°**.
- This follows from the **alternate-angle** parallel-line rule.
- By sides: **equilateral, isosceles, scalene**; by angle: **acute, right, obtuse**.
- **Exterior angle = sum of the two opposite interior angles.**

</Step>
