<Step title="The most famous theorem" kind="explain">

In any **right-angled triangle**, the square of the longest side (the **hypotenuse**, opposite the right angle) equals the sum of the squares of the other two:

<Formula tex="a^2 + b^2 = c^2" block />

It's over 2,500 years old, has hundreds of proofs, and connects the lengths of a right triangle with perfect precision.

</Step>

<Step title="See it as areas" kind="interactive">

The theorem is literally about areas: the square on the hypotenuse equals the two smaller squares combined.

<PythagorasViz />

</Step>

<Step title="Finding a side" kind="explain">

Know two sides, find the third. For the hypotenuse, add and square-root:

<Formula tex="c = \sqrt{a^2 + b^2}" block />

For a shorter side, rearrange and **subtract**: a = √(c² − b²). The famous **3-4-5** triangle works because 3² + 4² = 9 + 16 = 25 = 5² — a right triangle with whole-number sides.

</Step>

<Step title="Where it's used" kind="explain">

Pythagoras is everywhere distances matter: the diagonal of a screen, whether a corner is square in construction, the straight-line distance between two map points (which is exactly the coordinate distance formula from World 9, √(Δx² + Δy²)). Any time a right angle appears, Pythagoras is waiting.

<Callout type="tip" title="Only for right angles">
The theorem needs a right angle. For other triangles you'll use the cosine rule later — which is Pythagoras with a correction term.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- In a right triangle, **a² + b² = c²** (c = hypotenuse).
- It's an **area** relationship: the big square = the two small squares.
- Hypotenuse: **√(a² + b²)**; a shorter side: **√(c² − b²)**.
- Used for any distance involving a right angle (incl. coordinate distance).

</Step>
