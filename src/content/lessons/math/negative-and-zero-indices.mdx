<Lesson>

<Step title="What could a⁰ possibly mean?" kind="explain">

"Multiply a by itself **zero** times" sounds like nonsense. The answer comes not from the words but from the **pattern**: each step down the ladder divides by the base. Keep going past a¹ and you're forced to a⁰ — and there's only one value it can be.

</Step>

<Step title="Follow the pattern down" kind="interactive">

Step the exponent below 1 and watch the dividing continue.

<PowerExplorer />

</Step>

<Step title="Zero index = 1" kind="explain">

The divide law makes it airtight. Anything divided by itself is 1:

<Formula tex="\frac{a^m}{a^m} = a^{m-m} = a^0, \quad \text{but also } \frac{a^m}{a^m} = 1 \;\Rightarrow\; a^0 = 1" block />

So **any non-zero number to the power 0 is 1** — 7⁰, 1000⁰, even (−3)⁰ all equal 1.

</Step>

<Step title="Negative index = reciprocal" kind="explain">

Keep dividing below a⁰ and you cross into fractions:

<Formula tex="a^{-n} = \frac{1}{a^{n}}" block />

A negative index means "one over." So 2⁻³ = 1/2³ = 1/8, and 10⁻² = 0.01. The minus sign doesn't make the number negative — it flips it to a reciprocal. This is exactly how tiny numbers get written in standard form.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- The pattern "divide by the base each step down" forces the meanings.
- **a⁰ = 1** for any non-zero a (from aᵐ ÷ aᵐ).
- **a⁻ⁿ = 1/aⁿ** — a negative index is a **reciprocal**, not a negative number.
- These extend the index laws smoothly to all integers.

</Step>
