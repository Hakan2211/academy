<Step title="The average isn't enough" kind="explain">

Two factories both average 100 widgets a day. One makes 98–102; the other swings 60–140. Same mean, wildly different **consistency**. To capture that, we measure **spread** — and the gold-standard measure is the **standard deviation**.

</Step>

<Step title="Stretch the data" kind="interactive">

Same mean throughout — only the spread, and so the standard deviation, changes.

<StandardDeviation />

</Step>

<Step title="What standard deviation means" kind="explain">

Standard deviation (SD) is the **typical distance of a value from the mean**. You compute it by averaging the *squared* distances, then square-rooting:

<Formula tex="\text{SD} = \sqrt{\frac{\sum (x - \bar{x})^2}{n}}" block />

We square the distances so positives and negatives don't cancel, then take the root to return to the original units. A small SD means tightly clustered, predictable data; a large SD means spread out and variable.

</Step>

<Step title="Why it rules statistics" kind="explain">

Standard deviation is everywhere serious data lives. In a typical (normal) distribution, about **68%** of values lie within 1 SD of the mean and **95%** within 2 SD — the rule behind exam grade boundaries, manufacturing tolerances, and "statistically significant" results in science. It turns "how unusual is this value?" into a precise number of standard deviations from the mean (a **z-score**).

<Callout type="info" title="The bell curve">
So many natural quantities — heights, measurement errors, test scores — pile up into the symmetric bell-shaped normal distribution, fully described by just its mean and standard deviation. SD is the ruler of that bell.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Spread** measures consistency; **standard deviation** is the key measure.
- SD = √(average **squared** distance from the mean) — small SD = clustered, large SD = spread.
- In a normal distribution, ~**68%** fall within 1 SD, ~**95%** within 2 SD.
- SD underpins the **bell curve**, z-scores, and statistical significance.

</Step>
