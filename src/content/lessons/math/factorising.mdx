<Lesson>

<Step title="Expanding in reverse" kind="explain">

**Factorising** is expanding run backwards: instead of removing a bracket, you put one *in*. You spot what every term has in common — the **highest common factor** — and pull it outside:

<Formula tex="6x + 9 = 3(2x + 3)" block />

Check by expanding: 3 × 2x = 6x, 3 × 3 = 9. ✓ Factorising and expanding are mirror images.

</Step>

<Step title="The area model, read backwards" kind="interactive">

Expanding builds the rectangle's pieces from its sides; factorising starts with the pieces and finds the sides.

<ExpandBrackets />

</Step>

<Step title="Find the common factor" kind="explain">

Take out the most you can — both the **number** HCF and any **shared letters**:

<Formula tex="8x^2 + 12x = 4x(2x + 3)" block />

Here every term shares a factor of 4 *and* an x, so 4x comes out. Always pull out the **highest** common factor; 2(4x² + 6x) is factorised but not *fully*.

<Callout type="tip" title="Always check by expanding">
Multiply your answer back out. If it doesn't return the original expression, the factor was wrong or incomplete. It's a free, foolproof check.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Why factorising is powerful" kind="explain">

A factorised expression reveals when something equals zero: 3(2x + 3) is zero exactly when 2x + 3 = 0. That "**if a product is zero, a factor must be zero**" idea is the master key to solving equations — and it's the whole engine of the Quadratics world ahead.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Factorising** = expanding reversed: insert a bracket by taking out a common factor.
- Pull out the **highest** common factor — number *and* shared letters.
- **Check** by expanding back to the original.
- Factorised form exposes **zeros**, the key to solving equations.

</Step>
