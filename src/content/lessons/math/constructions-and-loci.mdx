<Step title="Geometry with compass and straight-edge" kind="explain">

Long before rulers had millimetre marks, geometers built exact figures with just a **compass** and a **straight-edge**. These **constructions** produce perfect bisectors and perpendiculars — and they reveal a deeper idea: a **locus**, the set of *all* points obeying a rule.

</Step>

<Step title="Three classic loci" kind="interactive">

Each rule about "where points can be" traces out a familiar shape.

<LociViewer />

</Step>

<Step title="The key loci" kind="explain">

- **Fixed distance from a point** → a **circle** (radius = that distance).
- **Equidistant from two points A and B** → the **perpendicular bisector** of AB.
- **Equidistant from two lines** → the **angle bisector** between them.

Each is constructed with compass arcs: equal arcs from A and B cross on the perpendicular bisector; equal arcs from a vertex give the angle bisector.

</Step>

<Step title="Loci solve real problems" kind="explain">

Combine loci to pin down a region. "Where can a goat tied to a 5 m rope at a corner graze?" → inside a circle (locus of fixed distance) bounded by the walls. "Where is a point both within 3 km of town A and nearer to road X than road Y?" → the overlap of a circle and a half-plane.

<Callout type="info" title="A locus is a graph in disguise">
"All points equidistant from the origin" is a circle — which is exactly the equation x² + y² = r². Loci are the geometric face of equations; the coordinate plane united them. Construction is doing geometry by hand; algebra does the same job with symbols.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Constructions** use compass and straight-edge for exact bisectors/perpendiculars.
- A **locus** is the set of all points obeying a rule.
- Fixed distance from a point → **circle**; equidistant from two points → **perpendicular bisector**; from two lines → **angle bisector**.
- Loci are the geometric form of **equations** (a circle is x² + y² = r²).

</Step>
