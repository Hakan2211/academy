<Step title="Writing the huge and the tiny" kind="explain">

The Sun's mass is about 1 990 000 000 000 000 000 000 000 000 000 kg. A hydrogen atom is 0.000 000 000 1 m across. Writing these out is error-prone and unreadable. **Standard form** (scientific notation) fixes that with powers of ten:

<Formula tex="a \times 10^{n}, \qquad 1 \le a < 10" block />

A single digit before the point, times a power of ten that records the scale.

</Step>

<Step title="Converting both ways" kind="explain">

The exponent counts how far the decimal point moves:

- **Big numbers** → positive power. 6 200 000 = 6.2 × 10⁶ (point moved 6 places left).
- **Tiny numbers** → negative power. 0.00043 = 4.3 × 10⁻⁴ (point moved 4 places right).

The rule for *a*: exactly one non-zero digit before the decimal point. 62 × 10⁵ is the right value but **not** standard form — fix it to 6.2 × 10⁶.

</Step>

<Step title="Why scientists live in standard form" kind="explain">

Beyond saving ink, standard form makes the **size** of a number obvious at a glance — the exponent *is* the scale. And it makes multiplying and dividing easy, because you just use the index laws on the powers of ten:

<Formula tex="(3 \times 10^{4}) \times (2 \times 10^{5}) = 6 \times 10^{9}" block />

Multiply the fronts, add the powers. That's the engine you'll run in the deep dive.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Standard form** is a × 10ⁿ with **1 ≤ a &lt; 10**.
- Positive power = big number; negative power = tiny number; the power counts decimal-point moves.
- Exactly **one** non-zero digit before the point.
- Multiply/divide by combining the fronts and **adding/subtracting** the powers.

</Step>
