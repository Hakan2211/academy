<Step title="Linking angles to sides" kind="explain">

Pythagoras connects the *sides* of a right triangle. **Trigonometry** connects the **angles** to the sides. The three trig ratios — **sine, cosine, tangent** — each compare two sides, and remarkably depend *only* on the angle, not the triangle's size.

</Step>

<Step title="SOH-CAH-TOA, live" kind="interactive">

Change the angle and watch the ratios. Relative to the angle, the sides are named **opposite**, **adjacent**, and **hypotenuse**.

<TrigRatios />

</Step>

<Step title="The three ratios" kind="explain">

The memory aid **SOH-CAH-TOA** captures all three:

<Formula tex="\sin\theta = \frac{O}{H}, \quad \cos\theta = \frac{A}{H}, \quad \tan\theta = \frac{O}{A}" block />

- **S**ine = **O**pposite / **H**ypotenuse
- **C**osine = **A**djacent / **H**ypotenuse
- **T**angent = **O**pposite / **A**djacent

Because similar triangles have proportional sides, these ratios are fixed for each angle — which is why a calculator can store sin 30° = 0.5 once and for all.

</Step>

<Step title="Why it works" kind="explain">

All right triangles with the same angle θ are **similar** (from the geometry world), so their sides are in the same ratio. That ratio *is* the trig value. The Greeks and Indians tabulated these ratios to do astronomy; today they underpin GPS, signal processing, and every wave in physics.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- Trig links a right triangle's **angle** to its **side ratios**.
- **SOH-CAH-TOA**: sin = O/H, cos = A/H, tan = O/A.
- Ratios depend only on the **angle** (similar triangles), so they're fixed.
- They power astronomy, GPS, and all wave science.

</Step>
