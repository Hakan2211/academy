<Lesson>

<Step title="A letter is a number you don't know yet" kind="explain">

Algebra's big move is using a **letter** to stand for a number. Sometimes it's a number we haven't found (an unknown, like x in a puzzle); sometimes it's a number that can **vary** (a variable, like t for time). Either way, the letter lets us write rules that work for *every* number at once.

"A number, doubled, plus three" becomes simply **2x + 3** — short, exact, and ready to calculate with.

</Step>

<Step title="The shorthand rules" kind="explain">

Algebra has tidy conventions:

- **3x** means 3 × x (the × is dropped).
- **x²** means x × x; **xy** means x × y.
- **x/2** means x ÷ 2.
- We don't write **1x**, just x.

A **term** is a single chunk (like 3x or −5); an **expression** is terms joined by + and − (like 3x + 2y − 5).

</Step>

<Step title="Why bother with letters?" kind="explain">

Because one expression captures infinitely many cases. The perimeter of any square is **4s**, whatever its side. A phone plan costing £10 plus 5p a minute is **10 + 0.05m** for *any* number of minutes. Algebra is the language of general patterns — write the rule once, use it forever.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- A **letter** stands for an unknown or a varying number.
- Conventions: 3x = 3 × x, x² = x·x, xy = x·y, and we never write 1x.
- A **term** is one chunk; an **expression** joins terms with + and −.
- Algebra writes a rule **once** that holds for every number.

</Step>
