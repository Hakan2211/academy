<Step title="The thing that makes maths different" kind="explain">

Welcome to the summit. Every other science gathers evidence; **mathematics demands proof**. A **proof** is an unbroken chain of logic from agreed starting points (**axioms**) to a conclusion that *must* be true — not probably, not usually, but **always and forever**. It's the reason a 2,000-year-old theorem is still exactly as true today.

</Step>

<Step title="Why examples are never enough" kind="explain">

Check a pattern for the first million cases and you have **evidence**, not proof — case a million-and-one might break it. (This isn't paranoia: some patterns hold for *billions* of cases then fail.) A proof closes that gap forever by arguing about *every* case at once, usually with algebra or logic. As you saw in the algebra world, "odd + odd = even" is settled by writing (2a+1) + (2b+1) = 2(a+b+1).

</Step>

<Step title="The shapes of proof" kind="explain">

Mathematicians have a toolkit of proof styles:

- **Direct proof** — reason straight from assumptions to conclusion.
- **Proof by contradiction** — assume the opposite, derive nonsense (Euclid's infinitely many primes; the irrationality of √2).
- **Proof by induction** — prove it for n = 1, then show "if true for n, then true for n+1" — toppling an infinite line of dominoes.

<Callout type="info" title="Beauty in proof">
Mathematicians prize *elegant* proofs — short, surprising, illuminating. The book "Proofs from THE BOOK" collects arguments so beautiful they seem divinely inspired. A great proof doesn't just confirm a fact; it shows you *why* it had to be true.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- A **proof** is airtight logic from **axioms** to a conclusion that is **always** true.
- **Examples are evidence, not proof** — a proof settles every case at once.
- Key styles: **direct**, **contradiction**, **induction**.
- Proof is what sets mathematics apart from every other field — and elegant proofs are prized for showing *why*.

</Step>
