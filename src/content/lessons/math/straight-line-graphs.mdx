<Step title="The equation of a line" kind="explain">

Every straight line (except a vertical one) can be written in one beautifully compact form:

<Formula tex="y = mx + c" block />

Two numbers say everything: **m** is the **gradient** (how steep, and which way it tilts) and **c** is the **y-intercept** (where it crosses the vertical axis). Change them and you can draw any line in the plane.

</Step>

<Step title="Tilt and shift" kind="interactive">

Slide m to tilt the line and c to slide it up or down.

</Step>

<Step title="Gradient = rise over run" kind="explain">

The gradient measures steepness as the **rise** (change in y) divided by the **run** (change in x):

<Formula tex="m = \frac{\text{rise}}{\text{run}} = \frac{\Delta y}{\Delta x}" block />

A positive m climbs left-to-right; a negative m descends; m = 0 is flat. Bigger |m| is steeper. To find m from two points, divide the change in y by the change in x.

</Step>

<Step title="Reading and building lines" kind="explain">

Given y = 2x + 1: the line crosses the y-axis at **1** and rises **2** for every **1** across. To plot it, mark (0, 1), step one right and two up, and join.

Going the other way — from a graph to its equation — read off c where it cuts the y-axis, then measure the rise-over-run for m. The equation and the picture are two views of the same line.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- A straight line is **y = mx + c**: gradient m, y-intercept c.
- **Gradient = rise ÷ run** (Δy/Δx); positive climbs, negative descends, 0 is flat.
- Plot from the intercept, then step by the gradient.
- Read c and the rise/run off a graph to recover its equation.

</Step>
