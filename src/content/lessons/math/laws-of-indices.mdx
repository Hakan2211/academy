<Step title="Three rules, one idea" kind="explain">

The laws of indices look like rules to memorise, but they're all the same idea: **counting copies of the base**. Once you see that, you'll never forget them.

</Step>

<Step title="Play with the laws" kind="interactive">

Switch between the three laws and change the indices. Watch the copies pool, cancel, or repeat.

<IndexLaws />

</Step>

<Step title="Multiply → add, divide → subtract" kind="explain">

Multiplying powers of the **same base** pools their copies, so you **add** indices:

<Formula tex="a^m \times a^n = a^{m+n}" block />

Dividing cancels copies top against bottom, so you **subtract**:

<Formula tex="a^m \div a^n = a^{m-n}" block />

For example a⁵ ÷ a² = (a·a·a·a·a)/(a·a) = a³. Five copies, two cancel, three remain.

</Step>

<Step title="A power of a power → multiply" kind="explain">

Raising a power to another power repeats the copies in blocks:

<Formula tex="(a^m)^n = a^{m \times n}" block />

So (a³)² = a³ × a³ = a⁶. And a product inside a power distributes: (ab)ⁿ = aⁿbⁿ.

<Callout type="warn" title="Same base only">
The add/subtract laws need the **same base**. 2³ × 5² has nothing to combine — it's just 8 × 25 = 200. You can only pool copies of the *same* thing.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Multiply** same-base powers → **add** indices: aᵐ × aⁿ = aᵐ⁺ⁿ.
- **Divide** → **subtract**: aᵐ ÷ aⁿ = aᵐ⁻ⁿ.
- **Power of a power** → **multiply**: (aᵐ)ⁿ = aᵐⁿ.
- The laws need the **same base** — they're just bookkeeping for copies.

</Step>
