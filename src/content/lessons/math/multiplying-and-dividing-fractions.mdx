<Lesson>

<Step title="Multiplying is easier than adding" kind="explain">

Surprise: multiplying fractions needs **no** common denominator. You just multiply across — tops together, bottoms together:

<Formula tex="\frac{2}{3} \times \frac{3}{4} = \frac{2 \times 3}{3 \times 4} = \frac{6}{12} = \frac{1}{2}" block />

"Of" means multiply: ½ **of** ⅓ is ½ × ⅓ = ⅙.

</Step>

<Step title="Why multiplying tops and bottoms works" kind="interactive">

Shade one fraction across the columns and the other down the rows. The overlap *is* the product — and it's clearly (tops × tops) cells out of (bottoms × bottoms).

<FractionMultiply />

</Step>

<Step title="Dividing: flip and multiply" kind="explain">

To divide by a fraction, multiply by its **reciprocal** (the fraction flipped upside down):

<Formula tex="\frac{3}{4} \div \frac{2}{3} = \frac{3}{4} \times \frac{3}{2} = \frac{9}{8}" block />

Why? Dividing asks "how many ⅔s fit into ¾?" Multiplying by 3/2 answers exactly that. Dividing by ½ doubles a number (how many halves fit?) — division by a fraction often makes things *bigger*.

</Step>

<Step title="Cancel before you multiply" kind="explain">

You can simplify *across* a multiplication before doing it, which keeps the numbers small:

<Formula tex="\frac{5}{8} \times \frac{4}{15} = \frac{\cancel{5}^{1}}{\cancel{8}^{2}} \times \frac{\cancel{4}^{1}}{\cancel{15}^{3}} = \frac{1}{2} \times \frac{1}{3} = \frac{1}{6}" block />

Spot a common factor between any top and any bottom and cancel it first.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Multiply** fractions straight across — no common denominator needed.
- "**of**" means multiply.
- **Divide** by multiplying by the **reciprocal** (flip the second fraction).
- **Cancel** common factors across the multiplication first to stay tidy.

</Step>
