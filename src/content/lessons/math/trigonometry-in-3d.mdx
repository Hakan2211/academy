<Step title="From flat to solid" kind="explain">

Everything so far lived on a flat page. The real world is **three-dimensional** — and the beautiful news is that 3-D trigonometry uses no new rules. The trick is always the same: **find a right triangle inside the solid**, then apply Pythagoras or SOH-CAH-TOA to it.

</Step>

<Step title="The right triangle is still your tool" kind="interactive">

Master the 2-D right triangle and you can attack any 3-D problem by spotting one.

<PythagorasViz />

</Step>

<Step title="The space diagonal of a box" kind="explain">

How long is the diagonal through the inside of a box, l × w × h? Use Pythagoras **twice**. First the diagonal across the base: d² = l² + w². Then the space diagonal rises h above that base diagonal:

<Formula tex="D = \sqrt{d^2 + h^2} = \sqrt{l^2 + w^2 + h^2}" block />

Two right triangles, stacked — and a tidy 3-D version of Pythagoras drops out.

</Step>

<Step title="Angles of elevation and bearings" kind="explain">

3-D trig shines in navigation and surveying:

- The **angle of elevation** (looking up) or **depression** (looking down) sets up a right triangle between you, the object, and the horizontal — so tan(angle) = height / distance.
- **Bearings** give directions as three-figure angles clockwise from North (e.g. 070°), and combine with the sine and cosine rules to find distances between points.

<Callout type="info" title="Why this matters">
This is the maths that lets a surveyor find a mountain's height from the ground, a ship fix its position, and a phone's GPS triangulate you from satellites. Find the right triangle, and three dimensions become as easy as two.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- 3-D trig adds **no new rules** — find a **right triangle** inside the solid.
- **Space diagonal** of a box: D = √(l² + w² + h²) — Pythagoras applied twice.
- **Elevation/depression** angles and **bearings** turn navigation into right-triangle trig.
- This powers surveying, navigation, and GPS.

</Step>
