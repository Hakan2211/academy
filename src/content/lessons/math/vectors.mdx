<Step title="Quantities with direction" kind="explain">

A **vector** has both a **size** (magnitude) and a **direction** — like a displacement, a velocity, or a force. We write it as a column of components saying how far across and how far up:

<Formula tex="\mathbf{a} = \begin{pmatrix} 3 \\ 2 \end{pmatrix} \;=\; 3 \text{ across, } 2 \text{ up}" block />

Unlike a plain number (a **scalar**), a vector points somewhere.

</Step>

</Step>

<Step title="Adding and scaling" kind="explain">

To **add** vectors, add their components separately:

<Formula tex="\begin{pmatrix} 3 \\ 2 \end{pmatrix} + \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 4 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 4 \\ 6 \end{pmatrix}" block />

Geometrically that's the "tip-to-tail" rule. To **multiply by a scalar**, multiply each component: 2**a** is twice as long, same direction. The negative −**a** points the opposite way.

</Step>

<Step title="Magnitude and uses" kind="explain">

A vector's **magnitude** (length) comes from Pythagoras on its components:

<Formula tex="|\mathbf{a}| = \sqrt{x^2 + y^2}" block />

So (3, 4) has length 5. Vectors are the language of physics (forces, velocities), computer graphics (every position and motion), and navigation. Adding a "drift" velocity to a "heading" velocity to find the true course is just vector addition.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- A **vector** has **magnitude and direction**, written as components (across, up).
- **Add** by adding components (tip-to-tail); **scale** by multiplying each component.
- **Magnitude** = √(x² + y²) (Pythagoras).
- Vectors model forces, velocities, motion and navigation.

</Step>
