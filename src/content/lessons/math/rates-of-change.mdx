<Lesson>

<Step title="How fast is it changing?" kind="explain">

A car's speed, a population's growth, a hillside's steepness — all are **rates of change**. On a graph, a rate of change is a **gradient**. The new question calculus asks is subtle: not the *average* rate over an interval, but the **instantaneous** rate at a single instant.

</Step>

<Step title="Average becomes instantaneous" kind="interactive">

The dashed secant gives the average rate between two points. Shrink the gap and it becomes the solid tangent — the instantaneous rate.

<DerivativeExplorer />

</Step>

<Step title="Secant versus tangent" kind="explain">

- The **average rate of change** between two points is the slope of the **secant** line joining them: (change in y) ÷ (change in x) — exactly the gradient from the graphs world.
- The **instantaneous rate** at a single point is the slope of the **tangent** line that just grazes the curve there.

The tangent's slope is the *limit* of the secant's slope as the two points slide together (the gap h → 0). This is where the limit earns its keep.

</Step>

<Step title="Speed: the classic example" kind="explain">

Drive 120 km in 2 hours and your **average** speed is 60 km/h — the secant slope on a distance-time graph. But your **speedometer** shows your *instantaneous* speed: the tangent slope at that exact moment. Calculus is what lets a speedometer exist — turning a whole graph of distance-vs-time into a graph of speed-vs-time.

<Formula tex="\text{instantaneous rate} = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{f(a+h) - f(a)}{h}" block />

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- A **rate of change** is a **gradient** on a graph.
- **Average** rate = slope of the **secant**; **instantaneous** rate = slope of the **tangent**.
- The tangent slope is the **limit** of the secant slope as the gap → 0.
- Distance-time gradients give speed: average (secant) vs instantaneous (speedometer/tangent).

</Step>
