<Step title="Rises, falls, and the multiplier" kind="explain">

A 20% pay rise, a 30% sale, 5% interest — these are **percentage changes**. The professional way to handle them isn't "find the change, then add or subtract." It's a single **multiplier**:

- increase by 20% → × **1.20**
- decrease by 30% → × **0.70**

You go straight from old value to new in one step.

</Step>

<Step title="Building the multiplier" kind="explain">

Start from 100% (the whole, = 1.00) and adjust:

<Formula tex="+15\% \to 1 + 0.15 = 1.15 \qquad -15\% \to 1 - 0.15 = 0.85" block />

A £200 phone with 15% added tax: 200 × 1.15 = £230. The same phone with 15% off: 200 × 0.85 = £170.

</Step>

<Step title="Compound change: multipliers multiply" kind="explain">

The real power shows when changes **repeat**. £1000 earning 5% interest a year doesn't gain a flat £50 each year — each year's interest earns interest too:

<Formula tex="1000 \times 1.05^{3} = 1000 \times 1.157625 = 1157.63" block />

This is **compound growth** (and, with a multiplier below 1, **decay** — like a car losing 15% of its value each year). Repeated percentage change is just repeated multiplication: the seed of the exponential functions you'll meet later.

<Callout type="warn" title="A rise then an equal fall doesn't return home">
Up 10% then down 10% is × 1.1 × 0.9 = × 0.99 — you end 1% *below* where you started. Percentages are taken of different amounts each time.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- Percentage change is best done with a **multiplier**: +p% → (1 + p/100), −p% → (1 − p/100).
- One multiply takes old value straight to new.
- **Repeated** change multiplies the multipliers → **compound** growth or decay.
- A rise then an equal-percentage fall does **not** return to the start.

</Step>
