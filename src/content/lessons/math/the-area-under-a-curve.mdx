<Step title="The other half of calculus" kind="explain">

Differentiation found slopes. The second great problem is **area** — the area trapped between a curve and the x-axis. It sounds unrelated, but it turns out to be differentiation's mirror image. Finding this area is called **integration**.

</Step>

<Step title="Build it from rectangles" kind="interactive">

Slice the area into rectangles and add them. More, thinner rectangles → a better fit → the exact area in the limit.

<AreaUnderCurve />

</Step>

<Step title="The Riemann sum" kind="explain">

The strategy is pure limit-thinking: approximate the region with n rectangles, add their areas, then let n → ∞ so the rectangles become infinitely thin:

<Formula tex="\text{Area} = \lim_{n \to \infty} \sum_{i=1}^{n} f(x_i)\,\Delta x = \int_a^b f(x)\,dx" block />

That elongated S, **∫**, is the **integral sign** — a stretched "S" for "sum." It's the same "add up infinitely many infinitely small pieces" idea from Zeno and the circle-area derivation, now made a precise tool.

</Step>

<Step title="Why area matters" kind="explain">

Area under a curve isn't abstract — it *means* something in every applied field:

- Under a **speed–time** graph → **distance** travelled.
- Under a **force–distance** graph → **work** done.
- Under a **rate** graph → the **total accumulated**.

Integration is how you go from a *rate* back to a *total* — accumulating change. Which raises a tantalising question: differentiation breaks a total into rates; integration sums rates into a total. Are they inverses? That's the deep dive.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Integration** finds the **area under a curve** — calculus's second great problem.
- Approximate with rectangles (a **Riemann sum**), then take the limit as they thin to zero.
- The integral **∫** is a stretched "S" for "sum."
- Area under a rate graph = the **accumulated total** (speed-time → distance, force-distance → work).

</Step>
