<Lesson>

<Step title="Multiply everything inside" kind="explain">

A bracket means "do me first," but often we'd rather **expand** it — remove it by multiplying the outside term into *every* term inside. This is the **distributive law**:

<Formula tex="a(b + c) = ab + ac" block />

Miss a term and the whole thing is wrong, so the watchword is *every* term.

</Step>

<Step title="See it as area" kind="interactive">

A rectangle of height a and width (b + c) splits into two pieces — its total area is the expansion.

<ExpandBrackets />

</Step>

<Step title="With letters and signs" kind="explain">

The same rule, now with algebra:

<Formula tex="3(x + 4) = 3x + 12, \qquad x(x - 5) = x^2 - 5x" block />

Mind the signs — a negative outside flips every term:

<Formula tex="-2(x - 3) = -2x + 6" block />

(−2 times −3 is +6.) After expanding, you often **collect like terms**: 2(x + 3) + 4x = 2x + 6 + 4x = 6x + 6.

</Step>

<Step title="Why expand at all?" kind="explain">

Brackets are great for *seeing* structure, but to **solve** equations and combine expressions you usually need them gone first. Expanding turns a compact form into an addable, comparable one. The reverse move — putting the bracket back — is **factorising**, next.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Expanding** removes a bracket: multiply the outside into **every** inside term.
- **a(b + c) = ab + ac** — the distributive law, visible as area.
- Watch **signs**: a negative outside flips each term.
- Expand, then **collect like terms**; the reverse is factorising.

</Step>
