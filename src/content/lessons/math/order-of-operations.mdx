<Lesson>

<Step title="A calculation can mean two things" kind="explain">

Quick — what is **2 + 3 × 4**?

If you add first you get 5 × 4 = 20. If you multiply first you get 2 + 12 = 14. The symbols are identical, yet the answers differ. Mathematics can't tolerate that kind of ambiguity, so everyone agrees on **one fixed order** of operations.

</Step>

<Step title="The order, by rank" kind="explain">

Work from the top of this list down. Two common memory aids spell the same rule: **BODMAS** and **PEMDAS**.

1. **B**rackets / **P**arentheses
2. **O**rders / **E**xponents (powers and roots)
3. **D**ivision and **M**ultiplication — together, left to right
4. **A**ddition and **S**ubtraction — together, left to right

So in 2 + 3 × 4, multiplication outranks addition: 2 + 12 = **14**.

<Callout type="warn" title="× and ÷ share a rank">
Don't always do multiplication before division. They're equal — just work left to right. In 12 ÷ 3 × 2, divide first (it's leftmost): 4 × 2 = 8, not 12 ÷ 6 = 2.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Brackets are your override switch" kind="explain">

Whatever order you actually want, **brackets force it**. They're evaluated first, so you can bend a calculation to your will:

<Formula tex="(2 + 3) \times 4 = 5 \times 4 = 20" block />

That's why the same digits gave us both 14 and 20 — the brackets changed which operation went first.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- One agreed order removes all ambiguity: **BODMAS / PEMDAS**.
- Rank: **Brackets → Orders → ×/÷ → +/−**.
- ×/÷ share a rank, and +/− share a rank — work those **left to right**.
- **Brackets** let you override the default order whenever you need to.

</Step>
