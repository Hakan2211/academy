<Step title="Mathematics is not finished" kind="explain">

It's tempting to think mathematics is a closed, completed subject — all the answers written down centuries ago. The truth is the opposite: it's a **living frontier** with deep questions no one can answer, some stated so simply a child could understand them, yet unsolved despite centuries of effort by the greatest minds.

</Step>

<Step title="Simple to ask, brutal to answer" kind="explain">

The strangest thing about these problems is how **easy they are to state**:

- **Goldbach:** is every even number the sum of two primes? (Tested past 10¹⁸ — still no proof.)
- **Collatz:** the "3n + 1" game — does it always reach 1? So hard that Paul Erdős said "mathematics is not yet ready for such problems."
- **Twin primes:** are there infinitely many prime pairs two apart?

No advanced notation needed to understand them — yet each has resisted everything thrown at it. Simplicity of statement is no guide to difficulty of proof.

</Step>

<Step title="Million-dollar questions" kind="explain">

In 2000 the Clay Institute named seven **Millennium Prize Problems**, each worth **$1 million**. Only one has been solved — the **Poincaré Conjecture**, proved by Grigori Perelman in 2003, who famously *declined* both the prize and the Fields Medal. The **Riemann Hypothesis** (about the deep structure of the primes) and **P vs NP** (about what computers can efficiently do) remain open and would, if solved, transform mathematics and technology.

<Callout type="info" title="Why open problems matter">
Unsolved problems are the engine of mathematics. Trying to crack them invents whole new fields — the centuries-long hunt to prove Fermat's Last Theorem (finally solved in 1995) built vast swathes of modern number theory along the way. The questions matter even more than the answers.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- Mathematics is a **living frontier**, full of unsolved problems.
- Many (Goldbach, Collatz, twin primes) are **trivial to state, impossibly hard to prove**.
- The **Millennium Prize Problems** offer $1M each; only **Poincaré** is solved (Perelman declined the prize).
- Chasing open problems **invents new mathematics** — the questions drive the field.
