<Lesson>

<Step title="Comparing quantities" kind="explain">

A **ratio** compares amounts of the same kind. Mix squash 1 part concentrate to 4 parts water and the ratio is **1 : 4**. It says nothing about the total amount — only the *relationship* between the parts. Double everything (2 : 8) and the drink tastes exactly the same.

</Step>

<Step title="Mix by ratio" kind="interactive">

Change the parts and watch the bar split. Notice the *proportions* stay fixed even as you change the total.

<RatioMixer />

</Step>

<Step title="Ratio, fraction — what's the difference?" kind="explain">

They're cousins. In a mix of 3 : 2 (orange : apple), there are 5 parts in total, so orange is **3 out of 5** = ⅗ of the whole. A ratio compares **part to part**; a fraction compares **part to whole**. You'll switch between them constantly.

<Formula tex="\text{ratio } 3:2 \;\Rightarrow\; \text{orange} = \tfrac{3}{5}, \quad \text{apple} = \tfrac{2}{5}" block />

</Step>

<Step title="Order matters" kind="explain">

A ratio is read in order: 3 : 2 of squash to water is *not* the same drink as 2 : 3. Always keep track of which quantity is which — the colon is not symmetric.

<Callout type="tip" title="Same units first">
Before forming a ratio, convert to the same unit. 50 cm : 2 m isn't 50 : 2 — it's 50 cm : 200 cm = 1 : 4.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- A **ratio** compares **part to part**, in a fixed order, in the same units.
- It captures proportions, not totals — scaling all parts keeps the ratio the same.
- A **fraction** compares **part to whole**: parts a : b means a/(a+b) of the total.

</Step>
