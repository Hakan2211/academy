<Lesson>

<Step title="One number to summarise many" kind="explain">

An **average** captures a whole dataset in a single typical value. There are three, and they can disagree — so knowing *which* to use is as important as the arithmetic.

- **Mean** — add them all, divide by how many.
- **Median** — the middle value when sorted.
- **Mode** — the most common value.

</Step>

<Step title="See them shift" kind="interactive">

Watch how an outlier pulls the mean but barely moves the median.

</Step>

<Step title="Which average, when?" kind="explain">

<Formula tex="\text{mean} = \frac{\text{sum of values}}{\text{number of values}}" block />

- **Mean** uses every value — but a single outlier distorts it (one billionaire raises a town's *mean* income, not its *median*).
- **Median** is robust to outliers — best for skewed data like incomes or house prices.
- **Mode** is the only average for categorical data (the most common shoe size, favourite colour).

</Step>

<Step title="Spread: the range" kind="explain">

Averages don't tell the whole story — you also need **spread**. The simplest measure is the **range**:

<Formula tex="\text{range} = \text{largest} - \text{smallest}" block />

Two classes can have the same mean score but very different ranges — one consistent, one wildly mixed. Average tells you the centre; spread tells you how reliable that centre is. (The range is easily skewed by one extreme value — the next lesson gives a sturdier measure.)

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- Three averages: **mean** (add ÷ count), **median** (middle), **mode** (most common).
- **Mean** uses all data but is **outlier-sensitive**; **median** resists outliers; **mode** works for categories.
- **Range = max − min** measures spread.
- Centre (average) + spread together describe a dataset.

</Step>
