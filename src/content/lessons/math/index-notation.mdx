<Step title="Powers, properly named" kind="explain">

You met powers as repeated multiplication. Now the vocabulary, because the rest of this world depends on it:

<Formula tex="\underbrace{a}_{\text{base}}{}^{\overbrace{n}^{\text{index}}} = \underbrace{a \times a \times \cdots \times a}_{n \text{ times}}" block />

The **base** is what's multiplied; the **index** (or **exponent**, or **power**) counts how many copies. "Index notation" is just this compact shorthand — and it's the language of growth, area, volume, and very large numbers.

</Step>

<Step title="Climb the ladder of powers" kind="interactive">

Pick a base and step the index. Each rung up multiplies by the base.

</Step>

<Step title="Reading and saying them" kind="explain">

- 5³ is "five to the power three" or "five cubed" = 125.
- 2⁸ = 256 (every doubling — bytes, cell division — is a power of 2).
- Indices apply only to what they're attached to: 3 × 2⁴ = 3 × 16 = 48, **not** 6⁴. The power binds tighter than the multiplication (remember BODMAS — Orders before ×).

</Step>

<Step title="Why powers matter" kind="explain">

Powers grow shockingly fast. Fold a paper 42 times — 2⁴² layers — and the stack would reach the Moon. This explosive behaviour is why powers describe compound interest, populations, and computing, and why we need a tidy notation to tame the huge and the tiny.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Base** to the **index**: aⁿ means n copies of a multiplied.
- The index attaches only to its base — powers come **before** ×/÷.
- Powers grow extremely fast, so we need compact, rule-governed notation.

</Step>
