<Step title="A line across parallels" kind="explain">

When a straight line — a **transversal** — crosses two **parallel** lines, it creates eight angles with beautiful, fixed relationships. Three pairs are worth knowing by name, and they unlock most geometry problems.

</Step>

<Step title="Spot the pairs" kind="interactive">

Toggle each relationship to see which angles it links.

<ParallelAngles />

</Step>

<Step title="The three relationships" kind="explain">

- **Corresponding angles** (an **F**-shape) are **equal** — same position at each crossing.
- **Alternate angles** (a **Z**-shape) are **equal** — opposite sides of the transversal, between the lines.
- **Co-interior angles** (a **C**-shape) **add to 180°** — same side, between the lines.

The F, Z and C letter shapes are a quick visual cue for which rule applies.

</Step>

<Step title="Why they hold" kind="explain">

Because the lines are parallel, the transversal meets them at the *same* angle — that's what "parallel" guarantees. So corresponding angles must match, and the rest follow from the straight-line and vertically-opposite facts you already know. Chain them and you can find every one of the eight angles from just one.

<Callout type="tip" title="Only with parallel lines">
These equalities need the lines to be parallel (shown by matching arrows). If the lines aren't parallel, the transversal hits them at different angles and the rules break.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- A **transversal** across parallel lines makes three key angle pairs.
- **Corresponding (F)** equal · **Alternate (Z)** equal · **Co-interior (C)** sum to 180°.
- They hold **because** the lines are parallel (same angle at each crossing).
- From one angle you can deduce all eight.

</Step>
