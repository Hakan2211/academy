<Step title="Shortcuts beat the limit every time" kind="explain">

Computing every derivative from the limit definition would be exhausting. Happily, patterns emerge that become **rules** — and the most important is the **power rule**, which differentiates any power of x instantly.

</Step>

<Step title="The power rule" kind="explain">

To differentiate xⁿ, **multiply by the power, then drop the power by one**:

<Formula tex="\frac{d}{dx}\,x^n = n x^{n-1}" block />

So x² → 2x, x³ → 3x², x⁵ → 5x⁴. Two more handy facts: a constant multiplier stays put (5x² → 10x), and a plain constant differentiates to 0 (it has zero slope). Terms differentiate one at a time, so 3x² + 4x − 7 → 6x + 4.

</Step>

<Step title="Finding turning points" kind="explain">

The killer application: **optimisation**. A curve's maximum or minimum sits where its tangent is flat — where **f′(x) = 0**. So:

1. Differentiate to get f′(x).
2. Solve **f′(x) = 0** for the turning points.
3. Check whether each is a max or min.

For f(x) = x² − 4x + 1: f′(x) = 2x − 4 = 0 gives x = 2, the minimum. This is how engineers find the strongest shape, the least material, the maximum profit — calculus turns "find the best" into "solve an equation."

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- The **power rule**: d/dx (xⁿ) = n·xⁿ⁻¹ (multiply by the power, drop it by one).
- Constant multipliers stay; plain **constants differentiate to 0**; differentiate term by term.
- **Turning points** are where **f′(x) = 0**.
- Setting the derivative to zero is the engine of **optimisation**.

</Step>
