<Step title="The perfect curve" kind="explain">

A **circle** is the set of all points the same distance from a centre. That single distance, the **radius**, defines everything about it. Circles need their own vocabulary — and getting the names straight makes every later circle formula and theorem click into place.

</Step>

<Step title="The key vocabulary" kind="explain">

- **Radius** — centre to edge.
- **Diameter** — across through the centre = **2 × radius**.
- **Circumference** — the distance all the way around the edge.
- **Chord** — a straight line joining two points on the edge.
- **Arc** — a part of the circumference.
- **Sector** — a "pizza slice" between two radii; a **segment** is cut off by a chord.
- **Tangent** — a line that just touches the edge at one point.

</Step>

<Step title="The number that runs the circle: π" kind="explain">

Divide any circle's circumference by its diameter and you always get the same number — the famous **π ≈ 3.14159…**:

<Formula tex="\pi = \frac{\text{circumference}}{\text{diameter}} \approx 3.14159\ldots" block />

This never-ending, never-repeating number (an irrational, from World 1) is built into every circle in the universe. It's why circumference = πd and area = πr², which you'll use in the Measurement world. A tangent, by the way, always meets the radius at the point of contact at exactly 90° — the seed of the circle theorems ahead.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- A **circle** = all points a fixed **radius** from the centre; **diameter = 2 × radius**.
- Parts: **circumference, chord, arc, sector, segment, tangent**.
- **π = circumference ÷ diameter ≈ 3.14159…**, an irrational constant in every circle.
- A **tangent** meets the radius at 90° at the point of contact.

</Step>
