<Step title="Which is bigger?" kind="explain">

Is ⅔ bigger than ⅗? You can't just compare the numerators (2 vs 3) or the denominators (3 vs 5) — both pieces are different sizes. To compare fairly, you need the pieces to be the **same size**, which means a **common denominator**.

</Step>

<Step title="Make the pieces match" kind="interactive">

Two equal-width bars show the answer instantly; the common denominator proves it.

<FractionCompare />

</Step>

<Step title="The common-denominator method" kind="explain">

Rewrite both fractions over a shared denominator (the LCM of the two), then just compare numerators:

<Formula tex="\frac{2}{3} = \frac{10}{15}, \quad \frac{3}{5} = \frac{9}{15} \;\Rightarrow\; \frac{2}{3} > \frac{3}{5}" block />

Once the denominators match, the bigger numerator is the bigger fraction.

</Step>

<Step title="Shortcuts worth knowing" kind="explain">

- **Same denominator?** Compare numerators directly: 3/7 < 5/7.
- **Same numerator?** The *smaller* denominator wins — fewer, bigger pieces: ⅓ > ⅕.
- **Cross-multiply** to compare a/b and c/d: compare a×d with c×b. (It's the common-denominator method in disguise.)

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- Compare fractions by giving them a **common denominator** (their LCM).
- Then the bigger **numerator** is the bigger fraction.
- Same numerator → smaller denominator is bigger (fewer, larger pieces).
- **Cross-multiplication** is a quick two-fraction shortcut.

</Step>
