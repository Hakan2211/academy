<Step title="The deepest mystery of all" kind="explain">

You've climbed from counting to calculus to the infinite. One question now towers over everything: **why does mathematics describe the universe so perfectly?** Numbers were invented to count sheep — yet the same mathematics predicts black holes, runs your phone, and decodes DNA. The physicist Eugene Wigner called this the **"unreasonable effectiveness of mathematics,"** and no one fully understands it.

</Step>

<Step title="Discovered or invented?" kind="explain">

Mathematicians have debated this for centuries:

- **Discovered:** maths is "out there," eternal truths we uncover — π was real before any human measured a circle, and aliens would find the same primes.
- **Invented:** maths is a human language we construct, and its fit to reality reflects which mathematics we *chose* to develop.

The eeriest evidence for "discovered": time and again, mathematics built for pure beauty — with no use in mind — turns out, decades later, to be *exactly* what nature needed. Non-Euclidean geometry became Einstein's spacetime; number theory became internet cryptography; imaginary numbers became quantum mechanics.

</Step>

<Step title="What you've actually learned" kind="explain">

This island took you on the real journey of mathematics: from **number** (counting, primes, fractions) through the language of **algebra** (the unknown, equations, the parabola), the world of **shape** (geometry, trigonometry), the taming of **uncertainty** (probability, statistics), and finally to the frontier — the **calculus** of change and the **infinite** itself.

The through-line was never the formulas. It was a way of thinking: **abstract** a problem into symbols, **reason** with airtight logic, **prove** rather than merely believe, and find the **single idea** that unlocks a thousand cases. That habit of mind is the real prize, and it transfers to everything.

<Callout type="info" title="Beauty as a guide">
Mathematicians chase **elegance** — and astonishingly, beautiful mathematics tends to be true and useful mathematics. Paul Dirac trusted the beauty of his equations enough to predict antimatter from them. As G.H. Hardy wrote, "there is no permanent place in the world for ugly mathematics."
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="The edge of mathematics" kind="explain">

And it never ends. Beyond this island lie further summits: imaginary and complex numbers, the calculus of many dimensions, the strange logics of Gödel (who proved that *any* mathematical system harbours true statements it can never prove), the still-open questions you just met. Mathematics is the one subject that is simultaneously thousands of years old and being created today. You've reached this island's summit — but the mountains beyond go up forever.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- Mathematics describes the universe with an "**unreasonable effectiveness**" no one fully explains.
- Is it **discovered** (eternal truths) or **invented** (human language)? — pure maths keeps turning out to fit nature.
- The island's real lesson is a **way of thinking**: abstract, reason, prove, generalise.
- Mathematics is **never finished** — Gödel's limits, complex numbers, open problems, and summits beyond await.

</Step>
