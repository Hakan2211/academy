<Step title="Do two things move together?" kind="explain">

A **scatter graph** plots pairs of values — like hours studied and exam score — one point per subject. Its shape reveals **correlation**: whether the two variables tend to rise and fall together, move oppositely, or have no link at all.

</Step>

<Step title="From negative to positive" kind="interactive">

Slide through the correlations and watch the cloud of points and the trend line.

</Step>

<Step title="Kinds of correlation" kind="explain">

- **Positive** — as one rises, so does the other (study time and grades); points trend up-right.
- **Negative** — as one rises, the other falls (price and demand); points trend down-right.
- **None** — no pattern; a shapeless scatter.

The tighter the points hug a line, the **stronger** the correlation. A **line of best fit** summarises the trend and lets you predict in-between values (interpolation).

</Step>

<Step title="Correlation is not causation" kind="explain">

The most important warning in all of statistics: two variables moving together does **not** prove one *causes* the other.

Ice-cream sales and drowning deaths correlate strongly — but ice cream doesn't drown anyone. A hidden third factor, **hot weather**, drives both. Always ask whether a lurking variable, coincidence, or reverse causation could explain a correlation before claiming cause.

<Callout type="warn" title="The mantra">
Correlation is not causation. Establishing cause needs a controlled experiment, not just a suggestive scatter graph.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- A **scatter graph** plots paired data to reveal **correlation**.
- **Positive** (up together), **negative** (opposite), or **none**; tighter points = stronger.
- A **line of best fit** summarises the trend and enables prediction.
- **Correlation is not causation** — beware lurking variables.

</Step>
