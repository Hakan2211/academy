<Lesson>

<Step title="When factorising won't help" kind="explain">

Most quadratics don't factorise into tidy whole numbers. **Completing the square** is a universal method that rewrites *any* quadratic as a perfect square plus a constant:

<Formula tex="x^2 + bx + c = \left(x + \tfrac{b}{2}\right)^2 - \left(\tfrac{b}{2}\right)^2 + c" block />

Once it's a squared bracket, you can solve it directly by square-rooting — no factor-hunting needed.

</Step>

<Step title="Why half of b?" kind="interactive">

Geometrically, x² + bx is a square plus a strip. Split the strip in two, wrap it round, and you've *almost* built a bigger square of side (x + b/2) — missing only the corner (b/2)².

<CompleteSquare />

</Step>

<Step title="Solving with it" kind="explain">

Take x² + 6x + 1 = 0. Half of 6 is 3, and 3² = 9:

<Formula tex="(x+3)^2 - 9 + 1 = 0 \;\to\; (x+3)^2 = 8 \;\to\; x + 3 = \pm\sqrt{8}" block />

So x = −3 ± 2√2 — exact answers a factorisation could never have given. The **± from the square root** is where the two roots come from.

</Step>

<Step title="A bonus: the vertex for free" kind="explain">

Completed-square form also reveals the parabola's **lowest (or highest) point** instantly. Since a square is never negative, (x + 3)² − 9 is smallest when the bracket is 0:

<Formula tex="y = (x+3)^2 - 9 \;\Rightarrow\; \text{vertex at } (-3, -9)" block />

The turning point sits at x = −b/2 — the very number you halved. That's why this form is prized in the next world, Functions & Graphs.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Completing the square** rewrites any quadratic as (x + b/2)² − (b/2)² + c.
- Use **half the x-coefficient**; subtract its square to compensate.
- Solve by **square-rooting** — the ± gives both roots, even irrational ones.
- The form also hands you the **vertex** (−b/2, …) directly.

</Step>
