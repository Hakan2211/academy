<Step title="The periodic table of numbers" kind="explain">

Chemists break matter into atoms; mathematicians break numbers into **primes**. The analogy is exact: every whole number above 1 is built from primes in exactly one way, just as every molecule is built from elements. The primes are the indivisible atoms of arithmetic.

This lesson is about three deep facts: primes are *unique* builders, there are *infinitely many* of them, and they quietly *guard the internet*.

</Step>

<Step title="Uniqueness: the Fundamental Theorem" kind="explain">

The **Fundamental Theorem of Arithmetic** says the prime factorisation of any number is unique. This is why 1 is excluded from the primes: if 1 were prime, then 6 = 2 × 3 = 1 × 2 × 3 = 1 × 1 × 2 × 3 … and uniqueness would collapse. Excluding 1 keeps every number's fingerprint one-of-a-kind.

</Step>

<Step title="Infinity: Euclid's proof" kind="explain">

Do the primes ever run out? Euclid proved, around 300 BC, that they don't — with one of the most elegant arguments in all of mathematics:

Suppose there were only finitely many primes. Multiply them *all* together and add 1, calling the result N. Now N leaves remainder 1 when divided by every prime on the list — so none of them divides it. Yet every number has a prime factor. So N is either prime itself or has a prime factor we missed. **Contradiction.** The list can never be complete.

<Callout type="info" title="A proof by contradiction">
Euclid didn't build infinitely many primes — he showed that assuming a finite list leads to nonsense. Showing "the opposite is impossible" is one of mathematics' sharpest tools, and you'll meet it again in the Infinity world.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Why primes guard your secrets" kind="explain">

Multiplying two primes is easy: 1009 × 3643 = 3,675,787 in a blink. But going backwards — given only 3,675,787, *find the two primes* — is brutally hard. There's no fast method, and that one-way difficulty is the lock.

Internet encryption (RSA) multiplies two enormous primes, each hundreds of digits long. The product is public; the primes are the secret key. To break it, an eavesdropper would have to factor a number so large that every computer on Earth working together couldn't finish before the Sun burns out.

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- Primes are the **atoms** of arithmetic — every number is a unique product of them (**Fundamental Theorem**).
- Excluding 1 from the primes is what keeps that factorisation unique.
- **Euclid** proved there are **infinitely many** primes by contradiction.
- Their factorisation is easy one way and hard the other — which is why primes **encrypt the internet**.

</Step>
