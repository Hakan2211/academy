<Lesson>

<Step title="Close enough, on purpose" kind="explain">

Not every number needs all its digits. A crowd of 38,412 people is "about 38,000"; a bill of £19.97 is "about £20." **Rounding** trades a little accuracy for a lot of clarity — and gives you a fast way to *check* exact answers.

</Step>

<Step title="The rounding rule" kind="explain">

To round to a given place, look at the **next digit**:

- 0–4 → round **down** (keep the digit).
- 5–9 → round **up** (add one).

The reason is distance. A number is rounded to whichever marker it's **nearer** to; the digit 5 is the exact midpoint, and the convention is to round it up.

</Step>

</Step>

<Step title="Estimation: rounding with a purpose" kind="explain">

**Estimating** means rounding *before* you calculate, usually to one significant figure, to get a sanity-check answer:

<Formula tex="312 \times 18 \approx 300 \times 20 = 6000" block />

The exact answer is 5616 — and our estimate of 6000 instantly tells us a result like "561.6" or "56,160" would be a slipped decimal point. Estimation is the cheapest error-detector in mathematics.

<Callout type="tip" title="Significant figures">
The first significant figure is the first non-zero digit. In 0.00408 it's the 4; rounded to 1 s.f. that's 0.004. Leading zeros only locate the number — they don't count as significant.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- Round by the **next digit**: 0–4 down, 5–9 up — because you snap to the **nearer** marker.
- The digit 5 sits on the midpoint; by convention it rounds **up**.
- **Estimate** by rounding to 1 significant figure to check an answer's size.
- A good estimate catches misplaced decimal points and missing digits.

</Step>
