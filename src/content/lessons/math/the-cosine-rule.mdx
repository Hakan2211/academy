<Step title="Pythagoras, generalised" kind="explain">

When the sine rule doesn't fit — you have two sides and the angle *between* them, or all three sides — the **cosine rule** steps in:

<Formula tex="c^2 = a^2 + b^2 - 2ab\cos C" block />

Look closely: it's Pythagoras (a² + b² = c²) plus a correction term that vanishes when C = 90°, because cos 90° = 0. The cosine rule *contains* Pythagoras.

</Step>

<Step title="Open and close the angle" kind="interactive">

Swing the included angle and watch the third side — and notice 90° gives plain Pythagoras.

<CosineRule />

</Step>

<Step title="Using it" kind="explain">

Two uses, mirror images of each other:

- **Find a side:** know two sides (a, b) and the angle **between** them (C) → c² = a² + b² − 2ab cos C.
- **Find an angle:** know all three sides → rearrange to cos C = (a² + b² − c²) / 2ab, then take cos⁻¹.

A triangle with sides 5 and 8 and a 60° angle between: c² = 25 + 64 − 2·5·8·cos 60° = 89 − 40 = 49, so c = 7.

</Step>

<Step title="The area of any triangle" kind="explain">

Trig also gives a triangle's area when you know two sides and the angle between them — no need for the perpendicular height:

<Formula tex="\text{Area} = \tfrac12 ab\sin C" block />

It's "½ × base × height" in disguise: a sin C *is* the height when b is the base. Together, the sine rule, cosine rule and this area formula let you solve **any** triangle completely.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Cosine rule:** c² = a² + b² − 2ab cos C — Pythagoras with a correction term.
- Use it for **two sides + included angle**, or **all three sides** (rearrange for the angle).
- At C = 90° it reduces to **Pythagoras** (cos 90° = 0).
- **Area = ½ ab sin C** finds a triangle's area from two sides and their angle.

</Step>
