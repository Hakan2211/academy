<Step title="Mapping a sequence of events" kind="explain">

When events happen in **stages** — flip a coin, then roll a die; draw a ball, then another — a **tree diagram** lays out every path. Each branch carries a probability, and following a path tells you the chance of that exact sequence.

</Step>

<Step title="Read the tree" kind="interactive">

Each fork is one stage; multiply along a path to get its probability.

<ProbabilityTree />

</Step>

<Step title="Multiply along, add between" kind="explain">

Two rules run every tree:

- **Multiply along** the branches of a single path (it's the AND rule): P(red then blue) = P(red) × P(blue).
- **Add between** different paths that satisfy your event (the OR rule).

For two flips of a fair coin, P(exactly one head) = P(HT) + P(TH) = ¼ + ¼ = **½**. The branch probabilities at each fork always sum to 1.

</Step>

<Step title="Why trees beat guesswork" kind="explain">

Trees keep multi-stage problems organised so you don't miss or double-count outcomes. They also make it obvious when stages **interact** — which is the whole point of the next lesson. Stick to "multiply along, add between," check each fork sums to 1, and even four-stage problems stay manageable.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- A **tree diagram** maps events happening in **stages**, branch by branch.
- **Multiply along** a path (AND); **add between** paths (OR).
- Branches at each fork **sum to 1**.
- Trees prevent missing or double-counting outcomes.

</Step>
