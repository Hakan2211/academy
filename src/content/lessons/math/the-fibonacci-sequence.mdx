<Step title="Each term from the two before" kind="explain">

The most famous sequence in mathematics starts simply: **1, 1, 2, 3, 5, 8, 13, 21, …** — each term is the **sum of the previous two**. Leonardo of Pisa ("Fibonacci") introduced it to Europe in 1202 while modelling rabbit populations, and it has turned up in the most unexpected places ever since.

<Formula tex="F_n = F_{n-1} + F_{n-2}" block />

</Step>

<Step title="The Fibonacci tiling" kind="interactive">

</Step>

<Step title="Fibonacci in nature" kind="explain">

The sequence appears throughout the living world — not by magic, but because it's an efficient way to pack and grow:

- The spirals of a sunflower head and a pinecone come in Fibonacci counts (often 34 and 55).
- Many flowers have a Fibonacci number of petals (lilies 3, buttercups 5, daisies often 34 or 55).
- New plant leaves spiral by the **golden angle**, so each gets maximum sunlight.

</Step>

<Step title="The golden ratio emerges" kind="explain">

Divide each Fibonacci number by the one before it and the answers settle on a single special number:

<Formula tex="\frac{F_n}{F_{n-1}} \to \varphi = \frac{1 + \sqrt{5}}{2} \approx 1.618" block />

This is the **golden ratio**, φ. A sequence about adding rabbits secretly contains one of the most celebrated constants in mathematics and art — the subject of this world's deep dive.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Fibonacci:** each term is the **sum of the previous two** (1, 1, 2, 3, 5, 8, …).
- The Fibonacci **tiling** of squares fits perfectly by that very rule.
- It appears in sunflowers, pinecones, petals and leaf spirals — efficient growth.
- Consecutive ratios converge to the **golden ratio φ ≈ 1.618**.

</Step>
