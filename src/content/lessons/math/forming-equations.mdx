<Step title="The real skill: writing the equation" kind="explain">

In the real world, nobody hands you "2x + 3 = 11." You're given a *situation*, and the hard part — the genuinely mathematical part — is **translating it into an equation**. Once it's written, the solving is mechanical.

</Step>

<Step title="From words to algebra" kind="explain">

The recipe:

1. Let a letter stand for the unknown ("let the number be n").
2. Translate each phrase into algebra.
3. Find the two things that are **equal** and join them with =.
4. Solve, then answer in context.

"I think of a number, multiply by 4, and add 5 to get 29." → 4n + 5 = 29 → 4n = 24 → **n = 6**.

</Step>

<Step title="A geometry example" kind="explain">

A rectangle is 3 cm longer than it is wide, and its perimeter is 26 cm. Find its width.

Let the width be w, so the length is w + 3. Perimeter = 2(width + length):

<Formula tex="2(w + (w + 3)) = 26 \;\to\; 2(2w + 3) = 26 \;\to\; 4w + 6 = 26 \;\to\; w = 5" block />

So the width is 5 cm (and the length 8 cm). Always translate the relationship into the equals sign.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- The mathematical work is **forming** the equation; solving is then routine.
- Define a letter, translate each phrase, set the two equal quantities with **=**.
- Watch for "consecutive", "longer than", "twice as many" — each becomes algebra.
- **Answer in context** (with units) after solving.

</Step>
