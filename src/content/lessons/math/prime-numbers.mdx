<Step title="The indivisible numbers" kind="explain">

A **prime** is a whole number greater than 1 whose only factors are 1 and itself. It cannot be split into a rectangle other than a single line.

2, 3, 5, 7, 11, 13, 17, 19, … Everything else above 1 is **composite** — it can be broken into smaller factors. Primes are the numbers that refuse to break.

</Step>

<Step title="Sieve out the primes" kind="interactive">

Eratosthenes found the primes 2,200 years ago with a beautifully simple idea: cross out every multiple of each prime, and whatever survives is prime. Try it.

<PrimeSieve />

</Step>

<Step title="Why stopping at 7 is enough" kind="explain">

Notice you only had to sieve 2, 3, 5 and 7 to clear all of 1–100. Here's why: if a number up to 100 is composite, it must have a factor no larger than √100 = 10. The only primes up to 10 are 2, 3, 5, 7 — so sieving those catches every composite.

<Formula tex="\text{to test if } n \text{ is prime, only check primes up to } \sqrt{n}" block />

</Step>

<Step title="Spotting primes quickly" kind="explain">

A few divisibility checks rule out most candidates instantly:

- even (ends in 0,2,4,6,8) → divisible by 2
- digits sum to a multiple of 3 → divisible by 3
- ends in 0 or 5 → divisible by 5

So 51 *looks* prime, but 5 + 1 = 6 is a multiple of 3, so 51 = 3 × 17. Not prime.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- A **prime** has exactly two factors; a **composite** has more.
- The **Sieve of Eratosthenes** finds primes by crossing out multiples.
- To test n for primality, only check prime divisors up to **√n**.
- Quick divisibility rules (2, 3, 5) eliminate most candidates fast.

</Step>
