<Step title="When the first event changes the second" kind="explain">

Sometimes one event **affects** the next. Draw a ball from a bag and *keep it* — now there's one fewer ball, so the second draw's probabilities have changed. This is **conditional probability**: the chance of B *given that* A has happened, written P(B | A).

</Step>

</Step>

<Step title="Dependent events" kind="explain">

- **With replacement** (you put the ball back): the bag resets, the draws are **independent**, and the second probabilities are unchanged.
- **Without replacement** (you keep it): the total drops and the counts shift, so the events are **dependent** — the second branch depends on the first.

From a bag of 3 red, 2 blue, P(red then red) **without** replacement is 3/5 × 2/4 = 3/10 — the second 2/4, not 3/5, because a red is already gone.

</Step>

<Step title="Conditional probability everywhere" kind="explain">

This idea is one of the most important — and most misunderstood — in all of mathematics. P(disease | positive test) is *not* the same as P(positive test | disease); confusing the two leads doctors, juries, and journalists astray. The general rule:

<Formula tex="P(A \text{ and } B) = P(A) \times P(B \mid A)" block />

Tree diagrams handle it naturally: the second-stage branches already *are* the conditional probabilities.

<Callout type="info" title="The base-rate trap">
A test that's "99% accurate" for a rare disease can still mean most positives are false alarms — because the disease is rare to begin with. Conditional reasoning (Bayes' theorem) untangles it, and it's why this topic matters far beyond exams.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Conditional probability** P(B | A) is the chance of B **given** A happened.
- **With replacement** → independent (unchanged); **without** → dependent (counts shift).
- **P(A and B) = P(A) × P(B | A)** — trees show this automatically.
- Confusing P(A|B) with P(B|A) is a famous, consequential error (the base-rate trap).

</Step>
