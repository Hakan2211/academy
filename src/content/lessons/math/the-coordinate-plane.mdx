<Step title="Turning geometry into numbers" kind="explain">

In the 17th century, Descartes had a revolutionary idea: pin down any point in a plane with **two numbers**. Draw a horizontal **x-axis** and a vertical **y-axis** crossing at the **origin** (0, 0), and every location gets a unique address — its **coordinates** (x, y).

This single idea fused algebra and geometry: shapes become equations, and equations become shapes.

</Step>

<Step title="Locate a point" kind="interactive">

Coordinates are always read **across first (x), then up (y)**. Move the point and watch its address.

<CoordinatePlane />

</Step>

<Step title="Across, then up" kind="explain">

The order is fixed and it matters: (3, 2) means 3 right and 2 up; (2, 3) is a different point entirely. A memory hook: "along the corridor, then up the stairs."

Negative coordinates go the other way — left for negative x, down for negative y. The two axes carve the plane into four **quadrants**, numbered anticlockwise from the top-right.

</Step>

<Step title="Why this changed mathematics" kind="explain">

Once points are numbers, a **line** or **curve** becomes a rule connecting x and y — an equation you can draw. "Where do two roads cross?" becomes "solve two equations." Distances, gradients, areas all become calculations. Every graph you'll meet in this world lives on this plane. It is the meeting point of algebra and geometry.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- Two axes meet at the **origin**; every point has coordinates **(x, y)**.
- Read **across (x), then up (y)** — order matters.
- Negatives go left/down; the axes make **four quadrants**.
- Coordinates turn geometry into algebra — the foundation of all graphs.

</Step>
