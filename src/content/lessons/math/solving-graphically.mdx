<Step title="A solution is a crossing point" kind="explain">

Graphs give equations a picture — and a powerful new way to solve them. The deep idea: **the solution of an equation is where two graphs meet.** Wherever curves cross, their y-values are equal, which is exactly what the equation demands.

</Step>

<Step title="Two lines, one solution" kind="interactive">

Simultaneous equations are two lines; their single crossing point is the (x, y) that solves both.

<SimultaneousGraph />

</Step>

<Step title="Solving with a curve and a line" kind="explain">

To solve x² − 3 = x graphically, plot **y = x² − 3** and **y = x** and read off where they intersect. The x-values of the crossings are the solutions. A parabola and a line can cross **twice, once, or not at all** — matching the two/one/no solutions you'd get algebraically.

<ParabolaExplorer />

</Step>

<Step title="Roots are crossings with y = 0" kind="explain">

Solving ax² + bx + c = **0** is the special case where the second "graph" is the x-axis itself (the line y = 0). So the **roots are the x-intercepts** — a fact you used in Quadratics, now explained: setting an expression to zero means finding where its graph meets the x-axis.

<Callout type="info" title="Graphical solving handles the unsolvable">
Some equations can't be solved with neat algebra at all (like x = cos x). Graphing both sides and reading the crossing gives an answer to any accuracy you like — which is how computers solve them.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- The **solution** of an equation is where the relevant **graphs intersect**.
- Simultaneous linear equations → the crossing of **two lines**.
- "= 0" equations → **x-intercepts** (crossings with the x-axis).
- Graphical methods solve even equations algebra can't, to any accuracy.

</Step>
