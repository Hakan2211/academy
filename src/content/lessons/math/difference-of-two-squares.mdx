<Step title="A pattern worth memorising" kind="explain">

One factorisation appears so often it deserves to be instant recognition — the **difference of two squares**:

<Formula tex="a^2 - b^2 = (a + b)(a - b)" block />

Any "something squared minus something squared" splits into a sum times a difference. There's no middle term because the cross terms cancel: (a+b)(a−b) = a² − ab + ab − b² = a² − b².

</Step>

<Step title="See why, with area" kind="interactive">

Cut a small square from the corner of a big one; the leftover L-shape rearranges exactly into a rectangle of sides (a + b) and (a − b).

<DiffOfSquares />

</Step>

<Step title="Spotting it" kind="explain">

It works whenever both terms are perfect squares and there's a minus between them:

<Formula tex="x^2 - 25 = (x + 5)(x - 5), \qquad 9x^2 - 16 = (3x + 4)(3x - 4)" block />

A **sum** of squares (x² + 25) does **not** factorise like this — the pattern needs the subtraction.

</Step>

<Step title="A calculator trick it gives you" kind="explain">

The identity makes some arithmetic effortless:

<Formula tex="49 \times 51 = (50 - 1)(50 + 1) = 50^2 - 1 = 2499" block />

Recognising 49 × 51 as a difference of squares turns an awkward multiplication into "2500 minus 1." Patterns aren't just for algebra class — they're shortcuts.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **a² − b² = (a + b)(a − b)** — a sum times a difference, no middle term.
- It needs **two perfect squares** with a **minus** between (a sum of squares won't factorise).
- Geometrically, a corner-cut square becomes an (a+b)×(a−b) rectangle.
- It speeds up arithmetic like 49 × 51 = 50² − 1.

</Step>
