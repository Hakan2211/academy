<Step title="Collecting like terms" kind="explain">

An expression like 3x + 2 + 5x − 1 has repeated kinds of term. **Like terms** have the *same letter part* — 3x and 5x are alike; 2 and −1 are alike. You can add like terms together, but you can't merge unlike ones.

<Formula tex="3x + 2 + 5x - 1 = 8x + 1" block />

Three x-tiles plus five x-tiles make eight x-tiles. Simple counting.

</Step>

<Step title="What counts as 'like'?" kind="explain">

The letter part must match **exactly**:

- 4x and 7x are like → 11x.
- 4x and 4x² are **not** like (x vs x²) — leave them apart.
- 2xy and 5xy are like → 7xy; but 2xy and 2x are not.

Add/subtract the **numbers in front** (the coefficients); the letter part stays the same. It's just like adding 3 apples + 5 apples = 8 apples — you can't add apples to oranges.

</Step>

<Step title="Multiplying terms" kind="explain">

Multiplication is different — there, unlike terms *do* combine, using the index laws:

<Formula tex="3x \times 4y = 12xy, \qquad 2a \times 5a = 10a^2" block />

Multiply the numbers, then multiply the letters (adding indices for repeats). So a × a = a², but a + a = 2a — keep multiplication and addition straight.

</Step>

<Step title="Recap" kind="recap">

- **Like terms** share the exact same letter part; only they can be combined.
- Add/subtract the **coefficients**; the letter part is unchanged.
- x and x² are **not** alike; neither are x and xy.
- **Multiplying** terms combines numbers and letters (a × a = a²; but a + a = 2a).

</Step>
